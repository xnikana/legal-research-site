Preamble
Signs are critical to identify and promote business, attract visitors and local customers who
otherwise would not stop, add to Barrington’s sense of place, and promote its image as a small
New England coastal town. Barrington’s regulatory signage framework aims to regulate all signs in
both the public and private realms to advance those purposes and protect Barrington’s
appearance, public health, safety, and general welfare.
The aim of the regulatory framework is to:
• Encourage signage that builds on Barrington’s sense of place, especially for signage placed on
public property (e.g., lamppost banners, wayfinding signage, gateway signage).
• Create clear and uniform standards that permit reasonable uses of signs consistent with the
character of Barrington.
• Avoid sign clutter, unsightly, or confusing elements that could distract from Barrington’s
character and appearance and contribute to driving hazards.
• Ensure an appropriate level of review and approval of sign permits, with the least amount of
regulations necessary to achieve these purposes.
Signage Guiding Principles
Building on existing signage practice, Barrington primary signage design principles are:
First, provide adequate opportunities for signage to meet business needs consistent with the
strengthening Barrington’s character. Signage can be a win-win.
Second, without create adverse impacts on the character of exempt temporary signs, window
signage, and very small signage from review and permitting. This does not replace the need for
applicants to comply with zoning requirements.
Third, allow most signs to be approved by Building with no discretionary review when they
comply with requirements. Barrington already generally follows this approach, but there may be
some areas where these approvals can be expanded.
Fourth, in the rare circumstances that require discretionary review and design judgement, the
approval process should be through a Special Use Permit issued by the Technical Review
Committee or the Zoning Board of Appeals. While an applicant can always apply for a
dimensional variance, it is not the appropriate standard of review and it should be an extremely
difficult standard to meet (hardship from unique conditions, least variance that will afford relief,
and not primarily to maximize a financial return).
Fifth, provide better design guidance for discretionary reviews that inform both the regulated
public and regulators.
Fifth, maximize the readability of the ordinance. Consultants should not be needed to
understand the regulations.
Sixth, ensure signage regulations are content neutral and avoid legal exposure.
Finally, create a parallel program to define public signage and build a sense of place.

Free Speech Content of Public and Private Signs
In 2015, the U.S. Supreme Court clarified the limits of communities to regulate signage content
(Reed v. Town of Gilbert, 135 S. Ct. 2218). Reed generally requires heightened scrutiny and a
clear public purpose (e.g., traffic safety) if you must read the sign to know if it is allowed.
Communities can reasonably regulate time, place, and manner of signage, such as sign locations,
size, setbacks, height, lighting, and materials. For example, content-neutral prohibition of off-
premise signs is allowed where on-premise signs are permitted (City of Austin v. Reagan National
Advertising,142 S. Ct. 1464, 2022) similar to previous upholding of billboard bans (Metromedia,
Inc. v. San Diego, 101 S. Ct. 2882, 1981) while courts are suspicious of code that requires a
content analysis (e.g., Young v. Conway, US District [trial] Court, 783 F.Supp.3d 588, 2025).
Content can only be regulated in rare circumstances and content regulations require strict
scrutiny. Barrington can provide an intent or legislative findings on the difference in sign
regulations by district (e.g., village center), by use (e.g., recreation and athletic fields) and explain
the compelling public interest for any sign regulation which is not content neutral. For example,
requiring that road signs on a private road or parking comply with the Manual on Uniform Traffic
Control Devices (MUTCD) would probably pass a challenge with a simple justification.
There are a few areas where Barrington’s sign standards are not content neural (e.g., in business
districts, flush wall “signs should be limited to only the address, the name of the business, a
business logo and/or product or service information”), some of which might not survive a legal
challenge. In some places, it is clear that this is simply an encouragement (e.g., a building
housing more than a single business) but in other cases it is not clear and could go too far.
Given the unsettled law on commercial speech, there are some gray areas where town counsel
needs to be consulted since different attorneys sometimes have distinct perspectives on some
details. Regardless, sign regulations should provide free speech opportunities, such as allowing
temporary political signs and for-sale signs. To minimize content-neutrality challenges, many
communities simply set time limits for the corrugated plastic (e.g., coroplast) signs that
proliferate on front lawns and require that they be on private property outside of the public right-
of-way and tree belt.
It is clear that communities can regulate temporary, permanent, structure, design, and location,
with different standards in different zoning districts or areas. It is also clear that governments do
not have their own free speech rights. They can, for example, install information signs and can
ban private signs on public property, but if anything is allowed on public property within a given
zoning district (e.g., A-Frame signs), then everything is allowed.
Purpose: It is Barrington’s purpose to minimize signage to avoid clutter and distraction, while
allowing sufficient signage in business districts to support businesses and allowing easy
navigation around the community. These regulations or content-neutral except when required for
traffic safety and navigation.

Illumination of Public and Private Signage
Barrington’s current zoning lighting standards detail some requirements (e.g., “Signs may be
illuminated externally by a stationary light of white or off-white color). In other areas, however, the
standards leave too much room for interpretation to be understandable and enforceable (e.g., “The
light illuminating any sign shall be …a sufficiently low level of intensity and brightness that it will not
adversely affect neighboring premises, nor the safe vision of operators of vehicles moving on public
roads and highways”).
Lighting is critical for visibility and safety, but inappropriate lighting adds to visual clutter, harms
wildlife, disturbs sleep, and can add a major safety risk when the contrast between brightly lit areas
and dimly lit areas is too great for human pupils to quickly dilate to allow for adequate night vision.
Purpose: Barrington follows the DarkSky International principles for outdoor and sign lighting:
• Useful and controlled. Light only if and when it is needed. All light should have a clear

Some businesses use lighting to project a sign or symbols on the sidewalk as a way to catch the

Direction and Timing of Signage Lighting (uplighting, downlighting, internal lighting)
Uplighting has the highest level of lighting that escapes into non-target areas and as a result many
communities ban this lighting. Downlighting is the traditional lighting that is most common for
pedestrian scale villages. Internal lighting and even backlighting can work so long if light levels are
strictly controlled. In addition, lighted dynamic signs and highly reflective signs (e.g., those using

Lamppost Banners within public or street rights-of-way
Barrington has debated its lamppost banner program, which is currently on hold. The program can
be restarted however, while avoiding the clutter and damage to the village appearance that can
come from a uncontrolled banner program without limits on sponsorship.
Purpose: The purpose of Barrington Lamppost Banner program is to help define and promote the
Barrington Village Center, with a financially self-supporting program, without distracting excessive
sponsorship messaging. This applies on any public (e.g., bicycle path) and street right-of-way.
Town banners, but not privately owned banners, can be limited to promoting the village center:
• Banners are only permitted when installed by or at the direction of the Town of Barrington.
• Barrington may partner with a vendor (e.g., Barrington Business Association) who is responsible
for designing, printing, installing, and maintaining banners. Costs can be recovered from
banner sponsorship fees, with limits on sponsorship size and term to avoid the proliferation of
advertising that could detract from the overall banner messaging.
• Banners are only allowed in commercially zoned areas within public (state or municipal) rights-
of-way.
• Sponsorship may not cover more than 20% of the banner, with sponsors messaging limited to a
designated section on the bottom of the banner.
• No artificial lighting, except from the street lamp itself. If desired, sponsorship messages shall
be in low chroma (2 and under) or medium chroma (3 and under) colors.
• There are no controls on the content of sponsorship messaging (e.g., words or symbols).
• Banner permits shall be issued for a six-month period with no vesting, allowing the town to
update banners, create new limits, or reserve the right to change approaches, with short term
banners allowed to accommodate special event messaging.
• The town may require multiple banners for each sponsor, such as seasonal banners where the
town messages changes over the course of a year.
Great banners are custom-made for a local identify, not drawn from a catalog.
Some communities (“main street banner project”) engage residents and
artists to create unique banners. Sponsorship should always be subtle (e.g.,

Regulatory Standards and the Rationale for Private Signage
The draft ordinance at the end of this report is designed to incorporate the following regulatory and
design standards.
Readability- to simplify administration and understanding
• Use tables instead of text description when possible
• Add graphic design standards instead of text description when possible
Free speech- comply with federal constitutional free speech requirements
• Remove non-content neutral sign standards in current ordinance with very limited exceptions
(e.g., traffic control devices)
• Signage requirements are limited to the time, place, and manner of allowable signage, based
on the kind of sign, its location, and the related land use, but no content restrictions
• Address allowed art locations, but also preserving content neutrality
Illumination and lighting- ensure measurable and enforceable standards
• Provide adequate lighting for sign visibility and getting attention
• Avoid light trespass, over lighting, or impairing night vision
• Detail the allowable period for artificial signage
• Create standards for emerging technologies (e.g., dynamic signage, highly reflective surfaces)
• Address form and color of sign lighting
• Standards for uplighting, downlighting, internal lighting, and backlighting
Public realm and public ways- detail allowable public signage
• Exempt certain municipal MUTCD, wayfinding, gateway, banner signage and bulletin boards
• Prevent private trespass onto public ways except in very limited defined circumstances
Technical review- ensure equal application of the law and simply administration
• Design standards to inform Building in their review of as-of-right signage
• Design standards consistent with Barrington’s character to inform the Technical Review
Committee and Zoning Board of Appeals in their discretionary reviews
• Design guidelines to inform signage designers of good options
• Allowable differences for shopping plazas and multiple tenant buildings
Sign construction- ensure well maintained and durable signage
• Study and durable sign materials and mountings
• Well-constructed and maintained signage
Temporary signage- ensure manageable and enforceable administration
• Address temporary signage for time-limited installations
• Address A-Frames (sandwich boards)
• Offer alternatives to A-Frames
Detailed standard for each type of commercial signage- clarify existing approaches
• Address all commercial signage types (time, place, and manner) and locations
• Expand locations for allowable signage consistent with preserving the Barrington’s character

Conclusion and Next Steps
Barrington generally has a strong public signage, regulatory, and administrative implementation
programs. There is, however, always room for improvement. Overall, the recommended changes
address three overall opportunities:
• Expand opportunities for business signage to promote town businesses.
• Reduce the uncertainty, discretion, and permit hurdles for applicants.
• Improve the standards so that the undesirable side effects of signage, side effects that are
currently addressed in a discretionary case-by-case review, are prevented.
• Build the institutional memory and documentation so that good administration does not
require as much local knowledge.
