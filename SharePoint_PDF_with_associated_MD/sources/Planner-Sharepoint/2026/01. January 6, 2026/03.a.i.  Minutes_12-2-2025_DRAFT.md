Chair Phipps outlined the procedure for the Planning Board's review of the Comprehensive Permit Preliminary Plan for 25 Watson Avenue. Planner Durfee reviewed the typical hearing process, including applicant presentation, staff input if requested, identification of Board issues, public comment, and Board deliberation.

Samuel Budway (applicant's counsel) introduced the application for a Comprehensive Permit Preliminary Plan for 25 Watson Avenue. He stated that the proposal involved subdividing one lot into eight lots, including twelve cottage-style ownership units on one lot, six single-family lots to be conveyed to the Town, and one lot designated as passive recreational open space. He noted that the project had received conceptual approval at Town Meetings and had previously been reviewed through pre-application and Technical Review Committee (TRC) processes. He invited questions and deferred technical discussion to Janelle Veary.

Ms. Veary (Horsley Witten, expert witness) presented an overview of the site design and TRC comments. She described proposed water and sewer connections to Watson Avenue and Fremont Avenue, including alternatives for serving Parcel 7 to eliminate the need for a pump station and sewer easement. She explained that stormwater would be managed on each individual lot due to site topography, with required systems to be installed by future lot owners.

Ms. Veary reviewed TRC recommendations, including centralized mail delivery and driveway access. She stated that a centralized mailbox and drop-off area were proposed near the site entrance and that delivery vehicles would not enter the driveways. She indicated that a formal turnaround was not proposed, as passenger vehicles could safely turn around, and that the Fire Department had approved the access design. She noted that fire apparatus access requirements were met and that two buildings would be sprinklered due to limited access.

Member Kilmartin asked about ambulance access. Mr. Budway responded that Fire Department life safety review had approved emergency access for both fire and ambulance services.

Chair Phipps expressed confusion regarding the requirement for sprinklers in the last two homes due to fire truck access limitations. Mr. Budway confirmed that the fire truck could not access or turn around for the last two homes, and the fire department had approved plans requiring sprinklers in these homes to ensure fire suppression.

Member Bacher noted that a formal letter documenting the fire department's approval should be obtained. Solicitor Goins stated that letters or reviews from the fire chief could be included as a condition of approval. Ms. Veary indicated that the chief had provided an email approving the plan, but a more formal letter could be requested.

Member Hermann inquired about the 50-foot turning radius. Ms. Veary clarified that it was a 50 foot access radius, explaining the fire truck plan and required door locations. Mr. Budway and Ms. Veary confirmed that the last two buildings did not meet the access circle requirement, necessitating sprinklers. Board members questioned that sprinklers provide effective fire suppression. Solicitor Goins confirmed that a life safety plan sheet was presented in an earlier version of the project. Mr. Budway indicated that the record would be supplemented, and Frank Spinella explained that the NFPA 150-foot limit requires sprinklers beyond that distance, though the fire chief would respond to fires regardless. Mr. Spinella added that the jog in the driveway reflected federal code compliance and could be widened for practical access.

Mr. Spinella explained that the Comprehensive Permit allows lot purchasers to implement approved drainage systems without each seeking CRMC approval, potentially saving costs. Chair Phipps enquired if they can require a purchaser of one of the single family lots to build up the storm drainage system that they have designed. Mr. Spinella noted they can either use the provided approved system they are proposing or go back to CRMC and try to change the design.

Chair Phipps inquired about trash and recycling for the cottage courts. Mr. Spinella indicated that the town was currently negotiating a contract with DPW, but that each cottage would have their own and would get picked up at the end of their driveway.

Chair Phipps inquired about the cottage units, noting concerns regarding size, affordability, accessibility, and the three parking spaces per unit. Mr. Spinella explained that the units were designed at maximum size for engineering and drainage purposes, but could be built smaller, with first-floor bedrooms and accessible features. Ms. Veary clarified that each unit would have two dedicated parking spaces, with three additional guest spaces per driveway. Member Kilmartin asked whether buyers would dictate unit layouts, and Mr. Spinella confirmed units would be sold for buyers to build according to their preferences, emphasizing responsiveness to community needs.

Member Rua inquired about ensuring that affordable units remained substantially equivalent in quality and design despite potential size variations; Mr. Spinella confirmed they would. Member Kilmartin raised questions regarding TRC recommendations for lot buffers, specifically a 30-foot boundary buffer, and whether encroachments were being addressed. Mr. Spinella explained that

existing encroachments by abutting neighbors would remain, with easements or buffers provided by the town as needed, and that the cottage developer would not own those lots. Member Simms asked whether permanent easements could affect lot values; Mr. Spinella suggested the Solicitor and Town Manager evaluate that. Ms. Veary clarified that encroachments were visible on the survey and included landscape features, with display houses adjusted to avoid encroachment.

Member Bacher requested clarification on delivery and service access, including UPS, Amazon, and food delivery. Mr. Spinella explained that a small turnout would accommodate mail and small package deliveries, with larger packages delivered to the units by foot. Ms. Veary described the turnout as a drop-off zone and noted three guest parking spaces per driveway, with access to units via yard paths or stone steps, and optional standard doors to garages. Mr. Spinella emphasized that front doors faced each other to maintain a cottage community design.

Member Bacher expressed concern about pedestrian safety, noting the steep slope to Fremont Avenue and the removal of cross paths from the original plan, which could create hazards for senior residents and visitors. Mr. Spinella distinguished between safety and ease of access, explaining that garages provided safe entry, while grade changes limited additional pathways. Member Kilmartin confirmed that back doors provided access from asphalt surfaces, avoiding lawn shortcuts.

Member Bacher questioned ADA compliance, noting that steps at each unit would require ramps, which could not fit due to slope constraints. Mr. Spinella explained that right-side units had driveunder garages with provision for residential elevators, while left-side units were at grade. Chair Phipps and Member Hermann expressed concerns that groceries and daily needs would require carrying items upstairs, making the design unsuitable for aging residents. Member Bacher recommended adding a sidewalk along Fremont Avenue to connect paved areas safely across the site.

Member Bacher raised concerns regarding deficiencies in the documentation, noting the absence of legal descriptions for the lots proposed in the subdivision. He stated that draft legal descriptions are required by the subdivision checklist, and the current survey plan was difficult to read, with some dimensions obscured. He also expressed concern about the housing documentation for the comprehensive permit, noting that without clear unit sizes, bedroom counts, and architectural details, the Board could not certify compliance with requirements for affordable units, parking counts, or architectural equivalency.

Solicitor Goins clarified that state law requires legal documents only at the final plan stage, superseding the local checklist. Mr. Budway explained that legal descriptions could not be prepared until engineering plan dimensions were finalized and approved. He noted that the plan included parcels one through five, parcel six for the cottages, and parcel seven for single-family lots, with dimensions indicated. Mr. Spinella added that only one note partially obscured a lot square footage, that this was a preliminary review, and that the state law governing comprehensive permits superseded local checklist requirements; no waivers were being requested.

Solicitor Goins cited R.I. Gen. Laws §45-23-39, which requires submission of legal documents and easements at the final plan stage. Chair Phipps acknowledged the Board received the solicitor's opinion and the explanation provided, confirming that draft legal documents were not required at this stage.

He explained that the development met affordability requirements, with five affordable units across the total number of units, equating to approximately 27.7%, which qualified under state standards and was confirmed by Rhode Island Housing. **a. Discussion and Action: Comprehensive Plan 2024-2025 Updates (Standing Agenda Item): Subject to Availability:**

#### **i. Discussion of Expired Plan**

Solicitor Goins provided an update regarding the status of the town's comprehensive plan. She noted that the state maintains a webpage through the Division of Statewide Planning, which lists all 39 municipalities, including links to each municipality's comprehensive plan, the dates of municipal and state approval, and the expiration of state approval. She reported that the town's current comprehensive plan was approved by the municipality on March 25, 2015, and by the state on May 11, 2015. State approval is valid for ten years, meaning the plan's state approval expired on May 10, 2025. She explained that while the plan remains binding on the town and continues to guide local land use decisions, it is no longer binding on the state. State agencies are no longer required to follow the plan, although in practice, the state may still exercise discretion.

Solicitor Goins emphasized that the expired status of the comprehensive plan is significant for comprehensive permit applications, as municipalities have less flexibility with an expired stateapproved plan. She reassured the Board that the town is actively working on updating the plan and is not among municipalities with "stale" plans—a term she clarified is informal, not legal. She referenced a 2023 law, effective in 2024, stating that comprehensive plans older than 12 years cannot be used to deny land use decisions, noting that the town will adopt its new plan before reaching that threshold.
