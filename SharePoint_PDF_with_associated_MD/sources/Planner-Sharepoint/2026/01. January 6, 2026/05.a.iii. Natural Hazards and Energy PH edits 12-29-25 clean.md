The risk will only grow in the years to come due to rising sea levels already affecting coastal communities, along with other natural hazards intensified by the effects of climate change including increased incidence of heat waves and more frequent and intense precipitation.

#### **EXISTING CONDITIONS**

### **Community Context**

The entirety of Barrington falls within two miles of the shoreline, such that many areas in town are vulnerable to sea level rise and intensified storm surges. The town's 19.6-mile coastline stretches across two peninsulas bounded by Narragansett Bay and the Palmer and Warren Rivers. The Barrington River bisects these land masses, connected by the Central and Barrington River Bridges. Geographically, the western neck abuts East Providence, while the eastern neck abuts Swansea, Massachusetts.

As described in detail in the Land Use chapter, the primary land use within town is residential, accounting for more than half of total land area, while commercial and institutional uses are comparatively small. Wetlands, forests, agricultural lands, and other open spaces – natural assets are increasingly crucial to climate resilience – comprise about 25 percent of land within town. Less than 10 percent of the land within Barrington remains available for development, such that much of future growth is likely to take the form of redevelopment of existing parcels.

With less than 2 percent of Rhode Island's population, Barrington's contribution to emissions that drive climate change is relatively small measured against the state overall. However, suburban communities such as Barrington have an outsized impact measured on a per-capita basis compared to more urbanized areas due to low-density land use patterns, auto-dependency, larger houses requiring more energy and other factors, according to University of California-Berkeley researcher[s](#page-1-0)<sup>2</sup> . The *Ready and Resilient Barrington* climate action plan established Barrington's baseline greenhouse gas emissions estimate that will measure progress cutting emissions going forward. Addressing the community's major sources of greenhouse gases – through improved building efficiency, sustainable transport, and waste reduction – will be crucial for meeting Barrington's climate goals.

## **Natural Hazards**

The Town's Hazard Mitigation and Flood Management Plan (2022) identifies the following hazards as having a greater likelihood of occurrence or producing a greater impact on the community:

- Flood-Related Hazards (inland flooding/dam failure, coastal flooding/storm surge, coastal erosion)
- Wind-Related Hazards (high winds, thunderstorms/lightning/hail, tornadoes)
- Winter-Related Hazards (heavy snow, ice, extreme cold)
- Infectious Disease (vector-borne diseases transmitted by ticks and mosquitoes, contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses or coronaviruses)
- Other Hazards (earthquakes, extreme heat, drought, brush fires)

# *Natural Hazard Identification*

Flood-Related Hazards. Flooding, the accumulation of a substantial amount of water in areas that are typically dry, generally results from heavy rainfall, storm surges and melting snow. With approximately seven square miles of inland water, including lakes, ponds, watercourses, inlets, and coves, Barrington's extensive inland water as well as coastal exposure make it particularly susceptible to flooding.

Based on GIS data analyzed for the 2022 Hazard Mitigation and Flood Management Plan, more than 40 percent of Barrington's land area falls within the Special Flood Hazard Area:

- Thirty-six percent of all parcels are in "AE" zones, which would be inundated in the event a storm with a 1% annual chance of occurring (also known as a "100-year storm").
- Six percent of all parcels are in "VE" zones (also known as "Velocity" zones), where damage from a 100-year storm could also include wave damage.

In addition, 24 percent of land area is within "X" zones, at risk of inundation in the event of a storm with a 0.2% annual chance of occurring (also known as a "Five Hundred Year Storm"). While precise flood event prediction remains challenging, FEMA provides this guidance: properties located within Zone AE and Zone VE flood zones face a 26 percent probability of experiencing a flood event over the 30-year span of a typical mortgage.

• *Coastal Flooding.* Coastal flooding is related to wind hazards, as this type of event is often a result of storm surge and wind-driven waves, which erode the coastline. These conditions are produced by tropical storms and hurricanes during the summer and fall. Nor'easters and other large coastal storms (extra-tropical storms) are produced during the fall, winter, and spring. Storm surge – the abnormal rise in water level caused by the wind and pressure forces of a hurricane or nor'easter – may push sea water up coastal rivers and inlets, blocking the downstream flow of inland runoff. Prolonged storm surges can persist through multiple high tides, severely threatening Barrington's expansive coastline.

Recent incidents of severe coastal flooding include winter storms that hit Barrington in back-to-back Decembers, in 2022 and 2023. Impacts from these storms included inundation of low-lying roads throughout town, including sections of Wampanoag Trail and County Road, and flooding of the RIDOT park-and-ride lot at the Congregational Church and parks located along the shore, including Town Beach, Latham Park and Walker Farm (**see Figure XX**). Cleanup of debris deposited by the storms onto roads and parks and other public properties is handled by Department of Public Works crews.

Hurricanes and tropical storms that have impacted the region include several such events that produced significant storm surge impacts. The most substantial storm surge was from the hurricane of 1938 (reaching heights of 12 to 15 feet) and Hurricane Carol of 1954 (up to 14 feet), both of which resulted in widespread destruction in the community.

With an average of six Atlantic hurricanes annually, Rhode Island faces a 6 percent chance of a hurricane strike each year. Depending on the hurricane's path, impacts to Barrington could be magnified by Narragansett Bay's funneling effect. Barrington's low, coastal location makes it acutely vulnerable; its small size means most properties face hurricane impacts. Even Category 1-2 hurricanes could severely inundate large areas and cause significant coastal erosion along southern and western shores. In addition, as sea level rise increases, weaker storms in the future could cause greater amounts of coastal flooding compared to stronger storms today.

Hurricane evacuation zones and routes are depicted on **Map XX**. All routes pass through a Zone A evacuation area (evacuation recommended prior to a Category 1 or 2 storm), except for portions of the County Road-Willett Avenue route that exits town through Riverside.

• *Inland Flooding.* Flooding has the potential to impact Barrington residents in many of the low-lying areas of town. Significant inland flooding could lead to extensive damage to structures and personal property, and flooded roadways can cause dangerous

conditions for motorists. Additionally, this type of flooding has the potential to disrupt water, stormwater, and sewer systems and carry toxic material or contaminants thereby spreading pollution, disrupting wildlife, and standing water following a flooding event can provide a conducive environment for breading mosquitos.

While Barrington lacks any significant non-tidal rivers, there are creeks and streams that represent a risk for riverine flooding for inland neighborhoods that are removed from the coast. These include:

- Annawamscutt Brook, which flows south from Riverside in East Providence, through the Riverside Plaza shopping center, into town through neighborhoods and Haines Park, underneath Bay Spring Avenue via a culvert, and past Barrington Cove Apartments where it ends at Allin's Cove.
- Mussachuck Creek, which flows westerly for about a mile from Brickyard Pond to Narragansett Bay, under Middle Highway, Washington Road and a small bridge near the bay constructed as a private driveway across the creek.
- Hampden Meadows Greenbelt streams and drainage channels. The main channel flows north-south, and near the Greenbrier Drive/New Meadow Road intersection flows through a culvert into the Barrington River.
- An unnamed creek/stream originates from Barrington River/Osamequin Park and connects to Upper Echo Lake (Little Echo), passing through a forested wetland to the west of the St. Andrews School campus, underneath Federal Road via a culvert west of the Public Safety Building, behind Atlantic Crossing and to the south of the Middle School property.

Localized flooding from storms and heavy rains often lasts just a few hours; however, during extensive rain events, water can extend for days. March 2010 stands out, with storms causing widespread state flooding, culminating in a Presidential Major Disaster Declaration for Rhode Island, including Bristol County. Barrington saw substantial inundation throughout town due to prolonged, heavy rains during the storm. The Fire Department responded to 93 requests for basement pump-outs. Persistent precipitation from multiple rain events over time can also create flooding issues for a community. For example, according to data from the National Weather Service, the period from December 1, 2023, to May 30, 2024, was the wettest six-month period in history for the Providence area, totaling over 42 inches of precipitation. With rising water tables due to excessive rain, the Fire Department was called to pump out 13 basements, according to records from March 1 through May 31, 2024.

In the Northeast, according to the US Department of Agriculture, the Northeast has already experienced "pronounced changes in climate" since the mid-1990s, with a 71% increase in the frequency of extreme precipitation events—the most of any region of the country. Climate change models predict New England will continue to see increases in precipitation over time due to rising global temperatures.

Winter-Related Hazards. Major winter storms and extreme weather events – heavy snow, blizzards, nor'easters, significant accumulations of ice and extreme cold – can cause widespread disruption, injuries, and substantial economic losses. Climate models suggest warming winters and wetter, but more frequent, winter storms[.](#page-6-0) 3

Over the past 100 years, the most significant nor'easter to impact Rhode Island was the Blizzard of 1978. The snow accumulation was up to 4 feet and wind speeds exceeded 60mph. Throughout the state more than 10,000 people were stranded on roadways, 232 injuries and 26 deaths were attributed to the storm, and damage totaled more than \$15 million. More recently, the state experienced a powerful nor'easter in February 2013, known as Winter Storm DR-4107. As a result, several areas in Barrington experienced power loss for as long as a week. Rhode Island received \$1 million in reimbursements from the Federal Emergency Management System (FEMA) for snow removal costs from the storm. Barrington received \$75,500 in FEMA reimbursements for DR-4107.

Another significant nor'easter, Winter Storm DR-4212, occurred in January 2015. It inundated Rhode Island with roughly 16 inches of snow, prompting a Presidential Major Disaster Declaration for Bristol and other Rhode Island Counties, and caused damages statewide of approximately \$8.4 million. Barrington received \$46,000 in reimbursements from FEMA for this storm.

Extreme Heat. Extreme heat – prolonged summer temperatures exceeding 10 degrees above the average combined with humidity – is a serious hazard. Rhode Island has warmed approximately 4 degrees since 1900, experiencing more hot days and record warm nights recently. Climate models forecast increasing extreme heat events, which threaten human health, particularly for outdoor workers, children, and seniors. Furthermore, heightened demand for air conditioning during heatwaves can overload power grids, risking widespread outages. Such power loss would critically exacerbate health risks, highlighting a dual challenge for community resilience.

In forecast periods of extreme heat and/or humidity, Barrington Public Library and the lobby of the Barrington Public Safety Building function as community cooling centers.

Droughts. NOAA's modeling projects increased annual and extreme precipitation for Rhode Island. However, higher temperatures will also intensify droughts by increasing evaporation, leading to faster soil and surface water depletion despite more rain. It would take an extensive drought to impair the Bristol County Water Authority's ability to supply potable water to Barrington. Nonetheless this risk must be considered given future climate uncertainties.

Low Risk Hazards. Barrington generally experiences low risk from several hazards. Earthquakes are minor due to no major fault lines. Tornadoes are rare, though Barrington experienced an F1 in 2008 causing minor tree damage. Wildfires are uncommon, confined mostly to brush fires in scattered wooded areas. Hailstorms are infrequent, with only four recorded in Bristol County between 1950-2023. Dams in Barrington are classified as Low Hazard by the Rhode Island Department of Environmental Management in its 2024 annual dam safety report. There are three dams in town - the Echo Lake and RI Country Club Dams, both earthen structures, regulate water flow and are situated in the 1% annual Flood Zone. A third, unclassified private dam on Annawamscutt Brook, also in the 1% annual zone, is situated adjacent to the parking lot of an elderly housing complex where its headwater is lower than the building.

*Natural Hazard Preparedness*

- Location of Emergency Center, Town Facilities. All of Barrington's municipal operations, including Police, Fire, Town Hall, and DPW, are located outside 1% annual flood zones and areas impacted by Category 1-2 hurricane surge. The Barrington Public Safety Building, however, is vulnerable to flooding in a rarer Category 3 or higher storm. As Barrington has no Red Cross-supported shelter available within town limits, the Town will direct residents to the closest shelter in the event of an evacuation – East Providence High School, which is about a six-mile drive from the center of town.
- Evacuation Plan*.* In the event of a major hurricane, a full evacuation of the town may be necessary. Owing to the town's topography, in the event of a storm with a 1% annual chance of occurring, the unflooded sections of town east of Brickyard Pond and south of Nayatt Road will effectively form an island, cut off from emergency services. Other neighborhoods not flooded may also become unreachable, presenting unacceptable risks to human life. Rhode Island has a robust, coordinated hurricane warning system. In the event of a major hurricane, a full town evacuation would likely be initiated by the Governor. In this scenario, an optional, advisory town evacuation for vulnerable populations such as seniors or families with young children might first be issued. Town evacuation would be managed on a timetable requiring completion before the start of inclement weather. As of September 2025, 3,220 residents or businesses had signed up for Barrington CodeRED emergency alerts. The Town, along with the state, is in the process of revising its evacuation plan.
- Posted Evacuation Routes. Barrington has evacuation route signs posted throughout town indicating the optional route to follow in the event of a hurricane/flooding (routes are shown on **Map XX**). Extensive sections of several major routes—including County Road, Sowams Road and New Meadow Road—are within the 1% and 0.2% annual chance flood zones. A portable, solar-powered message board has been purchased by the Town that can be deployed to support preparedness messaging and evacuation response.
- Town Website and Social Media*.* Throughout the year, the Town utilizes its social media platforms for relaying advisements and warnings of potentially hazardous weather conditions. During the Atlantic hurricane season (June through November), the Town makes weekly "Hurricane Preparedness Wednesday" social media re-postings of hurricane and flood preparedness messaging from FEMA, RIEMA and other authorities. A one-stop flood resources webpage on the Town website is in development.

## **Climate Mitigation**

#### Barrington's Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Addressing climate change at its source requires Barrington to reduce Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions, primarily from fossil fuels used in vehicles and buildings, and landfill waste. To craft an actionable strategy, as part of the development of the 2024 *Ready & Resilient Barrington* climate action plan, the Town undertook a thorough community-wide GHG inventory and baseline assessment. This data-driven approach allowed the Town to accurately identify Barrington's key emission sources and prioritize the most impactful opportunities for reduction.

**Figure XX: Barrington's 2023 Community GHG Emissions by Sector**

*Source: Ready & Resilient Barrington Plan*

According to a 2023 baseline energy assessment, Barrington emitted 134,524 MTCO2e (metric tons of carbon dioxide equivalent) of greenhouse gases. Buildings fueled 53 percent of these emissions, with vehicle transportation adding 39 percent. The remaining 8 percent came from solid waste and wastewater. The Plan states that addressing these major sources – through improved building efficiency, sustainable transport, and waste reduction – will be crucial for meeting Barrington's climate goals.

• *Buildings*

In 2023, about 39 percent of Barrington's emissions came from the fossil fuels used to heat and cool its residential and commercial buildings. As of 2024, less than 1% of residential and commercial buildings were electrified (e.g. heated by air-source heat pumps). In addition, there were approximately 1,200 underground home oil storage tanks in the floodplain, posing environmental risks in the event of a major storm.

Barrington has seen a steady increase in rooftop solar installations over the last ten years. Fostered by federal and state programs and Town support, more homes and businesses are embracing solar PV. From 2015-2025, there were [#] permits issued by the Town for residential solar installation. While significant, this rate of adoption is not on track to reach the climate action plan's 2030 targets of 25 percent of potential installed rooftop capacity by 2030, and 100 percent capacity by 2050.

Rhode Island recently adopted the 2024 International Energy Conservation Code. Key changes to the code include more stringent energy performance requirements, decreased allowable air leakage, and new requirements for electrical readiness. Residential buildings must meet stricter energy conservation measures for building envelopes, such as higher R values in walls and ceilings in the prescriptive method of compliance. Installation of high efficiency mechanical equipment, renewable energy resources and a credit-based system can help to reduce R values.
