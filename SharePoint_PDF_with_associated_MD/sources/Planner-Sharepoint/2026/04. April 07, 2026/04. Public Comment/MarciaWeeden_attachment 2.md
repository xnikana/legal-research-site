Re: Proposed Additional Student Resource Officer
To whom it may concern:
It was brought to my attention tonight by Steven Brown of the RI ACLU that the Barrington
School Committee is considering obtaining a second Student Resource Officer for the Barrington Middle
School.
As a quality assurance professional who has literally written a peer-reviewed book regarding
how to identify impacts of proposed actions and consequently, the options for preventing problems and
achieving the best outcomes, Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEAs) for Small Business Owners and
Non-Engineers: Determining and Preventing What Can Go Wrong, may I explain how a second resource
office strikes my professional opinion?
That approach is called “fire-fighting” in the quality field. It is recognized as a highly visible
action that gives a misleading and short-term satisfaction that something pro-active has been done,
often expressed as, “Finally! Somebody did something!” It is also recognized as not addressing the root
cause. Unless the root cause is addressed, the effort is merely putting out a fire that remains smoldering.
Because the problem has not been effectively identified, addressed, or eradicated, it will erupt and occur
again.
For the budget watchers, fire-fighting is financially very costly in the long run. In time, it may be
regarded as throwing good money after bad.

Teenagers go through phases where they often to do not know what is going on within
themselves or what is happening in their world. While it may seem logical to send them to school
counselors or personal therapists, what may be more helpful is to create something like training on life,
e.g., “What you’re encountering as a teen,” or “What to expect as you move through your teen years.” At
the very least, this training opens a dialogue between the teens and the adults involved in their lives. It
also empowers teens to take better charge of themselves, make better decisions, or ask for professional
help.
If the Town is interested, I can train school committee members and anyone else on how to
perform FMEAs. My book contains practical, easy-to-understand examples that walk users through
understanding and utilizing a FMEA worksheet. FMEAs are a great tool. I firmly believe it is better to
teach people how to fish than to feed them a meal, which is why I wrote the book.
Furthermore, if the Town is interested, after the training, I can guide the efforts to find the
solutions that would have the best long-term returns for the Town both on the school levels as well as
developing its young citizens. It isn’t just the students who could benefit from training and new outlooks.
Why not the Town’s employees and volunteers as well? It would make sense to include representatives
from the BPD as well.
Student resource officers are not a bad idea, and from what I have read, many students have
responded positively to their presence. However, the position short-changes the police as well as the
adults and students of Barrington because it is a band-aid. People want to help those who need help.
People are open to learning new tools if these tools are practical and they can see positive results.
