## From The Desk of

## **Jacob N. S. Brier, MBA, PHR®**

Dear Chair Phipps and the members of the Barrington Planning Board,

Thank you for your dedication to Barrington. Your efforts in service to the town will impact the community for decades — maybe longer. I appreciate the difficult position you are in with regard to the Comprehensive permit application. I am writing to share some questions that I think it is important to ask of the property owner's team, and to share my personal opinion of how the proposed project could be reduced to better comply with the needs identified in our Comprehensive Plan. I know that my personal opinion, individually, isn't relevant, but share it because I hope it will be aligned with other comments you've heard from our neighbors and it could support the basis for a proposed alternative/negotiation with the property owner.

## First, the questions:

- Has RIDOT commented on the proposal's impact on Route 114?
  - When I need to head to Providence, I merge onto 114S at Old County Rd and have to immediately cross 2 lanes to take the turnaround to 114N. With the current volume of cars merging from Primrose Hill Rd, this is already a challenging maneuver. With an additional 350 families living up that road, it is likely to become even more hazardous.
- What methodology was used to determine the impact on school enrollment?
  - The proposed 344 units represent an increase of more than 5% of the current homes in town.
  - It is most reasonable to assume the impact on the schools would be an approximate 5% increase in enrollment
- What are the expectations of parking enforcement?
  - Currently, Barrington Police Department has a relatively relaxed parking enforcement policy — few tickets are issued, outside of beach-goers on occasion in "no parking" zones. I have never heard of a person being ticketed for parking poorly in a permissible location (I don't doubt it's ever happened, but am confident it is infrequent).
  - The parking spaces proposed are significantly smaller than typical, causing a requested variance. Average car sizes have typically increased in recent decades and it should be expected that many cars will be parked outside their designated spaces, simply because they don't fit. This has a ripple effect on adjacent spaces.
  - This could present a significant safety concern as well as being detrimental to quality of life; necessitating higher enforcement.
  - Higher enforcement also harms quality of life, and would either cost the town more in wages or limit the police's ability to maintain the current level of service for the rest of town.
- In what ways will the property owner benefit the town?

- As a private owner, the owner is within their right to develop anything legal and to use their property as they deem fit. This permit request goes beyond that, asking for accommodations by the Town.
- Each accommodation comes with a cost that is borne by Barrington's residents, businesses and visitors. How will these people be made whole?
- Of note, while the proposal does substantially increase Barrington's inventory of dead-restricted LMI housing, it accomplishes this by meeting the *minimum* required in the town's local ordinance (this is just one area where the property owner could give back to the town in recognition of the accommodations for which they are asking

## My ideal situation:

- The total unit number would be reduced as a result of meeting the following design features (not a pre-determined "lower" number than the current total, which is too high and forcing a lack of the below-listed features)
  - Streets and sidewalks would aligned with the town's Complete Streets guidelines and with the NFPA guidelines
  - Green spaces are included, with at least one park of 1,500 sq ft each (this is below the minimum of 9sq ft per person recommended by the World Health Organization)
  - The original portion of the main house of Belton Court is preserved and renovated, used as a multi-purpose facility, including residences, businesses and potentially an event venue (bonus points if it can done as a public-private partnership and/or with a nonprofit organization)
- 33% of each type of unit is designed for and restricted to occupants/owners who are 55 years of age or older (age of at least one owner/lessee at the date of occupancy)
- 33% of total units are 30yr deed-restricted Affordable Housing
  - Including even splits along these types:
    - ⅓ each for single-family, townhouses, and apartments
    - ⅓ each for Age-restricted units and open-age units
  - Half of Affordable units are deed-restricted to 80% AMI

Thank you, again, for your time and dedication to our community. Please feel free to follow up if you have any questions about my input.
