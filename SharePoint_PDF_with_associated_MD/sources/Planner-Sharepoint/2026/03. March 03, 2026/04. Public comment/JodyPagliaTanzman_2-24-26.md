**Date: February 23, 2026**

**Subject:** Urgent Concerns Regarding the Proposed Belton Court Development

Dear Mr. Durfee and Ms. Elliot and members of the Planning Board:

I am writing as one of many concerned citizens regarding the proposed Belton Court Development.

Eleven years ago, when I first moved to Barrington, I "discovered" the magnificent Gilded Age mansion, Belton Court. Entranced and intrigued—this building rivals any of the famed Newport mansions—I contacted the town. Why wasn't this architectural gem cared for, offering tours or hosting events like the mansions in Newport? Even Linden Place and Blithewold in Bristol, while beautiful, do not compare to Belton Court in scope or interior artistry. I was told an out-oftown company owned it, and, off the record, the town was aware the owner was allowing the building to deteriorate but hadn't acted.

Here we are, in 2026, and the situation has only worsened. The shell company ShineHarmony continues to neglect—and in effect, demolish—an irreplaceable part of Barrington's history. Belton Court has been on the National Register of Historic Places since the late 1970s. Currently its windows are boarded, the roof is riddled with holes, and downspouts are pointed into the remaining windows, accelerating deterioration. Police are called to the building not infrequently—presumably at the town's expense—because the owner is neither present nor active. To make matters worse, the owner has failed to pay property taxes, now owing the town more than \$1 million. This is not the conduct of a responsible steward of our community.

Yet now, the town is being asked to allow this same individual to fundamentally alter Barrington—for the worse. The ShineHarmony proposal for a 344-unit housing development is excessive. Its density would transform our suburban town into the scale of a small city, changing the landscape of Barrington forever.

## **Safety Concerns**

The safety of our citizens must come first. The town is currently considering speed cameras near schools due to speeding and risks to children during drop-off and pick-up. Would adding 600+ cars from the Belton Court development—directly adjacent to Primrose Hill School—improve safety, or make it worse? Common sense suggests the latter.

A review of the *Barrington Times* police blotter shows at least two car accidents reported nearly every week on County Road near the Primrose Hill turn. Adding 600+ additional cars from this development will almost certainly increase accidents. And the impact doesn't stop there: the Shaw's shopping plaza is already difficult to navigate, especially with the Starbucks drivethrough, and summer traffic on the one lane route 114 through Warren and Bristol is already congested. Does adding more cars to these roads make life less stressful or safer—or more chaotic and hazardous?

Parking within the development is equally concerning. ShineHarmony's plan appears to provide too few spaces, and the ones included are narrow and undersized. Most families now drive SUVs and minivans—not compact cars. Where will residents and their guests park? And what about large delivery trucks, as well as mail trucks and the ever-present food delivery vehicles? Will they double park along the streets? And how will pedestrians safely navigate through the property? Most importantly, in emergencies, how will ambulances and other emergency vehicles get through quickly when seconds matter?

## **Community and Recreation**

Barrington has a proud tradition of athletics and active citizenship, yet we continue to lack adequate athletic fields. The Belton Court property is a 36-acre parcel of land that could be used to provide fields, walking trails, dog areas, green space to support pollinators, and areas for the community to gather. Why not include this in a thoughtful, balanced development plan rather than crowding hundreds of units into one dense complex?

## **Environmental Impact**

The proposed development's density will create copious amounts stormwater runoff that will overflow into the surrounding neighborhoods. The development plan proposes bio-retention areas, massive amounts of pervious pavement, and artificial grass to manage it—but these measures are unsatisfactory. The bio-retention areas are little more than ditches alongside the sidewalks—a recipe for pedestrian and bicycle accidents. And the combination of plastic grass and asphalt is grossly environmentally unfriendly, undermining the green benefits promised.

## **Preservation of Belton Court**

ShineHarmony has proposed demolishing the main mansion, claiming it is "not a practical business undertaking" to restore. They cite a cost estimate of \$65 million (plus 25% in additional project costs) to convert the building into 30 units. This argument is misleading: it covers only a conversion to specific housing units, not a restoration, and comes from a developer with an incentive to make the property seem undesirable. The high cost quoted is also a direct result of years of willful neglect. The developer cannot now claim repair is too expensive when they created the problem. The town should require a third-party assessment to establish the true cost of restoring of the building.

Barrington deserves better. This is a once-in-a-generation opportunity to protect a national treasure, support our schools and recreational programs, and plan responsibly for the town's future. Allowing this proposal as currently designed would do the opposite: it would endanger safety, harm the environment, strain infrastructure, and destroy a historic landmark.

As we celebrate our nation's 250th semi-quincentennial this year, reflecting on our past—and how we arrived at 2026—is essential to shaping our future. How will we honor both our national and local achievements over the last two and a half centuries? And what will our future selves and our children say about the choices we make today?

Will they applaud the construction of a densely packed housing complex? Or will they celebrate our foresight in preserving and restoring a remarkable National landmark while thoughtfully developing an appropriately scaled neighborhood—one that includes athletic fields, environmentally responsible green space, and welcoming places for the town to come together?

I respectfully urge the Planning Board to consider what legacy we intend to leave. Please reject the current proposal and work toward a development plan that preserves Belton Court, prioritizes safety, respects our environment, and serves the entire community.

Respectfully,

Jody Paglia Tanzman
