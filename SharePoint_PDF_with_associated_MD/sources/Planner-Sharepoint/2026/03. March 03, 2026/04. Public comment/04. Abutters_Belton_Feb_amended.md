### **Zoning, Comprehensive Plan, and Legal Conflicts**

Under Rhode Island law, municipalities are legally required to ensure that land-use decisions are consistent with adopted zoning ordinances and the municipality's Comprehensive Plan, pursuant to R.I. General Laws §45-22.2 and §45-24.

Rhode Island courts have repeatedly held that the Comprehensive Plan is not advisory, but rather a controlling document that zoning decisions must conform to.

- In Town of Coventry Zoning Bd. of Review v. Omni Development Corp., the Rhode Island Supreme Court emphasized that zoning decisions must be consistent with the Comprehensive Plan and that departures undermine the integrity of municipal planning.
- In West v. McDonald, the Court cautioned against zoning actions that favor a single parcel or developer at the expense of surrounding neighborhoods, reinforcing judicial skepticism toward spot zoning and preferential treatment.

This proposal conflicts with these legal principles in several critical ways:

- Inconsistency with the Comprehensive Plan: Barrington's Comprehensive Plan emphasizes controlled growth, preservation of neighborhood character, protection of historic resources, and development aligned with existing infrastructure capacity. A 344-unit high-density complex directly contradicts these goals.
- Excessive Density and Scale: The proposed density is incompatible with surrounding zoning districts and established residential patterns. Approving such a departure would undermine the purpose and intent of Barrington's zoning ordinance.
- Risk of De Facto Spot Zoning: Granting the approvals or variances necessary to allow this development would effectively single out one property for preferential treatment. Rhode Island courts have consistently found such actions legally suspect when they do not advance the public welfare.
- Failure to Promote Public Health, Safety, and Welfare: Rhode Island zoning law requires that land-use decisions promote the public convenience and welfare. As discussed below, the traffic, emergency access, infrastructure, and environmental impacts of this proposal fail to meet that standard.
- Precedent and Cumulative Impact: Approval would weaken zoning protections town-wide and create precedent for future high-density developments inconsistent with Barrington's planning framework.

### **Aesthetic and Community Character Impacts**

Barrington is defined by modestly scaled development, architectural cohesion, and historic continuity. A high-density apartment complex of this magnitude is wholly inconsistent with the town's established aesthetic and would permanently alter the visual and cultural fabric of the surrounding neighborhood.

### Traffic, Safety, and Municipal Service Concerns

- Traffic and Roadway Safety: Beaver Road and surrounding streets were not designed to support the traffic volume generated by hundreds of residential units. Increased congestion, unsafe turning conditions, and heightened risk to pedestrians, cyclists, and school traffic are foreseeable and unavoidable.
- Emergency Services Impact: A development of this size will significantly increase demand on police, fire, and EMS services. Rhode Island courts have recognized that developments which strain emergency access and response times raise legitimate public safety concerns.
- Schools and Infrastructure: The project will place additional strain on schools, water, sewer, and municipal services, with no clear demonstration that long-term costs will not be shifted to taxpayers.

### **Environmental and Stormwater Impacts**

- Stormwater Runoff and Flooding: High-density development substantially increases impervious surface area, elevating risks of flooding, erosion, and downstream impacts. These risks are particularly serious in Barrington due to its coastal geography and vulnerable drainage systems.
- Environmental Stewardship and Climate Resilience: Large-scale development without demonstrated climate resilience planning conflicts with state and local environmental protection goals and long-term sustainability objectives.

Barrington is not simply a location to grow a portfolio. It is a historic community built on careful planning, legal consistency, and respect for residents. Allowing a profit-driven holding company with no meaningful ties to the town to proceed with a development that conflicts with zoning law, the Comprehensive Plan, Rhode Island case law, infrastructure capacity, and environmental realities would undermine the principles the Town Council is obligated to uphold.

I respectfully urge the Planning Board to deny approval of this proposal and to protect Barrington's zoning integrity, historic character, and long-term public welfare.

### By Christian Silvia

As Tiverton braces for the possible influx of hundreds of new housing units, residents, legislators and members of the town council and planning board want state legislators to change a state law they believe strips them of their power to direct the town's future and reject proposals that might otherwise be denied on legitimate grounds.

Town planners are currently reviewing five "comprehensive permit" applications for housing developments that, if ultimately approved here, would see 737 new homes and condos built across town (see sidebar, bottom of story).

Comprehensive permits are a streamlined development tool allowed under the state's Low and Moderate Income and Housing Act. In towns like Tiverton, where less than 10 percent of the housing stock is deed-restricted as "affordable," comprehensive permit applications allow developers to side-step some zoning and other local requirements, if they set aside at least 25 percent of their planned units for those of low to moderate income. Tiverton's percentage of affordable housing units sits at 4.26 percent.

Those concerned about the law's impact here say it has the potential to affect nearly every aspect of life in town — population density, school populations, parking and traffic, environmental issues and development that isn't consistent with the town's Comprehensive Community Plan.

### Petition drive, and planning ideas

With little means for town officials to reject proposals they don't believe fit the town, planning board and council members have asked for help from Tiverton's legislative delegation. And residents are also stepping in.

Highland Road resident Stu Gilfillen, chairman of the Tiverton Recreation Committee, started a petition drive earlier this month which seeks changes to the state law. About a week after it was started, the petition has 300 signatures.

"We need more affordable housing in our community, but I believe there are better, more collaborative ways to accomplish that goal," Gilfillen said.

Last week, planning board members went over a host of strategies they hope will help. They include:

• Regular reports (more than one per year) from the state on where the town sits with regard to its affordable housing percentage;

- An increase in the number of units that must be designated affordable under comprehensive permits, from the current 25 percent to 50 percent, as recommended by board chairwoman Patricia Hilton;
- The reduction or elimination of "state density bonuses," which eliminate restrictions on how many housing units can be built on particular parcels, in exchange for at least 25 percent of the dwelling units being designated affordable. In a letter to the town council, board members wrote that "our underlying zoning is based on the amount of density determined to be sustainable by the carrying capacity of the land and should not be superseded by a one-size-fits-all state regulation;
- A change in state law that gives Rhode Island residents priority to purchase units deemed affordable.
- "I think that if we're going to address the community needs, including the greater Rhode Island community, I think the affordable units should be restricted to Rhode Island residency," Hilton said.
- Changes that would require more parking than comprehensive permits applications are now required to provide.
- To avoid "land banking," the practice of purchasing developable land but then sitting on it for years with little action, members want vesting and construction periods required under state law to be considerably shortened.

"If you're building 30 units, it should not take you five years to build the thing," Hilton said during the planning board meeting.

### Legislators working on it

The four members of Tiverton's legislative delegation (Representatives Michelle McGaw and Jay Edwards, and Senators Louis DiPalma and Walter Felag) told town council members Monday evening that they are working on bills they hope will address many of the suggestions previously made by planning board and council members.

Specifically, legislators said they are focusing on three key areas:

- Raising the minimum percentage of affordable housing units in any new development from 25 to 50 percent. A bill to that affect, sponsored by Sen. Felag, has already been introduced.
- McGaw and Edwards spoke in favor of changing vesting and construction windows within state law to avoid the 'land banking' referred to by planning board chairwoman Hilton;
- And legislators said they will look at how to account for mobile homes in future developments. McGaw and Edwards are expected to introduce a bill that would allow manufactured homes to be credited toward affordable housing requirements. Currently, such homes are not credited towards developers' affordable housing percentages.

Felag added that Section 8 homes, which are owned or rented by residents with financial assistance from the federal government's housing choice voucher program, will be included towards affordable housing going forward. He said that should bring the town's percentage of affordable housing units up.

### 'Affordable' housing developments: Where are they?

Five projects that together include 737 new housing units are currently under review by the Tiverton Planning Board. They include:

- The Pocasset Reserve Development, also known as Tiverton Heights, at 787 Fish Road. Proposed by Harking Homes LLC, the development would include 275 housing units, 69 of which are designated as affordable.
- The Sunset Ridge Development, 439 Main Road. Proposed by JML Excavation Inc., the development would include eight townhouses, two of which are designated affordable. The project has been given conditional approval by the planning board, but plans for final approval have not yet been submitted.
- The Vital at Tiverton Commons, Souza Road. Proposed by BCP Properties LLC, the development would include 256 units, 64 of which are designated as affordable. The proposal also includes a neighborhood daycare facility, a dog spa and a resort-style clubhouse.
- Friendship Farm, Stafford Road. Proposed by Tiverton Investments, the development would include 155 units, approximately 39 of which are designated as affordable.
- The Souza Road Condominiums, Souza Road. This development would consist of 43 units, 11 of which would be designated as affordable.

Re: Belton Court Village Development – Opposition to Requested Zoning Waivers

Dear Members of the Planning Board,

I am writing as a resident of Barrington to express my strong opposition to the extensive zoning waivers requested by Shineharmony Holdings for the proposed Belton Court Village development at 33 Middle Highway. While I recognize the importance of addressing housing needs in our community, I believe this project, as currently proposed with over a dozen requested waivers, fundamentally conflicts with our town's Comprehensive Plan and threatens the character and infrastructure capacity that makes Barrington a desirable place to live.

### Inconsistency with Barrington 2050 Comprehensive Plan

The Barrington 2050 Comprehensive Plan, adopted in 2015, specifically emphasizes maintaining low-density residential zoning in northern areas like the Belton Court site. The plan prioritizes single-family homes and open space preservation over high-density multi-family developments. The requested density of 12 units per acre directly contradicts this foundational principle of our community planning.

Our Comprehensive Plan was developed through extensive community input and professional planning expertise. It represents a carefully considered vision for Barrington's future that balances growth with the preservation of our town's suburban character. Granting these waivers would undermine the very purpose of having a comprehensive plan and set a concerning precedent for future development proposals.

### **Excessive Scope of Waiver Requests**

The sheer number and scope of waivers requested is alarming. The developer seeks relief from fundamental requirements including open space per unit, building height limits (requesting 75 feet), setback requirements, impervious surface coverage (requesting 60% site-wide), parking dimensions and quantity, street widths, and allowable building types. When a project requires this many exceptions to existing

zoning, it suggests the project is simply too large and inappropriate for the site, rather than that our zoning standards are flawed.

### Infrastructure and Service Impacts

The Technical Review Committee has raised serious concerns about this project's impact on our town's infrastructure. The inadequate parking (only 517 spaces for 344 units, with substandard dimensions), narrow road widths, and strain on stormwater management systems pose real risks to public safety and quality of life. Our schools are already operating near capacity, and a development of this density would significantly increase enrollment demands.

Traffic impacts on Middle Highway and surrounding streets cannot be overstated. The addition of 344 residential units will generate substantial new traffic in an area not designed for such density. Emergency vehicle access, particularly given the narrow roads and inadequate parking proposed, is a legitimate safety concern.

### **Historic Preservation Concerns**

The partial demolition of the National Register–listed Belton Court mansion is particularly troubling. Historic preservation is explicitly valued in our Comprehensive Plan. While the developer proposes to preserve the west wing and tower, the loss of significant portions of this historic structure cannot be undone. Our community has already lost too many historic buildings; we should not grant waivers that facilitate the destruction of this irreplaceable resource.

### **Affordability Without Compromise**

Barrington is currently at approximately 3.9% affordable housing, well short of the state's 10% goal. This isn't the first such developer to come into town with a project that leverages the fact we are under that goal. But that doesn't mean we have to make it an opportunity to run roughshod over what it means to build community in Barrington. We can still add valuable homes to our affordability portfolio without these damaging waivers.

### The Planning Board's Responsibility

The Planning Board has both the authority and the responsibility to deny these waivers if local concerns regarding health, safety, site design, or open space outweigh the need for low and moderate-income housing. The Comprehensive Permit process does not require automatic approval of all requested waivers. Rather, it requires the Board to make findings regarding consistency with our Comprehensive Plan, environmental impacts, and whether the benefits of affordable units outweigh the costs to our community.

In this case, I urge you to find that they do not. The extensive waivers requested, the conflicts with our Comprehensive Plan, the infrastructure concerns raised by the Technical Review Committee, and the historic preservation issues all point toward denial or, at minimum, substantial revision of this proposal to bring it into better alignment with our town's planning goals and zoning requirements.

Barrington's Comprehensive Plan exists to guide development in a way that maintains our community's character while meeting housing needs. This proposal, with its extensive waiver requests, does not strike that balance. I respectfully urge the Planning Board to deny the requested waivers and require a substantially revised proposal that respects our community's planning vision.

Thank you for your consideration and for your service to our community.

Sincerely,

The magnitude of unmitigated impacts to safety, emergency services, the environment, school capacity, and infrastructure indicate the proposal is not in the best interests of the Town.
