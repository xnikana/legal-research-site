Asking the Town to deny the waivers requested for the ShineHarmony development—not because we oppose development, but because this proposal undermines Barrington's long-term housing and economic goals. Waivers should be rare and granted only when a clear public benefit exists, not used to compensate for projects that fail to meet adopted community standards. A decision to approve these waivers would unwisely put short-term private gain ahead of the long-term needs of our town.

This is not an anti-development argument, though it may be framed that way. It is a call for development that aligns with Barrington's stated values, our comprehensive planning goals, and a clear long-term vision. We should be asking whether this project strengthens Barrington or erodes what makes it a sustainable, inclusive community—particularly for low- and moderate-income residents.

At present, Barrington lacks a clear affordable housing strategy beyond relying on private developers whose primary obligation is profit. We should be pursuing a thoughtful, intentional plan for affordability rather than reacting to individual proposals. That includes exploring public-private partnerships, incentivizing second-floor apartments above retail—especially in walkable areas like the Barrington Shopping Plaza—and working with local realtors and builders to convert existing single-family homes into multi-family housing where appropriate.

As proposed, this development excludes many of the people our town depends on to function. Families earning moderate incomes are already being priced out of Barrington, and without intentional planning, we risk becoming a community accessible only to high-income households. That outcome is neither sustainable nor equitable.

The portion of this proposal labeled "affordable" is affordable in name only. A buyer would need to earn at least \$137,000 to purchase the smallest two-bedroom unit, while the median household income in Barrington is approximately \$150,000—meaning half of our residents earn less than that. This proposal does not meaningfully serve teachers, DPW workers, police officers, young families, or seniors who want to remain in the town they helped build.

The most troubling part of this proposal is not simply the waivers themselves, but the lack of a broader vision they represent. We are focused on approving what we don't want, instead of intentionally building the future we say we do.

Strong communities grow through development that aligns with stated values, comprehensive plans, and long-term vision. The question before us is not whether to approve a waiver (or 16 of them), but whether this project meaningfully strengthens Barrington and serves the residents who rely on it today and in the future.
