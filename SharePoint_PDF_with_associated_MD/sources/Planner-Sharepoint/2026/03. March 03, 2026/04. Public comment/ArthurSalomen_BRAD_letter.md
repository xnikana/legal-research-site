Dear Members of the Barrington Planning Board,

I am writing as an abutting property owner at 18 Beaver Road, where I have lived since June 2005. I am also a member of the Environmental Impact Subcommittee for Barrington Residents for Appropriate Development (BRAD). I am a Full Professor at Brown University in the Department of Molecular Biology, Cell Biology, and Biochemistry. I earned my Ph.D. in Chemistry from Stanford University in 2000, followed by a postdoctoral fellowship at the Genomics Institute of the Novartis Research Foundation in San Diego, and then joined Brown University as an Assistant Professor in 2004. My training emphasizes careful, evidence-based evaluation of complex data and systems, and I am applying that same analytical approach here in reviewing the project's potential stormwater and groundwater impacts on abutting properties.

I am submitting the following compiled abutter impact statements regarding existing stormwater and groundwater conditions for homes located on streets directly adjacent to the former Zion/Belton Court property. These statements were collected through a written outreach letter mailed to abutters, requesting short written accounts of observed conditions (standing water, reliance on or failure of sump pumps, basement water intrusion, runoff/icing, drainage easements, and related impacts). Statements were received by email between February 14 and February 23, 2026. For privacy, email addresses are redacted. The statements below are otherwise reproduced unaltered.

**Condensed summary:** Across multiple abutting properties, residents describe longstanding high groundwater and recurring surface runoff issues, including nearcontinuous sump pump operation, basement flooding during power outages or pump failure, standing water and yard saturation after rain/snowmelt, and recurring flows onto sidewalks and streets (including hazardous icing). Several residents report significant mitigation costs (generators, waterproofing, multiple pumps, French drains, windowwell pumps) and express concern that additional impervious surfaces and development intensity will exacerbate existing problems. In addition, abutters have reported regular wildlife use of the adjacent wetland and buffer areas, including deer activity and recent observations of raptors, including a bald eagle (see Statement 13) and red-tailed hawk, underscoring the need for careful habitat review alongside stormwater and groundwater impacts.

**Connection to requested waivers and stormwater approach:** The applicant is requesting waivers that increase overall build-out intensity, including waivers to allow maximum building lot coverage of 35% and maximum impervious surface coverage of 60% across the site. Because these waivers increase impervious area and runoff generation at the source, they rise or fall with whether the proposed stormwater "diversion to infiltration/recharge" strategy is actually feasible under real subsurface conditions and seasonal groundwater levels in the abutting neighborhood. **The project's stormwater strategy appears to rely heavily on on-site engineered** 

**systems, particularly infiltration/recharge and stormwater ponds, and is presented as reducing off-site impacts by diverting surface runoff into the groundwater system.** Because this approach depends on actual infiltration performance and overflow routing during large storm events, the Board should require independent verification of key assumptions, including subsurface capacity, seasonal groundwater levels, and the direction of overflow when systems exceed design storm levels. Given the abutterreported baseline of near-constant sump pump operation and recurring groundwater intrusion (suggesting groundwater levels may already be high or seasonally saturated), as well as Mr. Kalocsay's statement at the February 3, 2026 meeting that high groundwater levels at Belton Court have been "very problematic to deal with", I respectfully request an independent, impartial review (e.g., a third-party hydrogeologist/stormwater engineer retained by the Town or Planning Board, not the applicant's consultants) and clear answers to the following questions:

- 1. Groundwater capacity and abutter baseline: Has an independent review evaluated whether the site and surrounding groundwater system can accommodate the project's proposed infiltration and recharge approach without increasing high groundwater conditions or water intrusion risks for abutters, especially at the maximum build-out enabled by the requested waivers?
- 2. Subsurface constraints (ledge and shallow soils): As documented in Statement 9, excavation at 18 Beaver Road encountered shelf rock or ledge at approximately 4 feet below grade, suggesting limited soil depth in this immediate area. Has the project's infiltration and recharge design been validated against field-verified subsurface conditions, including depth to ledge and seasonal high groundwater, and will the applicant conduct a sufficiently dense subsurface investigation, such as borings, boreholes, or test pits, across the site, particularly near perimeter abutters, so that infiltration and recharge features are not designed on assumptions?
- 3. Private wells and water quality: At least one abutter relies on a private artesian well, see Mr. Guay's statement below, and other nearby properties may as well. If runoff is being routed into the ground through infiltration and recharge systems, has an independent evaluation been completed to assess whether this could increase contamination risk to private wells, including through altered groundwater flow paths?
- 4. Wetlands and wildlife: The project area includes wetlands and wetland buffer zones. Has an independent or state-recognized screening been completed for sensitive habitat, including potential vernal pools, and for any rare, threatened, or protected species use of the wetlands and buffers? If seasonal verification is required, will it be completed and reviewed before major waivers are granted? In addition to habitat impacts, what evaluations have been conducted of existing wildlife use and displacement effects on abutting neighborhoods, including deer

and coyote movement and pollinator habitat, such as butterflies? For example, Statement 13 documents a recent observation of a bald eagle near 18 Great Road, which is adjacent to Belton Court, as well as red-tailed hawks.

- 5. Stormwater ponds near abutters: The plans reference stormwater ponds and perimeter stormwater features. For ponds and major stormwater structures located near Beaver Road and Riverside abutters, what independent review has confirmed (a) where water will go during extreme events or system exceedance, including emergency overflow routing, (b) how overtopping or seepage will be prevented from impacting adjacent properties, and (c) how long term maintenance will be enforced, including who inspects, how often, and what happens if performance degrades?
- 6. Post-construction monitoring and enforcement: If the Board approves a stormwater plan that relies heavily on infiltration/recharge and engineered ponds, what postconstruction monitoring and enforcement will be required to confirm real-world performance and protect abutters? Specifically, will there be seasonal groundwater observations and system performance checks, with clear enforcement triggers and corrective-action requirements if groundwater intrusion, pond performance, or runoff impacts worsen for neighboring properties?

These questions are raised in good faith and are directly tied to the scale of the requested waivers and the consistent baseline conditions reported by abutters. The abutter impact statements follow.

### **Statement 9**

Name: Arthur Salomon

Address: 18 Beaver Road, Barrington, RI 02806

Date received: February 21, 2026

"As an abutting property owner at 18 Beaver Road, I want to document a site condition that may be directly relevant to the applicant's stormwater approach, which appears to rely heavily on infiltration/recharge (diverting runoff from the surface into the subsurface via engineered systems).

During a past excavation on my property, ledge was encountered at approximately 4 feet below grade. This suggests that in this immediate area, there may be limited soil depth above the ledge and potentially limited available storage/infiltration capacity in the subsurface compared to what would be assumed under deeper soils.

If the project's diversion strategy depends on infiltrating large volumes of stormwater into the ground to reduce off-site impacts, shallow ledge conditions can materially affect (a) how quickly water can infiltrate, (b) where that water travels laterally, and (c) whether groundwater levels could rise or move laterally toward abutting basements and yards during wet seasons. The abutter statements collected on Beaver Road and adjacent streets indicate near-continuous sump pump operation and recurring groundwater intrusion, suggesting that groundwater levels are already high or seasonally saturated. In that context, a diversion-to-groundwater strategy should be supported by clear, site-specific subsurface data (borings/test pits/groundwater observations) and an independent hydrogeologic evaluation of capacity and mounding risk, rather than relying on assumptions.

Here is a video of the excavation showing the ledge at approximately 4 feet below grade:

[See attached movie "9. excavation ledge rock 18 Beaver.mov"]

Notes for follow-up/questions to raise:

- Has the applicant validated, using site-wide field borings, the infiltration/recharge design against actual subsurface conditions (including shallow ledge and seasonal high groundwater) along the site perimeter and for all abutters
- What is the plan if field borings show shallow ledge or limited infiltration depth in portions of the site? The project's 344-unit scale may not be feasible and needs to be scaled back.
- Has anyone evaluated groundwater mounding and lateral groundwater movement toward adjacent properties under the maximum build-out scenario?
