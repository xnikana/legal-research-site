Re: Proposed Belton Court Development

Dear Members of the Planning Board,

I serve on the Town's Committee on Appropriations and the Committee on Budgeting and Forecasting; however, the comments I offer below are solely my own.

I respectfully ask that the Commission reject the Belton Court development proposal as currently presented. While I strongly support thoughtful investment in this property that benefits the Town as a whole, I believe the current plan proposes a level of density that is materially inconsistent with surrounding neighborhoods and may create fiscal and safety impacts that have not been adequately evaluated.

### **Density**

Most residential neighborhoods in Barrington have densities of approximately 2–4 units per acre. The Belton Court proposal anticipates approximately 14 units per acre — more than three times prevailing neighborhood density.

The plan relies on two density waivers:

- A senior housing waiver allowing 6–8 units per acre
- A 25% affordable housing density bonus adding roughly five additional units per acre

Yet the current proposal does not include age-restricted senior housing. If the project is not limited to senior residents, the rationale for applying a senior housing density waiver becomes unclear. Age-restricted housing materially limits school enrollment impact. Without such a restriction, the density allowances compound in a way that raises unresolved questions regarding infrastructure capacity, traffic safety, and municipal costs.

# **Traffic and Safety**

The traffic study submitted in support of the development appears limited in scope. It evaluates traffic impacts primarily as a function of total unit count (344 units). However, the research framework used by the consulting firm itself recognizes that bedroom count is also a critical predictor of trip generation — a factor appropriately considered in the sewer study but not incorporated into the traffic analysis.

Additionally, the study does not fully evaluate the impact of potential increases in school-age children if the development is not age-restricted. Increased pedestrian, bicycle, and vehicle traffic during school hours could materially affect neighborhood safety and circulation.

In short, the current traffic analysis does not provide sufficient clarity to understand the full impact of this proposal on neighborhood flow and safety.

### **Fiscal Impact**

I am also concerned that the proposed density may generate school-related operating costs that exceed incremental property tax revenue.

Based on my own analysis and the developer's real estate valuation, I estimate the school system break-even point at approximately 151 new students. Using the Tax Assessor's revenue estimates included in tonight's packet, that break-even point declines to approximately 132 students.

To remain revenue-neutral under those assumptions:

- Between 56–62% of new units would need to generate no children; and
- The remaining 38–44% could have no more than one child.

However, local real estate professionals estimate that 80–85% of units are likely to be purchased by households with children.

Further, 80% of proposed units have more than one bedroom, and 37% contain three or four bedrooms. That unit mix suggests a meaningful probability of more than one child per household in a significant share of units — increasing enrollment pressure beyond break-even assumptions.

At present, we have not seen a comprehensive fiscal impact analysis addressing municipal and school operating costs under realistic occupancy scenarios. Without that analysis, we do not know whether this project will be revenue-positive, revenue-neutral, or revenue-negative.

## **Broader Fiscal Context**

Barrington residents are already facing multiple fiscal pressures, including anticipated property tax increases above the 4% cap in FY 2027–2029 to support the \$250 million bond for new school construction.

In that context, approving a development at exceptional density without clear evidence of fiscal sustainability introduces additional uncertainty and potential strain on operating budgets.

### **Conclusion**

I respectfully encourage the Commission to:

- Ask the developer to revisit the proposed density and align it more closely with surrounding neighborhoods, while retaining the affordable housing bonus;
- Clarify the basis for applying a senior housing density waiver absent age restrictions;
- Require a more comprehensive traffic and trip-generation analysis; and
- Request a detailed fiscal impact study addressing school and municipal operating costs under realistic occupancy scenarios.

Thoughtful redevelopment of this property is achievable. However, it must be grounded in transparent analysis and sustainable assumptions to ensure it strengthens — rather than strains — the Town of Barrington.
