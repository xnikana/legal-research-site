# <span id="page-3-0"></span>Introduction

Barrington is undertaking a comprehensive update of their signage regulations and policies:

- Assess their existing signage regulatory program and related plans to identify gaps and opportunities
- Identify opportunities from peer communities
- Assess the public signage policies (wayfinding, banners, recreation fields)
- Engage the community (kickoff forum, survey with visual preferences, forum testing initial finding, final forum), with public and business stakeholders)
- Draft regulatory framework and graphic design guidelines
- Draft public signage guidelines (banners, wayfinding, gateway)
- Deliver the final report

Barrington is a unique community with unique opportunities. Appropriate signage can help build the sense of place that attracts business and the information necessary to match customers with business destinations:

- Barrington is a suburban community (average 30 minute commute with 71% of commutes in single occupancy vehicles). Many of these commutes pass by commercial areas.
- It is also a desirable place for remote work (22% of workers work from home).
- Incomes are higher than the region, providing far more disposable income to spend in Barrington for both essential services and discretionary spendin (\$153,198 household income, versus \$114,400 for Bristol County and \$87,798 for RI) with lower poverty levels (4.5% versus 6.5% for Bristol County and 11.2% for the state).
- Barrington is a route for many people heading to points south (e.g., Warren, Bristol, Newport), providing an opportunity to grab passersby.
- Barrington is a popular destination, with several marinas, a yacht club, extensive waterfront with water access, and many other destinations *(Source: American Community Survey 2024[, www.censusreporter.org\)](http://www.censusreporter.org/)*

# <span id="page-5-0"></span>Preamble

Signs are critical to identify and promote business, attract visitors and local customers who otherwise would not stop, add to Barrington's sense of place, and promote its image as a small New England coastal town. Barrington's regulatory signage framework aims to regulate all signs in both the public and private realms to advance those purposes and protect Barrington's appearance, public health, safety, and general welfare.

The aim of the regulatory framework is to:

- Encourage signage that builds on Barrington's sense of place, especially for signage placed on public property (e.g., lamppost banners, wayfinding signage, gateway signage).
- Create clear and uniform standards that permit reasonable uses of signs consistent with the character of Barrington.
- Avoid sign clutter, unsightly, or confusing elements that could distract from Barrington's character and appearance and contribute to driving hazards.
- Ensure an appropriate level of review and approval of sign permits, with the least amount of regulations necessary to achieve these purposes.

#### **Signage Guiding Principles**

Building on existing signage practice, Barrington primary signage design principles are:

**First, provide adequate opportunities for signage to meet business needs consistent with the strengthening Barrington's character.** Signage can be a win-win.

**Second, without create adverse impacts on the character of exempt temporary signs, window signage, and very small signage from review and permitting.** This does not replace the need for applicants to comply with zoning requirements.

**Third, allow most signs to be approved by Building with no discretionary review when they comply with requirements.** Barrington already generally follows this approach, but there may be some areas where these approvals can be expanded.

**Fourth, in the rare circumstances that require discretionary review and design judgement, the approval process should be through a Special Use Permit issued by the Technical Review Committee or the Zoning Board of Appeals.** While an applicant can always apply for a dimensional variance, it is not the appropriate standard of review and it should be an extremely difficult standard to meet (hardship from unique conditions, least variance that will afford relief, and not primarily to maximize a financial return).

**Fifth, provide better design guidance** for discretionary reviews that inform both the regulated public and regulators.

**Fifth, maximize the readability of the ordinance.** Consultants should not be needed to understand the regulations.

**Sixth, ensure signage regulations are content neutral** and avoid legal exposure.

**Finally, create a parallel program to define public signage** and build a sense of place.

# <span id="page-6-0"></span>Public Engagement Findings

**Stakeholder and community engagement** included five key opportunities for public and stakeholder engagement:

- **Kickoff signage public forum** (October 15, 2025) was attended by stakeholders from town boards, the business community, and a few additional members of the public.
- The **Barrington 2025 survey** (141 respondents) to inform this process included visual preference and other signage questions.
- **A second signage public forum** (November 19, 2025) with stakeholders and the public provided an opportunity to test the initial findings that are informing this report.
- **A final planning board forum** (March or April, 2026).
- **A final signage public forum** (March or April, 2026).

Key ideas emerged from the first two **community and stakeholder forums**. These ideas inform the recommendations. Not all ideas, however, represent a consensus view.

- A signage program should address both private realm signage (through the ordinance) and public realm signage (wayfinding, lamppost, gateway, and scoreboards).
- Signage regulations must be content-neutral, to comply with free speech requirements
- Barrington aspires to feel like a traditional New England village, reinforced by carved wooden signs, pedestrian-scale signage, and other approaches, but limited by shopping centers
- Ensure a simple sign approval process
- Signage should include large letters, legible fonts, high-contrast colors, and tasteful design
- Signage should be unique, cohesive, and consistent with their neighborhoods

# <span id="page-14-0"></span>Planning and Regulatory Code Assessments

Barrington reached out to the community to interpret past planning efforts and engage the public and stakeholders in their signage assessment. Signage may attract less attention than some hot button issues, but the community spoke in three public forums, one past survey, and one new community survey.

The *Barrington 2015 Comprehensive Plan* (revised 2019) identifies proposed signage actions. Some of these actions have been advanced since the plan was adopted.

- Adopt lighting standards (Housing and Neighborhoods)
- Extend directional and wayfinding (Economic Development)
- Add design standards (Economic Develop.)
- Enhance the pedestrian environment (Housing and Neighborhoods)
- Enhance walkable retail zones (Economic Develop.)
- Improve wayfinding from the bicycle path (Economic Develop. and Circulation)

**Barrington 2050 survey** (2024, 506 respondents) to inform the *Barrington 2050 comprehensive planning* effort, received comments relevant to signage: "Our downtown feels like a commercial mini-mall designed for cars, not a place one wants

![](05b. Barrington signage diagnostic-WF02-23-26_attachments/_page_14_Picture_10.jpeg)

*Kickoff public & stakeholder forum with comment sample*

to wander and explore; Get rid of sign pollution; Like the signs; Need better signage [of historic sites]; No gigantic signs; More public signage; Signs on lamp posts are tacky; Need signs pointing to business; Business area lack [adequate signs]; and Too much money [is spent on town] signs."

Barrington has a relatively streamlined **sign approval process**, where most signage is allowed with only a Building department review.

#### **No approval needed**

Window signs <40% window Temporary event signs

### **Apply to Building**

Approve within 10 days if full compliance

#### **Technical Review Committee or Zoning Board of Review**

>1 sign/establishment, Directory signs, other Zoning relief

Approve or deny in accordance with general standards

Zoning Board-TRC appeals and zoning relief

There are, however, some additional opportunities for a more streamlined process and for

![](05b. Barrington signage diagnostic-WF02-23-26_attachments/_page_14_Picture_23.jpeg)

increasing as-of-right approvals to replace discretionary reviews. Discretionary reviews are critical for many projects but also add additional hurdles to applicants, town administrative burdens, and create an uncertain playing field for both applicants and their abutters. For example:

- Link the signage application available on the Planning Board landing page with the OpenGov portal links so applicants can see the application before logging in to the OpenGov. If these are two different applications, clarify which applies when.
- Provide Technical Review Committee members with electronic applications instead of requiring eight printed copies (TRC application procedures and signage application).
- Ban roof signs instead of allowing with a discretionary review.

In light of current case law, some sections of Barrington's **sign ordinance do not comply with free speech** and content neutrality requirements (see the Free Speech Content of Public and Private Signs of this report). Revise for content neutral regulations by sign type and district.

The current regulatory code has opportunities for stronger **sign lighting standards** at the same level of clarity and enforceability that Barrington already has for noise (decibels) and vibration.

- There should be measurable sign lighting limits (lumens, foot-candles, Dark-Sky standards)
- There should be measurable light limits (nits) for electronic sign or highly reflective signs (typically signs that use reflective glass beads)
- There can be clearer glare and sidewalk light trespass standards (currently there is only a requirement that internally illuminate signs have no more glare than externally lit signs.
- Sign lighting options can be improved:
  - o Shielding standards should be clarified if uplighting remains a code option
  - o Down lighting should be placed so as not to block a sign
  - o Backlighting and internal lighting should be clarified

*Many communities do not allow uplighting because of light escaping, or when they do that have detailed shielding requirements.*

*Downlighting can be more directed, but sometimes the fixtures block the very thing they are illuminating.* 

#### **Design standards with clear guidelines** can allow:

- More as-of-right approvals (e.g., A-Frames in compliance with the standards)
- Help inform discretionary approvals from the Technical Review Committee and the Zoning Board of Review.

# <span id="page-19-0"></span>Free Speech Content of Public and Private Signs

In 2015, the U.S. Supreme Court clarified the limits of communities to regulate signage content (*Reed v. Town of Gilbert*, 135 S. Ct. 2218). Reed generally requires heightened scrutiny and a clear public purpose (e.g., traffic safety) if you must read the sign to know if it is allowed.

Communities can reasonably regulate time, place, and manner of signage, such as sign locations, size, setbacks, height, lighting, and materials. For example, content-neutral prohibition of offpremise signs is allowed where on-premise signs are permitted (City of Austin v. Reagan National Advertising,142 S. Ct. 1464, 2022) similar to previous upholding of billboard bans (*Metromedia, Inc. v. San Diego*, 101 S. Ct. 2882, 1981) while courts are suspicious of code that requires a content analysis (e.g., *Young v. Conway, US District [trial] Court,* 783 F.Supp.3d 588, 2025).

Content can only be regulated in rare circumstances and content regulations require strict scrutiny. Barrington can provide an intent or legislative findings on the difference in sign regulations by district (e.g., village center), by use (e.g., recreation and athletic fields) and explain the compelling public interest for any sign regulation which is not content neutral. For example, requiring that road signs on a private road or parking comply with the Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices (MUTCD) would probably pass a challenge with a simple justification.

There are a few areas where Barrington's sign standards are not content neural (e.g., in business districts, flush wall "signs should be limited to only the address, the name of the business, a business logo and/or product or service information"), some of which might not survive a legal challenge. In some places, it is clear that this is simply an encouragement (e.g., a building housing more than a single business) but in other cases it is not clear and could go too far.

Given the unsettled law on commercial speech, there are some gray areas where town counsel needs to be consulted since different attorneys sometimes have distinct perspectives on some details. Regardless, sign regulations should provide free speech opportunities, such as allowing temporary political signs and for-sale signs. To minimize content-neutrality challenges, many communities simply set time limits for the corrugated plastic (e.g., coroplast) signs that proliferate on front lawns and require that they be on private property outside of the public rightof-way and tree belt.

It is clear that communities can regulate temporary, permanent, structure, design, and location, with different standards in different zoning districts or areas. It is also clear that governments do not have their own free speech rights. They can, for example, install information signs and can ban private signs on public property, but if anything is allowed on public property within a given zoning district (e.g., A-Frame signs), then everything is allowed.

**Purpose:** It is Barrington's purpose to minimize signage to avoid clutter and distraction, while allowing sufficient signage in business districts to support businesses and allowing easy navigation around the community. These regulations or content-neutral except when required for traffic safety and navigation.

# <span id="page-20-0"></span>Illumination of Public and Private Signage

Barrington's current zoning lighting standards detail some requirements (e.g., "Signs may be illuminated externally by a stationary light of white or off-white color). In other areas, however, the standards leave too much room for interpretation to be understandable and enforceable (e.g., "The light illuminating any sign shall be …a sufficiently low level of intensity and brightness that it will not adversely affect neighboring premises, nor the safe vision of operators of vehicles moving on public roads and highways").

Lighting is critical for visibility and safety, but inappropriate lighting adds to visual clutter, harms wildlife, disturbs sleep, and can add a major safety risk when the contrast between brightly lit areas and dimly lit areas is too great for human pupils to quickly dilate to allow for adequate night vision.

**Purpose:** Barrington follows the DarkSky International principles for outdoor and sign lighting:

- **Useful and controlled**. Light only if and when it is needed. All light should have a clear purpose.
- **Targeted.** Direct lighting so it falls only where it is needed, using shielding. Avoid:
  - o Up-light excess lighting directed into the sky (above 90o ).
  - o Glare horizontal light (60 o to 90 o), which is uncomfortable or hazardous.
  - o Backlight when not directed at the signage it causes light trespass.
- **Low level and warm.** Light is no brighter than necessary. Use warmer color lights when possible.

#### **Dynamic Signs**

Dynamic message signs (DMS), also known as changeable message signs (CMS), electronic message centers (EMC), kinetic signs, electronic paper signage (EPS), variable message, animated, and movable text signs are growing in popularity. They can convey a lot of information and are

especially useful for signs whose content often changes, from fast changing arts and community event signs to slower changing directory and service station pricing information.

**Purpose:** Dynamic signs are allowed for areas with changing messages, with subtle signs allowed for directory signs and larger signs allowed for municipal installations,

#### **Projecting Private Messaging on sidewalks (lighting and other methods)**

Some businesses use lighting to project a sign or symbols on the sidewalk as a way to catch the attention of pedestrians. Poorly done, this can create glare for cars and a garish Las Vegas look, but these can be subtle signs that help businesses and liven up sidewalks.

Since these signs are relatively inexpensive compared to permanent wall or freestanding signs, some communities allow projecting only for new businesses or in limited areas.

**Purpose:** The use of light to project messages onto sidewalks is permitted for new businesses, for private sidewalks within shopping plazas, and, in the village center only, for businesses that directly abut public sidewalks.

#### **Direction and Timing of Signage Lighting (uplighting, downlighting, internal lighting)**

Uplighting has the highest level of lighting that escapes into non-target areas and as a result many communities ban this lighting. Downlighting is the traditional lighting that is most common for pedestrian scale villages. Internal lighting and even backlighting can work so long if light levels are strictly controlled. In addition, lighted dynamic signs and highly reflective signs (e.g., those using reflective glass balls), like internal and backlighting, should have strictly controlled lighting levels (e.g., maximum nit levels). The timing of when signate lighting goes off can also help control lighting levels without negating the advertising benefits of signage lighting.

Purpose: Lighting levels should always be controlled so that signs are visible during the time when customers are present without lighting extending beyond the sign.

#### **Lamppost Banners within public or street rights-of-way**

Barrington has debated its lamppost banner program, which is currently on hold. The program can be restarted however, while avoiding the clutter and damage to the village appearance that can come from a uncontrolled banner program without limits on sponsorship.

**Purpose:** The purpose of Barrington Lamppost Banner program is to help define and promote the Barrington Village Center, with a financially self-supporting program, without distracting excessive sponsorship messaging. This applies on any public (e.g., bicycle path) and street right-of-way.

Town banners, but not privately owned banners, can be limited to promoting the village center:

- Banners are only permitted when installed by or at the direction of the Town of Barrington.
- Barrington may partner with a vendor (e.g., Barrington Business Association) who is responsible for designing, printing, installing, and maintaining banners. Costs can be recovered from banner sponsorship fees, with limits on sponsorship size and term to avoid the proliferation of advertising that could detract from the overall banner messaging.
- Banners are only allowed in commercially zoned areas within public (state or municipal) rightsof-way.
- Sponsorship may not cover more than 20% of the banner, with sponsors messaging limited to a designated section on the bottom of the banner.
- No artificial lighting, except from the street lamp itself. If desired, sponsorship messages shall be in low chroma (2 and under) or medium chroma (3 and under) colors.
- There are no controls on the content of sponsorship messaging (e.g., words or symbols).
- Banner permits shall be issued for a six-month period with no vesting, allowing the town to update banners, create new limits, or reserve the right to change approaches, with short term banners allowed to accommodate special event messaging.
- The town may require multiple banners for each sponsor, such as seasonal banners where the town messages changes over the course of a year.

# <span id="page-27-0"></span>Regulatory Standards and the Rationale for Private Signage

The draft ordinance at the end of this report is designed to incorporate the following regulatory and design standards.

#### **Readability- to simplify administration and understanding**

- Use tables instead of text description when possible
- Add graphic design standards instead of text description when possible

#### **Free speech- comply with federal constitutional free speech requirements**

- Remove non-content neutral sign standards in current ordinance with very limited exceptions (e.g., traffic control devices)
- Signage requirements are limited to the time, place, and manner of allowable signage, based on the kind of sign, its location, and the related land use, but no content restrictions
- Address allowed art locations, but also preserving content neutrality

#### **Illumination and lighting- ensure measurable and enforceable standards**

- Provide adequate lighting for sign visibility and getting attention
- Avoid light trespass, over lighting, or impairing night vision
- Detail the allowable period for artificial signage
- Create standards for emerging technologies (e.g., dynamic signage, highly reflective surfaces)
- Address form and color of sign lighting
- Standards for uplighting, downlighting, internal lighting, and backlighting

#### **Public realm and public ways- detail allowable public signage**

- Exempt certain municipal MUTCD, wayfinding, gateway, banner signage and bulletin boards
- Prevent private trespass onto public ways except in very limited defined circumstances

#### **Technical review- ensure equal application of the law and simply administration**

- Design standards to inform Building in their review of as-of-right signage
- Design standards consistent with Barrington's character to inform the Technical Review Committee and Zoning Board of Appeals in their discretionary reviews
- Design guidelines to inform signage designers of good options
- Allowable differences for shopping plazas and multiple tenant buildings

#### **Sign construction- ensure well maintained and durable signage**

- Study and durable sign materials and mountings
- Well-constructed and maintained signage

#### **Temporary signage- ensure manageable and enforceable administration**

- Address temporary signage for time-limited installations
- Address A-Frames (sandwich boards)
- Offer alternatives to A-Frames

#### **Detailed standard for each type of commercial signage- clarify existing approaches**

- Address all commercial signage types (time, place, and manner) and locations
- Expand locations for allowable signage consistent with preserving the Barrington's character

![](05b. Barrington signage diagnostic-WF02-23-26_attachments/_page_27_Picture_34.jpeg)

- Ban non-changeable message kinetic features (e.g., inflatable tube man)
- Flush wall signs size based on simple formula
- Freestanding signs reduce needs for discretionary review and supplement landscaping
- Awning and can canopy signs- with proper maintenance
- Projecting or blade signs- at both the moving vehicle and pedestrian scales
- Window signs ensure enforceable standards
- Incentivize well-designed carved/CNC routed wooden signs with contrasting letters and custom cut steel and metal signs

#### **Address discretionary permit standards- change to simplify the process**

- Use Special Use Permits instead of Dimensional Variance to avoid the difficult "hardship… due to the unique characteristics…not result primarily from the desire of the applicant to realize greater financial gain…and…is the least relief necessary" tests (e.g., "has not demonstrated a complete loss of all beneficial use of the property as a result of the denial of its zoning application" for a billboard, *OK Properties v. the Zoning Board of Review of the City of Warwick*, 601 A2d 953, 1992).
- Limit Special Use Permits to a few specific cases and instead anticipate most cases in the as of right standards.

# <span id="page-29-0"></span>Conclusion and Next Steps

Barrington generally has a strong public signage, regulatory, and administrative implementation programs. There are, however, always room for improvement. Overall, the recommended changes address three overall opportunities:

- Expand opportunities for business signage to promote town businesses.
- Reduce the uncertainty, discretion, and permit hurdles for applicants.
- Improve the standards so that the undesirable side effects of signage, side effects that are currently addressed in a discretionary case-by-case review, are prevented.
- Build the institutional memory and documentation so that good administration does not require as much local knowledge.

The next steps in revising Barrington's signage standards and ordinance are:

- Staff and planning board review
- Final public forum
- Revise this penultimate report to address comments
- Introduce the ordinance into the formal adoption process
- Adopt public signage standards
