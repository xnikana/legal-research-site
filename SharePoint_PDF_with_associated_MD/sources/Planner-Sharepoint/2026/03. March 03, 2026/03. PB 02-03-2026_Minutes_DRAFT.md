#### **4. Public Hearing:**

**a. Discuss and Act: Belton Court Village Comprehensive Permit Preliminary Plan Application located at 33 Middle Highway (former Zion Bible College), AP14, Lots 2, 4, 13, 341, 342, and 343, Senior Residential Village (SRV) District. The applicant seeks Preliminary Plan Approval, including relief from provisions of the Zoning Ordinance and the Land Development & Subdivision Regulations, pursuant to the Comprehensive Permit Process. ShineHarmony Holdings, Inc., applicant and owner, with business address at 650 Pleasant St., Franklin, MA, 02038, proposes the demolition of the existing Belton Court, storage area, gymnasium, residential building and shed buildings to construct a 344-unit residential development containing 220 multi-family units, 66 townhomes, and 58 detached, single-family homes along with common areas and certain off-site public improvements. Of the 344 units, 25% will be designated affordable according to the state's definition of low/moderate income housing.**

**Requested adjustments/waivers include the following: allowance for singlefamily, two-family, and multi-family dwellings; allowance for parking pursuant to RIGL Section 45-53-4(2) including one parking space/dwelling up to two bedrooms in size; reduction of width and length of parallel parking spaces each by one foot; reduction of minimum parking aisle width by four feet at 90 degree points; allowance for curb cuts greater than 25 feet wide; partial demolition of Belton Court; and preserve tower and to only be required**  **to retain monumental landscaping including the existing cedar hedge and portions of the stone wall; allow a density of 12 units/acre of developable land; allowance for land within the State Regulatory setback and including approximately 4.5 acres within the Belton Court Historic Register District as developable land; allowance to permit parking between Belton Court structures and Middle Highway; allowance for a diversity of housing types besides senior housing; allowance for maximum building height to be 75 feet; allowance for any new structure with height above 55 feet to have setbacks calculated as three feet for every foot of additional height above 55 feet; allowance for maximum building lot coverage of 35% and maximum impervious surface coverage of 60% across the entire site; waiver of the requirement for streets to have a centerline curve; and allowance for streets to have less than 25 feet at the edge of the paved surface; and waiver of the requirement to have 300 square feet of open space or recreation are per unit.**

*Audio and transcript of the full proceedings are available with the application materials in the Planning Building and Resilience Office. Please contact the office to obtain.* 

*Present: Charles Kalocsay, Shineharmony Holdings Inc. Suanne Santos, InSite Engineering Paul Carlson, InSite Engineering Bill Landry, Blish & Cavanagh LLP Noriko Miyakoda, Miyakoda Consulting, Inc.*

Chair Phipps explained the procedures for the public hearing on the Comprehensive Permit Preliminary Plan Application for the Belton Court development proposed by ShineHarmony Holdings. She stated that the Planning Board was required to act in accordance with the Rhode Island Low and Moderate Income Housing Act (R.I. General Laws Title 45, Chapter 53) due to the inclusion of low- and moderate-income housing.

Chair Phipps noted that informational materials had been provided and were available on the Town's website. She advised that no decision would be made that evening and that the public hearing would remain open and continue at the March 3 meeting, and probably another meeting beyond that. Chair Phipps outlined the order of the hearing process, including the applicant's presentation, Planning Board questions, public comment, applicant response, and subsequent deliberation.

Planner Durfee addressed seating accommodations and the availability of handouts. He then turned the meeting over to Attorney Bill Landry, representing the applicant, to introduce the project and presentation team.

Attorney Landry, representing Shineharmony Holdings, introduced the Comprehensive Permit application under the Rhode Island Low and Moderate Income Housing Act and discussed the following items:

• described the approximately 39-acre site, primarily located in Barrington, and outlined a proposed multi-generational residential campus with a mix of housing types, including a minimum of 25 percent deed-restricted low- and moderate-income units. He stated that the

- proposed density was approximately 344 units and noted that the final unit count could change through the review process.
- explained the statutory framework of the Low and Moderate Income Housing Act, emphasizing the State's authority over zoning for qualifying projects and the Town's obligation to contribute to the statewide affordable housing goal of 10 percent. He also stated that the proposal focused on moderate-income housing and relied on a mix of market-rate and affordable units to subsidize affordability without public funding.
- discussed the Town of Barrington's Comprehensive Plan, noting that the subject property was specifically identified as a key site for achieving housing objectives due to its size, existing infrastructure, and access to water and sewer. He referenced the prior approval for high-density senior residential development on the site and stated that the current proposal was consistent with both historic institutional use and the Comprehensive Plan's preference for multi-generational housing over exclusively senior housing.
- stated that the proposed density was significantly lower than what could be permitted under the Senior Village zoning designation and the density bonuses available under State law. He further explained that detailed engineering and environmental compliance would be addressed as the project advanced to final plan review.
- noted that requested waivers, referred to as adjustments under State law, were necessary due to the campus-style design and private infrastructure. He explained that the development would be organized as a land condominium to ensure unified ownership, maintenance, and operational controls for infrastructure, stormwater, utilities, and common facilities.
- stated that the project had received a Letter of Eligibility from Rhode Island Housing dated March 20, 2025, confirming compliance with the Act and establishing affordable unit pricing, with updates to be provided as the review progressed. He acknowledged staff and consultant comments and stated that the applicant agreed in principle to numerous conditions, including revisions to site plans, lighting compliance, snow removal, solid waste management, private infrastructure designation, traffic mitigation, recreational amenities, pedestrian connectivity, and operational requirements to be incorporated into condominium documents.
- addressed fiscal impact considerations, noting that while fiscal impacts were not approval criteria under the Act, the applicant would cooperate with the Town in providing information to assist with long-term planning. He also stated that additional review would be required should any rental component be proposed.
- discussed the historic significance of Belton Court and stated that, following extensive analysis, restoration and reuse of the structure was determined to be infeasible. He explained that demolition of the main structure was proposed while preserving historically significant elements of the site.
- concluded by outlining the planned sequence of witnesses, including the project principal, cost-estimating experts, civil engineers, and traffic engineers, noting that the full presentation would likely continue at a subsequent meeting.

Attorney Landry next introduced Charlie Kalocsay, principal of Shineharmony Holdings, to present the project vision and background. Mr. Kalocsay described the previously approved master plan from ShineHarmony's 2016 Comprehensive Permit and stated that it was never pursued due to lack of economic feasibility, particularly the high cost of restoring the Belton Court mansion. Mr. Kalocsay explained his professional background in real estate advisory and restructuring and stated that post-COVID market conditions further undermined the viability of senior housing development on the site.

Mr. Kalocsay stated that based on prior experience with historic properties, he believed restoration costs would be prohibitive and recommended engaging an independent cost estimator to evaluate feasibility. He then introduced Noriko Miyakoda, owner of Miyakoda Consulting, as an expert cost estimator with extensive experience in historic rehabilitation projects. He asked her to describe her findings.

Ms. Miyakoda testified that she prepared a cost estimate in 2022 for rehabilitating Belton Court into approximately 30 residential units. She stated that construction costs alone were estimated at approximately \$50 million in 2022 dollars and would now be approximately \$64–65 million, exclusive of soft costs, which would add approximately 25 percent. Ms. Miyakoda stated that the estimate reflected standard construction assumptions and industry-accepted cost estimating practices and was submitted to the Town for review.

Attorney Landry then questioned Ms. Miyakoda regarding her qualifications, methodology, and prior experience, including work on historic properties nationwide. Ms. Miyakoda confirmed that the estimate was prepared in good faith, consistent with professional standards, and anticipated peer review.

Attorney Landry next asked Mr. Kalocsay whether the cost estimate influenced the decision to abandon the senior village concept. Mr. Kalocsay responded that the findings confirmed the project was economically infeasible, even with potential historic tax credits, given the limited number of units that could be created and the affordability requirements under the Act. Mr. Kalocsay stated that renovation costs per unit would far exceed what could reasonably be recovered through sales. Attorney Landry concluded that the testimony supported the determination that restoration and reuse of the Belton Court mansion was not feasible.

Mr. Kalocsay presented a slideshow and the rationale for the proposed development strategy and described the anticipated benefits to the Town.

- stated that the goal was to create a thoughtfully designed, mixed-housing neighborhood that addresses identified housing needs while remaining economically feasible. He acknowledged public concern regarding the scale and impact of the project and stated that those concerns informed the proposed design.
- identified several housing challenges in Barrington, including limited options for empty nesters and seniors seeking smaller homes, a significant shortfall in affordable housing relative to the State's 10 percent goal, and community concern regarding school capacity. He stated that the proposed unit mix—smaller units with a predominance of one- and twobedroom homes—was intentionally designed to limit school enrollment impacts while accommodating seniors, empty nesters, and workforce households.
- testified that 25 percent of the proposed 344 units would be affordable and interspersed throughout the development, as required by Rhode Island law. He stated that the project would increase the Town's affordable housing percentage to approximately 3–3.5 percent to approximately 5 percent but would not eliminate the overall deficiency.
- described the proposal as a traditional neighborhood development designed by DPZ, emphasizing walkability, rear-loaded garages accessed by alleys, sidewalks, pocket parks, and New England-style architecture. He stated that higher-quality design and infrastructure would increase development costs but was intended to create a lasting neighborhood that would enhance surrounding property values.

- stated that portions of the Belton Court structure, including the tower and a wing identified as the stables wing, were proposed to be preserved for historical significance and potential limited community or neighborhood retail use, subject to feasibility constraints related to groundwater and structural conditions.
- testified that the development was expected to generate a positive fiscal impact through property taxes and consumer spending and stated that a mixed-age community would better support local businesses than a fully age-restricted development. He further stated that comparable developments designed by DPZ had demonstrated minimal school impacts and long-term neighborhood value enhancement.
- acknowledged that the project required higher density than conventional developments to offset infrastructure costs, affordable housing requirements, and architectural standards. He requested that the Planning Board consider flexibility from certain zoning or design standards where supported by comparable precedent.

Mr. Kalocsay concluded that the plan represented a long-term vision subject to refinement over time and stated that future modifications, including potential increases in neighborhood-scale retail, could be proposed as the project evolved.

Suanne Santos of InSite Engineering presented the engineering overview for the proposed Belton Court development along with a slideshow presentation.

- stated that InSite Engineering prepared the site plans in coordination with the project team, which included DPZ (architect), Traverse Landscape Architects, Crossman Engineering (traffic), Power Engineering (utilities), and Ecosystem Solutions (environmental).
- provided a brief historical overview of the property, noting its original construction in 1905, subsequent use by Providence Bible Institute and Barrington College, later ownership by Zion Bible Institute, and its vacancy following Zion Bible's relocation in 2008. She reviewed historic aerial imagery illustrating the evolution of the site, including the construction of campus buildings, parking areas, and the later subdivision of the southern portion of the property into the Lion's Head neighborhood.
- existing site conditions were reviewed, including the property's size (approximately 36.97 acres), zoning designation as a Senior Residential Village, remaining structures, and previously demolished buildings. Ms. Santos identified wetlands and associated buffer zones on the site and explained existing stormwater flow patterns toward Middle Highway, East Providence wetlands, Lion's Head subdivision, and existing drainage easements.
- reviewed the proposed site layout, including three access roads from Middle Highway and an internal roadway network designed with sidewalks, on-street parking, landscaping, and bioretention areas. She explained that residential garages would be accessed via rear alleyways rather than primary streets to improve pedestrian safety and neighborhood character. Parking would be provided through a combination of garages, on-street spaces, surface parking for multifamily buildings, and designated visitor spaces, with a total of approximately 517 parking spaces. Parking setbacks near intersections were designed to meet fire department requirements.
- proposed building types were summarized, including multifamily condominium buildings, six-unit buildings, townhouses, cottages, and single-family homes. Ms. Santos emphasized that building types were intentionally interspersed throughout the site to avoid concentration of similar housing types.

- utility infrastructure was reviewed, including a looped water system with a single master meter managed by the homeowners' association, new hydrants, and gravity sewer connections utilizing an existing sewer easement originally constructed for Barrington College. Ms. Santos noted that portions of the existing sewer infrastructure, including a sewer main along County Road, would require upgrades as part of the project.
- stormwater management was discussed in detail. Ms. Santos explained that the design incorporated low-impact development techniques, including bioretention areas within roadways, pervious pavement, infiltration basins, and detention ponds. These measures were designed to treat stormwater close to its source, improve water quality, and reduce runoff volumes. She presented pre- and post-development analyses demonstrating reductions in peak discharge rates and total runoff volumes, including flows toward Middle Highway, East Providence wetlands, and the Lion's Head subdivision. Ms. Santos stated that while stormwater impacts could not be eliminated entirely, the proposed system would significantly improve existing conditions and reduce impacts to abutting properties, exceeding Rhode Island DEM requirements.

Ms. Santos reviewed fire department access and maneuverability plans. She stated that the roadway and alley layouts were designed to accommodate the Town's largest fire apparatus, with turning movements verified throughout the site and building front doors located within 50 feet of fire access routes.

Chair Phipps noted the lateness of the hour and advised that the Planning Board would need to schedule an additional meeting, as the applicant's presentation had not yet concluded and the Board had not begun questions or public comment. She stated that discussion would be required regarding how the Board would proceed prior to the March 3 meeting date.

Attorney Landry requested that Ms. Santos place her professional qualifications on the record. Ms. Santos stated that she had over 40 years of experience as a civil engineer, held a Bachelor of Science in Civil Engineering from the University of Lowell, and had worked on a wide range of projects including single-family residential, commercial, and large multifamily developments. She further stated that she had prepared numerous applications before the Rhode Island Department of Environmental Management (RIDEM), including stormwater permitting applications.

Attorney Landry elicited testimony from Ms. Santos that the proposed stormwater system had been designed to meet, and in some cases exceed RIDEM drainage requirements and was intended to reduce impacts to surrounding properties. Ms. Santos confirmed that a stormwater memorandum containing the supporting calculations had been submitted to the Town and reviewed.

Ms. Santos explained that final approvals from the Bristol County Water Authority would depend on final building design details, including construction materials and number of floors, in order to complete pressure and fire protection calculations. She testified that compliance with NFPA standards was achievable within the scope of the proposed project, and that requirements could vary depending on building construction type and whether sprinkler systems were provided.

Ms. Santos stated that a meeting with the Fire Chief regarding fire access and maneuverability had been postponed due to weather and would need to be rescheduled. She indicated that remaining fire department concerns would be addressed following that meeting.

Attorney Landry clarified the project's proposed residential density, stating that the local ordinance allows 6 to 8 units per acre and that state law permits a density bonus of up to 5 additional units per acre. He stated that the proposed project density was slightly over 11 units per acre, which fell within the minimum density bonus permitted under state law. He further noted that state law does not establish a maximum density and that density determinations are typically informed by a site's importance to a municipality's affordable housing objectives and the availability of alternative sites.

Chair Phipps raised a question regarding the statutory review period, stating her understanding that the Planning Board had a 90-day window to act on the application, while Attorney Landry had referenced 120 days. Attorney Landry responded that 120 days applied to a standard land development application, but stated that regardless of the applicable timeframe, the applicant intended to work in good faith to provide the Board sufficient time to complete its review.

Chair Phipps asked whether the applicant would agree to extend the review deadline beyond March 29 in order to allow the continuation of the public hearing into March or further. Attorney Landry stated that an extension was acceptable. Chair Phipps then inquired as to how much additional presentation time the applicant would require. Attorney Landry estimated that the remaining engineering presentation would take approximately 30 minutes and that the architectural presentation could take up to one hour, stating that the applicant expected to complete its presentation at the next meeting.

Solicitor Goins stated that the Board and applicant could coordinate additional special meetings if needed, noting that the matter would require substantial time for both Board review and public comment. Chair Phipps asked whether a formal written extension would be required. Solicitor Goins responded that the extension would be memorialized in writing and placed on the record through the minutes and transcript. Solicitor Goins suggested that a one-month extension, moving the deadline to April 29, would be appropriate at that time. Attorney Landry agreed and stated that further extensions could be requested if necessary.

Upon conclusion of discussion, Solicitor Goins recommended that the public hearing be continued to March 3 at 7:00 p.m. Chair Phipps called for a motion.

**MOTION: Member Rua seconded by Member Costa, moved to continue the Public Hearing of Belton Court Village Comprehensive Permit Preliminary Plan Application to the March 3, 2026 Planning Board meeting. Motion carried (7,0)**
