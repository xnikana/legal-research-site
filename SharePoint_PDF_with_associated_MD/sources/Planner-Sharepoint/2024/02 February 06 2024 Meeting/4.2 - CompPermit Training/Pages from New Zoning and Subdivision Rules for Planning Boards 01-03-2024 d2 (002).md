### **Low and Moderate Income Housing Act \* Comprehensive Permits for Affordable Housing**

- Developments containing a minimum of 25% affordable housing
- Amends the definition of "meeting local housing needs" to include a showing that at least 20 % of the total residential housing units approved by the municipality in a calendar year be affordable as defined by law
- Revises the definition of a municipal government subsidy to include zoning incentives and adjustments
- Set forth required minimum zoning incentives (density increases) based on the availability or absence of pubic utilities
- Sets forth certain dimensional standards and other requirements that constrain a municipality's ability to regulate the development (for instance, municipalities are barred from limiting the number of bedrooms below 3 for a single‐family dwelling unit
- Prohibits municipalities from adopting any ordinance or policy that operates as a limit or moratorium with respect to comprehensive permits

\*Extensively Amended in 2023

Reference : Senate Bill No. 1037 SUB A / House Bill No. 6081 SUB A

Numerous changes made to the Low and Moderate Income (LMI) Housing Act, to set a higher requirements for communities for reviewing applications along with higher standards for the approval or denial of comprehensive permit applications. Changes now allow for‐profit developers to appeal an approval with conditions that make the development financially or logistically impracticable. Previously, only nonprofit developers were allowed to appeal an approval with conditions, and had to show that such conditions made it impossible to proceed with the development without financial loss. The changes also set the minimum zoning incentives that must be provided, as follows:

- For properties connected or eligible for connection to public utilities, a density of at least:
  - 5 units per acre for proposals where at least 25 % of the dwelling units are affordable

## **Low and Moderate Income Housing Act \* Comprehensive Permits for Affordable Housing**

- Eliminates the master plan stage of review
- The review and approval process will consist of
  - a preapplication conference,
  - preliminary plan & public hearing
  - and final plan at the administrative level (with an allowance for final plan review by the planning board if a waiver of checklist items was required at the preliminary stage)
- Eliminates one of the required findings for approval, so that a finding of "no significant negative environmental impacts" to approve an application
