### **5. Town Council Referrals**

**5.1 Discuss and Act: Review A-Frame Signage Ordinance and Regulations and the Town's Lamppost Banner Program.**

Town Planner Crean reviewed the September 11, 2023 Town Council discussion regarding A-Frame signs and lamppost banner signs, both which were referred to the Technical Review Committee, Planning Board, and Economic Development Commission. Town Planner Crean summarized the Technical Review Committee's recommendation to incorporate these two items as part of a comprehensive signage study/plan as part of the Comprehensive Plan update.

Chair Phipps commented that she concurred with the Technical Review Committee's recommendation.

Member Bacher commented on the following:

➢ There is a misunderstanding about what the lamppost banners were intended to do. The banner program began to serve as a unifying element in the Village Center. Now, summarizing Council President Kustell's comments, it is more cluttered. The banners were originally one color with white lettering, the businesses were paying for the banners and got a small recognition on the bottom of the banner, more so to identify the business district rather than serving as an advertisement. What is currently up has no design control, such as multicolored logos. Member Bacher noted it is cacophonous and there is a need for design control.

➢ The graduating seniors banners were during COVID-19, and the Veterans banners came after. Member Bacher noted he believes they are a traffic hazard as drivers are drawn to not missing the names rather than paying attention to the road.

Member Morley asked what Warren does. Member Bacher responded that Warren has no banners. Vice Chair Rua commented that the business banner program is the primary fundraiser for the Barrington Business and Community Association.

Chair Phipps concurred with Member Bacher's comments that the graduation signs were a traffic hazard and distraction. Chair Phipps commented that when the signs were uniform, they were more attractive. Vice Chair Rua there are currently limited to no design guidelines.

Member Bacher then moved the conversation to A-Frames and commented that the issue really comes down to the need of Bluewater Grille, and that he is unclear the Council's ask. Town Planner Crean noted there have been businesses with similar concerns and provided more context on the Bluewater Grille A-Frame complaint. Member Bacher noted the Town's stringent regulations about advertising signage, that the purpose of an A-Frame sign is to address pedestrian traffic, the A-Frame in the case of Bluewater is being used as a billboard, and if the business has a need there should be discussion about a variance for their unique circumstance. Member Bacher concurred with the Technical Review Committee and Chair Phipps's recommendation.

Member Godfrey asked about existing enforcement. Town Planner Crean noted that the Town relaxed signage enforcement during and after COVID-19 and noted limited staff capacity, making enforcement a complaint-based system. Assistant Solicitor Goins noted an affirmative decision on cutting businesses a break during the pandemic and increased residential complaints while everyone was at home more.

Town Planner Crean noted that the Comprehensive Plan consultant will be required to hire a signage specialist as a component of the rewrite. Chair Phipps noted she would like to see the signage consultant be creative, noting the Rhode Island School of Design. Town Planner Crean noted members of the Board will be part of the selecting committee for the consultant and can evaluate if the consultant is bringing forward a creative approach. Member Bacher noted the Technical Review Committee's discussion of cleaning up the language and providing clearer visual guidance as well.

**MOTION: Member Godfrey seconded by Vice Chair Rua, made a motion to recommend to the Town Council that the Town, as part of the Comprehensive Plan update, engage a professional consultant to develop a comprehensive sign ordinance. Motion carried unanimously.**

- **6. Legislative Updates**
- **6.1 Discuss and Act: Provide recommendation on modification and inclusionary zoning components of the zoning ordinance (as part of the amendments to the Zoning Ordinance up for public hearing at the December 4, 2023 Town Council meeting).**

*Present: Peter Friedrichs and Kim Salerno, Libra Planners*

Town Planner Crean reviewed that the agenda items are being organized by Zoning Ordinance updates and Land Development and Subdivision Regulations updates, and that the Town secured technical assistance from RIHousing to work with Libra Planners on both. Crean reviewed the Board has already reviewed the use table, and Libra will be drafting specific and objective criteria. Salerno reviewed Libra Planners and the organization's background.

Town Planner Crean reviewed there will be a special meeting of the Planning Board on Monday, November 27 for the Board to review the special use specific and objective criteria to make recommendations to the Town Council in advance of the Monday, December 4 public hearing. Friedrichs and Salerno confirmed they will have draft materials ready for Monday, November 20 to allow the Board a week in advance to review the completed draft.

The discussion then moved onto modification. Assistant Solicitor Goins noted they were once optional, but with the new legislation they will now be mandatory (aside from lot area).

Member Bacher commented on wanting to stick with the minimum of 15 percent for modifications, and that he does not like the process, specifically noting his concern with the notice requirement.

Member Godfrey noted that greater flexibility is important and commented that he is in favor of 25 percent modification.

Member Bacher noted the concern on building on an existing nonconforming. Clerk Gillibrand noted that the Building Official sends applications requesting to build upon an existing nonconforming to go before the Zoning Board.

Member Godfrey confirmed with Assistant Solicitor Goins that the notice requirement cannot be altered. The Board discussed if modifications could be done by zone, and Assistant Solicitor Goins noted that was crossed out of the State legislation and must be uniform.

**MOTION: Member Bacher seconded by Member Kurtz, made a motion to recommend to the Town Council that modifications from dimensional requirements of up to 15 percent be permitted as part of legislative changes effective January 1, 2024. Motion carried 5-2 (Vice Chair Rua and Member Godfrey opposed).**

Assistant Solicitor Goins reviewed that the next significant discussion is inclusionary zoning, which Barrington already has, and the current threshold is three units. The new law gives the flexibility to raise the threshold of 10 or less. Goins noted the Town's 2014 amendments with fee in-lieu, explained fee in-lieu further, and confirmed it expired and is no longer an option in the Town's Zoning Ordinance. Assistant Solicitor Goins noted that the new legislation sets the density bonus at a two-for one density bonus. Friedrichs noted there are some towns eliminating inclusionary zoning until after the new year to allow more time. Member Bacher commented that he feels the Town should eliminate fee in-lieu, and to keep the threshold at three.

Member A. Mueller confirmed with Assistant Solicitor Goins that the Town does have an off-site option. Assistant Solicitor Goins also confirmed that the percentage of units that will be required to be affordable will now be 25 percent to be consistent with Comprehensive Permits, rather than 20 percent currently in the Town's Zoning Ordinance.

Vice Chair Rua asked about removing the off-site option. Assistant Solicitor Goins responded that it is nice to have the option as every unit helps with the numbers, however, it is something to discuss.

Member J. Mueller asked Assistant Solicitor Goins about the process, intent, and effect of the new legislation.

**MOTION: Vice Chair Rua, seconded by Chair Phipps, made a motion to recommend to the Town Council that the minimum number of dwelling units that triggers the inclusionary requirement shall be three dwelling units, which is the current Town threshold. Motion carried unanimously.** 

Friedrichs and Salerno briefly reviewed several other items for introduction and further discussion at the November 27, 2023 meeting.

The first introductory discussion was on substandard lots, which Friedrichs commented that it goes to the Board's concerns with modification. Friedrichs summarized Providence's zero lot sizes for preexisting nonconforming lots and wanted the Board to start considering for the November 27 special meeting. The Board requested this explanation be written up for consideration.

The second consideration is if the Board wants to proceed with Land Development Project or Development Plan Review. Assistant Solicitor Goins noted the new legislation clarifies Development Plan Review and updates to Land Development Projects, explaining the difference between the two. Town Planner Crean commented that Land Development Project is clearer than Barrington's Development Plan Review procedures and is unsure how many projects would come before Barrington that would fall under Development Plan Review. Member Bacher noted that the Development Plan Review provides more concrete guidance than Land Development Project. The Board discussed the differences further.
