Special Meeting 7:00 PM, March 28, 2023 Barrington Public Library – 281 County Road, Barrington, RI

### **1. Call to Order**

Chair Roni Phipps called the meeting to order at 7:00 PM

### **2. Roll Call and Determination of Quorum**

*In attendance:* Lawrence Bacher, Bill Kurtz, Greg Lucini, Brian Morley, Alexander Mueller, Roni Phipps, Brian Rua, and Rick Simms

# **3. Overview of process to date and summary of Land Use Plan 1 and Land Use Plan 2 (Crean, 10 mins)**

Town Planner Crean stated several housekeeping notes, including justification for venue location, that the agenda item was assigned to the Board by the Town Council, and that the Planning Board Regular Meeting is scheduled for Tuesday, April 4, 2023. Crean then explained the difference between 4ward Planning and Union Studio, noting that 4ward Planning is done their work. Crean concluded by noting the 53-unit figure came from the financial feasibility study from 4ward Planning, but Union Studio sketches only go up to 36 units, and clarified the acreage of the parcel.

## **4. Presentation, Union Studio (20 mins)**

*Present: Don Powers and Arica Thornton, Union Studio*

Powers explained who Union Studio is, and provided a presentation including:

- ➢ **How Union Studio Works,** emphasizing importance of the community context.
- ➢ **Previous Feasibility Studies.** 
  - o Reviewed the two land-use plans provided by 4ward Planning, both including public open space, single-family lots, and age-restricted cottage development/pocket neighborhood.
  - o Determined the reuse of the existing building as housing is not financially feasible, noting another feasibility study from another client who reviewed the property over 5 years ago.
  - o Considerations for land-use plans, including 20 percent affordable component and density aim.
  - o The cost to maintain the existing building is approximately \$310,000 per year.
  - o Developer Guidance, including:
    - Need for Alternate and Attainable Housing.
    - Climate-Resilient Community.
    - Access to Open Space.

## ➢ **Site Analysis.**

o Reviewed that there are over 150 lots exist in the area that are under 10,000 square feet, and noted the variety of lots in the area, including 25 Watson Avenue itself. Powers provided a neighborhood analysis with pictures of examples, and reviewed images of the existing site.

o Reviewed opportunities and constraints, including slopes to the southwest and the north, potential connections to the neighborhood, and buffers and open space.

## ➢ **Precedents and Unit Types.**

- o Larger homes on the frontages of Watson Avenue and Freemont Streets.
- o Smaller-scale units in the internal pocket neighborhood.
- o Provided floor plan examples for each, along with images from communities including:
  - Weymouth, Massachusetts.
  - East Greenwich, Rhode Island.
  - Dennisport, Massachusetts.
  - Concord Massachusetts.

## ➢ **Concept Designs.**

- o Powers explained the process behind the designs and reviewed the proposed/preferred concept schemes.
  - Reviewed the Proposed Concept Plan, including 22 cottage units, 14 singlefamily units, and a common building, for a total of 36 units and 72 spaces. The open space would be separated from the housing units, closest to the waterfront. Powers acknowledged the hesitation that has been expressed by the public about demolishing the existing Monastery building and noted some opportunities for re-using components of the building, including the stained glass.
  - Reviewed the Alternate Concept Plan, including a plan with the existing Monastery building intact, 17 cottage units, 14 single-family units, for a total of 31 units and 62 parking spaces. Powers noted some constraints, including reducing the amount of available open space, and that the development community is looking for balance of outcome and profit. Powers then reviewed some pros and cons about the alternate concept.
  - Powers provided a density comparison between Sweetbriar and 25 Watson Avenue.

### **5. Planning Board questions / comments (20 mins)**

Member Mueller asked whether the road depicted by the proposed public open space provides parallel parking spaces and Powers confirmed that it does provide 10 parking spaces.

Vice Chair Lucini asked if the public parking spaces are included in the total parking presented, and Thornton confirmed that the 72 spaces for residential, plus the 10 parking spaces for the public, noting that came from the developer guidance. Vice Chair Lucini also asked about the former client Powers noted who inquired about the property, and asked if that information is public or private. Powers noted it is not publicly available as it was determined quickly that re-use would not pencil out.

Member Morley asked about the methodology of analyzing the existing conditions and how far away from the site they considered for context. Powers noted that the area analyzed depends on the purpose of the project, but generally it is a 5-minute walk, equating to about a quarter of a mile. Member Morley asked how outliers are calculated and considered, and Powers noted that they stay as outliers contribute to the perceived character of the area.

Member Bacher asked about stormwater runoff and how that may be accommodated. Powers noted that relatively speaking this property is easy to manage with stormwater runoff as the open space can be leveraged for infiltration and provided some best practices for this type of design of components like rain garden, and other infiltration measures. Member Bacher commented that he does not see the opportunity to drain this site without ponding the open space, and Powers noted he cannot demonstrate, but knows it is possible from his prior experience

Member Mueller asked if Powers has an opinion or vision on plantings, or any opportunity to preserve existing, mature trees. Powers noted though he cannot give a definitive answer, he would recommend a site survey with the trees, noting how they add value to the property, and provided some of his observation from the site visit and the state of the plantings he saw.

Member Morley asked what the confidence level is that if the building was demolished how that can be done safely, and Powers noted measures like tenting that could be implemented. Chair Phipps asked what an existing building would look like in the context, and how it would be safe for the whole neighborhood, noting measures like boarded up windows. Powers noted housing is not an option for the Monastery building, but could be another use, though noting the proximity to a neighborhood could be a challenge. Powers noted how the building would be surrounded by homes that would look after it, but there is not a definitive answer.

Vice Chair Lucini asked if the building remains if the Town would the still own it and be responsible for maintaining it. Powers responded that no developer would agree to take it, in his opinion.

### **6. Public Comments questions / comments (20 mins)**

Conway noted her involvement with the 25 Watson Ad-Hoc Committee and reiterated that the financial feasibility did not pan out, and that the Town does not want to hold onto a property. Conway noted that the schools were given the choice to take on the property, but were not interested. Conway concluded by reiterating the original goal of senior housing, and expressed her opposition to both plans presented by Union Studio.

Tracy Boothman, 254 Nayatt Road, explained that she listened to the presentation and feedback about density, and asked if DEM was contacted about runoff and density or was a consideration at all. Powers noted that the plans as of now are only a concept, but environmental impact would be required consideration for any development. Town Planner Crean noted that questions will be recorded that can be addressed later. Boothman concluded noting the importance of commercial shell fishermen in the area.

Nunn noted the financial viability and wanting to see the financial models and how calculations were done. Nunn reiterated the confusion of the process, and wanting to be involved in the process.

He noted that the projections of single-family homes will not do that either. Grieser expressed that the character is unique, called the Town to work for conservation grants to preserve open space, or to maintain the zoning for single-family homes.

Caracuzzo noted the quahog ecosystem, and if the resources like Shellfisherman Association, CRMC, and DEM have been contacted or consulted, and how the shell fishing industry benefits both the Town and State as a whole. Caracuzzo concluded by stating that the lack of communication and transparency is frustrating.

### **7. Discuss and Act, next steps for 25 Watson planning process (20 mins)**

Member Kurtz noted that the Board are partners in this process, and that the existing building is an obstacle. Kurtz noted the building is not feasible for senior housing, and that the building is more a dormitory style, and that the Town needs to get rid of the obstacle. Member Kurtz expressed that 53 units will not get his vote and believes that the rest of the Board will vote likewise. He said maybe 36 is off the table, but there needs to be a way to start moving forward. Kurtz noted the significant carrying costs and the rise of inflation, noting concern of either more taxes or less services. Kurtz concluded by reiterating the Board's charge from the Town Council.

Vice Chair Lucini seconded Member Kurtz's comments and reiterated the need to move forward in a meaningful way. Lucini reiterated Councilor Conway's comments from the beginning of the meeting, and that though there was not a plan to start, the Town must move forward. Vice Chair Lucini noted components of the proposed concepts that are appealing but reiterated that the existing building is a dorm style. Lucini reiterated the goals of the Comprehensive Plan, and parts of the concept plans he likes such as public access to the shore, but parts he does not agree with like there being too many units. Lucini expressed that he hopes the process can provide options for the Financial Town Meeting, and, in his opinion, does not want to hold onto the property much longer, whether it is a new idea or to put the property back on the market. Lucini concluded that he is looking forward to hearing feedback and thanked the public for comment.

Chair Phipps thanked the public comment, and asked if the questions can be addressed at the next meeting.
