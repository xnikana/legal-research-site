# **4.1 Discuss and Act: Preliminary Plan Decision, Comprehensive Permit, 17 Lorraine Street**

Major Subdivision/Comprehensive Permit Application: Robinson Property, 17 Lorraine Street. Proposal would subdivide a 15,687 square foot lot (Assessor's Plat 10, Lot 41) into three separate lots. The existing multi-family building will be demolished. The existing "old windmill" will remain; it will make up the single family residence on Lot #1. Lot #2 will have a single-family dwelling. Lot #3 will contain a two-family dwelling. The site is zoned Residence-10. One of the units, proposed as part of Lot #3, will qualify as Low-Moderate Income Housing. Relief from provisions of the Zoning Ordinance and Subdivision Regulations is being sought through the Comprehensive Permit process.

Town Planner Crean reviewed the draft Preliminary Plan decision that was distributed to the Board, outlining the overview of the application, procedural history, findings of fact, and conditions of approval.

Member Bacher wanted to amend documents of records to include the architectural plans for lots 2 and 3 that were reviewed at the Master Plan stage. Member Kurtz confirmed that the application received and addressed Technical Committee Comments.

MOTION: Member Bacher, seconded by Member Kurtz, made a motion to approve the Preliminary Plan decision with the inclusion of reference to the architectural plans for lots 2 and 3. Motion carried unanimously, with Member Godfrey abstaining.

# **5.1 Discuss and Act: Rhode Island Legislature Update and Summary of Relevant Bills**

**The RI Legislature passed several bills this session related to land use and housing production. The American Planning Association-Rhode Island Chapter (APA-RI) leadership noted that this is the most significant set of changes to Rhode Island's land use structure in 30 years. The effective date for most of this legislation is January 1, 2024, which gives municipalities about 6 months to comply. APA RI commissioned the law firm of Ursillo, Teitz, & Ritch, Ltd. (UTR) to draft a summary of the legislation that passed and the most important key takeaways for municipalities.**

Assistant Solicitor Goins provided an update on the recently passed legislation, effective January 1, 2024. Goins noted that the information still needs to be presented to the Town Council, and requires amendments to both the Zoning Ordinance and Land-Use/Subdivision Regulations. Member Godfrey asked how the new legislation will effect the procedural process of applications, noting the potential Zion property as an example, and Assistant Solicitor Goins explained further that anything certified complete between now and December 31 will be processed as is, but after January 1, 2024 will follow new legislation. Town Planner Crean noted it will be important to know how this effects the Planning Board and Zoning Board's jurisdictions.

Assistant Solicitor Goins reviewed the new legislation with the Board, highlights including:

- The accessory-dwelling unit bill failed to gain passage. Though it did not pass this year, it is anticipated to come back next year. The highlight is that state law governs, and there nothing for the Town to do at this point.
- Amendments to the Zoning Enabling Act, including:
  - o Standards for granting a dimensional variance revised or eliminated.
  - o Special-use permit granted in conjunction with a dimensional variance (something the Town already permits).
  - o Specific and objective criteria for special-use permit categories in the use table. Without specific and objective criteria, it is considered permitted.
    - The Board pursued discussion on the Municipal Technical Assistance Program through RIHousing.
    - Member Bacher asked about moratorium while regulations being set. Assistant Solicitor Goins stated that her opinion is that change to the law would not give a good legal basis to do so. Member Godfrey noted how there is the option to change all special-use permits to "N" until details can be determined. The Board discussed adding this as an agenda item to recommend interim modification to the Town Council.
  - o Shifting Zoning Board docket to the Planning Board, including unified development review requirement
  - o State law prohibits automatic mergers in some cases.
- Adaptive Reuse, which will be applicable to nonresidential buildings in town that wish to convert to at least 50-percent floor area residential. This curtails the Town's ability to limit density in these cases. These applications will be subject to land development review unless

determined to be reviewed as development plan review, to be determined by the Planning Board and Town Council.

- o Member Godfrey asked about existing nonconforming, and Assistant Solicitor Goins confirmed it does not matter the previous use.
- Transit-Oriented Development, which is awaiting statewide regulations. No specific community called out in the law, but Member Godfrey noted this is intended to be a pilot program. Assistant Solicitor Goins noted regulations and criteria are still in process. Acting Chair Lucini asked if "transit" also includes the East Bay Bike Path.
- Comprehensive Plan Implementation, forcing municipalities to spend more time on the Comprehensive Plan process, specifically regarding affordable housing. Municipalities have 18 months to bring their future land-use map current, which shall govern land-use decisions on municipal level. This also requires the Town to conduct an annual review of the implementation element of the Comprehensive Plan, needing a strategic plan within the Comprehensive Plan. Assistant Solicitor Goins noted Barrington's plan is current until May 2025, with zoning needing to be in conformance 18 months following.
- Amendments to the Development Review Act.
  - o Development Plan Review standardized and revised statewide, flushing out what it is, and limits the circumstances in which it can be used.
  - o The thresholds for major and minor projects have changed. Assistant Solicitor Goins will provide examples to help illustrate further.
- Amendments to the Low-to-Moderate Housing Act.
  - o Assistant Solicitor Goins noted RIHousing draft numbers of Barrington's affordable units, percentage totaling about 3.8 percent.
  - o Changes to further streamline and standardize projects and process, including minimum zoning incentives, the Master Plan stage eliminated, and environmental impact stricken from the conditions.
- Inclusionary Zoning, which the Town already has, must be tweaked from 20 to 25 percent.
- Judicial Review, where the Zoning Board is no longer the Planning Board of Appeals, and instead will go directly to Superior Court. The State Housing Appeals Board will be eliminated.
- Notice requirements includes being posted on the website.
- Land Use Commission and Affordable Housing Commission extended until 2025.
