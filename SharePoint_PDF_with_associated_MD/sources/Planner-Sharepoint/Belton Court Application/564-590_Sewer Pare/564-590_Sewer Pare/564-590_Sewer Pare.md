Pare Corporation (Pare) was tasked with completing a sewer capacity analysis of the existing municipal sanitary sewer collection system located immediately downstream of the former Zion Bible College campus in the Town of Barrington, RI (the Town). ShineHarmony Holdings, Inc. (SHH) is proposing a new residential development at the property with 344 residential units. The proposed development sits on an approximate 37-acre plot of land and is being referred to as "Belton Court Village".

#### Pare's analysis incorporated:

- metered sewer flows within the Town's collection system;
- an estimated amount of infiltration and inflow (I/I) that may not be reflected in the metered flows, based on seasonal conditions during the monitoring period; and
- projected sanitary flow from the planned development.

Note that this analysis did not account for additional buildout or other future development in the Town's collection system.

EST Associates, Inc. (flow metering contractor contracted by SHH) initially performed flow metering over a 4-week period from December 17, 2024 to January 16, 2025. Due to inaccuracies with some of the data collected during that time, EST performed an additional four weeks of flow metering, from February 14, 2025 through March 14, 2025. Previous draft versions of this memorandum presented preliminary findings, based on the initial round of flow monitoring data. This May 2025 update to the memorandum incorporates the most recent round of flow metering data.

Following review of Pare's Draft Technical Memorandum (revision date March 4, 2025), the Town expressed their concerns with potential impacts from sanitary flows from the proposed development on the existing Bay Spring Pump Station (located at the corner of Lake Avenue and Read Avenue).

#### Max Daily Flow Calculation:

The RIDEM OWTS Regulations estimate 115 gallons per day (gpd) per bedroom for sanitary flow, representing a 'Maximum Daily Flow' per bedroom. Using this as a benchmark estimate for flow per bedroom, and referencing the 759 total bedrooms proposed for the "Belton Court Village" from information provided to Pare, the following calculations were used to determine the estimated Max Daily Flow:

Max Daily Flow = # of Bedrooms x Max Flow per Bedroom

**Max Daily Flow** = 759 Bedrooms x 115 gpd per Bedroom = **87,285 gpd**

#### Peak Flow (Design Flow) Calculation:

In accordance with standard sanitary sewer design guidance (TR-16), sanitary sewers should be designed on a peak hourly design flow basis. The peak hourly flow rate is defined as the largest volume of flow to be received during a one-hour period.

Using TR-16 Figure 2-1: "Ratio of Extreme Flow to Average Daily Flow", the following ratios were interpolated from the figure to determine the Peaking Factor for the estimated sanitary flow under conditions where project average daily flows are under 100,000 gpd:

- 1.0 Average Daily Flow Ratio
- 3.0 Maximum 24-hr Ratio to Avg
- 5.5 Peak on Maximum Day Ratio to Avg
- 1.83 Ratio of Peak Flow to Maximum 24-hr Flow (Peaking Factor for this application)

Peak Hourly Flow = Maximum Daily Flow x Peaking Factor = Design Flow

**Peak Hourly Flow (Design Flow) =** 87,285 gpd x 1.83 **= 160,000 gpd**

A peak sanitary flow of **160,000 gpd** from the proposed development was used for this analysis.

#### Bay Spring Pumping Station

Following review of the existing pumping conditions at the Bay Spring Pumping Station, it appears that there may be additional capacity available to manage the projected peak sanitary flow from the proposed development, pending modifications to the current pump speed control parameters and/or current wetwell operating levels. The projected peak design flow from the proposed development is approximately 160,000 gpd, which is equivalent to a rate of 111 gpm. The data collected during Pare's pump evaluation on April 21, 2025 indicates that an increase in pump speed would conversely increase the pumping flow rate from the station. During our onsite testing, an increase in pump speed from the existing speed setting at 37 Hz [62% of the rated 60 Hz frequency] to 45 Hz [75% of the rated 60 Hz frequency] resulted in a net increase single pump output of approximately 400 gpm, far exceeding the projected peak flow from the proposed development.

#### **Conclusions and Recommendations**

Based on our analysis, the majority of the collection system between the Belton Court Village site and the Bay Spring Pumping Station appears to have sufficient capacity for the flow anticipated from the planned development. However, three sections of 8-inch pipe in County Road would exceed 55% or more of their carrying capacity with the added flow from the development. The Town has already expressed concern with the resulting condition on this part of the system, and ShineHarmony Holdings should be prepared to address this concern. Pipe slope will be governed by the existing manhole invert elevations located upstream and downstream of any proposed sewer pipe replacement, so installing new 8-inch pipe at increased slope is not feasible. Rather, increasing carrying capacity by replacing these sections of pipe with larger pipe (i.e., 12-inch or 15-inch diameter) is the more likely alternative. Also, constructing a pump station from the development to the Town sewer is not recommended, due to the anticipated design and construction costs as well as the long-term operation and maintenance requirements a pump station will present.

The projected flow from the development is projected to increase flow to the Town's Bay Spring Pumping Station on the order of 15% under average daily conditions, based on an average dry weather flow of 210,000 gpd provided by the Town and flow meter data collected as part of this evaluation. Because this represents a significant increase in flow to the pump station, ShineHarmony Holdings should be prepared to work with the Town to identify which modifications to pump operations are necessary to accommodate this increase in flow. Based on our onsite evaluation, it appears that the pumping capacity from the station can be increased, but the final operating condition will need to be optimized with input from the Town.

It is also understood that Town of Barrington officials inquired whether SHH performed a similar evaluation of the existing connection from the site, formerly used by the Zion Bible College, to the City of East Providence sanitary sewer collection system via County Road/Willett Avenue (Route 103). As noted above, Pare has not performed this evaluation and we understand connecting to the Town of Barrington collection system was explored first since all other site permitting is being pursued with the Town. While system upgrades and modifications to pump operations, such as those noted above, will increase the system's carrying capacity in response to added flow the development, SHH should explore the viability of connecting to the City of East Providence system on a parallel track.

Pare is available to meet and discuss our findings with you and the Town at your convenience.

This memorandum summarizes an onsite evaluation of pumping operations at the Bay Spring Pumping Station in Barrington, RI. Pare Corporation (Pare) performed the evaluation on April 21, 2025 to understand existing pump operating conditions relative to the firm pumping capacity of the station. Harbor Controls, a subconsultant to Pare and the Town's pump instrumentation and controls consultant, assisted Pare during this evaluation.

#### *( [ isW iQ J 3 X P S 6 W aW iR Q , Q I R UP aW iR Q aQ d 2 S eUaW iQ J & R Q diW iR Q s*

The Bay Spring Pumping Station, located on the corner of Lake Avenue and Read Avenue, was originally designed and constructed in the late 1970's/early 1980's and is of "dry-pit/wet-pit" construction. A single 18-inch ductile iron gravity pipe influent to the station enters a concrete influent channel within the "wet-pit" side of the station. Upon entering the primary influent channel, flow passes through a comminutor (cutting device for reducing debris size in the waste stream) and splits into either of two individual concrete wetwell sections via a secondary concrete influent channel that runs perpendicular to the primary influent channel. There is a standby bypass influent channel aligned parallel to the primary influent channel that contains a manually cleaned bar rack (for isolation and bypass of the comminutor and primary channel). The individual wetwell sumps are situated below the primary and secondary influent channels and are isolated from one another. They are of square configuration with concrete infill chamfers on all four sides, sloped towards a central sump where the pumps' suction piping intake is located. Influent flow can be directed to either one or both wetwell sections by operating a series of isolation sluice gates installed within the secondary influent channel feeding the wetwells. At the time of Pare's onsite evaluation, only one wetwell section was being utilized while the other section was empty and available for visual inspection. The Town's Sewer Superintendent informed Pare that keeping one wetwell section online and one offline is typical operating conditions at this station (for the purpose of inspection and maintenance).

The station was most recently upgraded in 2006, replacing the existing pumps while also adding a third pump to the station with new piping, valves, and controls amongst other upgrades to station equipment and appurtenances. There are currently three (3) dry-pit submersible style solids handling pumps (as manufactured by Morris Pumps/Yeomans Chicago Corporation) located at the pumping

Pare observed that each pump's speed is manually set to run at a maximum of 37 Hz, which is equivalent to approximately 62% of the full speed of the pump (100% speed = 60 Hz). At the time of Pare's onsite pump evaluation, this pump speed produced a pumping flow rate ranging between 550 and 600 gpm, as recorded by the station's flow meter. Discharge head pressure was observed to be approximately 50-55 feet (22-24 psi) under these operating conditions according to the pressure gauges in the station. According to the Town's Sewer Superintendent, the pumps have been operating at this speed and these settings since the 2006 upgrade at the station and pump settings are typically not adjusted (manually or automatically) for varying flow conditions such as elevated influent flows during and following wet weather.

#### *\$ X W R P aW iF : eW Z eO O / eveO & R Q W UR O \$ X W R 3 X P S 6 S eed & R Q W UR O*

Harbor Controls informed Pare that the existing PLC contains a user-customizable program for controlling pump speed automatically as a function of wetwell level. Pare understands that this program is currently not being used to control pump speed. According to Harbor Controls, the current program logic settings contain a linear function of pump speed to liquid level within the wetwell with a minimum pump speed setting of 30 Hz (50% speed) and a maximum pump speed setting of 60 Hz (100% speed). Wetwell liquid level range settings are currently set to a minimum height of 45-inches and a maximum height of 55-inches, as measured from above the bubbler tube opening within the wetwell. When in use, this program would automatically control and adjust the pumps' speeds to react to real-time wetwell levels, as measured by the level monitoring system.

A 24-hr historical flow chart provided to Pare by Harbor Controls indicates that the pumps currently cycle on/off approximately five times per hour under normal flow conditions. The Town's Sewer Superintendent stated that the designated "lead pump" is alternated on a weekly basis through manual operator selection (Pump #1, #2 or #3) on the SCADA panel.

Peak hourly flows, pumping flow rates, and historical pump operational trends were not initially provided to Pare by the Town, but anecdotal information from the Town indicates that one pump operates under typical dry-weather conditions and two pumps may occasionally operate during periods of high flow conditions. As reported by the Barrington DPW and Sewer Department, the Bay Spring Pumping Station currently manages approximately 210,000 gallons per day (gpd), or about 1.5 million gallons (MG) each week, of sanitary sewer flow under normal, dry-weather conditions. The station reportedly manages approximately 280,000 to 300,000 gpd, or about 2.0 to 2.1 MG per week, during and immediately following wet-weather events and resultant high flow conditions. This equates to an approximate 33% to 40% increase in flow to the pumping station during wet weather conditions.

According to the Town, the pumping station has had difficulty managing influent flows during extreme wet weather events, but no reported evidence of sewer system overflows or private property backups were presented to Pare. The Town also suspects that there is a potential downstream hydraulic restriction at the Town's force main connection into the City of East Providence's collection system, specifically observed during wet weather (high flow) conditions. Pare is aware that the force main discharge from the Bay Spring Pump Station directly connects into another, larger force main pipe (via a wye connection along the East Bay Bike Path) that conveys flow from at least one other pump station within the Town (Brickyard Pump Sation) prior to discharge to East Providence. The competing force main flow is likely leading to this hydraulic restriction experienced during wet weather flow conditions. The Town reports a reduced pump output from the Bay Spring Pump Station under these conditions.

As previously stated, the existing pumps have a rated maximum pumping capacity of 1,200 gpm at design system head conditions (130 ft TDH). The pump testing results appear to indicate that the actual system head under current operating conditions may be lower than the design system head. Therefore, when pump speed is set to full speed (100% = 60 Hz), the pumps are operating on the right-hand side of their pump curve, thus pumping a higher flow rate than their rated maximum capacity (1,600-1,650 gpm vs. 1,200 gpm). As indicated by pump testing data for Test No. 4, the firm pumping capacity of the pumping station appears to be approximately 2,200 gpm when two pumps run simultaneously in parallel, both operated at 100% pump speed.

The Town provided Pare with a copy of the pump manufacturer's operation and maintenance manual, which contains factory performance test curves for the pumps. Following review of this information, it appears that the optimal pumping efficiency for this pump (80%) is achieved when pumping approximately 1,400 gpm at 120-125 ft of TDH. At current pump speed settings (37 Hz), the pumps are operating at a lower efficiency of approximately 55-57%. For comparison purposes, at pump speeds of 45 Hz to 60 Hz, the pumps would operate between 72% and 80% efficiency, closer to the pumps' optimal efficiency.

The pump testing indicates that an increase in pump speed from 37 Hz (existing setting) to 45 Hz would increase pumping flow rate by approximately 400 gpm. At the current wetwell operating levels, this would increase pump starts per hour from 5 (existing normal flow conditions) to approximately 6 starts per hour under normal flow conditions (based solely on pump runtimes and wetwell fill times recorded onsite during the testing). This is generally considered acceptable for pump longevity and efficiency standards (6 pump starts per hour or less). Without adjustments to wetwell operation levels or pump speeds (manual or automatic), pump runtimes and starts/stops would increase and vary during periods of increased influent flow conditions, such as during and following wet weather events.
