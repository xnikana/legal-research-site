To be clearer than the original Notice of Incomplete Application, the Low & Moderate Income Housing Act includes definitions [RIGL § 45-53-3]. The term:

(1) "Adjustment(s)" [aka "waiver(s)"] means a request or requests by the applicant to seek relief from the literal use and dimensional requirements of the municipal zoning ordinance and/or the design standards or requirements of the municipal land development and subdivision regulations. The standard for the local review board's consideration of adjustments is set forth in  $\S$  45-53-4(e)(3)(iii)(E)(II).

RIGL § 45-53-4(e)(3)(iii)(E), referenced above, states that the Planning Board must make a finding on six specified standards including the following:

(II) Whether the proposed development is in compliance with the standards and provisions of the municipality's zoning ordinance and subdivision regulations, and/or where adjustments [aka "waivers"] are requested by the applicant, whether local concerns that have been affected by the relief granted do not outweigh the state and local need for low- and moderate-income housing...

The Planning Board cannot make a reasonable finding of compliance without having a list of specific waivers the applicant requires to carry out the project as currently proposed. In the applicant's original submittal, the applicant offered an "...understanding of the key modifications to the current Residential Village Zoning District that would be required to accommodate the development of the project..."

As part of the applicant's statement above, the applicant offered a list of changes the Town needs to make to its Zoning Ordinance to accommodate the development. Offering this information is <u>not</u> in the format required for requesting a waiver. Instead, to ensure the onus lies with the applicant, the applicant needs to specifically cite the section of the Town Code, paraphrase the regulatory language from the Town Code, given that regulation list what relief is necessary, and offer an explanation why local concerns affected by any relief granted will not outweigh the state and local need for low- and moderate-income housing.

#### FURTHER RESPONSE TO CHECKLIST PARA. 4(E)

The required adjustments and waivers are set forth below, and repeated on the revised Plan Set submitted separately herewith.

The reason / rationale for each of the requested adjustments / waivers is identical, and is as follows:

The purpose of the Low and Moderate Income Housing Act (R.I. Gen. Laws §45-53-1, et seq.) (the "Act") is to promote use and design flexibility in connection with land use regulatory requirements that are not essential for public health and safety or environmental protection, and that are not unreasonable and are consistent with local housing needs, and the aspirations in the community's State-approved affordable housing plan, an element of the community's Comprehensive Plan. Whereas, at one time the Act had required adjustments / waivers to be based on "impossibility" and/or "infeasibility", that is no longer the case. Rather, adjustments / waivers are now much more generally and flexibly available under the Act, as long as they "do not outweigh the state and local need for low and moderate income housing". R.I. Gen. Laws § 45-53-4(d)(3)(iii)(E)(II). That is a finding to be made by the Planning Board following the Preliminary Plan hearing process.

The Act itself includes a "finding" and legislative statement of "intent" to the effect that "there exists an acute shortage of affordable, accessible, safe, and sanitary housing for [the State's] citizens of low and moderate income, both individuals and families, that it is imperative that action is taken immediately to assure the availability of affordable, accessible, safe, and sanitary housing, for these persons". And that "the provisions of this [Act] – including its liberal approach to adjustments/waivers – "are necessary to assure the health, safety, and welfare of all the citizens of this state".

Extraordinary circumstances call for extraordinary measures. While they may represent zoning and planning preferences under ordinary circumstances, none of them involve any genuine risk to public health, safety, or welfare and are reasonably related to the establishment of high density multi-generational residential housing (i.e., housing not limited to senior citizens) on a site that has been singled out within the Affordable Housing Plan as one of the only remaining available – and suitable – sites (in terms of size, location, availability of public utilities, etc.) to accomplish the Town's affordable housing objectives.

As set forth in full in the "Statement of Consistency With Comprehensive Plan" that is included as part of the applicant's overall submission (Supplement #1 To Tab 9), one of the two most important land use issues facing the Town is, "adding to the affordable housing stock".

(p. 133). The Comprehensive Plan specifically identifies the subject site here – the "former Zion Bible Institute company on Middle Highway" as one of only two properties in Town that are suitable to implement creation of a compact "village" community with a range of housing types at varying densities". That is, "to achieve increase[d] diversity of housing types in Barrington, with dwelling units that meet the needs of residents at varying life stages, recognizing that single family homes in traditional subdivisions may not meet the needs of all segments of the population ... ". The Comprehensive Plan actually discourages exclusively "senior"

developments and prefers the diverse, multigenerational design proposed here. ("Rather than encouraging segregation of the elderly in separate retirement institutions, urban designers should factor this social integration into more lively communities". (id., p. 17).

The vision articulated in the Comprehensive Plan requires a retrofitting/retro-planning of what had been a private campus with multiple dormitories and other buildings all in close proximity, established for mixed educational and residential purposes, with a multigenerational planned unit community development/campus, all in close proximity, with private ways, roads, parking areas, and carefully planned attractive landscaping. The existing zoning and land development dimensional and design regulations in Barrington, as described below, were not built for such an innovative, uniquely crafted, approach in any of the existing zoning districts. While the existing zoning district's emphasis on senior housing would generally and approximately accommodate overall density proposed here, more site specific design planning is needed for the overall objective here — as outlined above — to be accommodated. The adjustments / waivers, which do not involve genuine health / safety, welfare issues are integral components of an overall design that harmoniously accomplishes the aforesaid goals in a way that clearly outweighs the combined rigidities (outlined below) of a local regulatory zoning/design structure that was not geared toward the objectives of the Act or the local housing needs of the Town of Barrington.

#### (Supplement #1 To Tab 9 (" ... Statement of Consistency With Comprehensive Plan ... ")

It is noted that the Town of Barrington Comprehensive Plan is expired. However, to the extent relevant under controlling State law, the proposal is consistent with the most recent Comprehensive Plan (2015 Update).

- The Land Use Element of the Comprehensive Plan update sets forth, in its very first paragraph, on its first page (p. 133 overall) the most important land use issue for the Town: "As Barrington approaches build-out, with few parcels that can support growth, the Town is facing critical issues integrally related to land use planning." The first and foremost such issue set forth was two headed: "Providing housing options for an aging population and adding to the affordable housing stock."
- The Land Use Element makes specific reference to the subject property here, the former Zion Bible Campus as a recourse that has the potential to add over 200 units (p. 137). "Special Approvals" on special properties like the Zion Bible Institute Campus alone were projected to increase housing development by 590 units (above and beyond 255 additional single family units, 70 multifamily units, and 112 residential demolitions in the normal course) from 2015 to 2035. (id., Table 6).
- The Land Use Element re-identified Zion Bible Institute Campus (*i.e.*, repeating similar references in the 2009 Comprehensive Plan), "as a site unique in terms of size, with almost 40 acres, and because of the fact that the site is in transition with the buildings mostly vacant after the Zion Bible Institute vacated the premises.". (*id.*, 145).
- The Land Use Element aptly elaborated that, "Communities with ample land for future growth often plan for large areas involving many property owners; however, because of the limited amount of land left, the Zion site is identified specifically as an opportunity area for meeting multiple land use objectives.
- The Land Use Element further references and re-incorporates a document entitled, "Appendix II; Developer Guidance", originally a part of the State-approved 2010 Comprehensive Plan that "provides a means to evaluate proposed development projects and/or proposing new zoning for the site." (*id.*, pp. 145, 146).
- The "Developer Guidance Appendix I" to the 2015 Comprehensive Plan Update states that, "Two sites have been identified, through a 2008 Housing Land Use Study, as candidates for future village development: the 40-acre Zion Bible Institute, and the Sowams Nursery property. ...."
- The Developer Guidance Appendix II" to the 2015 Comprehensive Plan Update contains specific findings as follows:

#### "2 Findings

- A. The Town of Barrington's Comprehensive Community Plan outlines strategies to achieve the state-mandated 10 percent low and moderate income housing goal, and expanding housing options for current and future residents of Barrington. The Comprehensive Plan identifies a need to provide housing for a growing senior population, workforce housing for municipal employees and other workers, and persons with disabilities. (*id.*).
- B. Two specific areas of the Town identified in the *Housing For Barrington's Future: Housing Land Use Study* are suitable to implement creation of a compact "village" community with a range of housing types at varying densities". The former Zion Bible Institute campus on Middle Highway (Plat 14 / Lots 2, 4, 13, 341, 342, 343 and the former Sowam's Nursery.
- The proposed project design is also compatible with the key design guidelines of the "Appendix II Developer Guidance", including: H (Walkability); I (Social Integration); K (retaining a portion of Belton Court), and N (Mixed use).
- As noted in the Plan, a purpose of including such sites in the Plan is, "to achieve the following: A. Increase diversity of housing types in Barrington, with dwelling units that meet the needs of residents at varying life stages, recognizing that single family homes in traditional subdivisions may not meet the needs of all segments of the population, especially the senior population." (*id.*).
- The "Senior Residential Village" and "Residential Village" categories on the Future Land Use Map in the 2015 Update are both described (in terms of proposed development) as follows:
  - "Master-planned developments for large multi-acre tracts that incorporate a wide variety of land uses as described in the developer guidance for establishment of these zoning districts (Appendix II)".

This is the designation of the Subject Property in the Plan.

- The 2015 Update recited in its "Housing And Neighborhoods" element that Barrington was 467 units short of the 10 percent affordable housing goal. (id., p. 20).
- The 2015 Update also underscores the reality that, "with much of Barrington essentially at buildout", it is important to determine, "how will Barrington meet future needs for housing, both affordable and market rate, given the limited supply of developable properties that have access to water and sewer and adequate roads to support this growth?" (id., 6).
- ... "As depicted on Map HN-1, the remaining vacant buildable parcels zoned residential or commercial are scattered throughout Barrington, with just a few with more than 5 acres of

developable upland that also have access to infrastructure necessary to support new development (water and sewer)." (id., 9).

• The 2015 Update articulated an "Objective" (Objective HN-3.2: "Produce affordable housing units at a rate that keeps Barrington on the path toward achieving the 10 percent affordable goal by 2035.")

At that time the Town was at 2.6% of the 10% goal. According to the Plan, this meant Barrington was 467 units short of the 10% affordable housing goal. (*id.*, p. 20). (The Town had, per census, 6,386 dwelling units at the time.) However, also According to the Plan, factoring in additional growth, including additional market rate units, "Barrington would need to produce approximately 544 more affordable units to meet the 10 percent standard over the next 20 years [*i.e.*, by 2035]. (*id.*, p. 15.

- At this point, roughly half way through the 20 years, the Town is still 373 units short of the 10% goal, not accounting for future growth. This means that if no additional market rate dwelling is built in Barrington in the next 10 years, it would still need to average a little over 37 new affordable housing units each year to reach the goal. (HousingWorks RI at Roger Williams University 2025 Housing Fact Book).
- And the Subject Property remains the only property left that was identified in the 2015 Update as a suitable site for higher density multifamily housing. (The Sowams Nursery property is already spoken for.)
- Barrington's current affordable housing needs are grave. 1,571 of its households are "cost burdened" (1,243 Owner Households [23% of all Owner Households]); and 328 Rental Households, 57% of all Rental Households. ("Cost Burdened" means a household is spending more than 30% of its income on housing costs.) Total households are currently 5,978. 89% own; 11% rent. (Housing Works RI at Roger Williams University 2025 Housing Fact Book).
- The Comprehensive Plan discourages exclusively "senior" developments and prefers the multi-generational design proposed. The Plan states, "Rather than encouraging segregation of the elderly in separate retirement institutions, urban designers should factor this social integration into more lively communities." (*id.*, p. 17.
