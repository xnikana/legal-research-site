# **HOUSING & NEIGHBORHOODS**

Barrington is a community defined by neighborhoods, each distinguished by scale, character, and history. Denser development patterns along Bay Spring and Maple Avenue reflect those areas' industrial heritage, while old stone walls on Rumstick Neck mark the historic borders of agricultural fields. Drownville's origin as an early railroad suburb is evident in its varied housing mix and lot sizes. Elsewhere in town, orderly plats are typical of the housing boom of the 1950s and 60s, with layouts optimized for the automobile age.

Barrington residents identified their housing priorities throughout the comprehensive planning engagement process. Respondents to surveys and participants in various public forums noted deficiencies in Barrington's current housing stock, including lack of affordability, limited opportunities for aging in place, and very few options for residents seeking housing options other than detached single-family homes. Overall, residents expressed a desire for future housing development that is affordable, resilient, and consistent with the architectural character of Barrington in general and of its individual neighborhoods.

With most of its land developed to the extent allowed by zoning, the expansion of Barrington's housing stock faces considerable constraints. Despite these challenges, the Town remains committed to working towards expanding and diversifying housing opportunities while balancing its needs for growing its economic tax base and managing impacts on existing neighborhoods.

Like other municipalities throughout Rhode Island, Barrington must play its part to address emerging housing needs. This chapter profiles Barrington's community demographics and housing stock and recommends the housing mix best suited to meet anticipated local need through 2035. Additionally, it outlines a strategy for Barrington to make meaningful progress towards its housing goals and meet the State's housing production targets for affordable and market-rate units. Implementing the recommended policies and actions set forth in this chapter will allow Barrington to uphold its statutory obligation to work towards state housing targets while satisfying the needs and preferences of its residents.

## *Population*

As of the 2020 Census, Barrington was home to 17,121 residents and 6,439 households. The population has remained steady in subsequent years (17,113 residents and 6,341 households as of the 2023 American Community Survey.) According to the latest official projection by the Rhode Island Division of planning, Barrington's population is predicted to slightly decline through 2040 (*Technical Paper 162*, Rhode Island Statewide Planning Program, 2013).

Barrington's population has trended upward slightly more than predicted since 2020, possibly as a consequence of the COVID-19 pandemic, which drove population growth in suburban areas across the nation. Over the next twenty years, however, Barrington's population is projected to remain stable or very slightly decline.

# *Housing production*

In it's *Housing 2030* plan, the State identified Barrington as a "continued growth community," meaning the Town "will be expected to permit, on average, housing units equal to at least 0.4 percent of [its] existing housing stock annually." To meet the 5-year minimum housing production target, Barrington would need to add at least 128 units over the next five years, including 19 units that qualify as affordable. Over the next ten years, the Town would need to produce approximately 245 units to accommodate new households expected to settle in Barrington and to maintain the overall health of the existing housing stock (see **Figure 21**).

The production targets in *Housing 2030* are considered by the State to be the Town's annual benchmarks for 2025-2030. This Plan will be reviewed to confirm inclusion of strategies likely to realize meaningful progress toward production goals. Municipalities are likewise "required to provide annual progress reports to the Department of Housing, documenting the implementation of promised policy changes, actual housing production numbers achieved, and explanations of any shortfalls or challenges encountered."

The ultimate rate of housing growth is dependent on factors outside the Town's control, including the timing and scale of developments proposed by private developers. Regardless of growth prospects, however, every local market should strive to maintain sustainable vacancy rates and offer housing stock that is affordable, diverse, and well suited to the needs of residents.

## *Accessory Dwelling Units*

Newly passed State legislation—[cite]—aims to further boost housing production by making it easier for property owners to develop accessory dwelling units (ADUs) on their property. It provides homeowners statewide the right to develop a single ADU on an owner-occupied property to accommodate a disabled family member, or within the existing footprint of their structures, or on any lot larger than 20,000 square feet, provided the ADU's design satisfies building code, size limits, and infrastructure requirements. The legislation also places restrictions municipal zoning regulation of ADUs. In 2025, Barrington amended its zoning ordinance to conform to the statute.

Barrington should capitalize on the opportunity presented by ADUs to create small, affordable housing units in zones throughout town. The Planning Board should consider whether the zoning ordinance should be further amended to allow ADUs on smaller lots than required in State legislation. If so, accompanying regulations should also be promulgated to ensure that ADU design and siting is appropriate to its neighborhood setting.

A planning-level assessment of existing water and sewer infrastructure was completed for these identified areas to determine whether the current systems have the capacity for additional housing units. The assessment also identified potential impacts that would need to be addressed to support future housing development. The full assessment and accompanying maps are available in the Appendix. Key findings were as follow:

- **Wastewater Utilities**. Proposed developments at each of the potential development sites would connect to existing sewer mains in adjacent roadways. A capacity analysis would be required to determine if the existing pipes need to be replaced with larger pipes to support increased wastewater demands. The existing pump station facilities would also need to be assessed for capacity.
- **Water Utilities**. Based on available data and flow results, the assessment indicates that there should be adequate water pressure to support new development at sites in Barrington's business districts (NB, B, R-B Flex). However, a new hydrant flow test would need to be conducted in these areas to confirm the latest pressure. The Middle Highway sites are served by a private system, so if large development is proposed, it will be important to conduct a hydrant flow test to confirm pressure and system capacity.
- **Stormwater Infrastructure**. Stormwater runoff associated with future developments will likely be managed on-site for each location. Stormwater management practices such

as surface basins, subsurface chamber systems, and green infrastructure can be used to promote infiltration and to attenuate peak runoff flows and volumes. Future developments will need to reserve on-site areas of suitable soils to accommodate the footprint of a surface or subsurface infiltration system. A geotechnical investigation at each site is recommended to identify soil conditions and depth to groundwater or bedrock that would affect stormwater management.

Most of Barrington's land is at risk of flood, storm surge, stormwater inundation, or sea level rise due to the Town's coastal location. Portions of the potential development sites in the B and NB districts are within these risk areas. Consideration for building and site resiliency as well as stormwater management will be important for future development in these parcels. The Middle Highway sites are not located within areas of high risk.

Further analysis is needed to fully vet the development potential of these sites. The infrastructure assessment recommends geotechnical evaluations to analyze the subsurface soil conditions of the sites to better understand how it would impact their ability to be developed.

The B and NB zones along the County Road and Maple Avenue are high priorities for infill development owing to their proximity to commercial shopping, services, and transit. Zoning studies should be undertaken in these areas to evaluate barriers to infill development under the current ordinance, including lot size, setback, and parking requirements. Elements of transitoriented development (TOD) zoning may be appropriate to encourage denser and higher development.

Where appropriate, B and NB zones may also be considered for conversion to R-B Flex—a zone which has successfully yielded production of "missing middle" housing (including affordable units) in the Bay Spring section. The existing R-B Flex district should be studied to identify challenges and opportunities for improvement.

Subdivision of large lots in zones targeted for growth could further drive infill development. The Town should identify zones where such development is appropriate and explore the creation of a subdivision incentive program.

*Preservation of Neighborhood Character*

Limited developable land has prompted the demolition of existing homes and their replacement with larger houses. It has also resulted in some higher density developments enabled through comprehensive permits. This has put pressure on Barrington's existing neighborhoods, raising concerns about the impact on neighborhood architecture and development patterns, traffic, infrastructure capacity, and the Town and school budgets. Since developable land continues to be a scarce resource, it will be important for the Town to review its regulations to ensure clear design standards are in place to adequately mitigate impacts of increasing density pressures, including promoting compatible neighborhood architectural style and scale within new and higher density developments.
