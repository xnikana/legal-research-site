### **NATURAL RESOURCES**

### **Overview**

From the shores of Narragansett Bay to the wetlands of Hundred Acre Cove, Barrington residents take immense pride in the Town's natural environment and are committed to preserving and restoring its natural resources. Barrington's fifteen square miles include scenic shorelines, the beaches and waters of Narragansett Bay, the Barrington and Palmer (Warren) Rivers, and a vital mosaic of woodlands, meadows, ponds, and wetlands. It is this abundance of natural resources and scenic landscapes that give Barrington its distinct sense of place.

Increasingly, however, the town's defining natural resource—its lengthy shoreline—poses a significant challenge to the community. Heightened risks of flooding and more frequent and damaging coastal storms are projected to increase over the next twenty-five years, underscoring the imperative to protect coastal and freshwater wetlands and other sensitive areas from development.

Respondents to the Barrington 2050 community survey emphasized the importance of protecting environmentally sensitive areas and mitigating the impact of climate change on the natural and built environment. These themes were likewise prominent in conversations with residents and stakeholders. In coming decades, it will be crucial to balance growth with conservation, allowing Barrington to remain a vibrant—yet sustainable—place to call home.

### *Management of Development Impacts*

The Town has adopted measures to help protect natural resources, including the following:

• *Wetlands Overlay District*. Barrington has stringent wetland protections in effect, with the adoption of the Wetlands Overlay District Zoning Ordinance, established as part of the Zoning Ordinance rewrite in 1994 (Article XXV). It requires the granting of a special use permit by the Zoning Board of Review, with advisory review by the Conservation Commission, prior to any development within 100 feet of either a coastal wetland or a freshwater wetland one-half acre or greater in size. The Wetlands Overlay District is more restrictive than State regulations requiring RIDEM approval for activity within 50 feet of a wetland. A 100-foot wetland setback, however, is a preferable standard in Barrington, where pressure to develop marginal sites will only increase as the community approaches build out.

Most applications for special use permits related to the wetland buffer are granted. From January 2010 through August 2014, for example, 42 special use permit applications were before the Zoning Board for encroachment into the wetland buffer. Of these, 38 were granted, while two were denied (and two withdrawn).

• *Groundwater Recharge Area*. Development proposed within the recharge area, as mapped on Map NCR-1, requires advisory review by the Conservation Commission.

### *Other Standards*

• *Stormwater design*. Applicants for qualifying development activity must develop and submit a stormwater management plan consistent with the Rhode Island Stormwater Design and Installation Standards Manual and the Rhode Island Soil Erosion and Sediment Control Handbook. The Planning Board in recent years has approved plans with low impact development features, such as at Walker Farm Lane, where surface runoff from the street drains into a retention pond, rather than through culverts and pipes; the development features narrow street widths and pervious pavement for on-street parking spaces and driveways. The Board has also granted waivers allowing private streets for subdivisions of up to two lots, allowing access through shared driveways that require much less pavement compared to development of a street to Town standards.

## *Management of Open Space*

Another issue relates to the stewardship of conservation lands held in public and private ownership. The Conservation Commission and Barrington Conservation Land Trust have advocated adopting and enforcing official management plans for Town-owned conservation lands across Barrington. Concerns include encroachments from neighboring parcels, enforcement of easements, illegal dumping and other deleterious activities, and public access to environmentally sensitive areas.

Comprehensive management plans provide a framework for the protection of publicly owned or otherwise protected conservation lands. At minimum, each management plan should include a description of the area's significant characteristics and features, management objectives, and a prioritized implementation schedule. Management plans may also include:

- Control of hydrological characteristics
- Removal or control of nuisance/exotic plant and animal species

### **Tree and Forest Management**

The natural senescence of Barrington's urban canopy—as well as increasing impacts to tree health from pests, pathogens, and wind—has resulted in a large population of dead and damaged trees throughout the town. Such trees threaten power lines and roads during emergencies.

To maintain the canopy, the town needs to actively plant trees to replace those lost to natural mortality, storms, development, pests, and neglect. While the town has been replacing trees, more trees need to be planted by both the public and private sectors at greater numbers to sustain and grow the current canopy.
