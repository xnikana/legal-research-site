### **HISTORIC & CULTURAL RESOURCES**

### **OVERVIEW**

Expansive views of Hundred Acre Cove. The facades of historic colonial homes. The white steeple of the Congregational Church. These are the images that greet visitors entering Barrington on R.I. 114, the main route into Barrington from the north. The drive into town provides a picturesque snapshot of just one section of Barrington. There also are the shorelines, beaches and waters of Narragansett Bay and the Barrington and Palmer Rivers; the distinct neighborhoods of West Barrington, Hampden Meadows, Rumstick Road, and Alfred Drown; the agricultural lands in the northeast corner of town; and numerous outstanding historic buildings scattered throughout town. It is this abundance of scenic landscapes and local history that give Barrington its special distinction, its sense of place.

More subtle, yet no less significant, are the archaeological sites which are the record of thousands of years of human activity on these lands. When Giovanni da Verrazzano sailed up Narragansett Bay in 1524, he was greeted by the indigenous inhabitants of the area—the Pokanoket people. A century later, Ousamequin (also known by his title, Massasoit,) the "Great Sachem" of the Wampanoag confederacy, allied with settlers in the recently established Plymouth Colony—events which eventually provided the genesis for America's Thanksgiving traditions. The lands that are now Barrington were a central part of the Pokanoket homeland, known as "Sowams." Indigenous history is apparent in the Town's many Pokanoket toponyms, from Popanomscut in the west to Chachcust in the east. Longstanding human occupation of the area is revealed in the discovery of artifacts, burials, and other traces.

Many respondents of the Barrington 2050 survey were unaware of the Town's significant cultural resources and historic sites, such as Belton Court and the history of the Pokanoket tribe. 58% of respondents said that the town could do more to promote existing historical and cultural resources. This deficit represents an opportunity to promote, and thus preserve, the town's rich history and cultural legacy. [NOTE: for a pull quote graphic, use image on page 8 of "Welcoming Community."]

Barrington has a wealth of historically significant properties (see Map HCR-X), including the Civic Center Historic District along County Road. This National Register District includes Town Hall, the Peck Center (Library/Senior Center), Prince's Hill Cemetery, and Wood Pond. The Alfred Drown historic district in West Barrington and Jennys Lane historic district off Rumstick Road are also on the National Register of Historic Places.

The Allen-West House at 153 George Street has been listed on the National Register since 2013. The circa 1763 house was once part of a large farmstead consisting of more than 100 acres. The area around the house was also the site of the first Baptist meeting house in America[.](#page-1-0) <sup>1</sup> This meeting house on Nockum Hill, erected in the 1660s, does not survive. A stone monument installed in 1905 commemorates the site's significance in early colonial religious history. More recently, archeological investigations have confirmed that this area was also the site of the earliest skirmishes in the conflict between settlers and Indigenous local peoples known as King Philip's War.

One of Barrington's most iconic structures is the 55,000-square-foot Belton Court. Located off Middle Highway, the mansion was constructed in the early part of the 20th century as a private estate. Later, it housed Barrington College and the Zion Bible Institute. The building was added to the National Register of Historic Places in 1976.

#### The Barrington Preservation Society

The Barrington Preservation Society, chartered by the State in 1965, is the town's principal force for historic preservation. The Society occupies space on the ground level of the Town Library. The room houses the Town Museum, archival storage of artifacts, and town records.

Through its on-going Plaque Program, the Society has identified approximately 174 private and public buildings that are at least 100 years in age and retain their architectural integrity. Other notable initiatives include the installation of interpretive wayside signs along the Barrington section of the East Bay Bike Path and in the town center, highlighting local history. Most recently, in 2022, they supporte[d](#page-2-0)<sup>2</sup> placement of a Rhode Island Slave History marker outside the Barrington Public Library, to raise public awareness of the presence and contributions of enslaved people in Barrington's early history.

## Scenic Views

In January 1990, the RI Department of Environmental Management completed an inventory of scenic areas throughout the State. Barrington was included within the Narragansett Bay Area. Three areas in Barrington are identified in the state inventory, which are also designated on Map NR-X:

- Hundred Acre Cove, as seen primarily from Wampanoag Trail.
- An area in the southwest corner of Barrington that includes Echo Lake, Mussachuck Creek, and a portion of the Nayatt Point area. This scenic area was described to have an attractive combination of open land, woodland and wetlands.
- The Smith Cove Marsh. This wetland with varied vegetation encompasses all the coastal marshes between Rumstick Point and Adams Point.

Both the Echo Lake and the Smith Cove sites were defined as distinctive landscape areas, having the highest visual appeal and containing a great deal of variety in form. The Hundred Acre Cove site was defined as noteworthy, an area of lesser but important scenic value. All three areas are threatened by coastal erosion and sea-level rise.

### **Nockum Hill Heritage Area**

Nockum Hill is believed to be the site of the first Baptist Meeting House in North America, built circa 1667. Early documents also identify it as the site of the first battle of King Philip's War in June 1675. The Town acquired the purported site of the meetinghouse in 2019 to preserve the property from development. In 2020, initial archeological investigations (metal-detection and ground-penetrating radar) performed by the nonprofit Public Archeology Survey Team, Inc. (PAST), located the possible meetinghouse site, as well as two possible 1667-75 English homesteads. The survey also uncovered impacted musket balls in a pattern consistent with a battle[.](#page-6-0)<sup>5</sup>

In 2024, Barrington was awarded a Preservation Planning Grant from the National Park Service's American Battlefield Protection Program. With this financial support, PAST conducted a second archeological field survey of the historic settlement area to determine the scale of the fighting that took place on and around Nockum Hill. The investigation ultimately confirmed the location of the Nockum Hill meetinghouse. Traces of defensive structures around the meetinghouse, a nearby burned 17-century homestead, impacted lead ball and shot, and a possible iron arrow point likewise confirm that a violent confrontation between English settlers and the local indigenous population occurred at the site. It is likely that this battle was the flashpoint that set off King Philip's War.

Given its significance in American history, it may be advisable to pursue designation of the Battle of Nockum Hill site as a National Historic Landmark (NHL). NHLs are historic properties that illustrate the heritage of the United States and represent an outstanding aspect of American history and culture. ## **Preservation of George Street's Agricultural Heritage and Rural Character**

George Street is an area of major historic significance for Barrington. It is also where the preservation of the predominant agricultural use and rural character are critical concerns. The Town's most critical agricultural land is in this section of town, which includes portions of the Douglas Rayner Wildlife Sanctuary at Nockum Hill and significant parcels farmed by Four-Town Farm. However, the area also has undeveloped parcels that, despite a lack of water and sewer and adequate roads, are under development pressure. Currently, there are only 10 houses, including two vacant, within the 75 acres zoned Residence 40- Conservation Development, where residential use is permitted. This equates to just one unit per 7.5 acres of land in the R40-CD zone.

The conversion of farmland to housing in this area consumes land historically used for agricultural purposes, diminishes the rural character and potentially impacts environmentally sensitive areas, in particular the terrapin habitat within Nockum Hill. Also, more people living in close proximity to active farming operations could make farming more difficult due to complaints related to dust, noise, etc.

These are not new issues. In 2012, the Town adopted a new zone for this part of town changing the area's Residence 40 zone to Residence 40-Conservation Development. The zone is intended to provide more flexible subdivision design, with the goal of preserving important open space and limiting the development footprint.
