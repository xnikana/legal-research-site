#### **4. New Business:**

**b. Discuss and Act: Application from Bay Spring Crossing Condominium Assoc. for Proposed Zoning Amendment and Intention to Offer the Town Council Findings and Recommendations as Required**

Solicitor Goins clarified that the Boards role in the Bay Spring application was to review the request and make a recommendation to the Town Council. Solicitor Goins also mentioned that the Town Council will hold a public hearing and ultimately decide whether to approve the zoning change. Goins further noted that there are two things the Town needs to be concerned with.

1. Procedure: for a map change, letters notifying public hearing before the Town Council and this Boards recommendations.

2. Substant Development: all zoning amendments need to be consistent with the town's comprehensive plan.

Chair Phipps asks Town Planner Durfee to outline what the applicant is presenting to the Board.

Town Planner Durfee explained the applicant was the president of the Bay Spring Crossing Condo Association and was present. He also announced that a business owner from the condo community was also present, at the Board's request. Durfee explained that BSCCA wanted to change their residential business flex zoning to a residential district but making sure it still allowed multifamily units in the rezoning. The issue that the association has with the current zoning has to do with how that affects parking. As a commercial building, the number of parking spaces with handicap accessibility are greater and the association believes this limits the already limited parking.

Chair Phipps asked Durfee to assure the Board that the applicant is acting for the entire Condo association and not just himself. Durfee explained he received payment from the Condo association and was confident the applicant wasn't acting alone.

*Chair Phipps welcomed the applicant to the microphone.*

Applicant Patrick Majewski of 60 Bay Spring introduced himself to the Board. Majewski explained that the Bay Spring Community is made up of three buildings. The first two buildings were both under neighborhood business zoning, however, the property was re-zoned into Residential-Business Flex zoning around 2017. Majewski explained that the idea was for small businesses on the first floor and residential units on the second. The zoning has led to conflicts, specifically regarding parking, between the condo association and a physical therapist's office in the complex. Majewski said the condo association tried to resolve the issue by addressing the business owner directly and found out through contact with the ADA that the way they currently had their handicap parking set up was incorrect. The condo association voted to change the layout of that handicap spot in September of 2023. The business owner moved to legal action because of the change in parking and found out that because Bay Spring is classified as mixed use, the building needs more handicap spots available to visitors. Majewski explained that rezoning Bay Spring residential would eliminate the need for making more ADA compliant parking spots. He also expressed that he believed that it fits in the comprehensive plan because it would allow for more units to be used for housing, which could assist with the senior housing issue in Barrington.

Chair Phipps addressed applicant Majewski about the number of active businesses, and he confirmed there is only one active business. Member Kilmartin asked the applicant to clarify because in a drive through the complex appeared to have more than one business. Mr. Majewski then explained that there were two active businesses.

Chair Phipps had two comments for the applicant. One, was it appeared that the BSSCA was trying to get the zoning change to resolve a parking issue. Two, the 2015 Comprehensive Plan and the new Comprehensive Plan both express public interest in mixed use developments.

*The Chair invited the business owner from the Bay Spring.*

Business owner Kimberly Serra, who owns a physical therapy practice in 60 Bay Spring came forward. Mrs. Serra stated that she had been operating out of 60 Bay Spring for 12 years and the practice had been thriving. Mrs. Serra addressed the issue with the BSCCA changing the parking stripping in June 2022 without proper communication to the rest of the Bay Spring tenants and business owners. In doing so, they moved the handicap access from the left to the right, and because of these changes, made it her handicap clients can no longer come because they are unable to get out of their vehicles. Mrs. Serra said this led her to contact the Department of Justice. The Department of Justice let her know it was not a legal issue and to contact the Governor's Commission on Disabilities. Mrs. Serra explained that through a site visit with that company, they found out that 20% of the parking needed to meet ADA

regulation for parking. She expressed that the condo association did not want to comply with this and so have been seeking the zoning change to get around making more handicap spots. Mrs. Serra further explained how she believed the mixed-use buildings were good for Barrington.

Business owner Serra also explained that the condo tenants have had issues with the BSCCA not communicating with them about condo issues.

*Chair Phipps asked if there were any other public comments about this application*.

Ben Andreozzi introduced himself to the Board and stated that he represents the Bay Spring Crossing Associates with the developers of Bay Spring Crossing. Mr. Andreozzi explained that he was supposed to get minutes from the BSCCA meetings and just received the minutes from October. Mr. Andreozzi explained that in those meetings minutes the President of BSCCA was stating that the first building was zoned residential, which Mr. Andreozzi says is false. They were mixed use from the beginning. He states that these were just opinions by the President and that Bay Spring has not been trying to wind down the business aspect of the buildings.

Patrick Majewski did come to the microphone again to address that the condo association is not doing anything adverse to the businesses and that they have stated that they would comply with the ADA requirements.

The Board discussed their recommendations to the Town Council, noting that the strongest point was that changing the zoning would go against the Economic Development section of the current Comprehensive Plan. The Board brought up that the residential flex zoning ordinance also mentioned voids of larger parking areas. The Board suggested the need for that ordinance to be more specific if it meant by area or by number of parking spaces. It was also mentioned that spot zoning creates a very undecidable precedent for the Board.

**MOTION: Member Bacher, seconded by Vice Chair Rua moved to recommend disapproval of the proposed rezoning of the parcel at 60 Bay Spring, based on the following findings as called for in §37-7E and §185-50A:**

- (1) **Statement on the general consistency of the proposal with the Comprehensive Plan, including the goals and policies statements, the implementation program and all other applicable elements.**
  - a. The Comprehensive Plan identifies the Bay Spring area as the Town's original industrial district, noting the industrial use has dwindled since termination of the railroad through Barrington (CCP p. 4)
  - b. The current Residence-Business Flex zoning was implemented in 2017 as a result of a zoning study of the Bay Spring corridor completed by Horsley Witten Group with extensive public input. Prior to that the Bay Spring area was zoned Neighborhood Business since 1994, which requires a business use at ground level but permits multi-family occupancy on upper levels. The Residence-Business Flex zone suggested by the zoning study permitted residential units at ground level, so long as ground level units were configured to accommodate Business use when desired (CCP pp. 10, 18)

- c. The Economic Development chapter of the Comprehensive Plan notes that there are limited opportunities for commercial expansion in existing business zones or to expand existing business zones (CCP pp. 26, 34)
- d. The following Comprehensive Plan Economic Development goals and policies are relevant to this rezoning request
  - 1. Policy ED-1.1.1: Support "walkable retail"development in the commercial/mixed-use zones.
  - 2. Policy ED-1.1.3: Promote well-designed, compatible mixed-use and commercial development, enhancing public spaces and expanding multi-modal access.
  - 3. Goal ED-2: Better manage parking in commercial areas to support economic development and to make these districts more attractive places to visit and shop.
  - 4. Goal ED-3: Expand the non-residential tax base while preserving community character, with an emphasis on infill development and building re-use over expansion of commercial zoning
  - 5. Policy ED-3.1.1: Encourage appropriate neighborhood-scale uses.
  - 6. Goal ED-4. Strengthen the Town's commercial base with a focus on promoting existing local businesses and economic sustainability.
  - 7. Policy ED-4.1.1. Work toward productive working relationship between the Town and the local business community.
    - E. Conduct study of options, such as zoning ordinance amendments, to permit uses that encourage the provision of services and amenities that will support creative and knowledge based businesses and consulting activity, including *[Note: the Horsley Witten Group Study addressed this policy action for the Bay Spring area, resulting in the current Residence-Business Flex zoning]*
  - 8. Goal ED-5: Promote desired commercial activities in mixed-use areas.
  - 9. Policy ED-5.1.1: Support economic development objectives in strategic areas, including the Neighborhood Business zone near County Road and on Bay Spring Avenue. Community input will be needed to determine the type of development desired for these areas, if different than existing zoning.
    - B. Complete a parcel/zoning study of the Bay Spring area to identify options to promote the type of economic development desired by the community in the area. The study should address the following:

. . .

• The feasibility of encouraging "live-work" units, where the business operator lives above first-floor office or retail space, in the Bay Spring Avenue area from Narragansett Avenue to Washington Road. *[Note: the Horsley Witten Group Study addressed this policy action for the Bay Spring area, resulting in the current Residence-Business Flex zoning]*

10. Policy HN-2.1.3: Allow well-designed, compatible mixed-use development in commercial areas, while enhancing the pedestrian environment.

**Summary Statement**: *The Bay Spring corridor has historically provided industrial and commercial opportunities which provide local jobs and expand the commercial tax base. The current Residence-Business Flex zoning was specifically applied to this district to encourage commercial development, permitting additional residential use as a means of making the development of commercial space economically feasible. The Bay Spring corridor has further been identified as one of the limited areas in Barrington available to commercial development. While the project at 60 Bay Spring has not achieved full commercial buildout, it has accommodated up to five businesses and may well find additional commercial occupancies as a result of the growing trend to work locally/work from home. It would be contrary to the Comprehensive Plan to rezone the property to preclude further commercial use and to make existing commercial occupancies non-conforming. It would specifically contradict Goal ED-2:*

Better manage parking in commercial areas to support economic development and to make these districts more attractive places to visit and shop.

*to rezone the property to resolve a parking issue to the detriment of the commercial occupants and thereby discourage economic development.*

*In review with §185-2 the proposed rezoning would be contrary to the goals and policies of the Comprehensive Plan.*

- **(2) Consideration of the applicable purposes of this chapter, as presented in § 185-3.**
  - A. Promoting the public health, safety and general welfare.

*The commercial owners of units at 60 Bay Spring provide physical therapy, psychotherapy and construction management services. It would be contrary to this purpose to disadvantage these providers.*

B. Providing for a range of uses and intensities of use appropriate to the character of the Town of Barrington, and reflecting current and expected future needs of its residents.

*Limiting the use of this parcel to residential would be contrary to the purpose of providing for a range of uses reflecting community needs.*

- C. Providing for orderly growth and development which recognizes:
  - (1) The goals and patterns of land use contained in the Barrington Comprehensive Plan; . . .
  - (5) The availability and capacity of existing and planned public and/or private services and facilities;
  - (6) The need to shape and balance urban and rural development; and
  - (7) The use of innovative development regulations and techniques.

*The current Residence-Business Flex zoning was created as a result of a detailed study incorporating significant public input in order to expand the range of commercial opportunities in Barrington. The proposed change requests the Town to apply a zone change ("Multifamily") which would abrogate that intent. The Multifamily zone is not currently defined in the ordinance; it would require careful consideration to determine the purpose, dimensional and use restrictions defining any new zoning district. The action of reclassifying existing commercial businesses that we encouraged to be non-conforming is contrary to the purpose of providing for orderly growth and development.*

*. . .*

L. Promoting implementation of the Barrington Comprehensive Plan.

*The proposed zoning change is directly contrary to the stated goals and policies of the Comprehensive Plan. The Comprehensive Plan would have to be revised, including State approval, to effect this policy change, in compliance with RIGL §45-24-34.*

. . .

**Motion passed unanimously.**
