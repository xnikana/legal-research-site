### *Community Context*

Barrington, Rhode Island, is defined by its extensive 19.6-mile coastline, stretching across two peninsulas bounded by Narragansett Bay and the Palmer and Warren Rivers. The Barrington River bisects these landmasses, connected by the Central and Barrington River Bridges. Geographically, the western peninsula's Village Center abuts East Providence, while the eastern Hampden Meadows area links to Swansea, Massachusetts. This configuration ensures that every location in Barrington is less than two miles from the coast, cementing its strong coastal identity and increasing its vulnerability to sea level rise and intensified storm surges. [2015]

Characterized as a medium-density developed community, Barrington contains significant natural and recreational areas. ### *Priority Impacts*

Flooding stands as Barrington's most pervasive natural hazard, stemming from various sources such as storm surge from hurricanes, winter storms, and extreme precipitation events. Its low, coastal, and flat topography, coupled with extensive wetlands, makes flooding a constant concern. State GIS data reveal nearly two-thirds of Barrington is within a FEMA flood zone:

- 36% in "AE" zones that would be inundated in the event a storm with a 1% annual chance of occurring (also known as a "Hundred Year Storm");
- 6% in "VE" zones (also known as "Velocity" zones) where this 1% annual flooding threat would be combined with significant wave damage; and
- 24% in "X" zones where would be inundated in the event of a storm with a 0.2% annual chance of occurring (also known as a "Five Hundred Year Storm").

High winds also pose a threat, especially coastally. Wildfires, earthquakes, tornadoes, and hailstorms are comparatively low-risk hazards. Accelerated biodiversity loss is a local threat as it is a state and national one. [2015, https://dem.ri.gov/earth-day]

## Severe Rainstorms and Floods

As a low-lying, water-surrounded community with 6.9 square miles of inland water, Barrington faces significant flood risk from heavy rains and storm surge. FEMA designates high-risk areas as Zone AE (1% annual flood chance) and Zone VE (Velocity Zone, 1% annual chance with destructive waves). Barrington's Flood Insurance Rate Maps (FIRMs), effective July 7, 2014, show Zone VE along its western and southern coasts near Narragansett Bay and the Warren River. Undeveloped coastal wetlands, like those near Rumstick Point, offer natural flood mitigation. Behind the VE zone, an AE zone extends inland, encompassing low-lying areas around Brickyard Pond, Echo Lake, and key estuarine wetlands, meeting floodwaters entering from the Barrington River and effectually cutting off the Nayatt neighborhood to the south. [2015]

Barrington frequently experiences localized flooding from storms and heavy rains. March 2010 stands out, with storms causing widespread state flooding, culminating in a Presidential Major Disaster Declaration for Rhode Island, including Bristol County. Barrington itself endured substantial inundation in various areas, with over [XXX] requests made to Barrington Fire Department for basement pump-outs. While precise flood event prediction remains challenging, FEMA provides this guidance: properties located within designated Zone AE and Zone VE high-risk flood zones face a 26 percent probability of experiencing a flood event over the typical 30-year span of a mortgage. This underscores a significant, long-term flood risk for many Barrington properties. [2015]

### Winter Storms

Nor'easters, with their strong northeasterly winds, are common winter phenomena. They often cause coastal flooding and significant erosion of beaches and dunes, increasing future storm damage. Their impact varies based on intensity, duration, and tidal phase. While most severe in winter due to temperature differentials, their winds can approach hurricane force, damaging structures. Prolonged storm surges can persist through multiple high tides, severely threatening Barrington's expansive coastline.

Historically, major events like the Blizzard of '78 and Winter Storm Nemo in 2013 caused widespread disruption, injuries, and substantial economic losses. More recently, a December 23, 2022 storm brought unforecasted and significant coastal flooding to Barrington. These events highlight continuous coastal vulnerability and the need for preparedness. Climate models suggest warming winters and wetter, but more frequent, winter storms [source: Picard, C.J., Winter, J.M., Cockburn, C. *et al.* Twenty-first century increases in total and extreme precipitation across the Northeastern USA. *Climatic Change* 176, 72 (2023)). [HMFMP]

### Extreme Heat

Extreme heat, defined here as prolonged summer temperatures 10+ °F above average combined with humidity, is a serious hazard. Rhode Island has warmed 4°F since 1900, experiencing more hot days and record warm nights recently. Climate models forecast increasing extreme heat events, which threaten human health, particularly for outdoor workers, children, and seniors. Furthermore, heightened demand for air conditioning during heatwaves can overload power grids, risking widespread outages. Such power loss would critically exacerbate health risks, highlighting a dual challenge for community resilience. [RIHMP]

The 2024 Barrington Strategic Tree Canopy Plan identified "heat islands"—areas of higher-thanaverage ambient summer temperatures—in the Bay Spring neighborhood, around Barrington High School, in the commercial district, and in the neighborhood roughly bounded by Foote St. and Maple Ave. While more urban areas of the state experience on the whole more intense heat island effects than in Barrington, these local areas merit strategic attention: by 2080, Rhode Island's climate could feel like the current climate of Mississippi. Augmenting canopy in these areas would over time reduce ambient temperatures and provide additional benefits. [STCP, RRB]

### Droughts

NOAA's 2022 modeling projects increased annual and extreme precipitation for RI. However, higher temperatures will also intensify droughts by increasing evaporation, leading to faster soil and surface water depletion despite more rain. It would take a fairly extensive drought to significantly impact the Bristol County Water Authority's ability to supply potable water to Barrington. Nonetheless this risk must be considered in light of future climate uncertainties. [2015, RIHMP]

### Biodiversity Loss

Climate change is significant driver of biodiversity loss in Rhode Island and in Barrington. Across the state, plants are flowering earlier (18 days for leaf-out) and migratory birds are departing later, disrupting food webs. Salt marshes and their flora and fauna—such as Barrington's saltmarsh sparrows—are being threatened by sea level rise. These ecological shifts raise public health concerns, including toxic algae blooms and increased vector-borne diseases. Declines in Barrington's pollinator populations have been noticeable. In a 2023 survey of Barrington High School students, biodiversity loss was placed as the second most worrisome climate change impact after coastal flooding. [Resilient Rhody, 2018; RRB]

### Low Risk Hazards

Barrington generally experiences low risk from several hazards. Earthquakes are minor due to no major fault lines. Tornadoes are rare, though Barrington experienced an F1 in 2008 causing minor tree damage. Wildfires are uncommon, confined mostly to brush fires in scattered wooded areas. Hailstorms are infrequent, with only four recorded in Bristol County between 1950-2023.

In 2023, solid waste and wastewater accounted for 8% of Barrington's GHG emissions. ### *Flood Mitigation*

### Building Codes

The Town's building official is responsible for ensuring that new or substantially improved structures meet building code requirements. Before a building permit is issued for construction in the AE or VE zone, an elevation certificate that verifies a proposed structure meets the minimum elevation requirements must be submitted. Related state building code requirements include:

- Elevation: New construction and substantial improvements must be elevated to or above the Base Flood Elevation (BFE) plus 1 freeboard for AE zones and 2 feet for VE zones. State law allows a freeboard of up to 5 feet to be excluded from height calculations.
- Foundations: Within AE zones, enclosed areas below BFE must have flood openings (vents) that allow for automatic entry and exit of floodwaters to equalize pressure, preventing structural damage. Within VE zones, structures must be elevated on piles, piers, or columns.
- Utilities: Electrical, heating, ventilation, plumbing, and air conditioning equipment and components must be elevated to or above the BFE.

- Anchoring: Structures must be adequately anchored to resist flotation, collapse, or lateral movement. ### *Critical Assets are Vulunerable*

Barrington's vulnerability analysis undertaken for its 2022 Hazard Mitigation & Flood Management Plan identified critical points in its infrastructure and population susceptible to natural hazards. This includes residential/commercial structures, bridges, roads, utilities, erosion-prone beaches, and vulnerable populations like children and the elderly. [2015]

High wind events can further obstruct transportation and damage roads, structures, and electric utilities, potentially blocking evacuation routes. Above-ground transmission and distribution lines are highly susceptible to heavy snow, ice, and strong winds, leading to power outages. These outages critically impede emergency management and compromise resident safety. Coastal erosion, accelerated by winter storms, further exacerbates flood risk by diminishing protective land buffers. Given these realities, mitigation efforts are judiciously focused on highrisk flood and wind hazards, rather than low-risk earthquakes, wildfires, or hailstorms. [2015]

*Nature-based solutions can be utilized to build resilience and mitigate the negative impacts of hazard events*

Barrington sees the value of nature-based solutions in adapting to climate change. Naturebased solutions use natural processes and restored or adapted ecosystems to build resilience to natural hazards. For example, in 2023 the Town established a marsh migration corridor at Walker Farm. This natural corridor of native wetland vegetation encourages gradual salt marsh expansion inland, mitigating harmful impacts to the park as sea levels rise. Barrington's 2024 tree inventory and canopy study are informing present and future efforts to increase tree cover, which can mitigate urban heat island effects, address stormwater flooding and support biodiversity. The Barrington Land Conservation Trust actively secures land donations, preserving open space and preventing development in flood-prone areas. Integral to the Ready & Resilient Barrington plan, resilient landscaping efforts are underway, aiming to protect and enhance natural systems to thrive amidst escalating climate impacts. [RET]

*A concerted effort is needed for community engagement on natural hazards*

Recent studies have equipped Barrington with vital data, enabling homeowners and businesses to make informed decisions for greater resilience against natural hazards and climate change. The Town is actively pursuing time-sensitive goals advancing climate resilience and energy transition that require immediate community-wide collaboration. Barrington firmly commits to expanding its residents' capacity to prepare for, respond to, and adapt to evolving climate impacts. [RET]

## **Issues and Opportunities: Energy**

*Rhode Island—and Barrington—Have Set Ambitious Energy Targets*

On April 14, 2021, Governor Dan McKee signed the 2021 Act on Climate bill into law. This landmark legislation updates the 2014 Resilient Rhode Island Act, establishing enforceable emission reduction mandates. It boldly positions the state to tackle climate change and transition towards a clean energy economy.

In accordance with the Ready & Resilient Barrington climate action plan, Barrington is committed to aligning its greenhouse gas mitigation goals to that of the state's:

- Achieve net zero greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by 2050;
- Support the State's transition to 100% renewable energy by 2033, in alignment with the General Assembly's 100% Renewable Energy Standard

Barrington will need to significantly increase the energy efficiency of its housing stock to support this goal of achieving net zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050. Increasing the energy efficiency and electrification of its existing and future housing stock should be prioritized through both policies and incentives that encourage homeowners and developers to adopt energy-efficient technologies and renewable energy solutions. In addition, energy efficiency measures and the installation of renewable energy systems can make living in Barrington more affordable through reduced energy bills.
