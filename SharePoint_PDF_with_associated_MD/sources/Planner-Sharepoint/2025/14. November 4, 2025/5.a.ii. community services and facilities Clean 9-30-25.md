**Overview**

This Comprehensive Plan element comprises an inventory and discussion of
the community services and facilities provided by the Town of Barrington
to ensure residents' health, safety, and welfare. Responsible
stewardship of existing resources and proactive planning for future
needs is essential to maintain a high quality of life throughout the
community and to preserve and enhance the environment.[^1] This element
addresses the adequacy of existing facilities and programs, as well as
plans for necessary improvements.

Barrington is well served by a range of community services and
facilities:

-   Municipal and School Facilities (see **Map CSF-1**): Town Hall, Peck
    Center (Senior Center & Public Library), Public Works, Public Safety
    Building, public school buildings and Bay Spring Community Center.

-   Municipal Operations: Town administration, police and fire
    protection, emergency management, harbormaster, public works,
    library services and senior services.

-   Municipal Infrastructure: stormwater, water and sewer facilities

This chapter meets the State's comprehensive plan requirements for
services and facilities planning. Certain municipal functions are
discussed elsewhere in the Plan, including transportation facilities
(Circulation), parks and recreation services (Open Space & Recreation),
energy (Energy) and emergency management (Hazards).

**Existing Conditions**

**Town Hall**

The historic Barrington Town Hall, built in 1888, houses Town
administrative offices, the School Department, and the Town Council
Chamber meeting room. Departments at Town Hall include the Town
Manager's Office, Finance (which also includes Tax Collections and the
Tax Assessor), the Town Clerk's Office, Human Resources, and Planning,
Building and Resilience (in the Lower Level). The Bay Team / Prevention
Coalition has offices on the second floor; the Town is the fiscal agent
for the regional organization.

Alterations to the building or the site are subject to review by the RI
Historic Heritage and Preservation Commission, which holds a historic
preservation easement that encompasses the Barrington Civic Center
National Historic Register District (for further discussion see
[Historic and Cultural Resources]{.underline}.)

Since 2005, renovations to Town Hall have included: replacement of the
slate roof, renovations to offices on the first and second floors, and
refurbished office space in the lower level (basement), including the
addition of a lift for ADA access.

**Public Schools**

The Town owns and maintains six school buildings: Barrington High
School, Barrington Middle School, Hampden Meadows School, Nayatt
Elementary, Primrose Hill Elementary, and Sowams Elementary. The school
administration offices are housed in Town Hall. In 2019, a new \$60
million Middle School building opened. The project required
reconfiguration of the school campus. The new school was built on what
was an athletic field near Lincoln Avenue. It replaced the old 1950s-era
sprawling school building that was torn down to make room for the
construction of athletic fields, parking, and courts (basketball and
tennis/pickleball).

The five remaining schools, all built more than 70 years ago (some with
more recent additions), have deficiencies that will be addressed as part
of a major school building construction projected funded by a \$250
million bond approved by voters in 2023 (63 percent for, 37 percent
against). Reimbursement by the State is expected to cover as much as 50
to 55 percent of the total cost. The plan calls for renovations and
building additions at the High School, Primrose Hill, Nayatt, and
Sowams, with students from Hampden Meadows (currently a townwide 4^th^
and 5^th^ grade elementary school) shifting to the expanded elementary
schools. The future use of Hampden Meadows School has not yet been
determined -- whether demolition or repurposing for some other school
department function.

Public school enrollment (grades K-12) fell by approximately 35
students, or about 1 percent, over the last decade. During this
timeframe, enrollment peaked at 3,358 in 2019, but this number has since
dropped, to[taling 3,212 in 2023-24.]{.mark} (see **Table
[xx]{.mark}**). Pre-Kindergarten enrollment has increased from 17 in
2014-15 to 56 in 2023-24.

**Emergency Management **

State law (Title 30, Chapter 15) requires cities and towns to establish
a local emergency management agency to individual municipalities, headed
by a director in charge of emergency preparedness and coordination with
the RI Emergency Management Agency (RIEMA). In Barrington, the Fire
Chief serves in this capacity with support from a part-time emergency
management coordinator.

The Town has formed a staff committee to work with the local Emergency
Management director on emergency / disaster planning and departmental
response during emergencies. The Town has worked to secure interagency
agreements with adjoining communities to strengthen response
capabilities. An agreement with East Providence has been secured to
provide an emergency shelter (East Providence High School) during
natural disasters, when necessary. Barrington lacks a facility that can
qualify as a Red Cross-certified shelter. With the exception of Primrose
Hill Elementary, all municipal and school buildings are in areas with
high potential for impacts from storm surge flooding -- either on-site
or on nearby streets.

**Public Safety**

The Public Safety Building on Federal Road, completed in 1999, serves as
headquarters for both the Police and Fire Departments and houses the
Police-Fire Dispatch Center and harbormaster office. The harbormaster
dock, adjacent to a public boat ramp, is located at the Police Cove Park
site on the Barrington River at County Road.

The Town has a full-time professional Fire Department with 29 trained
firefighters, including the Fire Chief, and one half-time civilian
personnel position.

**Barrington Public Works**

The Barrington Public Works Department operates out of a facility on
Upland Way, which includes offices, parking for DPW vehicles, a
maintenance garage, storage sheds and the Town's Recycling Center. The
department is responsible for maintaining Town-owned facilities and
infrastructure, including local roads, storm drainage system, sewer
system, sidewalks, and municipal buildings.

Public Works also provides a variety of other services, including
maintaining parks and Town Beach, lining and grooming of playing fields,
snow removal, road plowing and salting/sanding, and mosquito control.
The Department oversees the refuse collection and recycling contract
with a private hauler and manages the Town Recycling Center on Upland
Way. The Town has an agreement with the Barrington Schools in which the
school budget includes funding to support the Public Works Budget in
exchange for DPW handling maintenance of school grounds and removal of
snow from school drives and parking lots. The school credit also helps
cover the cost of recycling and refuse pickup and disposal.

**Refuse Collection and Recycling  **

Collection of refuse, recycling, and yard waste is on a weekly basis and
is provided by MEGA (Waste Connections) which is a private collection
company and the Town is in its last year of an 8-year contract.
Residents may also bring recyclables, brush and grass clippings to the
Town's Recycling Center adjacent to Public Works located on Upland Way.
Recyclables that are accepted at the drive-up facility, in addition to
standard curbside recycling, include: vegetable oil, rigid plastic,
textiles, white goods, computers, televisions, scrap metal,
leaves/grass, anti-freeze, motor oil/filters. Refuse is only collected
curbside; it is not accepted at the Recycling Center.

Curbside white goods collection is also provided for one week each
month; residents must call for an appointment during collection week.
Brush chipping takes place for six weeks in the spring and fall. Brush
is chipped curbside by Public Works crews. Residents must call for an
appointment; brush is accepted at the recycling center in a dumpster.
The brush chipping program is for small residential use and is not
intended for commercial and landscaper use.

Rhode Island Resource Recovery recycling tons are collected curbside as
well as from the Town's Recycling Center and brought to the Johnston
landfill facility. The metrics in **Table 4** show how Barrington's
recycling rates compare to the state overall. For calendar year 2024,
Barrington's diversion rate of 49.4 percent was significantly higher
than the statewide diversion rate of 29.5 percent. That year,
Barrington's diversion rate ranked fourth out of the 39 cities and towns
in the State. Town residents on a per-household basis recycled about 655
pounds for the year, compared to 393 pounds per household for the state.
The town's rejected recycling loads -- often due to excessive wetness --
totaled 21 pounds per household in 2024, compared to the statewide
household average of 159 pounds.

**Water Supply**

Barrington\'s public water supply, along with that of Warren and
Bristol, is provided by the Bristol County Water Authority (BCWA), a
public water authority which has been operational since 1984. As **[Map
CSF-2]{.mark}** shows, most of Barrington has access to public water,
with one notable exception being the George Street area. The BCWA
purchases 100 percent of its water from a single source: the Providence
Water Supply Board (PWSB).

Water is delivered from the Scituate Reservoir via the East Bay Pipeline
under the Providence River. Completed in 1998, the pipeline provides the
system with a supply of up to 7.5 mgd of treated water. A 2019 pipeline
leak under the Providence River underscored the vulnerability of this
connection, and reinforcing the need for BCWA to find an additional
water source.

The former surface water supply consisting of the Shad Factory Reservoir
and Anawan Reservoir in Rehoboth, Mass., the Swansea Reservoir in
Swansea, Mass., and the Kickemuit Reservoir in Warren were deemed to be
of insufficient quantity and poor water quality. All of these are
currently off-line.

A former 0.65 mgd groundwater source, located on Nayatt Road in
Barrington, was taken out of service in 2001 due to significant
reductions in flow capacity and increasing iron, manganese and salt
contamination. The treatment plant consisted of an iron and manganese
removal system designed for 2 mgd that treated groundwater from a
12-inch gravel-packed well located at the facility. There are no plans
for it to be returned to operation. The property is also the location of
the Nayatt Road booster pump station, which is a component of the East
Bay Pipeline.

BCWA has started taking steps towards removing obsolete water system
infrastructure. In 2023, the Authority completed Phase I of its
treatment plant demolition and in 2024 completed the removal of both the
Upper and Lower Kickemuit Reservoir Dams. In doing so, it removes the
on-going costs and liability associated with maintaining infrastructure
that is no longer part of the water supply system.

Average daily demand for BCWA water has dropped, from 3.65 million
gallons per day (mgd) in 2005 to 3.15 mgd in 2025. Today, average
household demand is 46,000 gallons per year, or 40 gallons per person
per day. According to the BCWA, consumption has decreased significantly
over the past 20 years due to conservation, slower growth rates, the
loss of industry, and smaller family size.

BCWA's 10-year capital plan allocates \$1 million to \$1.5 million
annually for water main rehabilitation projects to improve water quality
and the structural integrity of the system, including the cleaning and
lining of pipes in Barrington.

The Town Manager is responsible for coordinating Barrington's response
with the Bristol County Water Authority and the Water Resource Board in
periods of drought or extended dry conditions that require an emergency
response to reduce water usage in the community. In response to past
events, Barrington has appointed a citizen water conservation committee
to work with Town departments to notify the public about water
restrictions and encourage practices that reduce water consumption.

**Wastewater Treatment**

Public Works is responsible for maintenance of the sewer system
infrastructure within Barrington, including the force mains, laterals
and pumping stations. The Town has six major sewer pumping stations,
located at Police Cove, near the Barrington River Bridge, on County Road
near Prince's Pond, Freemont Avenue, Legion Way near Brickyard Pond,
Walnut Road, and Bay Spring.

The pump stations at Police Cove, Prince's Pond, and Freemont pump into
the Brickyard Pond station on Legion Way. The Brickyard station, along
with the Walnut Road and Bay Spring stations, pump into a sewer force
main located adjacent to the East Bay Bike Path. There are also smaller
ejector stations and sewer grinders that pump and move the wastewater
into the system into the various pumping stations ([see **Map
CSF-3**).]{.mark}

The Town paid a significant percentage of the recent upgrades to the
Headworks for the East Providence Wastewater treatment facility which
was funded by Federal ARPA. In 2019, the Town contracted with Evoqua
Water to treat the high levels of hydrogen sulfides at the Brickyard
Pump Station with a chemical (nitrate) drip system funded by the
operating sewer budget. In 2024, the Town hired C.B. Utility of Bristol
under an emergency contract to replace over 200' of 14" diameter pvc
pipe on Legion Way and repair the sewer manhole at the intersection of
Middle Highway at Legion. Also in 2024, the Town funded the new DPW
Assistant Superintendent (1/2 funded by the Sewer budget) to better
manage the Sewer Division and provide more routine maintenance on the
system.

The sewer force main connects to the treatment facility in East
Providence. The Town shares the cost of upgrades to the East Providence
treatment facility based upon the percentage of Town's flow through to
the plant. In 2023-2024 the meter pit off Bullocks Point Avenue was
upgraded by replacing all piping, wiring, meter, and controls due to
deterioration. The cost for this work covered through the sewer
enterprise fund, which is also the mechanism for covering future
multi-million-dollar improvements at the East Providence plant. The Town
also undertook upgrades to the six major stations as well as
improvements to the ejector stations and grinders.

In 2017, the Town replaced the vactor truck at a cost of nearly
\$280,000.00 which was funded by the Sewer operating budget. Costs of
monitoring the entire sanitary sewer system as part of the Town's
preventative maintenance program include video cameras checking sewer
lines, jet-rodding and cleaning of the lines with the vactor truck,
upgrading of pumping station components and computers, and monitoring of
nitrogen.

**Stormwater Management**

The Town maintains drainage systems within the public rights of way,
including the use of a street sweeper to keep catch basins clear of
debris. There are a few isolated stormwater detention basins that, in
general, are maintained by homeowners associations, as required through
the subdivision approval process. The Town has easement rights to access
stormwater facilities on private land, to complete maintenance as
necessary. Funding for personnel and equipment comes from property taxes
(there is no stormwater utility).

The roadway stormwater system maintained along State and local roads
includes total of 600+ catch basins (see **Map CSF-4**) and 340+/-
manholes. Most of this drainage infrastructure is along major roadways
such as Wampanoag Trail/County Road and the mixed-use sections of Maple
Avenue. Several State roads have no stormwater facilities along
significant stretches of roadway---including sections of New Meadow
Road, Sowams Road, Massasoit Road, Middle Highway, Nayatt Road and
Washington Road.

Most residential streets lack drainage structures. Exceptions include
Mathewson Road, Broadview Drive, south of Brickyard Pond, and several
neighborhood streets off New Meadow Road and Sowams Road and near the
Zion Bible Institute.

The Town's requirements for stormwater management within new
subdivisions and land development projects are detailed in the Land
Development & Subdivision Regulations. Drainage plans are reviewed by
the DPW director and, when required, a peer review engineer, subject to
Planning Board approval. Additional requirements---such as prohibitions
on draining to public roadways and on connecting sump pumps to the sewer
system---are covered in the Code of Ordinances (Chapter 91).

**Map CSF-5** depicts impervious coverage in town. Most of these
surfaces---roads, parking lots, rooftops, paved driveways, sidewalks,
etc.---are concentrated around the Village Center, Maple Avenue, and
large non-residential sites that have large buildings and/or large
parking lots, such as schools, marinas and churches. The map shows the
densest residential neighborhoods, such as Bay Spring, with a
significant percentage of impervious coverage.

As discussed in the Natural Hazards element, the Town has worked to
reduce impervious surface where feasible. For example, the Town in 2014
took advantage of financing from the State's Clean Water Fund to
complete drainage improvements at the Town Beach, a project that
involved the removal of excess asphalt, the installation of rain gardens
to capture runoff, and the replacement of an asphalt swale with a
rip-rap swale in Water Way, eliminating a point source discharge.

**Senior Center and Recreation Department**

[Senior Services]{.underline}

The Senior Center, located on the ground floor of the library building,
provides opportunities for social and recreational activities, personal
enrichment, wellness, advocacy and volunteerism for people who are 50
years and older as well as for younger individuals with disabilities. It
is a nationally accredited center managed by a part-time director, seven
part-time staff members, and a nine-member advisory board. Membership is
no cost for Barrington residents, with a small annual fee for
non-residents. The Peck Center also operates a lunch program that is
open to everyone, regardless of membership, providing an additional
opportunity for socialization and community connection. 

Services include health insurance counseling, RIPAE (RI Pharmaceutical
Assistance for the Elderly) application assistance, income tax
preparation, flu clinic, various health screenings, and a
senior-companion visitor program. Programs and activities (most at no
charge) include educational programs, discussion groups, health
promotion, and exercise classes. Transportation services are available
through a free weekday bus for senior residents to travel to and from
the Center, as well as for regular shopping trips; reservations are
required. 

Many programs are done in collaboration with other Town departments and
local organizations such as VNA of Newport County, Bayside Family YMCA,
Barrington Prevention Coalition, Barrington Learning Center, and AARP.
Additional financial support is received from the Friends of the
Barrington Peck Center organization which has provided a small meeting
room, café bar, kitchen equipment and fitness programs. 

The Senior Center's former Emergency-Response Dialer Program has been
discontinued, as the vendor no longer manufactures or supports the
emergency-dialer devices previously used. 

There are plans for adding an audio-visual system in the multipurpose
and community room, and a bike rack outside of the center.

**Bay Spring Community Center**

The 1910 two-story Town-owned building on Narragansett Avenue,
originally built as a firehouse with a community room on the second
floor, has provided space in recent years for meetings, events, and
programs through the Town's partnership with the volunteer group Friends
of the Bay Spring Community Center. The building has become an important
asset for the Town, given the growth in demand for space at the Public
Library.

The Town has completed several significant projects at the center since
2010, including the installation of a lift providing ADA access to the
large meeting room on the second floor, and renovations to the first
floor that addressed first floor ADA access, resolved fire code issues,
and replaced an inefficient boiler system.

In 2024, the Town was awarded \$1.37 million from the federal ARPA
Capital Project Fund Grant through the Governor's Community Learning
Center program to modernize the interior spaces of the building. Planned
improvements include converting the former fire station bay into a
level, multi-use meeting room and reception area, constructing a small
rear addition for flexible meeting space, and creating three soundproof
meeting pods equipped with audiovisual technology. A renovated kitchen
with new cabinetry and an island will also be added to support community
gatherings and programming. Infrastructure upgrades will include new
HVAC, electrical, LED lighting, IT and Wi-Fi systems, as well as
structural steel reinforcements and second-floor repairs. A new
ADA-access ramp with a canopy will further enhance accessibility.

The renovations are designed to establish the Center as a Community
Learning Center, supporting employment resources, telehealth and health
monitoring, after-school education programs, and wellness and
recreational activities.

Construction started in June 2025 and is expected to be completed by
August 2026. Under the terms of the grant, upon completion of the
project Barrington will be required to keep the building open on
weekdays so the public is able to utilize the spaces for educational,
employment, and health-related services and programs for a minimum of
five years. The Town will provide staffing to meet this requirement. A
portion of the cost to operate the center will be offset by a \$95,000
State support grant.

[Other Facility Issues]{.underline}

Additional issues related to Town and school properties and facilities
include:

· *Impact of Enrollment on School Facilities.* The school building
construction project funded by a \$250 million bond approved by voters
will expand three elementary schools to accommodate grades four and five
with the closure and possible demolition of Hampden Meadows School The
expanded schools are utilizing most of the developable land at each
school site. A long-term question is, how would additional growth in the
student population be accommodated at the school sites if additional
building expansions are needed due to overcrowding?

· *Stormwater Facilities.* As demonstrated by the drainage projects
completed within rebuilt parking lots at Town Beach and Latham Park,
infrastructure improvement projects provide an opportunity to retrofit
sites with stormwater measures that benefit water quality. The Town has
an opportunity to complete stormwater improvements as part of a \$4
million streets, sidewalks and drainage bond---which is funding street
resurfacing projects throughout town---as well as the \$1.6 million
streetscape improvement bond for the Village Center. Conceptual plans
for the streetscape project identified opportunities to reduce street
widths to incorporate rain gardens and trees along the street
edge---benefitting water quality within the Brickyard Pond and the
Barrington River watersheds. Other potential stormwater projects have
been identified by Save the Bay and are discussed in the Natural Hazards
element.

· *Stormwater Maintenance:* While DPW recently completed a grant-funded
GIS mapping project identifying locations of outfalls, the Town has only
partial GIS mapping data showing the location of stormwater facilities
in town---including retention basins and subsurface structures built as
part of subdivision and land development projects. More complete GIS
data would help the Town better track completed and planned stormwater
facility maintenance projects.

· *Condition of boat launch facilities at Town sites.* The ramps and
docks at Police Cove Park and Walker Farm provide not only public
access, but serve as critical emergency boat access for public safety
purposes. The Town has upgraded the boating facilities at Police Cove in
recent years, including a rebuilt boat ramp and construction of a new
floating dock to help with loading and unloading trailers at the ramp.
Funding for the work came from funding administered by the RI Department
of Transportation and RI Department of Environmental Management. The
Town in 2025 was working on a plan to repair the cement seawall at
Police Cove and, going forward, will need to evaluate and plan for
repairs and improvements to the Harbormaster dock located at the site.

Another boating facility that requires evaluation is the boat ramp at
Walker Farm; the ramp is in poor condition, while the Town makes regular
repairs to the adjacent dock.

**Evolving Information Technology (IT) Needs and Cybersecurity Risks**

[Artificial Intelligence (AI)]{.underline}

Cities and towns are developing policies for the use of AI by Town
employees and evaluating opportunities to leverage technology to improve
municipal services. Examples include thermal imaging for firefighters,
evaluating data to develop policy recommendations, improving
communications with the public, and incorporating AI in plan review and
planning projects.

The United States Conference of Mayors describes the opportunities
presented by this emerging technology:

> AI offers cities significant advantages. From streamlining city
> operations to optimizing resources to improving citizen engagement, AI
> can automate certain tasks while freeing up city staff for complex,
> human-centric work.[^2]

The Town will need to ensure its internal policies are kept up to date
as AI is incorporated in the workplace, with a focus on transparency and
protection of private data.

The Town must balance the potential for improved efficiency and
decision-making against The expense and environment impact of AI.
Commentary in Fortune magazine, *AI and the Future of Cities,* describes
the challenge as follows:

> The cost of building and running AI infrastructure is enormous, both
> in monetary and environmental terms. If generative AI can only solve
> the small problems, not the big ones, then municipalities may question
> whether these expenditures are worth it.[^3]

[Cybersecurity]{.underline}

Cyberattacks are among the most serious risks facing government
operations at all levels:

> Cyberattacks can cost localities millions of dollars and shut down
> services for months. As local governments have digitized over the past
> 20 years, such cyberattacks mean that local services can be brought to
> a complete halt for days or months in localities that are unprepared. 
>
> However, cyber insurance also poses several challenges. First,
> insurance policies covering monetary losses will not help a local
> government maintain the trust of its citizenry if it is hit by a
> cyberattack. Nor will it help local governments continue operating
> during a cyberattack. Second, many cyber insurance policies have large
> exclusions that can limit their applicability in practice, depending
> on the source of the attack.

Barrington has been working to enhance protection against attacks
through multiple strategies. For example, staff are required to complete
training on a regular basis with the goal of increasing awareness of
cybersecurity risks. The Town recently added a full-time IT coordinator
and contracted with a consultant to identify needs and assist with
upgrading IT infrastructure throughout all municipal departments.

The Town will need to continue investing in cybersecurity and IT
infrastructure to meet this ever-growing risk.

**Public Water Needs**

An ongoing, pivotal issue is one of future water supply. The East Bay
Pipeline provides up to 7.5 million gallons per day (mgd) for Bristol
County, more than sufficient to meet the current average daily demand of
3.15 mgd. However, the available capacity/cost is limited by contract
with the Providence Water Supply Board, and BCWA is not fully prepared
for a catastrophic event that could temporarily cut off the pipeline
supply.

To address the lack of sufficient alternative supply, the BCWA Board
aims to develop an interconnection with the Pawtucket Water Supply Board
in partnership with the City of East Providence Water Department.
Neither water system has a backup water supply, as both are completely
reliant upon Providence Water. Further, both systems receive their water
through separate cross-bay pipelines which are vulnerable to failure. In
June of 2022, the BCWA completed Phase I of the Pawtucket Pipeline
Project with a critical pipeline inter-connection with the East
Providence water system.

The construction contract for Phase I was initially valued at \$6.9
million and was completed for \$5.9 million. The City of East Providence
has shared in the costs for the design of Phase II (\$2 million) with
BCWA. The estimated construction cost for Phase II is approximately \$50
to \$70 million. To prepare for potential cost impacts, BCWA has built
reserves over the past several years, aiming to minimize the need for
significant rate increases. BCWA continues to seek State and/or Federal
funding for this project and has applied to the Rhode Island
Infrastructure Bank for project funding.

Another issue concerns water pressure, and whether the local water
distribution system is adequate to meet needs of future growth. The
development of the Zion Bible Institute site as well as other sites will
need careful evaluation to ensure water pressure is maintained.

Availability of adequate water is an issue in times of drought or
extended dry periods (see discussion of drought in the Natural Hazards
element). The Town's Emergency Operations Plan (EOP) provides a
framework for the Town to respond to natural disasters; however, the
current EOP lacks a section for responding to drought conditions. This
should be revised to provide a water emergency response plan, describing
the roles of the Town -- such as communications and enactment of local
ordinances -- in coordination with the Bristol County and the Water
Resources Board.

**Regional Planning Issues**

The Town works with adjacent communities on regional issues, including
emergency response, public safety, provision of emergency shelters,
purchasing, and harbor management. Additional coordination with adjacent
municipalities would help address regional planning issues such as:

-   The use and management of public parcels adjacent to adjoining
    towns---such as open space/conservation properties on Wampanoag
    Trail near East Providence.

-   Amendment of the State's Urban Services Boundary---which includes
    the George Street area, where there is no water or sewer, and
    adjacent agricultural lands in East Providence. The [Future Land Use
    Map (Map LU-6)]{.mark} calls for relocating the boundary, which will
    require coordination with the State as well as East Providence.

-   Planning for natural hazards, energy, transportation and other
    issues. For example, the Town worked with the Towns of Bristol and
    Warren on the State-funded Resilient Route 114 Study, completed in
    2025, which assessed the risk of flooding and sea level rise on
    Route 114 (Wampanoag Trail and County Road in Barrington). The Town
    also participated in a community electricity aggregation program
    involving Providence and other municipalities that has provided
    residents lower electricity supply costs compared to the "last
    resort" rates offered by the utility. Future regional coordination
    opportunities could include a multi-community streetlight
    maintenance contract and purchasing agreements.
