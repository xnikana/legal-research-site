**i. Public Hearing: Unified Development Review – Preliminary Plan – Minor Subdivision and 2 Dimensional Variances. Property known as 66 County Road, AP 27, Lot 067, Zoned: R-10. Matthew Antonio (applicant and owner), 47 Maple Avenue Suite 1, Barrington.**

Town Planner Durfee provided an overview of the application, stating the applicant sought permission to subdivide the existing lot of approximately 15,954 square feet into two lots. The first proposed lot would retain the existing multi-family home, while the second would be designated for a new single-family residence. Variances from minimum lot size requirements were requested. Planner Durfee clarified that the application involved two steps: first, approval of the variances, and second, approval of the subdivision, with the subdivision contingent upon the variance approval. He noted that public comments would be solicited during the hearing. Chair Phipps stated that after the presentation and board questions, the public would be invited to speak.

Robert Stolzman introduced himself as the attorney representing the applicant, Mr. Matt Antonio, who owns the property through a trust. He mentioned Mr. Brad Travers from Waterman Engineering, who prepared the plans, and Mr. Doug McLean, a town planner and zoning expert, who would testify regarding zoning compliance.

(Oath Administered)

He described renovation efforts, including structural repairs, new windows, siding, and roof. He expressed interest in subdividing the lot to create a new single-family home in the back, citing underutilized space and the need for more housing.

Mr. Antonio detailed his belief that the lot's configuration and location made it suitable for division and development, emphasizing the local housing shortage and referencing previous successful projects he had undertaken in Barrington. He shared that the new construction would help meet community needs for entry-level housing and provide opportunities for residents to stay in Barrington.

Mr. Stolzman announced that the next witness was Mr. Douglas McLean, the sole expert witness, in addition to the submitted plans. Mr. McLean explained that he was brought onto the project late and did not prepare a written report but shared key findings. He addressed the broader state housing crisis driven by an imbalance of supply and demand, emphasizing the importance of infill housing opportunities that align with existing infrastructure and neighborhood character.

Mr. McLean outlined the five main dimensional variance findings, beginning with the first, which considered whether the hardship was due to the unique characteristics of the land or structure. He described reviewing 24 lots on County Road, noting that this lot was one of only two with sufficient frontage to be subdivided without relief. He highlighted that the structure was located mainly on the northern half of the lot, with the southern half vacant, presenting an opportunity for subdivision. He added that this lot was also unique because it required no relief for setbacks or lot coverage when subdivided.

Regarding the second finding, Mr. McLean referenced recent state legislation (House Bill 5794) that changed the requirement to prove that hardships were not due to prior actions of the applicant, noting that the applicant had purchased the property recently, with conditions existing prior to their ownership.

On the third finding, Mr. McLean argued that granting relief would not alter the character of the surrounding area or impair the comprehensive plan. He conducted an analysis of lots along County Road, noting the mix of frontage patterns and densities, and concluded that the proposed development aligned well with existing neighborhood character, especially considering the density of two-family and three-family structures nearby.

Mr. McLean responded to questions about neighborhood character, explaining that the analysis extended beyond just the 200-foot radius and included the broader context of the area, including commercial zones and mixed-use zones. He emphasized that the density and lot sizes in the vicinity supported the proposal, which would not disrupt the neighborhood's character.

Mr. McLean further discussed the hardship argument, stating that the underutilization of the property due to its unique setting and structure constituted a hardship. He clarified that the relief sought was minimal and aligned with the standards of the ordinance and state law, which require that the hardship be more than a mere inconvenience.

Member Kilmartin questioned whether underutilization alone constituted sufficient hardship, suggesting that other potential uses, such as building additional structures or ADUs, might be feasible without subdivision. Mr. McLean responded that based on his discussions with the applicant, the most suitable opportunity was subdivision to create a standalone single-family lot, which met local housing needs.

Mr. McLean concluded by affirming that the proposal fit the neighborhood's character and the comprehensive plan, and that there was ample evidence to support positive findings on the variance standards. He welcomed further questions and suggested reviewing relevant comprehensive plan points.

Mr. McLean then addressed the comprehensive plan. He cited relevant sections, particularly the housing and neighborhood's introduction on pages five and six, which emphasized the need for Barrington to meet future housing demands—both affordable and market rate—by utilizing underutilized parcels with access to infrastructure. He explained that the plan encourages compatible development that respects neighborhood character and suggests that the proposed subdivision aligned with these goals.

Mr. McLean highlighted that the comprehensive plan's land use policies support reusing existing parcels with adequate infrastructure and maintaining compatibility with surrounding land uses, natural features, and community services. He pointed out that the future land use map designated this area for medium-high density residential use, supporting an allowance of four to eight units per acre, which matched the density sought in the application.

Member Bacher asked whether the comprehensive plan contained a specific goal to increase overall housing or affordable housing. Mr. McLean responded that the plan did not explicitly aim to increase market-rate housing but did emphasize the importance of generating affordable units. He further referenced the plan's focus on providing opportunities for infill development, especially on underutilized parcels that fit with neighborhood density, aligning with the proposed project.

Mr. Stolzman then clarified for the record that both lots would have access to public streets—one on Barton and the other on County Road—with both configurations being practical and compliant with environmental standards. He confirmed that the lots would meet all lot coverage requirements and that no further variances regarding setbacks or height would be sought beyond the lot size variance.

Chair Phipps inquired if any board member had additional comments or topics to discuss.

Member Bacher responded with a legal question directed to the town solicitor. He explained his understanding of the application and questioned whether the zoning ordinance prevented the granting of a dimensional variance because the applicant's hardship was the inability to realize the full value of the property. He suggested that the applicant bought an investment property, renovated it responsibly, and was now seeking to build an additional house, with the hardship being the restrictive zoning. Member Bacher referenced the language in the zoning enabling legislation, specifically the section stating that if the relief sought does not amount to more than a mere inconvenience, it is not grounds for granting the variance.

Solicitor Teitz responded, clarifying that the statute's language about "not being grounds for relief" meant that increased value or profitability alone could not justify granting a variance. However, he explained that such factors are not automatic grounds for denial either. He elaborated that the law has evolved and that the phrase "mere inconvenience" is based on case law, specifically a 1961 Rhode Island Supreme Court decision. Solicitor Teitz emphasized that the decision to grant or deny a variance must consider whether the relief is minimal and whether the project fits within the neighborhood context. He advised the Board to evaluate whether the proposed project aligns with the neighborhood character and whether the relief sought is proportional and appropriate. He confirmed that the Board could adopt a standard that assesses whether the project could be envisioned sitting on the lot and if it aligns with neighborhood standards. He warned that this does not mean that any lot where a house could be built should automatically be approved, especially as other accessory uses or structures could also be contemplated under recent zoning changes.

Member Bacher added that the applicant had arbitrarily selected a neighborhood of 45 houses, skewing the analysis, and that a more standard approach would be to consider the typical neighborhood within a 200-foot radius. He pointed out that, based on his review, 40% of those houses were non-conforming, and that the lot sizes in the immediate neighborhood were generally small, with some being smaller than the proposed lot. He suggested that neighborhood character should be assessed based on the standard radius rather than the applicant's chosen subset.

Based on the testimony and evidence presented to the Board, the Planning Board makes the following findings of fact and conclusions of law with respect to the requested dimensional variances for lot area for both of the proposed lots, pursuant to RIGL § 45-24-41(d) and (e):

(1) That the hardship from which the applicant seeks relief is NOT due to the unique characteristics of the subject land or structure and not to the general characteristics of the surrounding area; and is not due to a physical or economic disability of the applicant, excepting those physical disabilities addressed in § 45-24-30(a)(16);

**The Board finds that there is not any hardship because it finds that there are no significant unique characteristics of the subject land and structure. The existing house is a two-family dwelling which requires 15,000 sf of lot area and the existing lot contains 15,954 sf, so contrary to the testimony of Doug McLean, who was accepted an expert, this lot is not unusually large. Furthermore, the Board, following extensive questioning of Mr. McLean by the Board, was not persuaded by Mr. McLean's choice to focus on the County Road neighborhood, and mostly to the east of Barton Road, for a comparison of lot sizes. The Board instead finds that County Road serves as natural division between neighborhoods, and that the neighborhood of the northern part of Tyler Point, encompassing Barton Road and Tyler Point Road, is the more appropriate neighborhood with which to compare the subject property. The Board finds that when using this neighborhood, the proposed new lot will be one of the smallest of the lots in such neighborhood.** 

**No testimony was introduced about physical or economic disability of the applicant, so this is not relevant either way.**

(2) That the hardship is not the result of any prior action of the applicant; and

**This standard is not relevant as the General Assembly has amended this section so as not to apply to a UDR for a subdivision.**

(3) That the granting of the requested variance WILL alter the general character of the surrounding area or impair the intent or purpose of the zoning ordinance or the comprehensive plan upon which the ordinance is based.

**The Board finds that granting the requested variance will in fact negatively alter the character of the surrounding area because it will be creating a lot substantially (approximately 25%) smaller than required by zoning and significantly smaller than the surrounding Tyler Point neighborhood.**

**The Board finds that granting the requested variance will impair the intent and purpose of the Zoning Ordinance and Comprehensive Plan because although it will provide additional housing, it will not be providing affordable housing, and also because this is increasing density and development in a significant flood zone. Because the Comprehensive Plan and Zoning Ordinance emphasize the production of affordable housing and discourages increasing density and development in flood zones, this application does not satisfy this standard.** 

- (e) The zoning board of review, or, where unified development review is enabled pursuant to § 45- 24-46.4, the planning board or commission, shall, in addition to the above standards, require that evidence is entered into the record of the proceedings showing that:
- (2) In granting a dimensional variance, that the hardship suffered by the owner of the subject property if the dimensional variance is not granted WILL NOT amount to more than a mere inconvenience, meaning that relief sought is minimal to a reasonable enjoyment of the permitted use to which the property is proposed to be devoted. The fact that a use may be more profitable or that

a structure may be more valuable after the relief is granted is not grounds for relief. The zoning board of review, or, where unified development review is enabled pursuant to § 45-24-46.4, the planning board or commission has the power to grant dimensional variances where the use is permitted by special-use permit.

**The Board finds that if the dimensional variance is not granted, the hardship suffered by the owner will not amount to more than a mere inconvenience because there is already reasonable enjoyment of the permitted use, which is residential, and which use provides yard area for both units of the two-family dwelling, as well as seasonal boat storage as testified to by the owner. The Board also finds that the relief sought is not minimal, but is somewhat maximal to a reasonable enjoyment of the permitted use because of the negative impact on the surrounding neighborhood.**

Chair Phipps then asked for clarification regarding the land conveyance process involving the lots. Mr. Budway explained that the project aimed to create a minor subdivision, with six lots for the single-family homes deeded to the Town of Barrington and one lot for the 12 cottage-style homes deeded to the development entity overseeing their construction. He detailed that the land on which the cottage homes and park sit would initially be in the developer's name but would be deeded back to the town after the subdivision was recorded. Mr. Budway elaborated that the process involved recording the deeds so that the land could be transferred back and forth temporarily for the purpose of creating the subdivision and conveying the individual lots. He emphasized that ultimately, all of the land would be owned by the town until the lots were sold to individual homeowners, with the exception of the land for the cottage homes, which would remain with the developer until sale.

Solicitor Teitz added that the plan was to sell the six single-family lots through requests for proposals, possibly in a staggered manner to avoid flooding the market. He explained that the proceeds from these sales would help cover the costs of demolition and remediation, with any remaining funds potentially used for other projects at the Town Council's discretion.

Chair Phipps questioned whether the funds could be used for the park, and Town Manager Hervey responded that the use of funds was still to be determined, with the primary goal of covering shortterm demolition costs.

Solicitor Teitz clarified that the overall process was essentially about ensuring the land title was correctly transferred back and forth, with the final goal being that the land for the cottage homes would be owned by the development entity until sold, and the rest by the town.
