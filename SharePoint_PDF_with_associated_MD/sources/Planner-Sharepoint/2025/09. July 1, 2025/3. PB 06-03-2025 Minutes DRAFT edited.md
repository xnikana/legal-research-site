#### **5. New Business:**

**a. Discuss and Act: At the Town Council's request, recommendation on lowering or eliminating the 20,000 SF threshold minimum lot size for**

## **Accessory Dwelling Units (ADU's) regarding the Council's pending amendments to Chapter 185 of the Town Code (Zoning Ordinance).**

Solicitor Goins explained that the Council had requested a review of the proposed zoning amendments related to ADUs, particularly the removal of the 20,000 sq. ft. lot size requirement. She clarified that under state law, the minimum lot size for ADUs was not fixed and that municipalities could decrease or eliminate it but not increase it. Currently, the 20,000 sq. ft. threshold was one of three pathways to establish an ADU, the others being:

- ➢ ADUs for disabled family members (allowed by right)
- ➢ ADUs within existing structures or footprints (like garages or sheds)

Solicitor Goins noted that many municipalities, including Barrington, had been granting variances for lots smaller than 20,000 sq. ft., and that increasing the number of lots qualifying by right for ADUs could promote affordable housing options, particularly for seniors or those unable to afford larger homes.

Town Planner Durfee expressed a cautious perspective, suggesting that eliminating the lot size requirement entirely might introduce issues on very small lots and recommended reducing the threshold to around 10,000 sq. ft. He emphasized the importance of understanding how reducing the threshold would impact the community, using the map in the packet to illustrate areas affected.

Member Bacher asked whether other setback requirements would change if lot size restrictions were lowered.

Solicitor Goins confirmed that setbacks and lot coverage standards would remain, but the ability to build ADUs as of right would expand with a lower lot size threshold.

The Board discussed how existing laws already allow ADUs within the setbacks, and how tiered approaches (by right, with variance, or full elimination) could affect development.

Chair Phipps invited the Town Council Vice President to speak on behalf of the Council.

Town Council Vice President Berard provided extensive research data, emphasizing that the current 20,000 sq. ft. threshold limits ADUs to about 22% of property owners, which she deemed insufficient for meaningful housing expansion. She cited studies from California and Seattle showing that relaxing ADU restrictions could serve as an effective tool for increasing affordable and attainable housing, especially considering Rhode Island's housing shortage and aging population. She mentioned Massachusetts' recent legislation, which has similar restrictions but no specific size cap, and expressed her desire for Rhode Island to move ahead in this arena.

Member Kilmartin expressed support for reducing the lot size requirement, noting that the policy aligns with the town's Comprehensive Plan to encourage ADUs as part of housing strategies. She discussed potential intermediate solutions, such as pre-approved designs or variances for smaller lots, to balance density concerns with affordability.

*The Chair opened the floor for public comment.*

Charles Brown, 5 Carpenter Avenue, Barrington, voiced concerns about over-density, noting that very small lots could lead to excessive neighborhood congestion and strain on public services. He urged adherence to the state-mandated minimum lot size and cautioned against unintended consequences of overly permissive ADU policies.

Chair Phipps thanked them for their comments and posed a question regarding the regulation of ADUs—specifically, how the municipality would prevent these units from being used like Airbnb's. She inquired about the intersection between municipal regulations and state law.

Solicitor Goins explained that state legislation explicitly prohibits ADUs from being used for shortterm rentals, though the language is not exact. She clarified that this restriction pertains to enforcement and that towns like Barrington do not regulate short-term rentals directly, whereas Newport has more comprehensive regulations. Solicitor Goins also noted that enforcement typically involves investigating complaints initiated through calls to Town Hall, and that the issue is complicated by pending legal cases at the Rhode Island Supreme Court, which could influence future legislative actions.

The Board discussed concerns about enforcement, including restrictions on short-term rentals, parking, and potential lot subdivision.

Solicitor Goins suggested implementing a sunset clause if the Council adopts a more permissive lot size policy, allowing for ongoing review and potential adjustments in the future.

Alternate O'Neal shared his personal perspective, describing the neighborhood characteristics and emphasizing that most houses are on lots below 5,000 square feet. He expressed concern that without size restrictions—preferably maintaining a threshold of 20,000 or at least 10,000 square feet—development could disrupt neighborhood character. Alternate O'Neal advocated for a size limit to preserve the small-town feel.

Mr. Brown requested that the recommendation be to keep the ADU regulations as they currently are.

Chair Phipps confirmed that the current process involves the Planning Board making recommendations, but the final decision rests with the Town Council.

*The Chair closed the floor for public comment.*

Chair Phipps asked if there were any further comments or questions. Seeing none, she called for a motion.

**MOTION: Member Rua, seconded by Member Kilmartin moved to recommend that the Town Council lower the minimum lot size for ADUs from 20,000 SF to 5,000 SF in Chapter 185 of the Town Code, with <sup>a</sup> review scheduled 12 months after implementation to evaluate outcomes. Motion did not carry. (3/4)**

**MOTION: Member Kilmartin, seconded by Member Costa moved to recommend that the Town Council lower the 20,000 SF threshold for minimum lot size for Accessory Dwelling Units (ADU's) to 10,000 SF regarding Chapter 185 of the Town Code (Zoning Ordinance),**

**with <sup>a</sup> review scheduled 12 months after implementation to evaluate outcomes. Motion carried (5/2)**

## **iii. Outdoor Recreation**

Chair Phipps initiated the discussion on Outdoor Recreation, noting that the agenda would focus on recreational resources, infrastructure, and public access.

Planner Durfee suggested keeping the discussion moving forward for the July or August agenda, dependent on the schedules of the Board members

Chair Phipps inquired about playground resources, noting that Hampden Meadows had a playground but was not mentioned previously. The Board discussed Hampden Meadows playground and then referenced other playground resources, such as Kids Cove (the library playground) and an area designated for preschool children, emphasizing the need to consider ageappropriate facilities.

The Board noted that the public feedback had focused on maintenance issues, particularly the aging condition of existing playground equipment, although they are generally functional.

Member Kilmartin highlighted the importance of serving all age groups, from birth through 18, and noted some segments, like teens and very young children, might be underserved. She discussed the potential to expand existing facilities with small, low-cost enhancements, such as adding extra swings or creating separate toddler areas.

The Board discussed Kids Cove and the possibility of a dedicated tot lot, suggesting constructing a small, fenced-off area for the littlest children, emphasizing the need for safety and separation from larger children. Chair Phipps mentioned the possibility of expanding the splash pad, which is popular during the summer months.

Member Kilmartin also addressed the community's interest in developing a skate park for older children and teenagers. The Board noted a facility for skating already existed on Legion Way, which was prefaced by limited success and current inadequacies.

There was discussion about land on College Lane that the town owns that has remained undeveloped for years, suggesting it could be repurposed for a skate park or play facilities.

Chair Phipps raised the underutilized space near Zion and the old barn site, which could accommodate a toddler or skate park, emphasizing the benefit of converting open fields with minimal development. Member Kilmartin confirmed that the barn site is too narrow for construction but could serve as a developed park really well.

The Board discussed the potential for increased community activities at the Bay Spring Community Center following renovations, including outdoor events and expanded amenities.

Chair Phipps suggested adding the newly acquired Watson Park, which is approximately 2.5 acres, to the list of recreational resources. Member O'Neal endorsed the idea of developing the Watson site for a skate park or similar facility, citing its scenic water views and parking availability.

Member Rua described trail connections around Watson Park, including routes from Veterans Memorial Park and Brickyard Pond, emphasizing the importance of trail systems for outdoor recreation and community connectivity.

Member Kilmartin raised ongoing concerns about shoreline access, particularly the lack of parking along rights-of-way and encroachments by neighbors, notably on St. Andrew's Farm. She expressed concern over enforcement mechanisms and the need for better management. The Board reviewed existing solutions, such as the small parking lot at Moussachuck Grove which was created through town negotiations and CRMC (Coastal Resources Management Council) involvement. Member Kilmartin proposed exploring additional small parking spots or easements, such as on Crown Avenue or along old paper roads, to improve access.

Member Kilmartin recommended evaluating on-street parking regulations and enforcement, although she acknowledged community resistance, particularly from neighbors, to parking restrictions.

Chair Phipps mentioned the town's previous policy of residents-only parking at the town beach, which was relaxed, possibly due to the influence of state grants and external factors such as the closure of certain public access points.

The Board also highlighted the closure of the Tillinghast Place property (Rhode Island School of Design property), which had served as a popular dog-friendly beach, and expressed concern about its future accessibility. The Board discussed that a formal plan to reopen or negotiate access with RISD might be warranted.

New alternate, Lili Hermann, a faculty member at RISD, emphasized the importance of ongoing dialogue with the institution regarding public access and shared use of land. The Board agreed, adding that a formal approach or possibly even taxation considerations could be explored, though they acknowledged legal complexities.

Alternate Hermann expressed concerns about the accessibility and clarity of the plan, questioning the presence of a clear vision and actionable steps. She emphasized the importance of integrating the plan's elements into a cohesive, understandable document. Member Kilmartin emphasized that the current version is introductory and technical, with visual and summary components to be added later. She encouraged input and explained that the plan would ultimately serve both the Planning Board and the public.

Member Kilmartin encouraged members to submit suggestions or edits to her as they review the draft, noting she would conduct one final revision before the draft could be considered for approval.
