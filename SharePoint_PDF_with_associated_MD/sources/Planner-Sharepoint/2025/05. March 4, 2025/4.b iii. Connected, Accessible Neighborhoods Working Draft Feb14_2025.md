## **Introduction**

Barrington 2050 establishes goals, actions, and policies to build connected, accessible neighborhoods within the Town of Barrington. This chapter includes Town priorities, existing issues and opportunities, and goals, actions, and policies to create a community where housing, circulation, recreation, and open space are seamlessly integrated to create connected, accessible neighborhoods.

During the planning process and community engagement, residents shared their visions and priorities for the future of the Town by talking about the neighborhood character, identity, and connectivity. The connections between town facilities, residential areas, schools, parks, and natural resources – including the beachfronts – were frequently mentioned as a gap within Barrington. Residents enjoy walkable (and ADA-accessible) neighborhoods with active transportation routes and easy access to outdoor amenities. When asked about topics such as housing, circulation, and recreation, a clear theme emerged: Barrington residents desire neighborhoods that cohesively plan for and provide all of these to be accessible and available for all ages.

A clear theme emerged to describe a vision for Barrington 2050:

*Connected, accessible neighborhoods providing safe, quality housing for all ages and households.*

#### **Increased infrastructure investment in sidewalks, bicycle lanes, and trails.**

While most residents use personal vehicles out of necessity, there is a local desire for sidewalks, trails, and bike lanes for short trips. While the Town has prioritized Complete Streets projects and trail access since the 2015 Comprehensive Plan, there is high interest in continued expansion of the active transportation network as well as increased separation to provide safe, accessible, and enjoyable routes for users of all ages and abilities.

![](4.b iii. Connected, Accessible Neighborhoods Working Draft Feb14_2025_attachments/_page_5_Picture_5.jpeg)

66% of survey respondents noted that sidewalks and paths were the top infrastructure need in Barrington.

Barrington's commitment to making its transportation network safer is also evident through its recent Safe Streets for All (SS4A) plan.[1](#page-5-0) The SS4A plan is a comprehensive initiative aimed at improving safety and accessibility for all road users, including pedestrians, bicyclists, motorists, and transit riders. The plan was developed with significant community engagement, gathering input from the public, community stakeholders, and government agencies to identify existing safety concerns and priority locations for potential safety improvements. Key elements of the plan include:

- Infrastructure Improvements: The plan proposes new and reconstructed sidewalks, handicap ramps, crosswalk striping, lane width adjustments, and signage to enhance safety and accessibility.
- Speed Limit Adjustments: Adjustments to speed limits are recommended to improve safety throughout the community.
- Data-Driven Approach: The plan used primary crash data and systemic risk factors to develop a data-informed, community-driven final Safety Action Plan (SAP) for Barrington.
- Collaboration: The plan involved collaboration between various departments and organizations, including the Rhode Island Department of Transportation (RIDOT) and the Rhode Island Public Transit Authority (RIPTA).

## Work towards compliance with the State's 10% affordable housing goal through provision of well-integrated and compatible affordable housing.

While Barrington recognizes it is unlikely that it can meet the State's 10% low- and moderate-income (LMI) housing goals given its spatial and infrastructure constraints, it also recognizes the importance of working towards this goal so it can provide housing opportunities it currently lacks. Barrington understands its current housing stock does not meet the needs of its local workforce, of cost-burdened residents, and of seniors interested in smaller units. It also understands it does not have enough housing to accommodate the additional households who would like to live in Barrington and is therefore missing out on capturing regional demand for market rate housing.

Barrington's 3.51% affordable housing rate demonstrates there is work to be done to diversify its housing stock and expand the housing opportunities available within its community. The town is eager to make progress in a manner that preserves its limited economic real estate while also supporting housing development that can better meet the needs and preferences of its existing and future population. Barrington can likely achieve its 245-housing unit production target over the next 10-15 years without significantly altering its development trajectory. Assuming the successful implementation of all strategies, Barrington can make good progress towards the State's 10% LMI housing requirement by the year 2040.

## Diversify Barrington's housing stock to accommodate the needs of residents across a wider spectrum of income levels, age groups, and abilities.

Most of Barrington's housing stock is detached single-family and ownership units. While the housing stock has been diversifying with the development of multifamily and rental units, there is still more demand than units available. All rental units are fully occupied indicating demand remains strong. Multifamily housing continues to be gradually added in Neighborhood Business, Business, and Residential-Business Flex zones where it a permitted use. To continue to foster the development of multifamily and rental units, the Town will need to consider what might be restricting further infill and mixed-use development, particularly in areas of town well-suited for this development due to their proximity to goods and services and the RIPTA bus route.

## Ensure that future development is sustainable, resilient, and maintains the architectural style and scale of Barrington in general and its individual neighborhoods.

Barrington's extensive shoreline, waterfront properties, and existing floodplains place much of its housing stock at risk of the impacts of climate change. Not only does this further reduce the Town's already limited supply of developable land; it also impacts the longevity of existing homes to remain habitable as properties face more frequent and intensive flooding, storm surge, and projected sea level rise. The Town will need to take proactive measures to plan for climate change and increase the resiliency of its housing stock. Reducing density in impacted areas and conducting initial planning to accommodate displaced households in less impacted parts of town should be prioritized. The feasibility of retrofitting existing housing stock in locations where adaptation is possible should also be explored.

#### **Key Issue 3: Rhode Island state mandates require the Town to increase its affordable housing supply, but land is limited.**

Rhode Island's Low- and Moderate-Income Housing Act requires that at least 10% of each community's housing supply consist of income-restricted affordable units. The State defines affordable rental units as those affordable to 80% AMI and below and, for homeownership, 120% AMI and below. Communities that do not meet this standard are subject to comprehensive permit applications for developments containing at least 25% affordable units which can override zoning controls like density, parking, bedrooms, and floor area.

The law includes a provision for some market rate units to count against the 10% goal if they are included in a mixed-income development that contains mostly low- and moderate- income housing.

3.5% of Barrington's housing stock satisfies the law's requirement. To keep up with projected growth, Barrington should add 245 new units over the next 10 years. Combined with development already in the pipeline, Barrington should plan for a production target of about 610 total units over this period. To make realistic but meaningful progress toward the state's 10% LMI housing goal, new housing development should be at least 20% affordable (combined with the pipeline's 25% affordable rate). This mix would increase Barrington's affordable rate from today's 3.51% to 5.23% by 2035.

For more information on this state mandate and Barrington's eligible housing stock, please see the *Housing & Neighborhood Barrington Comprehensive Community Plan 2025 Update*.

#### **Key Issue 4: Changing Demographics will Further Strain the Housing Supply**

*Housing Demand & Target Unit Count*

Barrington has a statutory obligation to meet the state's housing needs, as well as those of its residents. In order to calibrate future housing production to best meet the community's needs, two factors must be established: how many units are needed in total and what is the right mix of unit types and prices. The following section addresses each of these factors to inform recommendations that effectively meet local need and reflect the community's values and priorities.

Based on the economic and equity goals identified through the public engagement process and other Town planning efforts, there are three groups of potentially underserved current and future residents whose needs should be understood and considered in this study:

- Today's cost-burdened residents: The portion of the current population that is spending more than 30% of its income on housing in Barrington.
- Tomorrow's new residents: The households expected to move to Barrington over the next decade.
- Today's workforce: The people who currently work within Barrington but might not be able to afford to live in town.

#### **Opportunity #7: Integrate Missing Middle Housing Typologies** Problem Statement

The housing stock is predominantly single-family homes and lacks options for smaller or more diverse housing needs.

#### Recommendations

Missing middle housing typologies refer to a range of multi-unit, house-scale buildings that are compatible in scale and form with detached single-family homes. These housing types include duplexes, fourplexes, cottage courts, and multiplexes, which fit seamlessly into existing residential neighborhoods. They are designed to support walkability, locally serving retail, and public transportation options, providing diverse housing solutions along a spectrum of affordability. Missing middle housing addresses the mismatch between the available U.S. housing stock and shifting demographics, combined with the growing demand for walkability. These buildings typically have small to medium-sized footprints, lower perceived density, and fewer off-street parking spaces, making them an attractive option for creating vibrant, inclusive communities.

Missing middle housing typologies are suitable in Barrington for the following reasons:

- Neighborhood Character: Limited developable land has prompted the tear-down and demolition of existing homes to rebuild larger houses or elevate those at risk in floodplains. It has also resulted in some higher density developments enabled through comprehensive permits. This has put pressure on Barrington's existing neighborhoods, raising concerns about the impact on neighborhood character, traffic, infrastructure capacity, and the Town and school budgets. As developable land continues to be a scare resource, increasing density pressures are likely to continue making it important for the Town to have clear objectives that balance preserving neighborhood character with new and higher density development.
- Multifamily and Infill Housing: Most of Barrington's housing stock is detached single-family and ownership units. While the housing stock has been diversifying with the development of multifamily and rental units, there is still more demand than units available. All rental units are fully occupied indicating demand remains strong. Multifamily housing continues to be gradually added in Neighborhood Business, Business, and Residential-Business Flex zones where it a permitted use. To continue to foster the development of multifamily and rental units, the Town will need to consider what might be restricting further infill and mixed-use development, particularly in areas of town well-suited for this development due to their proximity to goods and services and the RIPTA bus route.

![](4.b iii. Connected, Accessible Neighborhoods Working Draft Feb14_2025_attachments/_page_28_Picture_0.jpeg)

• Housing for First Time Homebuyers and Young Families: High home prices and limited options pose a challenge for first-time homebuyers and young families looking for entry-level housing and homes that can fit their evolving needs. While this demographic represents a small proportion of the Barrington community it is important that they are able to stay and that more families are able to move to Barrington so the town can capture existing demand. The town needs to put greater emphasis on developing new housing geared towards young families and starter homes.

#### **Opportunity #8: Innovative and Collaborative Financing to Reduce Costs** Problem Statement

Housing costs are increasingly a burden on residents who struggle to find options they can afford.

#### Recommendations

High construction costs and the current market conditions make the development of affordable housing challenging, especially for developing units for 60% AMI and below, without subsidies or the space to develop larger scale, higher density development projects. Because land is limited, leveraging Low Income Housing Tax Credits (LIHTC) and other subsidies are needed to advance affordable housing and greater levels of affordability. Barrington has already seen successful affordable housing be developed using a variety of funding sources. The Sweet Briar and Palmer Pointe developments leveraged RI Housing construction loans and mortgages, HOME Program funding, LIHTC equity, Building Homes Rhode Island (BHRI) state bond funding, and additional funds from Community Development Block Grants (CDBG). With these successful models, Barrington has the opportunity to further promote itself as a community where LIHTC and development partnership are possible and successful.

## **Connected, Accessible Neighborhood Goals**

- 1. Provide a balanced multi-modal transportation system throughout the town
- 2. Provide a safe and well-maintained transportation system throughout town.
- 3. Relieve growing traffic congestion on County Road/Route 114 in order to enhance the vitality and attractiveness of downtown Barrington
- 4. Create an "emerald chain" of parks by establishing and improving linkages between and within recreational areas throughout town, including facilities at schools
- 5. Meet the community's evolving recreational needs, both passive and active, by providing high quality multiple-use outdoor recreation facilities benefiting people of all ages.
- 6. Provide access to the shore through public recreational sites and public rights of way
- 7. Work towards compliance with the State's 10% affordable housing goal through provision of well-integrated and compatible affordable housing
- 8. Diversify Barrington's housing stock to accommodate the needs of residents across a wider spectrum of income levels, age groups, and abilities.
- 9. Ensure that future development is sustainable, resilient, and compatible with the character or Barrington in general and that of its individual neighborhoods.

### **Circulation**

#### **Moving Forward Rhode Island**

*Moving Forward Rhode Island 2040* is the long-range transportation plan for the state. The goals, actions, and policies in Connected, Accessible Neighborhoods support *Moving Forward Rhode Island 2040* goals to:

- Strengthen Communities through the local transportation network to enhance travel, place, and quality of life.
- Maintain Transportation Infrastructure to create a reliable network providing adequate travel choice.
- Connect People & Places across all modes and options for more efficient and effective travel.
- Promote Environmental Sustainability by prioritizing non-single occupancy vehicle focused strategies and investments.
- Support Economic Growth through transportation connectivity and choices to attract employers and employees.

#### **Land Use 2025: Rhode Island's State Land Use Policies and Plan**

*Land Use 2025* includes goals and objectives to promote intermodal centers and greater reliance on transit and maintain the functional integrity of existing and planned roadways. Barrington 2050 supports these goals by its actions and policies that support the goal of providing a safe and wellmaintained transportation system throughout town.

#### **Additional State Guidance**

Rhode Island's Municipal Road and Bridge Revolving Fund, which stresses the importance of the state's roadway network for economic development and opportunity, and Safe Access to Public Roads policy, which states that all transportation systems and plans should consider the needs of all users of our roadways, provides guidance for transportation planning. The circulation goals, actions, and policies in Connected, Accessible Neighborhoods are consistent with this guidance.

## **Outdoor Recreation and Open Space**

#### **Land Use 2025: Rhode Island's State Land Use Policies and Plan**

## **Housing**

#### **State Housing Goals & Planning Efforts**

These State actions build upon several other state planning efforts that have focused on equity, strategic planning, fair housing for subsidized low- and moderate-income households, and homelessness, among other topics. Some of the key documents that support planning for housing at the State level include:

- [Equity Profile of Rhode Island](https://nationalequityatlas.org/find-resources/library/an-equity-profile-of-rhode-island#:~:text=An%20Equity%20Profile%20of%20Rhode%20Island.) This report provides an equity analysis of the State of Rhode Island. It was developed to help A Sustainable Rhode Island Consortium effectively address equity issues throughout its process of planning for a more integrated and sustainable region.
- [Rhode Island Five Year Strategic Housing Plan](https://www.rihousing.com/wp-content/uploads/Rhode-Island-5-Year-Strategic-Housing-Plan.pdf#:~:text=The%20Rhode%20Island%20Five%20Year%20Strategic) (Element 423) The long-range planning report quantifies housing needs, identifies implementation strategies to address these needs, and provides guidelines for higher density residential development
- [State Housing Plan](https://planning.ri.gov/sites/g/files/xkgbur826/files/documents/guide_plan/shp421.pdf) (Element 421)- The long-range plan establishes housing goals and policies. It also analyzes major housing problems in Rhode Island and makes recommendations for action.
- [Rhode Island Annual Integrated Housing Report](https://housing.ri.gov/media/2601/download) The 2023 Integrated Housing Report presented by the Department of Housing is an important initial step in the framing of the Department's upcoming statewide housing planning efforts. The report develops a baseline understanding of the state of housing in Rhode Island, while also pointing towards known needs as well as gaps in data.
- [RI Housing Annual Action Plan](https://www.rihousing.com/wp-content/uploads/AAP-PY21-Final.pdf#:~:text=The%20State%20of%20Rhode%20Island%E2%80%99s%20Annual) The State of Rhode Island's Annual Action Plan serves as an application to the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) for funding of the Community Development Block Grant Program (CDBG), HOME Investment Partnerships Program (HOME), the National Housing Trust Fund Program (HTF) and Emergency Solutions Grant Program (ESG).

#### **Relevant State Laws and Requirements**

Rhode Island's Division of Statewide Planning sets the direction for local municipal planning efforts through the centralized long-range policy planning document known as the State Guide Plan. The [State](https://planning.ri.gov/state-guide-plan)  [Guide Plan](https://planning.ri.gov/state-guide-plan) and Rhode Island General Laws include elements that drive state policy and legislation related to housing as well as plans and actions taken at the local level. Most notably, the Comprehensive Housing Production and Rehabilitation Act of 2004 mandated the incorporation of a five year strategic plan for housing into the State Guide Plan. The subsequent [Low and Moderate](http://webserver.rilin.state.ri.us/Statutes/TITLE45/45-53/INDEX.HTM#:~:text=Definitions.%20[Effective%20January%201,%202024.]%20%C2%A7)  [Income Housing Act](http://webserver.rilin.state.ri.us/Statutes/TITLE45/45-53/INDEX.HTM#:~:text=Definitions.%20[Effective%20January%201,%202024.]%20%C2%A7) (RIGL section 45-53-2) further declared immediate action be taken to assure the availability of affordable, accessible, safe, and sanitary housing for low and moderate income individuals and families. It also requires Rhode Island cities and towns to maintain 10% of their yearround housing stock as affordable to low-or-moderate income households. A unit is considered affordable if it receives a subsidy from a federal, state, or local entity and has an affordability restriction in place for at least 30 years.

Newly passed State legislation—Bill 2024-H 7062A, 2024-S 2998A—additionally aims to boost housing production by making it easier for property owners to develop accessory dwelling units (ADUs) on their property. It provides homeowners statewide the right to develop a single ADU on an owner-occupied property to accommodate a disabled family member, or within the existing footprint of their structures or on any lot larger than 20,000 square feet, provided that the ADU's design satisfies building code, size limits and infrastructure requirements. The legislation also places restrictions on what municipalities can regulate in their local zoning on ADUs.

These State laws and plans create standards for planning that must be incorporated into a community's comprehensive plan.

#### **The State's role in local decision making on housing**

The Department of Housing assists Statewide Planning in reviewing local comprehensive plans for consistency with State goals and supports communities in meeting the State's 10% low- and moderateincome housing requirements alongside achieving their affordable housing goals. State approval of a municipality's comprehensive plan is required for municipalities to be eligible for various funding provided from the State and, in some cases, Federal level.

While the State mandates certain requirements, municipalities have the ability to shape the implementation of those requirements at the local level through municipal planning processes like the comprehensive plan and zoning ordinances. Using these tools, communities can not only decide how State requirements can fit into their local planning goals, but also play an integral role in State-wide efforts to address the housing crisis. A community's Housing Element creates a roadmap for this process.
