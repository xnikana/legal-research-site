# **BARRINGTON PLANNING BOARD**

## **Special Business Meeting Minutes**

7:00 PM, Tuesday, October 15, 2025 Collis Family Room – Barrington Public Library 281 County Road, Barrington, RI

#### **1. Call to Order.**

Chair Roni Phipps called the meeting to order at 7:15 PM.

#### **2. Roll Call & Determination of Quorum**

*Present*: Roni Phipps (Chair), Heather Kilmartin, Brian Rua (Vice Chair), Lawrence Bacher, Richard Simms, Connor O'Neal,

*Also Present*: Genevieve Elliot, Planning/Zoning Assistant; Liana Cassar, Town Council Liaison; Azley Savelle; Resilence & Energy

### **3. Old Business:**

**a. Discussion and Action: Comprehensive Plan 2024-2025 Updates (Standing Agenda Item): Subject to Availability:**

#### **i. Land Use**

Member Kilmartin provided an overview of Land Use topics discussed two months prior. She recapped the original issues highlighted in the 2015 Comprehensive Plan, emphasizing the importance of housing—particularly affordable and transitional senior housing—as well as climaterelated planning such as sea level rise and development in flood-prone areas. She also pointed out the focus on expanding the commercial tax base, preserving community character—especially along George Street—and maintaining municipal facilities, open space, and environmental protections. She explained that the primary emphasis of the upcoming plan would be on affordable housing, aligning with the state's latest requirements for housing production, affordability, and accessibility. Member Kilmartin highlighted the need for clear strategies and benchmarks and noted that land use would be implicated in this process. She discussed the tension between maintaining existing commercial zones and allowing residential infill, emphasizing that the Town should operate under strategies within its control rather than relying on private developers.

The Board discussed the state's 2025 land use guidance, which advocates for increased, denser housing—particularly in corridors with existing public transportation. Member Kilmartin expressed concerns about ensuring that commercial areas are livable, avoiding excessive light pollution and signage. The Board examined flood maps, noting that flood risks in the commercial district along County Road and County Road 114 pose challenges for emergency access and development.

Member Kilmartin proposed that future land use maps should delineate denser districts to facilitate zoning changes that allow smaller lots and multi-family housing without requiring case-by-case variances. The Board discussed that zoning amendments could enable up to four floors of residential above commercial spaces, contingent upon structural feasibility and economic incentives. Member Bacher responded that current commercial buildings, such as those on County Road, were not structurally capable of supporting additional floors without significant redevelopment, which would require owners to demolish and rebuild. Member Simms referenced a previous experiment allowing mixed-use on Bay Spring, which had mostly transitioned to residential due to demand, and questioned why the model had not been more successful, citing insufficient traffic as a factor.

Several members discussed the opportunity to develop the area around Atria, suggesting that a small collection of businesses could positively influence neighborhood engagement and activity, especially considering the proximity to the bike path. Member Bacher pointed out that Atria could serve as a nexus for such development, and proposed extending the residential-business flexible zoning along Bay Spring.

Member Kilmartin asked about the presence of businesses within Atria, noting that it contains a beauty shop and physical therapy office, but few other commercial amenities. Member Bacher observed that many residents would appreciate more accessible spaces, such as a coffee shop or gift store, within walking distance. Town Counselor Cassar reflected on the existing infrastructure, highlighting that residents already used the community center and would benefit from additional local businesses.

Member Rua questioned why the town maintained low-density zoning across most areas and restricted business districts. He suggested that higher density could be allowed in more places, especially near commercial zones, to encourage development.

Member Kilmartin agreed, noting that her neighborhood already contained many multifamily units that appeared as regular houses from the outside. She highlighted Maple Avenue as another area where commercial and residential activity historically intertwined, but recent decline in local businesses had left the neighborhood searching for its identity. She suggested that increased density might attract new businesses, emphasizing that encouraging customer-driven development could benefit the area.

Member Bacher recalled a previous discussion about eliminating the neighborhood business district designation, which had proved ineffective. He explained that the neighborhood business zone required ground-floor commercial use with residential above, but many applications sought to convert these to all-residential, which had been approved through comprehensive permits. He proposed that the town consider a flexible zoning approach allowing ground floors to be residential if businesses did not occupy the space, citing unused buildings on Maple Avenue as examples. Member Simms acknowledged the benefit to owners if they could switch between commercial and residential as dynamics in the area shift or change.

Member Kilmartin added that the residents-business flexible zoning would require renovations to make ground floors suitable for commercial use, which would be easier if more multifamily units were added. She suggested that this flexibility could help attract small businesses like coffee shops without significant retrofitting.

The group discussed land use options for undeveloped, formerly industrial parcels off Bay Spring Avenue. Member Bacher noted that development would be prohibitively expensive due to contamination. He suggested that some contaminated areas could be repurposed for housing, especially if they were subdivided. Member Kilmartin and Member Simms agreed that remediating the brownfield sites was costly, but some portions might be suitable for residential development if properly cleaned up.

The conversation then shifted to flood zones, since some of the industrial zones were in floodprone areas, making development problematic. Member Bacher emphasized that building in flood zones should be avoided, citing his experience with FEMA buyouts after storms, where repetitive flood-damaged homes were purchased and converted to marshland.

Members discussed the potential for retreat plans for high-risk properties, noting that Warren was already working on such initiatives. Member Kilmartin and Member Rua discussed a property near the Barrington Early Childhood Center, which was owned by the town and considered for lowintensity recreational use. Town Counselor Cassar mentioned previous ideas of activating the space with activities such as off-road biking, emphasizing the potential for community use.

Member Kilmartin emphasized the importance of assessing the distribution of recreational resources across town, particularly focusing on underserved neighborhoods. She suggested that developing passive recreation spaces, such as parks, near new developments could serve community needs. She pointed out that the neighborhood on the other side of Old County Road was cut off by high-speed roads and lacked accessible recreation options.

Member Kilmartin raised the idea of integrating Zion development with recreational amenities, given its proximity. She explained that she often drove past the abandoned science building near the ECC and believed that if the town owned that land, it could be better utilized rather than left idle.

Member Bacher pointed out that the neighborhood was not walkable due to unsafe roads and emphasized the need to improve sidewalk and bicycle access across town to support neighborhood parks. Member Kilmartin agreed and suggested that the parking lot at the ECC could be expanded and improved as a local park, though traffic and safety concerns remained.

Resilience & Energy Member Savelle echoed the importance of enhancing walkability through sidewalk and infrastructure improvements, referencing studies and a TED Talk advocating for neighborhood centers similar to downtown areas. She suggested that the Comprehensive Plan should prioritize sidewalk expansion and connectivity, especially in areas like Zion.

The group discussed the potential for improving walkability and connectivity, noting that current infrastructure limited safe pedestrian access. Also highlighted the need for better zoning and standards to ensure that new developments, especially mixed-use and residential, would be livable without causing nuisance issues like noise or light pollution.

The discussion centered on a historic site on George Street, with Member Bacher noting the need for a restrictive historical zone around the area due to its significance. Member Kilmartin added that the site was linked to King Philip's War, with remnants of the meeting house and artifacts found there, and that efforts were underway to establish a nationally recognized historic district, including interpretive signage and tours to promote tourism and preserve the site. She mentioned that the final report was drafted and that she planned to meet with the Sowams Historical District Project. She clarified that the artifacts were found in already conserved land within the preserve, and if the district gained federal recognition, it might require special zoning for added protection, noting that no development was currently permitted there due to its conservation status.

Member Kilmartin discussed the complexity of allowing multifamily units, noting that some might require special permits or zoning amendments. She emphasized the importance of reviewing existing regulations and exploring opportunities to streamline approvals for conversions, especially in commercially zoned areas like Maple Avenue and Bay Spring.

Member Rua mentioned that the town had previously permitted some multifamily conversions, but these often required special use permits. He suggested that making such conversions by right could incentivize more affordable housing and increase overall housing stock.

Member Kilmartin suggested that zoning amendments could allow for mixed-use developments, with residential units above commercial spaces, especially in areas like Maple Avenue, Bay Spring, and near the business district, to incentivize higher density and support local businesses. She noted that some areas could be rezoned to allow up to four stories of residential above commercial, provided the floodplain and structural considerations are addressed.

Member Bacher noted that building heights of five or six stories were feasible and discussed structural and architectural considerations. He emphasized that tall buildings would require appropriate street design, parking, and sidewalks. Member Rua referenced Warren's innovative flood-resilient development practices, which included green energy and flood mitigation measures.

Member Kilmartin highlighted existing standards for signage and flood-prone areas, noting the importance of managing density and promoting development in higher ground areas projected to be safe from flooding within the next 20-25 years. She mentioned that the Planning Board should have the flexibility to reduce lot sizes and concentrate development out of flood zones.

Member Kilmartin discussed the Bed and Breakfast and Inns that was in the 2015 chapter. R&E Member Savelle also brought up the idea of incorporating Bed and Breakfasts into residential zones, suggesting that incentivizing conversions of vacant buildings into B&Bs could boost local tourism and community character. She proposed that a vacant building on Maple Avenue could serve as a pilot project, demonstrating proof of concept for such conversions.

The group discussed promoting conversions and supporting small-scale hospitality businesses like B&Bs could benefit the community. They discussed the potential for turning long-vacant properties into vibrant, community-oriented accommodations, especially along the bike path and in highvisibility locations. The group recognized that these strategies could help realign development priorities, boost local tourism, and enhance community livability. They concluded that these ideas should remain part of the comprehensive plan and considered for future policy updates. Member Kilmartin expressed that it was part of the recent discussion about economic development remained in the plan. She noted that the plan included a goal to study options such as zoning amendments to support creative and knowledge-based businesses and expand live-work opportunities. She highlighted that COVID-19 had changed work patterns, with more people telecommuting or working part-time outside their homes, and emphasized the importance of activating more areas in town to retain these workers and support local businesses.

Member Bacher responded that he believed expanding home-based work did not require extensive zoning changes. Member Kilmartin clarified that Town Manager suggested that community centers and libraries could be expanded to include office amenities, providing coworking spaces. R&E Member Savelle added that community planning should leave opportunities open for future use, especially as architectural layouts may evolve over the next decade. She emphasized that walkability

and flexible workspaces could help keep residents in town, especially as remote work becomes more prevalent.

Member Rua noted that demand for coworking spaces had been inconsistent, partly due to COVID, and that some existing coworking spaces had struggled. Member Bacher explained that the return to office work had decreased demand for shared workspaces. Member Kilmartin agreed, suggesting that in Barrington, demand might differ from other areas. Member Bacher commented that any new coworking spaces would be a commercial decision and that zoning could facilitate their development, particularly through flexible zoning options like the residence-business flex zone.

The Board discussed Courier Park as a potential site for passive recreation, located behind Primrose Hill School. The site had previously been considered for a field house but was not suitable for that purpose. The Town Manager believed that the site, currently open space, could accommodate small, affordable housing projects or private development, especially if road access and infrastructure were planned accordingly.

The Board discussed that the parcel was a separate lot, currently zoned as open space, and expressed skepticism about its potential for affordable housing due to its underutilized state. He suggested that the town could rezone it to support housing if desired. Member Rua asked whether the site was a separate lot, and Town Counselor Cassar confirmed its location behind the conservation area, noting that it was not in a flood zone and could be a feasible development site.

Member Simms mentioned a private application to develop a former horse field on George Street, which the town had considered buying. Member Bacher explained that the town had purchased the land as part of its farmland preservation efforts and that any future development would need to be carefully considered.

Member Kilmartin pointed out the importance of understanding the status of properties like the Bristol County Water Authority site, which was a long-unused pump station. She suggested that the property should be rezoned from government use, and that the town might consider acquiring it for future development.

The Board discussed zoning and traffic impact by the dental offices at a busy intersection. Member Bacher analyzed potential traffic generated by various businesses, warning that a extending the business zone could allow for stores like convenience stores that could significantly increase traffic and stress the corner. He suggested creating a subzone allowing only professional offices to limit business types and address concerns. Member Bacher indicated that if the property owner retired or sold, it would be safe from high volume business going into that spot.

The group discussed the potential for residential or mixed-use development on various underutilized parcels, including the area near the highway and other town-owned properties. Member Bacher raised concerns about the current zoning restrictions in some areas and recommended rationalizing zoning in areas like Palmer Point and Sweetbriar, which were currently in zones that did not reflect their actual use, often requiring variances.

Member Bacher suggested reverting zoning for the Zion senior residential project back to the previous zoning, noting spot zoning had been conditional and the project was no longer proceeding. He emphasized that the zoning should reflect current land use and that future amendments could be made as needed.

The meeting concluded with a consensus to continue reviewing these issues and to follow up on zoning and land use strategies in upcoming sessions.

#### **4. Adjournment**

**MOTION: Member O'Neal, seconded by Member Rua, made a motion to adjourn at 8:42 PM and the meeting was adjourned.**
