**LAND USE** 

**Overview** 

Barrington is largely a mosaic of contiguous residential neighborhoods.
In recent years, housing demand (both rental and ownership) has
outstripped availability. Barrington must adopt strategies to better
meet this demand while simultaneously preserving the distinct
characteristics that draw residents in the first place. In practice,
this entails meaningful growth that does not outpace the Town's ability
to maintain a high level of facilities and services. Changes to the
Zoning Ordinance and/or Land Development and Subdivision Regulations
will encourage increased density in local growth centers and
neighborhood-compatible development elsewhere, ensuring that future
changes reflect and respect Barrington's historic pattern of
development, unique natural setting, and geographic constraints.

As Barrington approaches build-out, with few parcels that can support
new growth, the Town is facing critical issues integrally related to
land use planning. These include: 

-   Providing a range of housing options and adding to the middle-market
    and affordable housing stock.

As the list above demonstrates, land use is intrinsically linked to the
other elements of this Plan. While the goals identified in each element
reflect the intentions and desires of the Barrington community, they
sometimes exist in tension with one another. For example, the Housing
element identifies increased housing availability and affordability as a
key goal on both the state and local level. Given that Barrington is
close to build-out, some of this growth will necessarily involve
increased density in some areas, however such changes must be balanced
against goals of protecting open space and natural resources, public
safety, and planning for future climate-related impacts.

Barrington's Future Land Use Map (FLUM) is intended as our contribution
toward a "sustainable Rhode Island that is beautiful, diverse, connected
and compact with a distinct quality of place...an abundance of natural
resources, and a vibrant sustainable economy." See *Land Use 2025: Rhode
Island's State Land Use Policies and Plan*, LUG 1, p. 2-6. Careful
planning must preserve what makes Barrington an attractive place to
live---its abundant natural beauty, its public services, and its
distinct suburban identity---while responding to evolving needs and
changing conditions. Thoughtful planning around future land use will
allow the Town to meet emerging challenges as a vibrant, resilient
community.

**Residential Density**

Shown on the land use map is residential use depicted for 20[XX]{.mark}
at various density levels.  These categories are:

· *High Density Residential* (less than 1/8-acre lots). High density
areas are primarily in western Barrington with some areas located near
Barrington Beach. Overall, just 1.2 percent of land area in Barrington
falls in this category. 

· *Medium High Density Residential* (1/8-acre to 1/4-acre lots). Almost
21 percent of the town falls within this residential density range.
Examples: Roberta Plat and the Maple Avenue area. 

· *Medium Density Residential* (1 to 4 houses per acre). This density is
consistent with the lot area requirements of the three single-family
housing zones---Residence 10 zone (10,000-square-foot minimum lot size),
Residence 25 (25,000-square-foot minimum) and the Residence-40 zone
(40,000-square-foot minimum). About 30 percent of land in town is
categorized "medium density" residential. Much of the residential land
in Hampden Meadows, Nayatt Point, and Rumstick Point falls into this
category. 

· *Medium Low Density Residential* (1 house per 1 to 2 acres) and *Low
Density Residential* (\>2 acre lots). Areas include residential land
between Adams Point Road and the Warren River (zoned R25). Just 0.8
percent of all land falls into these categories. 

These numbers suggest the vast majority of existing residential areas in
Barrington are within the medium-density to medium-high density range.
The pattern of development generally follows the State's Future Land Use
Map from *Land Use 2025*, with most of the town's land area falling with
the "Sewered Urban Development" or "Urban Development" potential use
categories. Current densities largely reflect the guidance set forth in
that report. All of Barrington falls within the Urban Services Boundary,
as identified on the state FLUM, although much of the relatively rural
George Street section lacks water service (the addition of such services
are not contemplated at this time.)

**Nonconforming Lots**

Historically, Barrington allowed for a diverse range of lot sizes, a
much different development pattern than currently permitted. For
example, the 1926 Zoning Map, the first for Barrington, included zones
requiring as little as 2,000 square feet per lot, and allowed
multi-family housing (2,000 square feet of lot area per family) and
"double-cottages."

The presence of a wide variety of lot sizes across Barrington today
reflects a number of superseded zoning maps. For example, many, if not
most, of the lots in Bay Spring are smaller than 10,000 square feet,
contrary to the requirements of the existing R10 zone covering much of
that section. As a result, many lots are non-conforming due to a lack of
minimum land area, and many houses fail to satisfy current setback
requirements. In practice, this means that even small additions to a
house can require applying for dimensional variances from the Zoning
Board of Review.

Town-wide, undersized lots are scattered throughout town, particularly
in the Residence 10 and Residence 25 zones. As **Maps LU-3** through
**LU-5** show, undersized lots tend to be concentrated not only in the
Bay Spring section, but also Maple Avenue (R10 zone) and Ferry Lane
(R25). There are comparatively few undersized lots in the R40 zone.

**Development Trends**

*Housing*

Building permit data (discussed in greater detail in the **Housing &
Neighborhoods** element) reflect periods of strength and weakness in the
real estate market. For example, the town hit peaks of more than 80
units per year in 1987 and 1988 before tapering off to about 20 units
per year in the mid-1990s on the heels of a recession. Until the 2000s,
there was almost no multifamily development in Barrington in the 1980s
and 1990s. One exception was the 60-unit Barrington Cove Apartments on
Bay Spring Avenue built in 1996. In addition, Atria Bay Spring Assisted
Living, built in 1999, added 126 assisted-living units.

In the mid-2000s, developers began taking advantage of Zoning Ordinance
amendments adopted in 2000 allowing for mixed-use development, with
apartments above first-floor commercial space. The largest multifamily
development in recent years, Sweetbriar on Washington Road, added 46
two-family and townhouse-style units---approved through the
comprehensive permit process. Another 10 duplex-style units, built in
2008 on a lot adjacent to Sweetbriar in the Neighborhood Business zone,
also were approved under the Comprehensive Permit process.

Since 1980 the preponderance of new housing has been in the form of
single-family detached houses. A total of 1,122 housing units were built

*Non-Residential*

The Economic Development section discusses commercial development in
greater detail. The largest commercial developments in recent years have
involved redevelopment and upgrades of existing properties. New
standalone commercial buildings are rare. Since 2015, there has been
only one such development in the Business zone---the construction of a
fast-casual restaurant on the former site of a car-service station.

Limited mixed-use development has also occurred outside the town center.
For example, the Residence-Business Flex district has seen the addition
of several units equipped to serve as either residential or commercial
space, including six first-floor units at 60 Bay Spring Avenue and one
live-work unit at the corner of Bay Spring and Washington Road.

Recent expansions of institutional uses have included the Bayside YMCA
(now 36,000 square feet), and school buildings at St. Andrew's and
Barrington Christian Academy. The School Department is also currently
undertaking major building projects at Barrington High School and all
three neighborhood elementary schools (see **Services & Facilities**).

*Changes to State Policy and Developments in State Law*

high-quality places, orderly growth and development that respects and
reflects the context in which it occurs, with a focus on development

*Issues of Build-Out*

Issues related to build-out include the following:

· *More tear-downs.* Recent housing construction trends indicate more
residential lots are being redeveloped for many of the new units built
in town---pointing to a diminishing supply of available vacant land. For
example, 251 housing units (188 single-family units and 63 multi-family
units) were built from 2000 to 2009. During that period, the town saw an
overall net increase of just 187 housing units, according to census
data. A review of demolition permit data suggest a substantial number of
these units were built on lots where the original house was torn down
--- approximately 80 residential demolition permits were issued from
2000 to 2009.

· *Preserving community character*. The increasing pressure to tear down
existing houses to redevelop existing lots is altering the character of
established neighborhoods one lot at a time. Larger scale
developments---such as the 40-unit Palmer Pointe on Sowams Road and the
potential future redevelopment of the Zion Bible Institute campus---are
on land abutting existing houses, requiring careful consideration of
design issues such as buffers, building design, drainage, parking lots
and lighting.

· *Providing parks and recreation facilities*. Provision of additional
park facilities, including athletic fields, requires land. The Open
Space and Recreation Element identifies a need for four new athletic
fields, which will require either utilizing land currently owned by the
Town or purchasing additional sites. The Plan also stresses a need to
develop parks that benefit people of all ages and physical abilities,
including walking and biking trails and tot lots/playgrounds.

· *Addressing community facilities and services needs*. The Plan (see
Community Services & Facilities Element) discusses concerns regarding a
need for improved facilities.

· *Conservation of sensitive lands*. The Town and other organizations,
including the Barrington Land Conservation Trust and Audubon Society,
have purchased an extensive amount of open space parcels throughout
town, mostly wetlands and other environmentally sensitive lands.
Development on sites once considered marginal could fragment open spaces
for Town parks and greenways and habitat corridors.

· *Preservation of prime agricultural land*. The Town also has taken
steps to preserve its ties to agriculture, including lease agreements
for farming operations to utilize Town-owned parcels. Fields used for
growing crops and raising horse are maintained in the Four-Town Farm
area off George Street; however, there are pressures to develop the area
despite the lack of water and sewer and adequate roads.

· *Promotion of economic development*. Land available for commercial
development is also scarce, with just a handful of vacant parcels in the
Neighborhood Business and Business zones. As a result, future
development is likely to occur incrementally, as smaller sites are
filled in with new construction. Opportunities to expand commercial
zoning are limited, and the Town in the past has adopted a position to
maintain the size of commercial zones and emphasize the town's existing
residential character (see the Economic Development Element).

Barrington has secured funding for an updated build-out analysis and
review of zoning regulations that will allow the Town to develop
strategies to incentivize production of affordable and accessible
housing. Recommendations arising from the resultant report should be
reviewed promptly and considered for implementation.

*Land Use and Climate Change*

Barrington's extensive shoreline, waterfront development, and
floodplains place significant housing stock at risk due to the effects
of climate change. Many parts of the town will be subject to more
frequent and severe flooding, storm surge, and sea level rise (for
further discussion and mapping, see **Natural Resources**). Not only
does this reduce the Town's already limited supply of developable land,
it threatens the long-term habitability of many existing properties.

The Town will need to take proactive measures to plan for climate change
and increase the resiliency of its vulnerable housing stock. Within the
future floodplain, development and design standards should be adopted
that increase structural and neighborhood resilience. Where appropriate,
existing structures may be retrofitted to improve resilience. The Town
can play an important role in educating homeowners about the importance
of resilient design and linking them with available financial resources.

Notwithstanding preparation, density will need to be reduced in less
adaptable areas. The Town must begin to plan for managed retreat,
including accommodation displaced households in less impacted parts of
town. These strategies should be coordinated with neighboring
municipalities facing similar challenges. New development should be
targeted in areas with minimal flood risk and lesser exposure to sea
level rise. This may require increased density and smaller lot sizes
within proposed development and/or subdivision sites outside the current
or projected floodplain.

*Protecting Historic and Cultural Resources*

Town-owned Lot 3A on George Street, currently zoned Agricultural-Rural,
has been identified as the site of an early colonial Baptist
meetinghouse as well as one of the first battles of King Philip's War.
This lot will be rezoned Open Space-Passive to further protect the
historic and cultural resources of the site.

Land Use 2025 directs municipalities to "Preserve historic buildings,
districts, and archeological sites." *Land Use 2025*, LUP 24. To that
end, Barrington will consider creation of a Historic-Archeological
Overlay District to protect Lot 3A and other significant sites
throughout the town. For further discussion of potential sites, see
**Historic & Cultural Resources*.***
