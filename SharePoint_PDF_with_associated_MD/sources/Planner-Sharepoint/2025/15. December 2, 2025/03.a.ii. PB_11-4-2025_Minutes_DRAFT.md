#### **4. New Business:**

**a. Ratify Solicitor's draft written decision for the UDR of 66 County Road.**

#### **The decision is as follows:**

**MOTION: Chair Phipps, seconded by Member Bacher, moved** to direct the Town Solicitor's office to draft a motion denying the requested dimensional variance for minimum lot size requirements.

Based on the testimony and evidence presented to the Board, the Planning Board makes the following findings of fact and conclusions of law with respect to the requested dimensional variances for lot area for both of the proposed lots, pursuant to RIGL § 45-24-41(d) and (e):

(1) That the hardship from which the applicant seeks relief is NOT due to the unique characteristics of the subject land or structure and not to the general characteristics of the surrounding area; and is not due to a physical or economic disability of the applicant, excepting those physical disabilities addressed in § 45-24-30(a)(16);

**The Board finds that there is not any hardship because it finds that there are no significant unique characteristics of the subject land and structure. The existing house is a two-family dwelling which requires 15,000 sf of lot area and the existing lot contains 15,954 sf, so contrary to the testimony of Doug McLean, who was accepted an expert, this lot is not unusually large. Furthermore, the Board, following extensive questioning of Mr. McLean by the Board, was not persuaded by Mr. McLean's choice to focus on the County Road neighborhood, and mostly to the east of Barton Road, for a comparison of lot sizes. The Board instead finds that County Road serves as a natural division between neighborhoods, and that the neighborhood of the northern part of Tyler Point, encompassing Barton Road and Tyler Point Road, is the more appropriate neighborhood with which to compare the subject property. The Board finds that when using this neighborhood, the proposed new lot will be one of the smallest of the lots in such neighborhood.** 

**No testimony was introduced about physical or economic disability of the applicant, so this is not relevant either way.**

(2) That the hardship is not the result of any prior action of the applicant; and

**This standard is not relevant as the General Assembly has amended this section so as not to apply to a UDR for a subdivision.**

(3) That the granting of the requested variance WILL alter the general character of the surrounding area or impair the intent or purpose of the zoning ordinance or the comprehensive plan upon which the ordinance is based.

**The Board finds that granting the requested variance will in fact negatively alter the character of the surrounding area because it will be creating a lot substantially (approximately 25%) smaller than required by zoning and significantly smaller than the surrounding Tyler Point neighborhood.**

**The Board finds that granting the requested variance will impair the intent and purpose of the Zoning Ordinance and Comprehensive Plan because although it will provide additional housing, it will not be providing affordable housing, and also because this is increasing density and development in a significant flood zone. Because the Comprehensive Plan and Zoning Ordinance emphasize the production of affordable housing and discourages increasing density and development in flood zones, this application does not satisfy this standard.** 

- (e) The zoning board of review, or, where unified development review is enabled pursuant to § 45-24-46.4, the planning board or commission, shall, in addition to the above standards, require that evidence is entered into the record of the proceedings showing that:
- (2) In granting a dimensional variance, that the hardship suffered by the owner of the subject property if the dimensional variance is not granted WILL NOT amount to more than a mere inconvenience, meaning that relief sought is minimal to a reasonable enjoyment of the permitted use to which the property is proposed to be devoted. The fact that a use may be more profitable or that a structure may be more valuable after the relief is granted is not grounds for relief. The zoning board of review, or, where unified development review is enabled pursuant to § 45-24-46.4, the planning board or commission has the power to grant dimensional variances where the use is permitted by special-use permit.

**The Board finds that if the dimensional variance is not granted, the hardship suffered by the owner will not amount to more than a mere inconvenience because there is** 

**already reasonable enjoyment of the permitted use, which is residential, and which use provides yard area for both units of the two-family dwelling, as well as seasonal boat storage as testified to by the owner. The Board also finds that the relief sought is not minimal, but is somewhat maximal to a reasonable enjoyment of the permitted use because of the negative impact on the surrounding neighborhood.** 

Chair Phipps asked if there were any comments on the draft decision prepared to reflect the Board's October 7 discussion and vote. Member Bacher stated that the decision was well drafted, and no other comments were offered.

**MOTION: Member Bacher seconded by Member Kilmartin, moved to approve the written decision reflecting the motion passed on October 7. Motion carried (5/0).**

#### **5. Old Business:**

- **a. Discussion and Action: Comprehensive Plan 2024-2025 Updates (Standing Agenda Item): Subject to Availability:**
  - **i. Natural Hazards and Climate Change**

Town Planner Durfee introduced the Resilience Consultant Karlo Berger as the primary author of the Natural Hazards and Energy chapter and recommended that, after a few final edits with Kilmartin, the draft be sent to the State for preliminary review.

Mr. Berger reported that the second draft was more than 90% complete, with missing sections filled in, comments integrated, and state guidance and Route 114 references added. He recommended submitting the draft for state feedback later in the month.

Mr. Berger noted a few remaining edits, including removing the word "incentivize" from an action item about energy-efficient housing. He also discussed the proposed Coastal Resilience Overlay District, explaining that it remained intentionally broad due to its sensitivity and the need for extensive community engagement.

Member Bacher expressed support for addressing coastal issues but requested clearer information on how such an overlay district would differ from existing zoning.

Planner Durfee responded that additional clarification would be provided and noted that floodrelated overlay standards are influenced by federal and state regulations.

The Board continued its discussion of floodplain management and the proposed Coastal Resilience Overlay District. Member Bacher questioned why flood-related requirements needed to be placed in zoning rather than left to the building code. Solicitor Goins explained that NFIP rules required landuse regulations, including flood standards, to be adopted through zoning and shown on the zoning map.

Planner Durfee added that participation in the National Flood Insurance Program—and potentially the Community Rating System (CRS)—required the Town to enforce FEMA regulations locally. He used the high school project as an example of how FEMA standards triggered elevation and dryproofing requirements during substantial improvements.

Member Bacher noted that FEMA's commercial standards were more flexible than its residential ones and cautioned that imposing strict rules through an overlay district could significantly affect commercial properties. Planner Durfee agreed that the Town would need to consider how best to apply such standards while maintaining NFIP compliance.

Mr. Berger stated that many proposed actions in the plan could contribute to CRS credit and offered to share a paper on coastal resilience overlay district case studies.

Member Kilmartin suggested reorganizing the Natural Hazards chapter by moving overlapping topics—such as coastal wetlands, tree canopy issues, and certain action items—into other comprehensive plan elements like Land Use or Natural Resources. Mr. Berger agreed that there was significant overlap.

Member Kilmartin also questioned the value of reinstating a "green business" program. Member Bacher responded that a similar past program saw little participation and was ultimately discontinued; he noted DEM now offers a certification program, though participation is similarly low. Member Kilmartin recommended focusing on higher-impact actions.

The Board then discussed potential waste-reduction measures, including "no bin, no barrel," "payas-you-throw," and education campaigns. Member Bacher described successful waste-management practices in Ontario emphasizing limited trash pickup, expanded recycling, and free composting. Member Kilmartin reported that the local compost program remained convenient and under capacity. Planner Durfee confirmed the program's subsidy was capped at 500 households but could accommodate more participants.

Member Costa expressed a preference for incentive-based approaches rather than punitive ones. Member Kilmartin noted that enforcement sometimes works better in practice, while Chair Phipps suggested framing policies positively. Member Costa stated he was open to "no bin, no barrel."

Member Hermann asked whether the Town had explored second-life recycling opportunities, such as repurposing waste materials locally. Planner Durfee said related concepts had been discussed, including a reuse area at the transfer station, and noted a local partner was interested in exploring markets and opportunities. He offered to connect with Member Hermann for further discussion.

Chair Phipps invited further comments. Member Bacher cautioned against removing pavement at shoreline dead-end streets, noting that the extra pavement was required for snow-plow operations and now also provides parking for shoreline access. He recommended coordinating with Public Works and the Harbor Commission before altering these areas.

Member Kilmartin observed that shoreline parking was referenced elsewhere in the plan. She then raised concerns that the middle school was built solar-ready but never received solar panels, and suggested the comprehensive plan should encourage the School Committee to revisit installation. She noted that new school buildings were also being constructed with renewable-energy readiness.

Member Bacher agreed the School Committee should be consulted so they understand the Planning Board's intentions. Planner Durfee stated that the Resilience & Energy Committee had already pressed the school project team on this issue and that staff changes might help advance it. He said he hoped to arrange a joint TRC/Planning Board presentation on school construction, at which the Board could formally offer recommendations.

Member Costa asked whether a cost-benefit analysis had been done for solar at the middle school. Planner Durfee believed one likely existed and said similar analyses were being incorporated into planning for the new schools.

Chair Phipps asked if there was any remaining path for revisiting solar at the middle school. Planner Durfee replied that the Planning Board could only issue an advisory recommendation to the School Committee.

Solicitor Goins reiterated that the School Committee retains statutory authority over school facilities, though the Town Council can require review of renewable-energy opportunities in future school-construction approvals.

Member Rua suggested sending recommendations to both the School Committee and Town Council. Chair Phipps said the comprehensive plan should state that all public buildings incorporate renewable energy where feasible. Member Kilmartin agreed that the middle school should be included.

Planner Durfee added that any recommendation should include or request an updated cost-benefit analysis, noting that current incentives may make solar installation more financially viable than before.

Member Kilmartin asked what types of projects were meant under goal 2 action C calling for project screening at parking lots and public sites. Mr. Berger explained that solar canopies were the primary example, and Planner Durfee added that a microgrid for the Government Center was another possibility.

Member Kilmartin also asked about microgrids and geothermal systems. Mr. Berger said geothermal was included by consultants but had not been discussed locally. Member Bacher explained that Barrington's sandy soils made the town ideal for individual-building geothermal systems, which were simple and cost-effective to install. He noted that district-scale systems were more complex due to utility regulation. He added that the systems use plastic tubing resistant to corrosion and described their basic operation.

Mr. Berger asked whether the state's Office of Energy Resources promoted geothermal; Member Bacher responded with examples from Long Island, where heavy use had reduced system efficiency, though Rhode Island was not near that saturation. Member Kilmartin concluded that the geothermal option should remain in the plan.

Chair Phipps asked for final comments. Mr. Berger and Member Kilmartin agreed to collaborate on adjustments to the Natural Resources chapter to help finalize the remaining work. Planner Durfee asked whether the Board was comfortable with Mr. Berger and Member Kilmartin finalizing the draft for submission to the state, and members agreed informally.

Solicitor Goins asked whether the Planning Board would ultimately vote on the full final document; Chair Phipps confirmed that a final vote would occur at a later stage. The discussion closed with thanks among members and Mr. Berger for this continued work on the chapter.

#### **ii. Services and Facilities**

Chair Phipps asked about the status of the Services and Facilities section. Member Kilmartin reported that Phil had made significant progress on the required maps, and Planner Durfee noted these would be reviewed at the next meeting.

Member Costa asked how the Planning Board would ultimately vote on the full comprehensive plan. Chair Phipps and Member Kilmartin explained that multiple rounds of discussion were still expected, including a joint session with the Town Council. Planner Durfee outlined a recommended process: assemble the full document after receiving preliminary state comments, conduct a full Planning Board review with Steering Committee participation, then solicit early feedback from the Town Council before formal hearings. He emphasized that the Planning Board should lead the drafting while the Council focuses on review and adoption. Chair Phipps confirmed the plan was being developed collaboratively and that all Board comments would be incorporated before any submission.

The Board went back to the discussion on services and facilities, Chair Phipps asking whether the Board wanted to review the draft or take comments. Member Kilmartin explained that most of her comments had already been submitted and that highlighted sections in the draft represented placeholders where Phil was incorporating updates from various departments, including the Senior Center and library. Member Bacher noted that some information appeared outdated, and Member Costa observed that certain sections would need further review. Chair Phipps pointed out that the draft contained many blanks. Member Kilmartin clarified that some updates had been included, but work remained piecemeal, and she recommended waiting until all departmental updates were incorporated before sending the draft to the state. Planner Durfee suggested the Board could either wait another month to allow more updates or send preliminary comments if comfortable, noting upcoming agenda items including maps, Watson applications and Belton Court.

Solicitor Goins reported that she was preparing a general FAQ document to help the public understand comprehensive permit applications, including what qualifies, who the decision makers are, and the process for review. She indicated that the FAQ would be general and not projectspecific, and she would provide both public-facing guidance and privileged advice to the Board during upcoming hearings, including for the Watson and Zion projects.

Member Bacher emphasized the importance of clarifying for the public the points that the Board is allowed to consider when approving or denying comprehensive permits, noting that many comments received are typically outside the Board's jurisdiction. Solicitor Goins agreed and added that while large applications were forthcoming, smaller applications would continue regularly, making the FAQ a useful ongoing resource.

Member Costa inquired about the timeline, and Planner Durfee explained that both the Watson and Belton Court applications were currently incomplete. He outlined deadlines for submission to meet notice requirements and noted that if materials were not completed in time, hearings would likely be scheduled for January. Planner Durfee cautioned the Board to be mindful of the 90-day review clock for comprehensive permits, explaining that applicants must agree to any continuances and that timely review may require special meetings.

Chair Phipps asked whether the Watson applicant, being the Town, was more likely to agree to a continuance, and Planner Durfee confirmed that they were generally cooperative. Member Bacher noted that Belton Court had an active public following and suggested that hearings for such projects might require multiple sessions to accommodate public input and responses. Planner Durfee cautioned the Board not to discuss specific details of pending applications but agreed that awareness of process timelines and potential scheduling needs was important.

Chair Phipps asked how the Board wanted to proceed with submitting the draft. Member Kilmartin stated that the approach depended on what the Board felt comfortable with. She explained that Phil had indicated where he would plug in updated information and that the Board could either review everything before sending it to the state or allow Phil to complete the updates and submit it for comments. Member Kilmartin noted that the Board was not required to send it to the state for review and could make changes later.

Member Bacher expressed concern that some departments might submit detailed plans independently, and the Board might want to review those before sending the draft. Member Kilmartin acknowledged this but noted that some information seemed either too specific or too general. Members Costa and Rua indicated that, in their opinion, the draft was mostly data and that they were comfortable with Phil filling in the missing information. Chair Phipps noted that a majority of the Board favored sending it off.

Member Kilmartin emphasized that even if the state provided feedback, the Board was not bound to adopt all suggestions. Planner Durfee added that the Board could continue reviewing and refining the draft after preliminary submission to the state, and that resubmission with targeted updates was always an option. Member Rua suggested that the final version sent to the state be shared with the full Planning Board for transparency. Chair Phipps concluded that there appeared to be sufficient time for further changes in the future if needed.
