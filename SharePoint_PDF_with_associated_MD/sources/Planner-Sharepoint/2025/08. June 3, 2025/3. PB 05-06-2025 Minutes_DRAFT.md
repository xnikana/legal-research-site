# Housing Chapter discussion:

Member Kilmartin discussed ongoing discussions about population projections, emphasizing the importance of accurate future data for effective planning. She noted that projections from Michael Baker and Stantec were outdated, referencing a 2013 technical paper, and that recent trends suggest the projections are less reliable.

The Board discussed strategies for addressing housing needs, including the challenges with current projections and the need for outreach to statewide agencies to improve future estimates. Member Kilmartin explained that the state's benchmarks from the Housing 2030 plan set minimum goals for the next five years, but the broader population trends remain uncertain.

Member Kilmartin articulated issues such as the lack of diversity in housing types—particularly missing middle housing options—and the need to update zoning to facilitate diverse housing forms. She referenced strategies from the Housing chapter, including allowing by-right development of duplexes and multi-family units, especially along transit corridors (Transit-Oriented Development, or TOD). The Board considered potential Zoning Amendments, such as relaxing parking requirements, increasing buildable area, and revising Land Use Zones to encourage development.

Members discussed the possibility of reducing or waiving fees for middle-market and affordable housing development and the adoption of pre-approved blueprints for such housing types. The idea of form-based codes and Design Standards was also considered to ensure new multi-family developments are visually compatible with existing neighborhoods.

Member Kilmartin reviewed the town's existing housing goals, noting the challenges in meeting the 10% affordable housing benchmark due to limitations in existing units and restrictions on counting certain types of housing. The importance of conducting a build-out analysis was emphasized to understand current housing stock and identify gaps, particularly for low- and moderate-income households.

The Board discussed strategies for preserving existing affordable housing, including streamlining funding processes for Housing Trust Funds, possibly establishing a non-profit or joint regional entity to support affordable housing initiatives. There was also consideration of incentivizing infill development, conversions, and adaptive reuse of existing structures.

Planner Durfee explained that in the Town Council meeting from the previous night the Council discussed their support to remove the 20,000-square-foot lot size restriction for ADUs, mentioning that it they would want the Planning Boards recommendation.

The discussion touched on the need to humanize affordable housing initiatives to counteract stigma and to emphasize their role in supporting essential town staff. Members acknowledged the importance of encouraging ADUs, especially for aging-in-place or family support, and debated the permanence of restrictions once such units are established.

Planner Durfee commented expressed support for relaxing these restrictions and suggested that the town could benefit from an advocate to assist property owners with the complex permitting process, possibly through regional cooperation with neighboring municipalities.

The Board discussed the potential for convening property owners, developers, and town officials to foster interest in adding ADUs and other housing types, recognizing that many property owners might be unaware of or hesitant about development options. Member Kilmartin suggested hosting informational sessions to facilitate these conversations.

The Borad agreed that there was consensus to amend the Housing chapter, including the removal or reduction of the 20,000-square-foot ADU lot size limit, and to incorporate these updates into the draft plan for submission to the state.

The Board discussed next steps, including scheduling joint sessions with the Town Council, sharing drafts for feedback, and establishing a timeline for finalizing and submitting the Comprehensive Plan.

#### Cultural and Historical:

Member Kilmartin explained that most of the historic houses, including those on the state and national registers, remained unchanged, except for a few updates: the removal of some old town bridges from the 2015 plan, and the addition of a house on Lincoln Avenue last month. Chair Phipps commented that the maps in this section were still largely reusable.

Member Kilmartin noted that a recent multi-town effort aimed at creating a National Historic Area in Sowams. She highlighted that archaeological work in town had uncovered a site of national significance at Nockum Hill, linked to King Philip's War and the earliest colonial settlements. The findings, which were in final review, revealed remnants of the First Baptist meeting house in America, and signs of a battle. The area was historically Pokanoket homeland, called Sowams, covering parts of Rhode Island and Massachusetts. Representatives from several towns and museums had been exploring the idea of a Sowams Heritage Area, with some towns, including Barrington, not yet represented. The concept involved creating maps and trails, with potential federal designation providing grants and tourism opportunities. Member Kilmartin emphasized the significance of Nockum Hill and suggested that the town consider protected status independently while awaiting the broader heritage district's realization.

The Board discussed the possibility of establishing local Historic Districts. The Board was uncertain whether this topic was actively debated, noting a failed attempt in the early 2000s. Planner Durfee explained that Warren had a voluntary Historic District offering tax credits, but also a mandatory demolition ordinance for certain historic buildings. Chair Phipps expressed skepticism about notification requirements for demolitions, feeling they were ineffective.

Member Kilmartin suggested forming a committee to study voluntary historic districts. Planner Durfee provided context, explaining Warren's ordinances, noting that the voluntary designation applied mainly to tax incentives, while demolition regulations were not voluntary but mandated for older or historic buildings. Planner Durfee warned that the term "voluntary" could be misleading and that historic demolition was regulated by strict ordinances. The Board discussed the challenges of creating formal districts and the reluctance of property owners to be restricted. The Board considered whether to pursue a similar scheme, with Member Bacher pointing out that such initiatives might be burdensome and unlikely to garner sufficient capacity or interest from local preservation groups. Ultimately, the Board leaned toward dropping the idea to avoid unnecessary complications.

The Board discussed a proposed stop-work ordinance, and Chair Phipps questioned its value, suggesting it merely delayed inevitable actions and allowed for complaints. Member Bacher noted that this proposal originated from the subcommittee, which was concerned about preventing unregulated demolition at Belton Court. The Board agreed it might be a redundant process, as current procedures already provided some safeguards.

The discussion moved to the design standards related to the Village Center, with Member Kilmartin suggesting that efforts for signage, building, and storefront consistency might be better placed within the Economic Development chapter rather than Historic Resources and the Board concurred and decided to move this content accordingly.

On community outreach, Member Kilmartin explained that the Preservation Society lacked capacity for additional projects but suggested partnering with other community groups to promote local history and culture.

The Board acknowledged that Barrington's scenic resources and public shore access should be emphasized and proposed relocating some scenic view references from the Historic chapter to the Shoreline Access section, as they more appropriately belonged there.

Finally, references to cemeteries, including Watson and Tyler Point, were reviewed with Member Kilmartin confirming that the Watson cemetery had been moved, and all structures at Zion were demolished, with only part of the main Zion building remaining. Member Kilmartin concluded that she would double check that all the historic reference were still accurate. The Board agreed to verify and update these details as needed in the plan.

# Natural Resources:

Member Kilmartin clarified that any inconsistencies, such as in the wetlands district, were solely her own work. She explained that many notes were simply updates based on her limited knowledge of the past decade's developments, and she acknowledged that a thorough review by staff would be necessary to verify accuracy.

Member Kilmartin discussed a key issue that had emerged from her experience with the Open Space Committee and was echoed in focus groups conducted by Michael Baker: there was a lack of communication and unclear lines of responsibility among various Boards and Commissions. The Conservation Commission had expressed frustration over difficulty reaching officials, or obtaining contact information for DEM. Chair Phipps confirmed that the Resilience Committee experienced similar frustrations, emphasizing that this was a recurring problem particularly related to natural resources and was compounded by the fact that volunteers often used their own personal resources and time, feeling stymied by a lack of clear management plans.

Member Kilmartin explained that part of the issue involved developing management plans for outdoor recreation properties, which would include not only passive conservation areas but also spaces with no passive use and limited oversight. She questioned how much oversight was currently happening on conservation parcels, noting incidents of dumping and encroachment, and observing that there was no systematic process for regular inspections. She praised the stewardship program run by the Land Trust, which conducted regular site visits, and proposed the town adopt a similar approach, focusing on high-value or sensitive areas. She suggested that volunteers, including high school students, community members, or trail walkers, could be enlisted to monitor specific properties, akin to the Land Trust's model.

Member Kilmartin added that micro-grants were available for cleanup efforts, which could be used to purchase supplies like gloves and garbage bags and pointed out that many town-owned properties, especially those that had never been cleaned, were littered and in need of attention.

Member Kilmartin updated the Board on the town's ongoing tree program, questioning whether the target was to increase canopy coverage from 51% to 55% or simply maintain it at 51%. She acknowledged conflicting statements within the tree report and expressed that increasing the canopy might be a better goal, especially as climate change made urban forests more critical. She mentioned that the Steering Committee had discussed revising the tree ordinance but had not yet clarified specific improvements needed. Planner Durfee shared his experience working on a similar initiative

in Warren, noting that the town had received recommendations from the Green Infrastructure Institute to enhance the tree ordinance, particularly to support climate resiliency and reduce heat islands.

Member Kilmartin observed that the Conservation Commission's focus group had suggested revising the tree ordinance to better protect trees and conserve specimens. Planner Durfee clarified that the Conservation Commission likely served as the town's tree committee and that the recommended amendments aimed to strengthen existing policies. Member Bacher added that residents had previously expressed a desire for legal means to prevent the removal of significant or specimen trees on private property.

The Board discussed the town's existing tree ordinance applied primarily to town-owned trees and that there was no private tree ordinance. The Board agreed to follow up with the Conservation Commission for clarification on how the ordinance was being ignored or not enforced.

The Board reviewed the goal for Barrington to protect important Natural Resources and evaluate potential open space protections. Member Kilmartin explained that most actions in this section were unchanged from the 2015 plan, such as reviewing and updating the Open Space Plan, which was outdated and highlighted the new objective to develop and implement management plans for town-owned open spaces, starting with priority sites like Nockum Hill, Allens Cove, and other key parcels.

The Board discussed how the Building Official could address encroachment issues, noting the importance of involving town staff. Member Kilmartin proposed moving the Natural Hazards, Energy, and Climate Change section to focus more on resiliency projects, as it was broader than Natural Resources alone.

Planner Durfee recommended limiting each goal to no more than three action strategies to keep plans concise and effective. Member Kilmartin decided to prioritize actions like native species identification and aggressive tree maintenance to reduce power outages, emphasizing these as practical steps over lengthy studies.

The Board discussed whether adopting an Urban Forest Management Plan was a high priority, with Planner Durfee expressing caution about overcommitting to extensive studies. The Board agreed that ongoing programs like the tree planting and heat island mitigation efforts should be prioritized.

The discussion then moved to surface water quality, referencing the 2015 plan's mention of managing water quality in areas like 100 Acre Cove, Brickyard Pond, and Annawamscutt Creek. Member Kilmartin noted that Save the Bay had completed a Comprehensive Water Quality Restoration Plan for 100 Acre Cove in 2021, and it might be unnecessary to duplicate efforts. Planner Durfee confirmed that such plans could facilitate participation in federal programs, providing funding opportunities.

Member Kilmartin also mentioned the older Watershed Plan from 2012, which addressed stormwater management and best practices for impaired waterways. The Board discussed that the town could refer to these existing plans rather than developing new studies, saving resources.

Member Kilmartin outlined next steps, explaining that the Town Manager was working on Transportation and Facilities, and she would review and edit those chapters once drafts were available. She aimed to finalize Outdoor Recreation, Economic development, and other sections promptly. She noted that the Natural Hazards and Climate Change chapter was in good shape but would require some updates to incorporate new information and align with the other sections.
