**Overview**

Barrington\'s Climate Action Quick Polls and 2050 Comprehensive Plan
survey illuminated citizen desire for a resilient and sustainable
future. Energy emerged as the third most important topic, with
significant agreement that public buildings should be powered by
renewables. Over 50% of 650 respondents identified mitigating climate
change impacts in high-risk areas as a primary land use concern. Student
input specifically emphasized \"coastal flooding/storm surge\" as a
primary concern. Collectively, public feedback, including observations
such as \"Barrington is a very wet town\" from the CompPlanPalooza
public engagement fair, signals a clear community mandate to build
resilience against future climate hazards and reduce the community's
carbon footprint. \[RET\]

**Existing Conditions**

*Community Context*

Barrington, Rhode Island, is defined by its extensive 19.6-mile
coastline, stretching across two peninsulas bounded by Narragansett Bay
and the Palmer and Warren Rivers. The Barrington River bisects these
landmasses, connected by the Central and Barrington River Bridges.
Geographically, the western peninsula\'s Village Center abuts East
Providence, while the eastern Hampden Meadows area links to Swansea,
Massachusetts. This configuration ensures that every location in
Barrington is less than two miles from the coast, cementing its strong
coastal identity and inherently increasing its vulnerability to sea
level rise and intensified storm surges. \[2015\]

Characterized as a medium-density developed community, Barrington
contained significant natural and recreational areas. The town\'s land
use is primarily residential, accounting for \[ \# \]% (\[ \# \] acres)
of its total area. Commercial and institutional uses are comparatively
small, with transportation covering\[ \# }%. Crucially, a substantial \[
\# \]% (\[ \# \] acres) of the landscape is dedicated to vital wetlands,
forests, agricultural lands, and various open spaces. These natural
assets are becoming increasingly important for their role in climate
resilience, offering natural buffers against flooding and providing
critical ecosystem services in a changing climate. This highlights
Barrington\'s commitment to both its developed character and the
preservation of natural systems absolutely vital for its future.\[2015\]

A car-reliant, residential town, Barrington emitted 134,524 MTCO2e in
2023. Buildings fueled 52% of these emissions, with vehicle
transportation adding 40%. The remaining 8% came from waste. Addressing
these major sources -- building efficiency, sustainable transport, and
waste reduction -- is crucial for Barrington\'s climate goals.\[RRB\]

*Priority Hazards*

Flooding stands as Barrington\'s most pervasive natural hazard, stemming
from various sources such as storm surge from hurricanes, winter storms,
and extreme precipitation events. Its low, coastal, and flat topography,
coupled with extensive wetlands, makes widespread flooding a constant
concern. State GIS data reveals nearly two-thirds of Barrington is
within a FEMA flood zone: 6% in Velocity, 36% in 100-year, and 24% in
500-year zones. High winds also pose a threat, especially coastally.
Wildfires, earthquakes, tornadoes, and hailstorms are comparatively
low-risk hazards. Accelerated biodiversity loss is a local threat as it
is a state and national one. \[2015, https://dem.ri.gov/earth-day\]

[Hurricanes]{.underline}

The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers created \"worst-case scenario\" maps,
using NOAA\'s SLOSH model, illustrating coastal inundation in Rhode
Island from Category 1-4 hurricanes\[MAP NHE-1\]. With an average of six
Atlantic hurricanes annually, Rhode Island faces a 6% chance of a
hurricane strike each year, magnified by Narragansett Bay\'s funneling
effect. Barrington\'s low, coastal location makes it acutely vulnerable;
its small size means most properties face hurricane impacts. Even
Category 1-2 hurricanes could severely inundate large areas and cause
significant coastal erosion along southern and western shores. The
threat is substantial and pervasive.

[Severe Rainstorms and Floods]{.underline}

Barrington, a low-lying, water-surrounded community with 6.9 square
miles of inland water, faces significant flood risk from heavy rains and
storm surge. FEMA designates high-risk areas as Zone AE (100-year flood
zone, 1% annual flood chance) and Zone VE (Velocity Zone, 1% annual
chance with destructive waves). Barrington\'s Flood Insurance Rate Maps
(FIRMs), effective July 7, 2014, show Zone VE along its western and
southern coasts near Narragansett Bay and the Warren River. Undeveloped
coastal wetlands, like those near Rumstick Point, offer natural flood
mitigation. A 100-year flood corridor extends inland, encompassing
low-lying areas around Brickyard Pond, Echo Lake, and key estuarine
wetlands, meeting floodwaters entering from the Barrington River and
effectually cutting off the Nayatt neighborhood to the south. \[2015\]

Barrington frequently experiences localized flooding from storms and
heavy rains. March 2010 stands out, with storms causing widespread state
flooding, culminating in a Presidential Major Disaster Declaration for
Rhode Island, including Bristol County. Barrington itself endured
substantial inundation in various areas. While precise flood event
prediction remains challenging, FEMA provides this guidance: properties
located within designated Zone AE and Zone VE high-risk flood zones face
a 26 percent probability of experiencing a flood event over the typical
30-year span of a mortgage. This underscores a significant, long-term
flood risk for many Barrington properties. \[2015\]

Barrington features three dams on its west side. The Echo Lake and RI
Country Club Dams, both earthen structures, regulate water flow and are
situated in the 100-year Flood Zone. They were both classified as Low
Hazard by the Rhode Island Department of Environmental Management in
their 2024 annual dam safety report. A third, unclassified private dam
on Annawamscutt Brook, also in the 100-year zone, sits near an elderly
housing complex where its headwater is lower than the building.
Low-hazard dams pose minimal risk of harm or significant economic loss
upon failure.\[2015\]

[Winter Storms]{.underline}

Nor\'easters, with their strong northeasterly winds, are common winter
phenomena. They often cause coastal flooding and significant erosion of
beaches and dunes, increasing future storm damage. Their impact varies
based on intensity, duration, and tidal phase. While most severe in
winter due to temperature differentials, their winds can approach
hurricane force, damaging structures. Prolonged storm surges can persist
through multiple high tides, severely threatening Barrington\'s
expansive coastline.

Historically, major events like the Blizzard of \'78 and Winter Storm
Nemo in 2013 caused widespread disruption, injuries, and substantial
economic losses. More recently, a December 23, 2022 storm brought
unforecasted and significant coastal flooding to Barrington. These
events highlight continuous coastal vulnerability and the need for
preparedness. Climate models suggest warming winters and wetter, but
more frequent, winter storms \[source: Picard, C.J., Winter, J.M.,
Cockburn, C. *et al.* Twenty-first century increases in total and
extreme precipitation across the Northeastern USA. *Climatic
Change* 176, 72 (2023)). \[HMFMP\]

[Extreme Heat]{.underline}

Extreme heat, defined here as prolonged temperatures 10+ °F above
average combined with humidity, is a serious hazard. Rhode Island has
warmed 4°F since 1900, experiencing more hot days and record warm nights
recently. Climate models forecast increasing extreme heat events, which
threaten human health, particularly for outdoor workers, children, and
seniors. Furthermore, heightened demand for air conditioning during
heatwaves can overload power grids, risking widespread outages. Such
power loss would critically exacerbate health risks, highlighting a dual
challenge for community resilience. \[RIHMP\]

The 2024 Barrington Strategic Tree Canopy Plan identified "heat
islands"---areas of higher-than-average ambient summer temperatures---in
the Bay Spring neighborhood, around Barrington High School, in the
commercial district, and in the neighborhood bounded by Foote St. and
Maple Ave. While more urban areas of the state experience on the whole
more intense heat island effects than in Barrington, these local areas
merit strategic attention: by 2080, Rhode Island's climate could feel
like the current climate of Mississippi. Restoring canopy in these areas
would over time reduce ambient temperatures there and provide additional
benefits.\[STCP, RRB\]

[Droughts]{.underline}

NOAA\'s 2022 modeling projects increased annual and extreme
precipitation for RI. However, higher temperatures will also intensify
droughts by increasing evaporation, leading to faster soil and surface
water depletion despite more rain. It would take a fairly extensive
drought to significantly impact the Bristol County Water Authority's
ability to supply potable water to Barrington. Nonetheless this risk
must be considered in light of future climate uncertainties. \[2015,
RIHMP\]

[Biodiversity Loss]{.underline}

Climate change is significant driver of biodiversity loss in Rhode
Island and in Barrington. Across the state, plants are flowering earlier
(18 days for leaf-out) and migratory birds are departing later,
disrupting food webs. Salt marshes and their flora and fauna---such as
Barrington's saltmarsh sparrows---are threatened by sea level rise.
These ecological shifts raise public health concerns, including toxic
algae blooms and increased vector-borne diseases. Declines in
Barrington's pollinator populations have been noticeable. In a 2023
survey of Barrington High School students, biodiversity loss was placed
as the second more worrisome climate change impact after coastal
flooding. \[Resilient Rhody, 2018\]

[Low Risk Hazards]{.underline}

Barrington generally experiences low risk from several hazards.
[Earthquakes]{.underline} are minor due to no major fault lines.
[Tornadoes]{.underline} are rare, though Barrington experienced an F1 in
2008 causing minor tree damage. [Wildfires]{.underline} are uncommon,
confined mostly to brush fires in scattered wooded areas.
[Hailstorms]{.underline} are infrequent, with only four recorded in
Bristol County between 1950-2023.

*Barrington's Greenhouse Gas Emissions*

Addressing climate change at its source requires Barrington to reduce
Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions, primarily from fossil fuels used in
vehicles and buildings, and landfill waste. To craft a truly actionable
strategy, as the part of development of the 2024 Ready & Resilient
Barrington climate action plan, the Town undertook a thorough
community-wide GHG inventory. This data-driven approach allowed the Town
to accurately identify Barrington\'s key emission sources and prioritize
the most impactful opportunities for reduction. \[RRB\]

Barrington's housing stock is the leading contributor to the Town's
overall GHG emissions. In 2023, about 39% of Barrington's emissions came
from the fossil fuels used to heat and cool its residential and
commercial buildings. As of 2024, less than 1% of residential and
commerical buildings were electrified (e.g. heated by air-source heat
pumps). There were approximately 1200 underground home oil storage tanks
in the floodplain, posing environmental risks in the event of a major
storm. As of 2025, \[ \# \] % of residential and commercial buildings
had solar roofing. Electrifying homes, businesses, schools, and
municipal buildings is Barrington's biggest opportunity to reduce

**Issues and Opportunities: Natural Hazards**

*Barrington's Vulnerability to Climate Change, Sea Level Rise, and
Flooding*

Rhode Island is predicted to experience 10 inches of sea level rise by
2050, and climate change in Barrington has been noticeable over the last
several years with more intense and frequent storms and an increase in
the amount of days with temperatures over 90 degrees. These and other
pressing climate-related issues prompted Barrington Town Council's 2021
Resilient Future Resolution. A staggering \$1.1 billion worth of
Barrington property---about 1 in 3 primary structures---face potential
damage in a 100-year storm, a probability magnified by climate change
and sea level rise. \[RET\]

Barrington's extensive shoreline, waterfront properties, and existing
floodplains place significant housing stock at risk to the impacts of
climate change. Not only does this further reduce the Town's already

*Critical Assets are Vulunerable*

Barrington\'s vulnerability analysis undertaken for its 2022 Hazard
Mitigation & Flood Management Plan identifies critical points in its
infrastructure and population susceptible to natural hazards. This
includes residential/commercial structures, bridges, roads, utilities,
erosion-prone beaches, and vulnerable populations like children and the
elderly. Of all potential hazards, Barrington\'s coastal location makes
its assets most susceptible to flooding. Hurricane storm surge poses the
highest impact potential, capable of rapidly flooding extensive town
areas even from lower-category storms. While the Public Safety Complex
might be vulnerable to a category 3 or 4 hurricane, Town Hall and Public
Works are situated outside critical storm surge zones. \[2015\]

High wind events can further obstruct transportation and damage roads,
structures, and electric utilities, potentially blocking evacuation
routes. Above-ground transmission and distribution lines are highly
susceptible to heavy snow, ice, and strong winds, leading to power
outages. These outages critically impede emergency management and
compromise resident safety. Coastal erosion, accelerated by winter
storms, further exacerbates flood risk by diminishing protective land
buffers. Given these realities, mitigation efforts are judiciously
focused on high-risk flood and wind hazards, rather than low-risk
earthquakes, wildfires, or hailstorms. \[2015\]

*Nature-based solutions can be utilized to build resilience and mitigate
the negative impacts of hazard events*

Barrington sees the value of nature-based solutions in adapting to
climate change. Nature-based solutions use natural processes and
restored or adapted ecosystems to build resilience to natural hazards.
For example, in 2023 the Town implemented a marsh migration corridor at
Walker Farm. This natural corridor of native wetland vegetation
encourages gradual salt marsh expansion inland, mitigating impacts to
the park as sea levels rise. Barrington's 2024 tree inventory and canopy
study are informing present and future efforts to increase tree cover,
which can mitigate urban heat island effects, address stormwater
flooding and support biodiversity. The Barrington Land Conservation
Trust actively secures land donations, preserving open space and
preventing development in flood-prone areas. Integral to the Ready &
Resilient Barrington plan, resilient landscaping efforts are underway,
aiming to protect and enhance natural systems to thrive amidst
escalating climate impacts. \[RET\]

*A concerted effort is needed for community engagement on natural
hazards*

Barrington has obtained helpful data and other information from recent
studies that will equip homeowners and businesses with the information
they need to make informed decisions and become more resilient to
natural hazards and climate change. The Town has time-sensitive goals
and needs the whole community to work together to accomplish them.
Barrington is committed to increasing the capacity of the community
members to prepare for, respond to, and adapt to climate change
impacts.\[RET\]

**Issues and Opportunities: Energy**

*Rhode Island---and Barrington---Have Set Ambitious Energy Targets*

On April 14, 2021, Governor Dan McKee signed the 2021 Act on Climate
bill into law. This landmark legislation updates the 2014 Resilient
Rhode Island Act, establishing enforceable emission reduction mandates.
It boldly positions the state to tackle climate change and transition
towards a clean energy economy.

In accordance with the Ready & Resilient Barrington climate action plan,
Barrington is committed to aligning its greenhouse gas mitigation goals
to that of the state's:

-   Achieve net zero greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by 2050;

-   Support the State's transition to 100% renewable energy by 2033, in
    alignment with the General Assembly's 100% Renewable Energy Standard

Barrington will need to significantly increase the energy efficiency of
its housing stock to support this goal of achieving net zero greenhouse
gas emissions by 2050. Increasing the energy efficiency and
electrification of its existing and future housing stock should be
prioritized through both policies and incentives that encourage
homeowners and developers to adopt energy-efficient technologies and
renewable energy solutions. In addition, energy efficiency measures and
the installation of renewable energy systems can make living in
Barrington more affordable through reduced energy bills. The upfront
costs, however, can be steep, so the Town should explore providing
financial assistance for lower income residents to make these
investments. \[Housing\]
