# **a. Housing – (please be prepared to discuss at least the action steps as it pertains to 10% affordable housing goal)**

Member Kilmartin went over the letter the town received back from the state and explained that some of the suggested changes had already been addressed, mainly stylistic or typographical errors, which were easily corrected. Other comments involved providing specific citations to relevant state plans to ensure ongoing compliance, which was being incorporated into the chapters to prevent similar feedback in the future. Many comments acknowledged the fact that the chapters were being finalized before the implementation plan, and some suggested that maps, which were incomplete, would be completed at the end. There was also discussion about potentially engaging an outside professional to assist with map creation. It was also mentioned that the Town Manager, who has GIS experience, would be assisting in the map creation.

Member Kilmartin highlighted a substantive comment under the housing chapter concerning the need for specific strategies with timelines to meet the 10% affordable housing goal. She referenced a draft excerpt from the housing chapter, which listed strategies from Housing 2030, such as creating smaller units and encouraging affordable housing development, which the state had suggested. She emphasized the importance of incorporating strategies that could be realistically implemented, inviting suggestions from others.

Member Bacher recalled that the Town Manager previous housing element listed specific strategies with projected unit counts, such as 17 or 43 units, but expressed skepticism about the precision of these figures. He also highlighted that the town had already made progress with ADUs, which could contribute significantly toward the goal.

Further discussion addressed legislative changes affecting zoning and planning, including bills that would allow attached single-family dwellings, multifamily units by right or permit, and mixed-use developments, which could facilitate increased housing density. Assistant Solicitor Goins clarified that recent legislation would take effect in January 2026 and could influence zoning practices, such as permitting duplexes and multi-family housing in certain zones.

The Board discussed potential zoning amendments, including allowing multifamily housing by right in commercial districts, and the importance of publicizing these changes to stimulate interest. There was consideration of reducing administrative barriers to facilitate development, as well as the possibility of conducting build-out analyses with the assistance of Libra Planners, contingent upon approval from Rhode Island Housing.

Concerns were also raised about the potential impact of densification on community standards, with some members expressing apprehension about the possibility of taller buildings and reduced yard sizes. The discussion acknowledged that some standards might need to be relaxed to achieve affordable housing goals, and that identifying suitable locations for increased density beforehand would be beneficial.

It was suggested that the plan include visual representations of progress over the past 10 to 20 years and projections based on proposed strategies, even if the goal might not be fully reached within the plan's timeframe. There was consensus that the focus should be on demonstrating significant progress, with an understanding that the goalpost might shift over time.

Azley Savelle, Economic Development Committee member, suggested examining how similar communities across the country approached this issue and how such strategies could be integrated into Barrington's 10-year comprehensive plan. They expressed skepticism about reaching the 10% affordable housing goal within ten years, noting community sentiment that residents valued their lots and community aesthetics, despite interest in ADUs.

Planner Durfee responded by emphasizing that the town should challenge the state's approach, advocating for deed restrictions that last in perpetuity rather than only 25 years, to prevent loss of affordability over time. He proposed incentivizing developers to build beyond the minimum 25%, with longer-term restrictions to address the goal more sustainably.

Member Bacher agreed that the 25% requirement was difficult to meet without subsidies, as building affordable units below cost was not commercially viable. He stressed the importance of mixed housing to foster healthier communities and noted that the original comprehensive permit law aimed to streamline affordable projects for community development corporations, which built entirely affordable housing. He suggested that policies could be adjusted to incentivize more affordable housing, including deed restrictions.

The Board discussed creating a table to outline strategies for increasing affordable housing, with Member Bacher volunteering to draft it. Member Costa asked about how the town communicates with the state regarding the 10% goal, and Planner Durfee explained that advocacy involved engaging legislators, advocacy groups, chambers, and community organizations. He stressed the importance of community-specific strategies to protect Barrington's character amid development pressures.

The Board discussed the importance of identifying priority locations for increased density and ensuring design standards to make denser housing attractive. Concerns about character preservation and the potential for taller buildings were raised, with an emphasis on maintaining design control.

The Board addressed other housing policies, including ADUs and neurodiverse housing. There was debate about the vagueness of the term "neurodiverse housing" and whether it should be included in the plan, given the limited local need and potential legal implications. Planner Durfee noted that existing state law already addressed setback standards and suggested removing redundant language from the zoning code to avoid unnecessary confusion.

#### **iii. Land Use**

The Board then addressed a letter related to land use, specifically referencing a request from Dr. Asaro concerning a zoning modifications in the village area. Chair Phipps clarified that the discussion of land use and zoning was relevant to tonight's agenda, although no formal action was planned at this time. Planner Durfee suggested that the chair allow Dr. Asaro to briefly present his request, which could be further addressed when appropriate.

The Board heard a presentation from Dr. Matthew Assaro, a long-term resident of Barrington, regarding his property at 37 Ferry Lane. Dr. Assaro explained that his property has operated as a dental office for nearly 70 years, with past and current uses deemed commercial by state agencies, utility providers, and insurers. He requested that his property be officially recognized as a commercial use in the comprehensive plan update to alleviate hardships related to financing, insurance, and utility fees.

Board members discussed the inconsistency between the property's long-standing use and its current zoning designation. Some members noted that the property was on the commercial block on the map and surrounded by other commercial and institutional uses, making a zone change logical. Others expressed concern about potential impacts of re-zoning, such as allowing larger developments like multi-story buildings, given the property's location in a transition zone with surrounding residential areas.

Legal considerations about non-conforming uses and spot zoning were discussed, with Board members emphasizing that non-conforming uses are generally permitted to continue but may face limitations on expansion or alteration. The possibility of creating a new zoning district, such as a limited business zone permitting professional offices, was proposed as a middle ground.

Dr. Assaro expressed that his primary motivation was financial hardship caused by the current zoning, which he felt did not reflect his property's actual use. The Board acknowledged his concerns and indicated that any zoning change would require a comprehensive review and public input.

The discussion concluded with the understanding that his request would be considered during the ongoing comprehensive plan update process. The Board agreed to monitor the process and stay in touch with Dr. Assaro regarding potential zoning amendments.

Planner Durfee emphasized the importance of the land use chapter in the comprehensive plan, describing it as the foundation for guiding community growth and development. He recommended starting with the 2015 land use plan and updating or amending it based on current community needs and changes. The Board discussed the value of detailed mapping and possibly holding a special session dedicated solely to land use, with collated maps and tables to facilitate focused discussion.

Member Kilmartin highlighted that her memo identified key land use issues, including zoning considerations, climate resilience, flood risks, and the development potential of undeveloped parcels such as those off Bay Spring. She stressed the importance of integrating climate and resiliency data, especially for areas at risk of flooding or impacted by climate change, and noted ongoing efforts related to the Route 114 corridor and potential brownfield remediation.

The Board discussed the status of various parcels, such as the former tannery and industrial sites, noting that some might be limited by environmental contamination or unbuildable due to cost or environmental restrictions. They also considered the importance of protecting wetlands and habitats, such as the turtle nesting grounds, through collaboration with CRMC and land trust efforts, including possible future wetlands migration strategies.

Additional topics included the need for asset management of parks and recreational facilities, with suggestions to conduct a thorough review and develop a capital improvement plan. The Board acknowledged that some parks were underutilized and that funding for new parks, like the proposed Watson site, needed clearer definition.

The discussion touched on potential development pressures, including future population growth and the interest of developers in nearby properties. The Board also heard from the town's resilience contractor, Karlo Berger, who discussed how he had been working on the Natural Hazards and Climate Change chapter. He discussed using parts of the Ready and Resilient Barrington. The Board also discussed the possibility of establishing a stormwater utility, which would provide dedicated funding for stormwater infrastructure, including flood mitigation and management of stormwater systems, with assistance from the Southeast New England Program (SNEP), which offers free technical support.

Finally, the Board noted that federal delays were not currently impacting the SNEP program and that the town should consider incorporating climate adaptation strategies, such as marsh migration corridors and zoning adjustments, into the comprehensive plan to address future environmental challenges.

#### **iv. Circulation/Transportation**

The Board discussed the circulation section of the comprehensive plan. Member Kilmartin confirmed that the tables, though well-designed, might require revisions once new data became available. The Board agreed to send the draft for feedback with the understanding that updates would be necessary later.

Member Rua provided feedback on Goal C4, specifically regarding the removal of a left turn lane onto Lincoln. He expressed concern that easing traffic congestion could encourage more driving, conflicting with goals for a walkable district. Member Rua suggested that reducing congestion may simply increase traffic, which could undermine efforts to promote walkability and economic development. Member Kilmartin acknowledged this tension and discussed the challenge of balancing public feedback with other planning goals.

Member Bacher made specific comments on various sections:

- **C1, Action 4b:** He suggested that electric bike charging stations should not be installed at schools.
- **C2**: He questioned the practicality of installing bike lanes and crosswalks, noting the long timeline, until 2033 with the state plan, and the need for the town to consider doing some improvements themselves.
- **C3, Action 1**: He addressed the park and ride request, stating that they have put in one at Police Cove and the need is no longer needed.
- **Action 3:** Expressed that modifying existing infrastructure in this zone of County Road to facilitate pedestrian crossing/waiting would be expensive, and service very few people to make it safer to cross.
- **C4, Action 1:** improving traffic signals and pedestrian crossing. Regarding the upcoming roadway project, Bacher discussed the potential for narrowing lanes and adding bike lanes, emphasizing the importance of early involvement in the corridor analysis to influence design.
- **Action 2:** Concerns about implementing land development standards requiring internal connections between shopping areas to reduce curb cuts. He questioned how to enforce cooperation from property owners, especially when development proposals only involve one parcel. Planner Durfee shared an example from Essex, where requiring right-of-way connections in parking lots during new developments helped reduce traffic issues. He explained that such connectivity can be achieved over time through development requirements, even if some property owners resist initially. Member Kilmartin mentioned the idea of encouraging joint development, like creating a larger shared space off County Road, to promote shared use and development benefits.
- **Action 3**: He questioned regulations of curb cuts on County Road was a state function and questioned whether it should be included in the local plan. Herb Durfee suggested encouraging the state to minimize new curb cuts, especially within the context of a significant upcoming project, estimated at \$20 million, which would include roadway milling, paving, and potential improvements for pedestrian and bike access. He explained that the project aimed to reduce traffic congestion and improve safety, but widening sidewalks was unlikely.

The Board discussed the potential for narrowing lanes to slow traffic and create space for bike lanes or sidewalks, emphasizing the importance of corridor analysis to inform these improvements. They acknowledged the need to remain engaged in the design process to advocate for pedestrian and cyclist-friendly features.

The committee also addressed dangerous intersections, such as New Meadow and Sowams, noting that these areas had been in previous plans and required future improvements. The ongoing Massasoit project was highlighted, including completed sidewalk installations and pending additional enhancements, contingent on funding reinstatement.

Regarding zoning, the Board confirmed that existing regulations already ban new drive-throughs on County Road and discussed the potential to extend such bans town-wide. They noted that drivethroughs on County Road could not exit onto the road, which was an existing restriction.

The Board expressed it was ok to submit this version of the circulation plan to the state, acknowledging that maps and additional documentation were still in progress. They agreed to acknowledge this and prepare for feedback, with a plan to include maps once finalized. Members recognized the substantial effort involved in preparing the materials and appreciated everyone's contributions.
