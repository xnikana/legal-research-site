# <span id="page-3-0"></span>**Barrington 2015 vs. Today**

The 2015 update to the Town of Barrington's Official Comprehensive Plan put forth new goals, objectives, and actions for the Town to take on over the following 10 years. As such, it is important to identify which of those goals, objectives, and actions have been completed, which ones are currently underway, and which ones need to be addressed in the updated plan. It is also important to understand which some of these goals, objectives, and actions, for one reason or another, may no longer be appropriate or applicable for consideration through the next round of the planning process.

#### <span id="page-3-1"></span>**Housing**

The Housing element of the 2015 plan included a series of goals and actions aimed at increasing the supply of housing for the growing number of seniors within the community, ensuring that new development is compatible with the character of Barrington, and maintaining compliance with the Sate's affordable housing goals. While some of the actions to meet these goals were completed, such as creating a new Senior Residential Village district, many of the actions from the 2015 plan are still opportunities for the Town to study and address as housing affordability continues to be an issue within Barrington, and across the country.

#### <span id="page-3-2"></span>**Economic Development**

The goals and actions within the 2015 plan that focused on economic development focused on strengthening Barrington's non-residential tax base and enhancing infrastructure in the Town Center area. Most of these actions have been completed – such as installing new wayfinding signs; constructing new sidewalks on Maple Avenue, Wood Avenue and West Street; and providing additional support to the Barrington Business Association. However, there are still some actions from the 2015 plan, such as the need to conduct a market study of the retail environment in the Town's business districts, that have not been addressed that need to be taken into consideration for inclusion into the next Comprehensive Plan.

## <span id="page-3-3"></span>**Circulation**

As it related to traffic and circulation in Barrington, the 2015 Comprehensive Plan focused on relieving congestion on County Road/Route 114, improving the safety of all transportation options throughout Town, and providing a well-balance multimodal transportation system. While initiatives were advanced such as the amended zoning and subdivision requirement to require new sidewalks and bike racks for nonresidential development and the advocacy for the repaving of Nayatt Road, there are actions from the 2015 plan that are either in process or have not been started that need to be reassessed in terms of their applicability and ease-of-implementation into the updated plan.

## <span id="page-3-4"></span>**Community Services and Facilities**

Maintaining high-quality public facilities, anticipating the community's long-term needs, and limiting the community's overall environmental impact were the main goals of the Community Services and Facilities chapter of the 2015 Comprehensive Plan. Most of the actions from the 2015 plan are still in progress or ongoing as the Town continues to evaluate its long-term needs and the needs of its residents and will be revisited and reassessed.

#### <span id="page-3-5"></span>**Natural and Cultural Resources**

The goals and actions within the Natural and Cultural Resources chapter of the 2015 Comprehensive Plan highlighted the importance of preserving the rural character of certain areas of the Town, protecting important natural areas, and working to effectively manage the Town's open space areas. Work has been completed on some of the actions within this chapter, including protections that were adopted to limit the impact of development on the rural character of the George Street area. Other actions, such developing partnerships with the Land Trust, property owners, and stakeholders are still ongoing, and are considered for inclusion in the updated plan.

## <span id="page-4-0"></span>**Outdoor Recreation**

Meeting the recreational needs of the community, improving the linkages between recreational areas, and improving recreational access to the shore were the key goals of Outdoor Recreation chapter of the 2015 plan. Within these goals, actions included seeking to acquire property, developing and updating a park master plan, and providing new amenities at key park sites. Some actions were completed and other are still ongoing, but there remains an opportunity to incorporate some of the goals and actions into the updated Comprehensive Plans.

#### <span id="page-4-1"></span>**Energy**

The goals and actions of the Energy chapter of the 2015 plan were centered around making investments to reduce energy use, providing resources to achieve the Town's energy goals, and promoting energy efficiency. Critical actions, including creating a Climate Action Plan and working towards a revolving energy fund through the capital improvement program, have been vital towards reaching these goals. Other actions from the 2015 plan are still ongoing and could prove useful in meet the goals of the next comprehensive plan.

#### <span id="page-4-2"></span>**Natural Hazards**

At the forefront of the Natural Hazards chapter of the 2015 plan were the goals of reducing the risk of certain natural hazards and improving the Town's resiliency against these hazards. Due to the complexities and difficulties of addressing many of the issues defined within these goals, nearly all of their associated actions are either in-progress or ongoing efforts. The risk of natural hazards and ways to mitigate their impact on the community will be of high important in the updated comprehensive plan.

#### <span id="page-4-3"></span>**Land Use**

While it came with several associated actions, the main goals of the land use chapter of the 2015 comprehensive plan were to preserve the prominent residential character of the Town while providing other open space, commercial, industrial, and institutional lands to meet the needs of the community. Many of the actions in the land use chapter tie into goals and actions from other elements of the plan. Some of which, like the establishment of the Senior Residential Village zone, have been completed. Other actions from the land use chapter of the last comprehensive plan are ongoing and will be reviewed for inclusion in the updated plan.

# <span id="page-4-4"></span>**Introduction and Research Methods**

The remainder of the Community Profile intends to serve as the basis for the development of the Comprehensive Plan (Barrington 2050), presenting data on the demographics, resources, housing, economics, services, transportation, natural hazards, land use, and tourism of Barrington. The following data was compiled to help develop a shared understanding of the community and the unique characteristics that it illuminates; highlighting the trends, gaps, and opportunities that will help inform the Barrington 2050 Comprehensive Plan.

The data in this profile has been sourced from federal and state agencies, Bristol County, and local stakeholder input. Census data was gathered from the decennial 2020 census and the 2022 American Community Survey. Although these sources may be subject to sampling errors, they are the most reliable information available for the Town of Barrington.

Results from the Barrington 2050 Community Survey are also included in this profile. The survey was open for a 10-week period from July 23, 2024, to September 30, 2024. 506 responses were collected from the community that helped to contextualize the data gathered for this section and will further help inform the goals and recommendations of the Comprehensive Plan.

## <span id="page-4-5"></span>**Who and What is Barrington?**

Barrington is a community approximately 15 square miles in size located in the northwestern part of Bristol County, Rhode Island. As of the 2020 Census, the town is home to 17,121 residents and 6,439

households. Barrington is a community of distinct neighborhoods shaped by the town's extensive shoreline and its agricultural and industrial history. Today, Barrington is largely a bedroom community within the greater Providence metropolitan area.

#### **ESRI Tapestry Segmentation**

Through ESRI Tapestry Segmentation data, Barrington can be characterized within their *Affluent Estates* LifeMode Group, with 78.8% of Barrington's households falling within this category. ESRI's analysis provides a general classification of neighborhoods across the United States into 67 unique market segments based on socioeconomic and demographic characteristics. The Tapestry is then divided into 14 LifeMode groups, which reflect life stages and social status, and six (6) urbanization groups, which take into consideration development patterns such as density, size, and location.

The *Affluent Estates* market consists of some of the wealthiest households in the country. These communities are marked by high incomes, large homes, and upscale lifestyles. Residents of *Affluent Estates* tend to be well-educated, with professional or managerial careers, and enjoy a comfortable lifestyle. These areas are often found located in older neighborhoods outside the urban core, like the relationship between Barrington and Providence. This market type is attractive to businesses that offer luxury goods and services, as well as to developers seeking to build high-end residential communities.

Within the *Affluent Estates* LifeMode groups, *Savvy Suburbanites* and *Top Tier* are the two prominent submarkets that exist in Barrington.

- Savvy Suburbanites make up 33.8% or 2,048 households within Barrington. These households are typically middle-aged, well-educated, and affluent households who live in established suburban neighborhoods. They are homeowners who enjoy home improvement projects, gardening, and outdoor activities. These residents are often seen investing in their homes and retirement and are focused on maintaining a healthy lifestyle. Their shopping habits reflect a preference for organic food, high-end electronics, and premium clothing brands.
- Top Tier makes up 31.2% or 1,893 households within Barrington. These residents live in exclusive neighborhoods with large, luxurious homes and often work in high-paying professional or managerial positions. *Top Tier* households are highly educated and have significant disposable income, which they spend on luxury goods, high-end travel, and premium services. These residents value quality and are willing to pay a premium for quality products and experiences.

While the ESRI tapestry classifications can be supported by the data found throughout this Community Profile, such as an elevated median household income and relatively hefty monthly housing costs, it is important to note that this is not an end-all-be-all representation of the homes and residents of Barrington. Those that are not within the socioeconomic groups described above feel the difficulties of economic highs and lows close to home and should not be forgotten as part of the comprehensive planning process that this profile leads into.

## **Topography and Floodplain**

Due to the Town of Barrington's geography, all of the land lies within one type of FEMA-defined floodplain. The two most significant floodplains, the 100-year flood zone (having a 1 percent or greater chance of flooding in a given year) and the velocity zones (coastal area within the flood zone likely inundated by a wave surge during hurricanes), cover a significant share of land. The 100-year flood zone covers 1,998 acres within Barrington while the velocity zone covers 267 acres of coastline. The velocity zone mostly covers the southern coastline of the Narragansett Bay.

To mitigate and prepare for extreme flooding events there are several Coastal Barrier Resources System Areas (CBRS) within the Town. CBRS encourages the conservation of storm-prone and dynamic coastal barriers by withdrawing the availability of federal funding and financial assistance. These areas include undeveloped coastal barriers and other important coastal habitats that are considered environmentally sensitive and important for natural storm protection.

#### <span id="page-14-0"></span>**Watersheds**

The Town of Barrington Falls within the larger Narragansett watershed which encompasses most of Rhode Island. Within this major watershed, there are found subwatersheds that fall within Barrington Town limits. These include:

- 1. The Barrington River-Warren River Watershed
- 2. The Old Mills Creek-Narragansett Bay Watershed
- 3. The Palmer River Watershed
- 4. The Seekonk River-Providence River Watershed

The Barrington River-Warren River and the Seekonk River-Providence River cover most of the Town with Palmer and Old Mills watersheds covering sections on the eastern section of the municipality.

#### <span id="page-15-0"></span>**Wetland Areas**

The Town of Barrington's wetland areas make up about 17.5% of the Town, consisting of 955 acres. Most of these areas are on conservation land owned by the Town, the State, the Land Conservation Trust, Audubon Society, or by other conservation methods.

#### <span id="page-15-1"></span>**Water**

Inland water bodies in Barrington total approximately 200 acres with the largest body of water being BrickyardPond within Veterans Memorial Park. Other inland bodies of water include Echo Lake (22.5) and Prince's Pond (8.3). Annawamscutt Brook and Mussachuck Creek also flow through the Town.

Barrington receives drinking water from the Bristol County Water Authority that obtains all of its water supply from the Providence Water Supply Board's Scituate Reservoir. The Scituate, along with 4 small reservoirs, contains 41 billion gallons of high-quality water. The Providence Water Supply Board actively manages the watershed to protect the quality of the water that flows into the reservoirs through its forestry management and resident education programs, involvement in Town zoning and permitting, and with patrols that monitor activities and restrict access.

# **Historical and Cultural Resources**

#### <span id="page-16-0"></span>**Introduction**

Barrington's landscape is shaped by its historical and cultural resources. Built heritage, scenic landscapes, and the shorelines, bays, and rivers that make up Barrington's natural resources, combine to create a unique cultural landscape worth investing in and preserving.

Barrington was designated a Preserve America Community in 2011. The Preserve America program, through the Advisory Council on Historic Preservation, recognized communities that protect and celebrate their heritage, use their historic assets for economic development and community revitalization, and encourage people to experience and appreciate local historic resources through education and heritage tourism program.

#### <span id="page-16-1"></span>**Historic Districts, Buildings, and Sites**

Barrington has several districts, buildings, and sites included on the National and State Registers of Historic Places. There are no local historic districts.

#### **Listed Districts**

Barrington has three historic districts listed on the National Register of Historic Places.

#### **Architectural Styles**

Barrington's historic evolution from a farming community to a suburban area can be seen in the architectural styles of its buildings, including:

- 1. *Colonial and Federal Styles*: Many of the oldest buildings in Barrington reflect Colonial and Federal architectural styles, characterized by their symmetry, simple rectangular shapes, and minimal ornamentation.
- 2. *Greek Revival*: This style became popular in the early 19th century, featuring columns, pediments, and heavy cornices, often inspired by classical Greek temples.
- 3. *Victorian Styles*: Including Gothic Revival, Italianate, and Queen Anne, these styles are noted for their decorative trim, asymmetrical facades, and complex rooflines. The Gothic Revival style often includes pointed arches and intricate woodwork.
- 4. *Tudor Revival*: This style is exemplified by the Barrington Town Hall, with its steeply pitched gable roofs, decorative half-timbering, and tall, narrow windows.
- 5. *20th Century Styles*: The document also covers early 20th-century styles such as Colonial Revival, which sought to revive elements of American colonial architecture, and the Craftsman style, known for its handcrafted stone and woodwork, low-pitched roofs, and wide eaves.
- 6. *Modern and Contemporary*: More recent architectural trends in Barrington include modern and contemporary styles, which emphasize clean lines, open floor plans, and the use of new materials and technologies.

#### **Historic Preservation Efforts**

#### **Barrington Preservation Society**

The Barrington Preservation Society, charted by the State in 1965, is the town's principal historic preservation organization. The Society's mission includes identifying, acquiring, maintaining, and making accessible to the public various historically significant buildings, places, tracts of land, objects, and other information. The society operates a museum that is home to several artifacts and collections related to Barrington's history, providing a tangible connection to the past for the community.

In addition to maintaining the museum, the society runs several key programs. Including the Historic Structure Plaquing Program, which awards plaques to historic structures, and the Elizabeth Sargent Warren Historic Preservation Award, recognizing significant contributions to historic preservation.

One notable initiative is the installation of interpretive wayside signs along the Barrington section of the East Bay Bike Path and in the town center. These signs provide historical context and information to residents and visitors, enhancing their understanding of the area's heritage.

Another key program is the "Barrington by the Bay: A Brief History," an interactive curriculum designed for third graders. This program includes a teacher's manual and a book and involves visits to the Barrington Civic Center Historic District and the Barrington Preservation Society Museum.

The society also organizes educational and social events throughout the year, such as exhibitions and gatherings, to engage the community and promote historical awareness.

#### **Zoning**

While there is no historic district commission, Barrington's zoning ordinance has historic building and site standards that apply to land development applications that involve, adjoin, or is within 200 feet of a historic property listed on the National or State Registers of Historic Places. The standards include:

- 1. *Retention of Original Characteristics*: Preserve the original characteristics, materials, and setting of historic structures whenever possible.
- 2. *Compatibility of New Elements*: Ensure new elements are compatible with the original in design, material, color, texture, and other visual qualities.
- 3. *Facade Proportions*: Maintain the original facade proportions, including window and door placement, and roof shape, pitch, and detail.
- 4. *Exterior Details*: Retain, repair, or replace exterior details such as siding, corner boards, cornice, brackets, lintels, and window and door styles in kind.
- 5. *Additions and New Construction*: Ensure additions or new construction are compatible with the design of the original structure and/or the style and character of the historic district.
- 6. *New Building Compatibility*: New buildings within a historic district should match the style, scale, height, facade, and materials of the original buildings.
- 7. *Setback and Orientation*: Match the setback pattern, building orientation, and spacing patterns of new buildings with those of the original buildings within the district.
- 8. *Landscaping and Site Treatment*: Landscaping and site treatment should be compatible with the historic setting.

# **Services, Facilities, and Utilities**

#### <span id="page-25-0"></span>**Water Suppliers**

All of Barrington's water is provided by the Bristol County Water Authority (BCWA). 100% of the BCWA supply come from the Scituate Reservoir, the largest inland body of water in the State of Rhode Island. In combination with four smaller reservoirs, contains 41 billion gallons of high-quality freshwater. The Scituate is owned by the Providence Water Supply Board which is tasked with the protection and management of the watershed through its forestry management and resident education programs, involvement in Town zoning and permitting, and with patrols that monitor activities and restrict access.

All water gathered from the Scituate is treated at the Holton Water Treatment Plant in Hope, RI. The Holton can filter 140 million gallons per day, 65 million of which are readily available for consumption.

To ensure adequate and sustainable distribution of water BCWA has undertaken several multi-milliondollar projects that will result in significant improvements to their distribution system. Some of these projects include installing and repairing water mains along Governor Bradford Drive, Wilcox Lane, and Riverview Drive.

#### <span id="page-25-1"></span>**Wastewater**

The Town of Barrington's Public Works Department is tasked with the maintenance of the town-wide sewer systems. The Town has six major sewer pumping stations located at Police Cover, near the Barrington River Bridge, on County Road near Prince's Pond, Freemont Avenue, Legion Way near Brickyard Pond, Walnut Road, and Bay Spring.

The Town's force main, a pressurized pump that moves wastewater from one location to another, leads to a treatment facility in East Providence which is shared by several other municipalities. Due to this agreement, Barrington shares the cost of upgrades to the East Providence treatment plan dependent upon the percentage of the Town's wastewater flow that comes to the plant.

#### **Bridges**

The town's three roadway bridges, all replaced since 2010, include the Veterans Memorial Bridge, the Lance Corporal Victor Patrick Andreozzi and Vietnam Veterans Bridge, and the Warren River Bridge. These bridges have sidewalks but lack bicycle provisions. Temporary modifications in 2021 added an 8-foot-wide wood walkway for bikes and pedestrians on the Barrington and Warren River Bridges, protected by jersey barriers. The bike path bridges over the Barrington and Warren Rivers, originally railroad bridges, were closed in 2019 due to severe deterioration. Rated in poor condition, their replacement is a high priority for the town, requiring immediate funding.

#### **Bike Network**

The East Bay Bicycle Path connects several towns in Rhode Island, including Bristol, Warren, Barrington, East Providence, and Providence via the I-195 bridge. It is popular with walkers, joggers, and bicyclists, including commuters. The path is owned by RIDOT and maintained by the RI Department of Environmental Management.

In Barrington, the path links local neighborhoods to destinations like Haines State Park, Bicknell Park, the Bayside Y, Veterans Park, and downtown areas. The town has one street with unprotected bike lanes on Kent Street and "sharrows" on Wood Avenue from a 2018 project.

#### **Sidewalk Network**

Barrington has about 17 miles of sidewalks, mainly along major roads and near schools, commercial areas, and the Government Center. The Town mandates sidewalks in new commercial projects and subdivisions near schools. In 2012, the Zoning Ordinance was updated to allow payments instead of sidewalks for funding off-site improvements when sidewalks aren't necessary. The Barrington 2050 survey found an overwhelming need for more sidewalks and sidewalk improvements. Most of the sidewalks within the Town are too narrow, obstructed with objects, such as telephone poles, and are in disrepair.

#### **Public Transportation**

Barrington's public transit is primarily served by the Rhode Island Public Transit Authority (RIPTA) bus service. The main route, Route 60, travels along Route 114 between Providence and Newport, with about 14 stops in Barrington. There are bus shelters at key locations like the White Church and Police Cove. Route 61X offers additional daily trips.

West Barrington no longer has direct bus service; the nearest stop is at Shaw's shopping center in Riverside (Route 33).

<span id="page-30-0"></span>There are two park-and-ride lots: a 100 space lot at the White Church and a 36 space lot at Police Cove Park. The Senior Center bus provides free transportation for residents aged 60 and above to the Senior Center, shopping trips, and local appointments on weekdays, with reservations required.

# **Natural Hazards and Climate Change**

#### <span id="page-32-0"></span>**Introduction**

Barrington is particularly susceptible to a range of natural hazards, primarily due to its coastal geography and low-lying areas. Flooding presents itself as the most likely and significant natural that the Town faces, especially during heavy rainstorms, nor-easters, and hurricanes. The proximity to the greater Narragansett Bay makes the Town vulnerable to storm surges and coastal flooding that can inundate homes, roads, and critical infrastructure. Hurricanes, like those seen in 1938 and 1954, are relatively infrequent, but have the potential to cause extensive damage. The compounding effects of ongoing climate change having the potential to impact their intensity and impact on the community.

## <span id="page-32-1"></span>**2022 Hazard Mitigation and Flood Management Plan**

The 2022 Hazard Mitigation and Flood Management Plan for the Town of Barrington provided for an assessment of the hazards identified in the Rhode Island State Hazard Mitigation Plan (RI-SHMP) that are considered as having a greater likelihood of occurring or having a greater impact on the Town should they occur. The threats that are assess in the 2022 Barrington Hazard Mitigation and Flood Management Plan are:

- Flood-Related Hazards
- Wind-Related Hazards
- Winter-Related Hazards
- Infectious Disease-Related Hazards
- Other Hazards (Earthquakes, Extreme Heat, Drought, Brush Fires)

The 2022 plan also provides an analysis as to how climate change and ongoing sea level rise impact and exacerbates the risks of many of these hazards. The plan generally found that flood, wind, and winter-related hazards have the greatest change of occurrence and high level of potential impact on the community, which is relatively consistent with the NRI data analysis through FEMA. More information, including the analysis and mitigation strategies, can be found in the final report located here[:https://www.barrington.ri.gov/DocumentCenter/View/1193/2022-Barrington-RI-Hazard-](https://www.barrington.ri.gov/DocumentCenter/View/1193/2022-Barrington-RI-Hazard-Mitigation--Flood-Management-Plan?bidId=)[Mitigation--Flood-Management-Plan?bidId=](https://www.barrington.ri.gov/DocumentCenter/View/1193/2022-Barrington-RI-Hazard-Mitigation--Flood-Management-Plan?bidId=)

# **Recent and Ongoing Planning for Barrington**

Over the past decade, the Town has invested in continued planning for the future of Barrington with efforts focused on advancing transportation and mobility, hazard mitigation and climate action, and more. Along with this Comprehensive Plan for Barrington 2050, recent and current ongoing efforts have gathered additional data and gathered community input to guide improvements and investments that meet Town goals. The following plans are reflected in the development of this Comprehensive Plan and the goals it sets forward for the future. These also serve as further resources for growth and preservation.

## **Route 114 Corridor Study for Bristol County**

Resilient 114, a report outlining the characteristics, geography, issues, opportunities, and goals for Route 114 and assesses the vulnerability of the Route 115 corridor through Barrington, Bristol, and Warren. Resilient 114 started in the fall of 2023 and is expected to be completed in the winter of 2025. The Resilient 114 team will develop an inventory of all road segments that are vulnerable to flooding, categorize assets by vulnerability and risks impacted from various results of climate change, identify risks to public safety and services by analyzing evacuation routes, and have an estimated value of at-risk assets by time horizon. Additionally, a vulnerability assessment and alternative routes analysis are currently developing. Strategies to improve resilience within the corridor and the final Resilient Route 114 Plan are yet to be completed.

#### **Complete Streets Plan (2022)**

The Barrington Complete Streets Plan, approved in October 2022, outlines a comprehensive strategy to enhance the town's transportation infrastructure, focusing on safety and accessibility for all users, including pedestrians, bicyclists, motorists, and transit riders. The plan includes policies for new and existing road projects, prioritizes multimodal transportation, and aims to improve connectivity and safety through infrastructure upgrades such as sidewalks, bike lanes, and crosswalks. It also addresses critical issues like pedestrian and bicycle safety, gaps in the sidewalk system, and limited bike facilities. The plan emphasizes the importance of community input and aims to secure funding for long-term investments to create a balanced, multi-modal transportation system that enhances the quality of life in Barrington.

## **Proposed Bikeways (2015)**

The proposed bikeways identified in the 2015 Comprehensive Plan update focused on providing connections from several of the Town to the E Bay Bike Path and other general bikeways throughout Barrington. Many of the proposed bikeways from the 2015 plan have not yet been completed but can serve as a guide for the planning initiatives in the next Comprehensive Plan.

# **Ready and Resilient Barrington Climate Action Plan**

The Ready & Resilient Barrington Climate Action Plan outlines the Town's comprehensive strategy to address climate change by reducing greenhouse gas emissions and enhancing community resilience. The plan, developed with extensive community input, aims to achieve net zero emissions by 2050 and support the State's transition to 100% renewable energy by 2033. Key focus areas include community health & resilience, energy & buildings, infrastructure, transportation, & land use, natural resources, and operations & waste. The plan emphasizes community health, equity, and preparedness for climate impacts, with specific actions and goals to ensure a sustainable and resilient future for all residents.

## **Hazard Mitigation and Flood Management Plan (2022)**

Barrington is a low-lying community with about 20 miles of shoreline. All locations in town are within 2 miles of the coast with 36% of town located within the 100-yr flood zone, 6% in velocity. Narragansett Bay creates a funnel effect producing high storm surges. Up to 6.5 ft of sea-level rise is projected by 2100. The likelihood of continued coastal erosion is high and will be accelerated with sea level rise and more frequent and intense storms. The Town is in good standing with NFIP not a CRS community (applied in March 2021 – on hold until after FEMA Community Assistance Visit can be scheduled) but the last plan update integrated the 10-step planning process for CRS Activity 510. Several of the town's critical facilities are in areas at risk to storm surge and flood prone areas. As identified in the report, it is important to the town to have a comprehensive and aggressive mitigation strategy.

## **Bay Spring Resilience Plan (2020)**

The Bay Spring Resilience Plan for Barrington aims to enhance the Bay Spring neighborhood's resilience to climate events and sea level rise. The plan included community engagement through workshops to identify and prioritize projects such as improving the neighborhood's perimeter, managing stormwater, building overall resiliency, improving power supply, evacuation preparedness, and post-event recovery. Key projects include shoreline protection at Latham Park, erosion protection at Allin's Cove, and stormwater management at various road ends. The plan also emphasizes emergency preparedness, creating a resilient code overlay district, and post-event recovery strategies. The Town will continue seek funding and facilitate further planning and implementation of these projects to ensure a more resilient Bay Spring neighborhood.

## **Barrington Resilient Future Resolution (2021)**

On February 1, 2021, Barrington adopted a resolution to support Rhode Island's goal of achieving 100% renewable energy by 2030, as mandated by Governor Gina Raimondo. The resolution emphasizes the need for collaborative efforts among government, citizens, and the private sector to combat climate change and its effects. The Town Council intends to prioritize green power and clean fuels, explore community choice electric aggregation programs, install zero carbon energy systems on public properties, phase in green building codes, and update the Town's Strategic Energy Plan. These actions aim to reduce Barrington's carbon footprint, enhance energy efficiency, and foster a sustainable and resilient community.
