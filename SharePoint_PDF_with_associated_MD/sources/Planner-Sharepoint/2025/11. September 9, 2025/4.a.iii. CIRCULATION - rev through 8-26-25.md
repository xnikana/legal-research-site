**[OVERVIEW]{.underline}**

Barrington's dependence on the automobile is evident in the driveway of
a typical home. More than 98 percent of households in town own at least
one vehicle, and three out of four own at least two.[^1] In this
primarily single-family residential suburban community, the personal
vehicle will remain a vital part of daily life.

In recent years, however, Barrington has taken steps to create a more
balanced circulation system by planning and building a connected network
of sidewalks, bike lanes, and multiuse paths. These multimodal features,
along with access to transit, produce significant quality of life
benefits that include:

-   Increased access for all people of all ages to community
    destinations, including schools, parks, open spaces, the library,
    restaurants, shops, etc.

-   Creation of a sense of responsibility and independence and
    opportunities for social interaction for children who are able to
    safely walk and bike to school.[^2]

-   Reduction of the town's carbon footprint through fewer vehicle trips
    and less traffic.

-   Promotion of healthier lifestyles by creating more opportunities for
    physical activity.

Community support for improving connectivity in town for pedestrians and
cyclists is substantial. For instance, a survey completed for the

another 3.6 percent carpooling (see **Table C2**). Just 1.5 percent
regularly use public transportation based on 2023 estimates, while 1.5
percent of residents walk to work, and 0.5 percent ride their bike. The
mean commute time to work has grown by about 4.5 minutes since 2000, to
28.1 minutes as of 2023, according to Census Bureau estimates. Of the
estimated 8,296 residents in the workforce, 33.2 percent work in
Barrington, Warren, or Bristol (2023 estimate). About 1,319 (15.9
percent) work outside Rhode Island---a number influenced by Barrington's
proximity to Massachusetts.

Barrington residents are working from home at an increasing rate due to
evolving workplace telecommuting policies, a trend that accelerated in
response to the COVID-19 pandemic. Based on the American Community
Survey (ACS) 5-year estimate, about 19.4 percent (1,258 people) of
Barrington residents in the labor force worked from home in 2023 -- much
higher than in 2000 when census data estimated just 4.5 percent of the
working population worked from home. As a result of this shift, there
are fewer commuters on the road from Barrington (see Table C2). Driving
alone was down 8 percent, carpooling was down 42.9 percent, and taking

[Transportation Infrastructure]{.underline}

*Roads*

Barrington has approximately 110 miles of State and Town-maintained
roadways. Barrington\'s major artery is County Road (Route 114/103), the
only direct route connecting Barrington with the neighboring community
of Warren, with bridge crossings at the Barrington River and the
Palmer/Warren River. Local roads, which account for about 75 percent of
the total road length, provide access within neighborhoods and
districts. These streets generally lack sidewalks and have low vehicular
traffic volumes. Critical transportation corridors that are priority
multimodal roadways are classified as arterials and collectors (**see
Table \_\_\_).**

Private roads represent a small fraction of the street network. The most
significant are Mallard Cove Way off New Meadow Road and Woodhaven Road
off Nayatt Road. The remaining private streets are short dead-end roads
serving up to two lots.

**Recent & Planned Roadway Improvements**

Recent roadway projects include:

-   Maple Avenue resurfacing and sidewalk project. This work was
    completed in segments -- in 2019 from County Road to West Street,
    2021 from Middle Highway to Walter Street, and 2025 from Walter
    Street to West Street. A State Municipal Roads and Bridges program
    grant helped cover a portion of the cost. The Town used funding from
    a streets and drainage bond for the balance of the repaving cost. As
    part of the 2025 project, the Town used local funds reserved for
    sidewalks and bicycle facility improvements to reconstruct the
    sidewalk on the south side of Maple Avenue from Prince's Hill Avenue
    to Walter Street and added crosswalks with ADA ramps.

-   Town Center Streetscape Project. In 2017 to 2018, the Town built new
    and rebuilt existing sidewalks and repaved roads utilizing funds
    from a streetscape improvement bond, including Wood Avenue, Maple
    Avenue (County Road to West Street), West Street, and one block of
    Cottage Lane.

-   Lincoln-Middle Highway intersection project. The State in 2023 added
    a dedicated left-turn lane on Lincoln Avenue for eastbound vehicles
    turning onto Middle Highway, replaced the traffic and pedestrian
    crossing signals, added crosswalks with ADA ramps, and rebuilt the
    sidewalk on the north side of Lincoln from Middle Highway to Peck
    Avenue. The project was initiated to improve safety and relieve
    traffic congestion around the Middle School.

-   County Road, from the Town line at Riverside to Wampanoag Trail
    (2024-25). As part of a State Transportation Improvement Program
    project, the State completed the resurfacing of County Road and
    added new sidewalks (on the south side from east of the Washington
    Road intersection to the County Road-Wampanoag Trail merge, and on
    the north side from east of the "traffic circle" near Riverside to
    Middle Highway).

-   Route 114 - Wampanoag Trail in Barrington / East Shore Expressway in
    East Providence. The State completed the resurfacing project in
    2022, which also included reconstruction of existing sidewalks.

-   Reconstruction of Nayatt Road, from Rumstick Road to Washington
    Road. The State in 2019 completed the reconstruction of 1.8 miles of
    Nayatt Road from Washington Road to Rumstick Road. The project
    included reconstruction of sidewalks on the north side of Nayatt
    Road from Rumstick to Broadview Drive, new crosswalks, and ADA
    ramps.

*Bridges*

The State owns and maintains the three roadway bridges in town: Veterans
Memorial Bridge ("White Church Bridge" or "Central Bridge"), the Lance
Corporal Victor Patrick Andreozzi and Vietnam Veterans Bridge
(Barrington River Bridge) and Warren River Bridge, all replaced since
2010. The bridges all have sidewalks on both sides, but no provisions
for bicycles.

*Bikeway System*

Constructed on the bed of a deactivated rail line as an alternative
transportation system, the East Bay Bicycle Path ties together Bristol,
Warren, Barrington and East Providence, as well as Providence via the
I-195 bridge. Many types of users frequent the bike path \-- walkers,
joggers, bicyclists and others -- including commuters. The bike trail is
owned by RIDOT and maintained by the RI Department of Environmental
Management.

In Barrington, the Bike Path is centrally located (see Map C-1) and
directly connects local neighborhoods with a range of destinations
including Haines State Park, Bicknell Park, the Bayside Y and Veterans
Park, and the downtown Barrington and Bay Spring Village areas.[^7]

*Public Transportation*

The RI Public Transit Authority (RIPTA) provides bus service between
Providence and Newport via Route 114/County Road in Barrington. There
are about 14 bus stops located between Massasoit Avenue and the Warren
River Bridge, and additional stops along Route 114 north of Massasoit
(although the presence of guardrails presents obstacles for riders
crossing the busy roadway.) Bus shelters are located at the East Bay
Mental Health Center, the Barrington Congregational Church, just north
of Town Hall, and Police Cove. Route 61X provides additional daily bus
trips.

Up until 2013, RIPTA Route 32 provided West Barrington with twice daily
trips to Providence via Riverside. The discontinued route made a loop
along Narragansett Avenue, Bay Spring Avenue, and Washington Road,
serving several senior housing developments and other densely developed
neighborhoods. RIPTA eliminated the route due to low ridership. Today,
the closest bus stop for residents in West Barrington is at the Shaw's
in Riverside (RIPTA Route 33).

There are two park-and-ride commuter lots in Barrington: a 100-space lot
at the Barrington Congregational Church and a 36-space lot at Police
Cove Park by the Barrington River bridge. The Town owns the Police Cove
lot, while the church allows use of their privately-owned parking under
a lease agreement with the RI Department of Transportation. The Police
Cove location has a bus pull-out to allow traffic on Route 114 to pass
stopping buses.

Senior Services also offers transportation to residents aged 60 and
above. This bus is free and available to local seniors on weekdays for
transportation to and from the Peck Center for Adult Enrichment, weekly
shopping trips, and local appointments when possible. Reservations must
be made in advance.

Bus system improvements within Barrington since 2015 include expansion
of the Police Cove Park parking lot/park and ride lot from 20 to 36
spaces, and reconstruction of the bus shelter on County Road at
Barrington Congregational Church.

The vast majority (over 90%) of Barrington residents use private
vehicles to access services and travel within and outside of the Town.
However, there are populations within Barrington that cannot operate
and/or do not have access to a private vehicle and require access to
public transportation. While 57% of respondents to the comprehensive
planning community survey said nothing would increase their ridership on
the RIPTA buses, other respondents would be more likely to ride with the
addition of new stop locations, more frequent service, and alternative
routes.

**[Plans, Projects, and Funding]{.underline}**

Over the past 10 years, Barrington has taken concrete steps toward
creating a multimodal circulation system, including:

-   Adoption of the Barrington Complete Streets Policy (2019),

*Complete Streets and the Bicycle and Pedestrian Advisory Committee*

Barrington's 2015 Comprehensive Plan's called for creation of a robust
multimodal network "that safely accommodates automobile, bicycle and
pedestrian activity within critical corridors for all users of all ages
and abilities, with an emphasis on improving routes in the vicinity of
schools, commercial and mixed-use areas, major roadways, and parks and
recreational areas." In response, in 2019 Barrington adopted a Complete
Streets policy, which set forth four primary objectives:

1)  Remove or reduce barriers that limit access to destinations;

2)  Connect destinations, including transit stops shopping centers, and
    > playgrounds and pedestrian oriented destinations;

3)  Create safe routes to schools; and

4)  Promote linkages to trail network, Town Beach, access points to the
    > shore.

The Town's subsequent Complete Streets planning process proceeded in two
phases. First, in 2022, the Town developed the Barrington Complete
Streets Plan, establishing goals and priorities consistent with the 2019
policy, as well as concepts for several high-priority transit corridors.
The Plan also called for the formation of a Bicycle and Pedestrian
Advisory Commission (BPAC) to build momentum and community support for
Complete Streets projects. The BPAC was established in 2023 and is
charged with evaluating projects for consistency with the Town's
Complete Streets Plan, making recommendations on complete streets
elements in land development projects and subdivisions proposals before
the Planning Board, and providing opportunities for community input.

The Barrington Complete Streets Plan set the stage for the
implementation phase of the initiative. The Complete Streets
Implementation Plan, adopted in April 2025, was developed with input
from the Town and stakeholders, including the BPAC. The Plan includes:

-   Identification of gaps in the bicycle and pedestrian route networks
    and "community activity nodes" that generate high levels of
    bike/pedestrian users;

-   Prioritization of projects that have maximum benefits for the
    largest number of facility users;

-   An analysis of existing safety concerns, which in turn informs
    recommendations for facility and intersection upgrades for risk
    reduction;

-   Strategies to advocate for inclusion of complete street design
    objectives in projects involving State roadways; and

-   Concepts for the five priority projects for implementation in the
    short term.

*Safe Streets for All Safety Action Plan*

The Barrington Safe Streets for All Safety Action Plan was completed in
2025, funded through a "Safe Streets and Roads for All" grant from the
U.S. Department of Transportation awarded to RIPTA. The grant program
supported data-driven planning initiatives to prevent roadway fatalities
and serious injuries at the state level and in 31 participating
municipalities. The Safety Action Plan commits Barrington to reduce
fatal and serious injuries on roadways under the Town's jurisdiction by
half by 2030 and completely by 2035. The Town will, likewise, partner
with RIDOT to achieve the same goals on roadways under State
jurisdiction by 2035 and 2040, respectively.

Components of the plan include data analysis, public engagement,
identification of high-risk areas, safety-improvement recommendations,
and a commitment to progress and transparency. The Plan puts Barrington
in a position to secure further federal implementation funding, if
available, and identifies critical safety needs that can inform other
local, regional, and state planning initiatives.

1.  **Bicycle and Pedestrian Safety**

Resident surveys show that even if a very short section of bike or
pedestrian facility (like a sidewalk or wide shoulder for cycling as
short as 20' feet) is missing along a preferred path, then potential
walkers and cyclists will instead opt for another way to get to their
destination (usually by car) out of fear that they will not be safe.

Barrington's existing roadway network is designed primarily for vehicles
and only a limited number of areas are served by dedicated pedestrian
and cycling infrastructure. This is a substantial but surmountable
challenge. Removing or reducing the gaps and barriers that limit both
neighborhood and townwide travel for nonmotorized road users is the most
critical step in creating a safe and accessible multi-modal network.
Examples of these gaps and barriers include missing sidewalks and
inadequate bicycle facilities, especially on major connectors linking
key community destinations.

Many neighborhoods in Barrington are considered by residents to be safe
for cyclists and pedestrians because of their low vehicle speed and low
traffic volumes. Within these (primarily residential) areas, cyclists
and pedestrians can move with relative ease and safety on existing
streets and, where available, sidewalks. This is a typical 20th century
development pattern. Unfortunately, these neighborhoods are often
isolated from important community destinations, such as schools,
recreational facilities, shopping areas, and other residential
neighborhoods by dangerous collector roads and arterials that have
minimal or nonexistent separated facilities for cyclists and
pedestrians.

Barrington has a significant bike facility that cuts through the center
of town -- a 3.5-mile section of the East Bay Bike Path. Other than the
Bike Path, the Town has few formal off-street bike routes for getting
around town by bike or on foot. These consist of paths that pass through
Town parks or open space, connecting one part of town with another.

The State relies almost entirely on the East Bay Bike Path to serve as
the bicycle infrastructure for Barrington (and the entire East Bay
region) and has constructed very little additional infrastructure for
non-vehicular users. Exceptions to this rule are the areas where the
Town has advocated or secured funding for sidewalks around schools.
Missing, undersized, and disconnected infrastructure results in high
levels of pedestrian and cyclist stress on State roadways, many of which
serve as key connectors within the Town. Stress is highest on roadways
with narrow or nonexistent shoulders. This is exacerbated on roadways
with high vehicle volumes and high vehicle speeds. [The Town's Complete
Streets plan identified several areas of acute concern, including:
County Road/RI 114; Washington Road; Federal Road; Maple Avenue; Middle
Street ; Rumstick Road; Nayatt Road; New Meadow Road; Sowams Road; and
Massasoit Avenue.]{.mark}

2.  **Sidewalk System Improvement and Maintenance**

There remain significant gaps in the town's sidewalk system affecting
the community's desire to provide an attractive, safe pedestrian
environment and to encourage walking as an alternative mode of local
transportation. **Map C-4** shows existing locations prioritized for

adjacent to commercial zones, and the Zion Bible Institute site (for
further discussion, see **Housing**). Planned improvements along
Massasoit, Federal, Primrose, and Middle Highway are currently in the

Sufficient funding to support regular maintenance, repair, and snow
removal should also be included in the Public Works Department's
Operating Budget to keep sidewalks in good condition year-round. Spot
repairs of sidewalks are essential to minimize hazards.

Once sidewalks have been improved and/or installed at identified high
priority locations, and ongoing maintenance needs are met, the Town may
wish to install additional sidewalks. A long-term goal would be to
install new sidewalks along all major roadways in Town.

Accessibility and ADA compliance should be considered in scoping future
sidewalk projects. The Town has added handicap warning devices at
intersections and encouraged private developers to include off-site
improvements in the course of their projects. For example, the Planning
Board required the installation of handicap ramps at the northwest
corner of Washington Road and Bay Spring Avenue as a condition of
approval for the conversion of 187 Washington Road into four
apartments. The Town should continue to use its leverage to encourage
developers to consider off-site improvements as part of the planning
process.

4.  **Need for Consistent Funding**

Sixty-three percent (63%) of locations targeted for improvement in
Barrington\'s Complete Streets Implementation Plan are state roads (the
other 37% are town roadways or within town parks.) Traditionally,
improvements to State roads are funded through the State Transportation
Improvement Program (STIP). The STIP is a list of transportation
projects the State intends to implement using United States Department
of Transportation funds. Projects are nominated by municipalities and
State agencies. It falls to the municipality to make the case that
proposed projects should be prioritized to achieve state transportation
objectives. Barrington will need to organize Town resources and create
coalitions with community partners to advocate for STIP funding.

The Town can undertake improvements on its own roadways, but the size
and scale of those projects is limited by funding and staff resources.
Barrington can accelerate progress by including priority projects in the
town's Municipal Capital Improvement Plan (CIP). The Barrington
Department of Public Works has an annual plan for roadway paving
projects. It would be possible to add a sidewalk fund to the department
budget that could be used to implement critical sidewalk projects. The
town can also pursue funding through grants and local bonds. Inclusion
of line item for "Complete Streets Infrastructure" in the Capital
Projects section of the town's annual budget could unlock matching funds
for grant funded projects.

5.  **Access to** **Public Transit and Commuter Parking Options**

Barrington saw a reduction in bus service with the removal of Route 32
from West Barrington in 2013. Reinstatement of bus service to
higher-density areas in West Barrington, including senior housing, is
desirable. Extension of RIPTA's Route 33 from the shopping center on
Willett Avenue, down Washington Road or Middle Highway to Maple Avenue,
could connect the west side of town to the Village Center.

If ridership on RIPTA's Route 60 line continues to grow, it will
increase pressure to improve commuter facilities in Barrington,
particularly since many residents have access to free bus passes through
work. This is apparent due to the heavy usage of the White Church park
and ride lot (see Figure 1), and the high volumes of traffic along
County Road during commuting hours.

Shared commuter lots or other publicly owned lots could be promoted for
both Barrington town center shoppers and commuters. Improvements such as
new sidewalks and crosswalks on the route to and from the bus stops and
shelters may be needed to encourage usage of these lots. Agreements with
local churches for weekday use is another option.

Construction of secure bicycle storage facilities and the provision of
bicycle facilities, such as on-street bike lanes, would also improve
access to public transit. The Town also could encourage the provision of
amenities for bus riders as part of new commercial development projects
on the County Road bus line. For example, a new commercial building
built near the street could provide an extra-wide sidewalk with a
sitting wall or bench underneath an overhang/awning. Another option to
alleviate traffic as well as support commuters, would be to consolidate
bus stops along Route 114 to provide fewer, more visible and comfortable
stops, with shelters and seating.

**Goal C-3: Improve access to transit to promote increased use of RI
Public Transportation Authority bus routes.**

Policy C-3.1: Take local action to support and require the installation
of facilities that make the use of public bus transportation more
attractive and convenient and encourage greater utilization of RIPTA
buses at local stops.

**Actions**

1.  Look for opportunities to accommodate additional park and ride
    capacity or for joint utilization of parking lots located near bus
    stops preferably focusing on Route 114 (such as commercial and
    church lots in the vicinity of County Road).

2.  Encourage RIPTA to expand Riverside bus route into Barrington to
    provide service to the former Zion Bible Institute campus in the
    event the property is redeveloped as housing.

3.  Request State to improve pedestrian infrastructure (sidewalks,
    crosswalks) to provide safe access to bus stops on Route 114 north
    of White Church bridge.

4.  Meet with RIPTA to identify low usage bus stops on County Road for
    consolidation, and make the consolidated stops more prominent with
    shelters, in order to improve the efficiency of bus service and help
    alleviate traffic congestion due to stopped buses.

5.  Prioritize access to transit

6.  Assess proximity of pedestrian and bicyclist crashes to transit
    stops

7.  Implement pedestrian safety improvements for access to transit stops

8.  Connect transit and housing via multimodal infrastructure

9.  Encourage State to identify new locations for bus pull-off along
    County Road and to expand park-ride facilities throughout East Bay.

**Goal C-4: Relieve traffic congestion on County Road (Rte. 114/103)
without compromising the safety of pedestrians and bicyclists.**

Objective C-4.1: Enhance the vitality and attractiveness of downtown
Barrington as a place to visit, shop and do business.

Policy C-4.1: Improve traffic flow on Route 114/103 through physical,
technological, and traffic management improvements.

**Actions**

1.  Request the State to:

    a.  Improve timing of traffic signals and pedestrian crossing
        signals on County Road at key intersections, including at the
        East Bay Bike Path.

    b.  Provide a left-turn lane onto Lincoln Avenue from Route 114
        northbound.

    c.  Evaluate and implement measures to improve safety for vehicles
        turning onto County Road from New Meadow Road, Sowams Road, and
        other side streets in between.

2.  Develop zoning and land development standards requiring internal
    connections between shopping areas to minimize curb cuts when
    property is redeveloped.

3.  Limit new curb cuts from commercial properties that front on County
    Road; reduce existing curb cuts where feasible as part of the County
    Road reconstruction Transportation Improvement Program project.

4.  Revise zoning to ban new drive-throughs.
