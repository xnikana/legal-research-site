**a. Discussion: (Introduction Only) Proposed amendments to the Land Development & Subdivision Regulations (Chapter 200 of the Town Code)**

Planner Durfee explained that the agenda item was intended as an introduction to the potential amendments. He stated that the regulations had been provided to the solicitor, but no formal comments had been received yet. The proposal was presented as a conceptual discussion, including some issues raised during Planning Board reviews and discussions with the Town Manager.

Planner Durfee suggested defining a "substantial municipal impact" with different thresholds that could trigger additional review. He emphasized that this impact could be positive or adverse and intended to promote further discussion and possible review by the Planning Board.

The proposed regulations aimed to support goals aligned with the Comprehensive Plan, such as promoting compact, walkable development, improving public infrastructure, and emphasizing environmental sensitivity and energy efficiency. He detailed categories including environmental protection, traffic and transportation, infrastructure, community character, public engagement, interagency coordination, and other matters identified by the Planning Board or peer review contractors. He also included sub-issues for each category.

Planner Durfee acknowledged that some ideas, like design control issues, had been overlooked initially but could be integrated later. Additionally, he mentioned including provisions related to electric vehicle infrastructure, such as EV chargers and readiness for developments.

Member Bacher inquired whether the town had statutory authority for these amendments. Planner Durfee responded that he had preliminary discussions but had not confirmed whether a definition of "substantial municipal impact" was permissible under current law. If not, he would revise the proposal to fit within legal bounds.

Member Bacher expressed support for the concept but emphasized the need to coordinate existing regulations to avoid conflicting requirements. Planner Durfee noted that some revisions were already underway through the Town Council, with new amendments taking effect, and additional housekeeping adjustments might be necessary. He suggested that future amendments could be integrated once the Comprehensive Plan process was completed.

Solicitor Teitz added that recent legislative changes had shifted many approvals to an administrative process, removing some from the Planning Board's purview. He explained that thresholds for minor development had increased, allowing conforming subdivisions to be approved administratively, often without Planning Board review. He clarified that these changes were based on state law, which superseded local regulations, and emphasized the importance of understanding the effective dates of new laws, which could be immediate upon passage or veto.

Member Bacher expressed concern about the confusion caused when state law and local regulations conflict, noting the potential for uncertainty among Planning Board members. Solicitor Teitz acknowledged this complexity, explaining the legislative process and the timing of new laws' effective dates, which could be immediate following gubernatorial approval.

Chair Phipps summarized that the amendments would be finalized with legal review and presented at future meetings.

### **b. Discussion and Action: Long Range Transportation Plan (LRTP)**

Planner Durfee explained that the packet included the main body of the plan, which was quite substantial, along with approximately 12 appendices. He emphasized that the main body provided an overview of the plan's overall thrust. He described the Long Range Transportation Plan as a mandatory document developed by RIDOT and the Office of Statewide Planning, serving as a 20 year vision for transportation issues across the state. It functions as the backdrop for the Transportation Improvement Program (TIP). He pointed out that the TIP update was happening concurrently, which was unusual because typically the long-range plan is completed first to inform the TIP. He explained that the state was working to keep these synchronized to meet federal requirements, as the plan is necessary for federal funding eligibility.

Planner Durfee also shared that, when adopted, the plan would also be used by the state to assess the conformance of local comprehensive plans. He advised that the Board pay attention to the plan's goals, policies, and directions, as they could influence Barrington's planning efforts.

Planner Durfee encouraged the Board members to review the document and provide any comments, either collectively or individually, through the online comment form. He relayed that public comment had to be in before the mid-July deadline.

Chair Phipps suggested that if the comments could be submitted after the July 31st training meeting, it might be beneficial to have more time for review. Planner Durfee agreed that some opportunities might exist after the deadline, but there were no guarantees.

Member Bacher stated that he had reviewed it and found some positive aspects, such as current strategies and regional planning efforts. However, he expressed disappointment that Barrington's specific needs were largely overlooked. He noted that the plan grouped Bristol County with Newport and Aquidneck Island, providing only a brief mention of Bristol, and focused heavily on Aquidneck. He reviewed the appendix listing projects and found no planned work in Barrington, and only two in the vincinity on Metacom Avenue in Warren and another near Riverside. Member Bacher also expressed frustration that Barrington's planning efforts, including the Complete Streets program, were not reflected in the plan, and questioned whether the planned sidewalk improvements on Massasoit would be eliminated if they did not appear in the new plan.

Planner Durfee responded that he could not answer definitively but reassured that many Barrington projects remain in the state's STIP, including significant investments in County Road, which had \$20 million allocated. He explained that the STIP and the Long-Range Plan are related but not fully synchronized, as updates are handled through addenda over time.

Member Rua inquired whether the current plan and the funded projects were separate from future projects, and Planner Durfee clarified that the LRTP is meant to serve as a broad vision, while the STIP is an implementation tool. He described how the STIP is periodically updated to reflect funding and project priorities, including recent adjustments due to the Washington Bridge project.

Chair Phipps asked whether it would be appropriate for the Barrington Planning Board to submit comments about the plan's omissions, especially regarding the sidewalk projects on County Road and Massasoit Avenue. She considered whether emphasizing that these projects are essential for implementing the town's Comprehensive Plan. The consensus was that it would be appropriate to advocate for the continuation and consideration of those projects.

Planner Durfee agreed, noting that the Board's comments could include a call for better coordination between RIDOT and the Office of Statewide Planning to improve communication and project prioritization.

Alternate Member Hermann commented on the plan's grouping of East Bay regions and noted the lack of discussion regarding cross-Bay connections, such as ferries or other transit options. She suggested that the perception was that the East Bay could manage itself without considering broader connections.

Planner Durfee recounted past efforts, including a ferry trial when the Washington Bridge was initially closed, which was ultimately deemed unfeasible due to high costs and logistical challenges. Solicitor Teitz added that ferry studies had been conducted over the past 20 years, but past proposals were found to be impractical because of slow travel times and infrastructure constraints, such as the hurricane barrier and low bridges.

The discussion concluded with general agreement that, while the plan had shortcomings, ongoing updates and coordination efforts could eventually address some of these issues. The Board was encouraged to submit comments to influence future planning.

### **ii. Economic Development**

Chair Phipps asked Member Rua if he would lead the discussion on economic development.

Member Rua began by noting that he had recently received input from the Economic Development Commission, though it was somewhat scattered, and he had only just gotten the comments the day before. He highlighted their notable suggestions.

The first suggestion was about wanting to create a connection from Wood Street to the Shaws parking lot. Member Rua clarified that the EDC favored a road connector over just a pedestrian connector. Member Rua also explained it would need to cross the bike path, creating an additional crossing for vehicles, which could be problematic. Chair Phipps asked if the town wanted that road, and Member Rua responded that the EDC supported it but that it probably conflicted with the Complete Streets Plan, making it unlikely the state would approve it. Member Bacher recalled that there was an old plan from 2016 about this connector, and he mentioned that the idea had been around for about 20 years.

Member Rua also explained the EDC's comments about including discussions about improving connections between existing parking areas to facilitate easier access to multiple destinations without returning to the car.

The discussion then moved to the parking study. Member Rua noted that there had been ongoing discussions about conducting a parking study, but there was agreement that the language about doing another study should be removed. Instead, they should focus on implementing some of the actions from the previous study, especially in the village district, since conditions had not changed much and new parking needs had not emerged.

Member Rua explained that the previous parking study encouraged shared parking among businesses to reduce parking requirements and create a "park once" environment. He noted that Barrington had already removed minimum parking requirements from its zoning regulations and that sharing parking lots might be difficult with local businesses.

The Board questioned where the parking shortage was, as there is not any trouble finding parking in Barrington. Member Bacher recalled that discussions focused more on improving connections between parking lots so people could visit multiple businesses without driving back and forth. Member Rua agreed and suggested removing the "do a study" language and concentrating on actions aligned with the Complete Streets Plan.

The Board discussed the possibility of widening sidewalks along County Road to create more walkable paths, possibly on private property. There was uncertainty about the extent of the right-ofway beyond the sidewalk, especially in front of certain buildings. Chair Phipps suggested that public-private partnerships could help enhance pedestrian infrastructure. Member Rua added some minor comments, including a suggestion to promote locally owned businesses without encouraging large chains like Chipotle. He also mentioned fostering partnerships with the Barrington Business Association to improve relationships between the town and local businesses, as there was a perception that Barrington was not very business-friendly.

Planner Durfee noted that Deborah, who worked as a town liaison to the business community, could potentially assist in economic development efforts. He emphasized the importance of collaboration among organizations like the East Bay Chamber of Commerce and the Barrington Business Association.

Member Rua explained that efforts to involve the Chamber of Commerce had been challenging, citing past events like the East Bay Economic Development Forum, which was organized and funded by the BBCA. He suggested that stronger organizational collaboration could help promote downtown and business district initiatives.

Planner Durfee added that Warren's Walkabout program was a good example of community and arts engagement, with local businesses and artisans involved in public art projects and festivals. He encouraged Barrington to identify its unique niches and create events or festivals that highlighted local strengths, similar to Warren's approach. He stressed the importance of planning, funding, and organizational effort, suggesting that a small dedicated team or grant funding could help develop such initiatives.

Alternate Member Hermann shared her plans to create detailed maps of the town, starting with natural features like wetlands and progressing to buildings and roads. She explained that these maps would help her better understand the town's layout and identify potential economic hubs or open space linkages. Member Rua suggested she reach out to the BPAC, as they had developed detailed maps and GIS data.

The discussion concluded with Member Rua noting that he would send the current draft of the Economic Development chapter with comments to Member Kilmartin and Town Manager Hervey for review.

Member Simms asked if the sale was also intended to raise capital. Solicitor Teitz confirmed that was the intent, but the timing—before or after demolition—was flexible, depending on the financial strategy.

Chair Phipps asked if the sale would be conducted via a formal RFP process. Solicitor Teitz explained that they would use a sealed bid process rather than listing the lots on the open market, as the law governing sales favored sealed bids.

Member Simms asked if the town would be obligated to accept the highest bid. Solicitor Teitz clarified that the town would issue a request for proposals, allowing for contingencies and negotiations, and was not bound to accept the highest bid outright.

Planner Durfee added that the sale process was still in the planning stage, with no formal RFP issued yet, and that the Council had not specified whether sale proceeds would be used for demolition or other purposes. Chair Phipps mentioned that the Council had discussed selling the lot to fund demolition.

Planner Durfee noted that the town had recently been notified of an additional round of grants from Rhode Island Housing, including a project to develop zoning and subdivision regulations that support affordable housing. He explained that he had contacted four pre-qualified consulting firms to prepare proposals for this project, which would include public engagement and staff interfacing. The goal was to develop recommendations for regulations that foster innovative, affordable owneroccupied and rental housing.

Herb added that the upcoming focus was on preparing for future infill projects and the potential for new affordable housing developments, such as a proposed 18-unit project on Maple Avenue. He emphasized the importance of being prepared and having regulations that can accommodate such projects.

Member Bacher mentioned a recent presentation by a firm in Boston specializing in zoning modifications to achieve specific housing outcomes, which could be a useful resource. Planner Durfee expressed interest in learning more about that firm.

Member Rua suggested members go to strongtowns.org for some good articles, videos or content on why Barrington's main street does not foster economic development and Warren's does.
