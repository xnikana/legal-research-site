**HISTORIC & CULTURAL RESOURCES **

**[OVERVIEW]{.underline}**

Expansive views of Hundred Acre Cove. The facades of historic colonial
homes. The white steeple of the Congregational Church. These are the
images that greet visitors entering Barrington on R.I. 114, the main
route into Barrington from the north. The drive into town provides a
picturesque snapshot of just one section of Barrington. There also are
the shorelines, beaches and waters of Narragansett Bay and the
Barrington and Palmer Rivers; the distinct neighborhoods of West
Barrington, Hampden Meadows, Rumstick Road, and Alfred Drown; the
agricultural lands in the northeast corner of town; and numerous
outstanding historic buildings scattered throughout town. It is this
abundance of scenic landscapes and local history that give Barrington
its special distinction, its sense of place. 

More subtle, yet no less significant, are the archaeological sites which
are the record of thousands of years of human activity on these lands.
When Giovanni da Verrazzano sailed up Narragansett Bay in 1524, he was
greeted by the indigenous inhabitants of the area---the Pokanoket
people. A century later, Ousamequin (also known by his title,
Massasoit,) the "Great Sachem" of the Wampanoag confederacy, allied with
settlers in the recently established Plymouth Colony---events which
eventually provided the genesis for America's Thanksgiving traditions.
The lands that are now Barrington were a central part of the Pokanoket
homeland, known as "Sowams." Indigenous history is apparent in the
Town's many Pokanoket toponyms, from Popanomscut in the west to
Chachcust in the east. Long-standing human occupation of the area is
revealed in the discovery of artifacts, burials, and other traces.

Many respondents of the Barrington 2050 survey were unaware of the
Town\'s significant cultural resources and historic sites, such as
Belton Court and the history of the Pokanoket tribe. 58% of respondents
said that the town could do more to promote existing historical and
cultural resources. This deficit represents an opportunity to promote,
and thus preserve, the town's rich history and cultural legacy. \[NOTE:
for a pull quote graphic, use image on page 8 of "Welcoming
Community."\]

**[EXISTING CONDITIONS]{.underline}**

[Historic Sites and Buildings]{.underline}

Barrington has a wealth of historically significant properties (see Map
HCR-X), including the Civic Center Historic District along County Road.
This National Register District includes Town Hall, the Peck Center
(Library/Senior Center), Prince's Hill Cemetery, and Wood Pond. The
Alfred Drown historic district in West Barrington and Jennys Lane
historic district off Rumstick Road are also on the National Register of
Historic Places.

The Allen-West House at 153 George Street has been listed on the
National Register since 2013. The circa 1763 house was once part of a
large farmstead consisting of more than 100 acres. The area around the
house was also the site of the first Baptist meeting house in
America.[^1] This meeting house on Nockum Hill, erected in the 1660s,
does not survive. A stone monument installed in 1905 commemorates the
site's significance in early colonial religious history. More recently,
archeological investigations have confirmed that this area was also the
site of the earliest skirmishes in the conflict between settlers and
Indigenous local peoples known as King Philip's War.

One of Barrington's most iconic structures is the 55,000-square-foot
Belton Court. Located off Middle Highway, the mansion was constructed in
the early part of the 20^th^ century as a private estate. Later, it
housed Barrington College and the Zion Bible Institute. The building was
added to the National Register of Historic Places in 1976.

Belton Court, along with the entire 40-acre campus, was acquired at
auction by a private investor in 2011. The intervening years have seen
several proposals for redevelopment, but none have yet come to fruition.
Due to age and condition, at least some portion of historic Belton Court
may be unsuitable for restoration and re-use. Most of the other
buildings on the greater estate, none historically significant, would be
demolished to make room for the proposed development.

Additional structures listed on the National Register are:

-   The Nayatt Point Lighthouse (listed 1988), a private residence;

-   St. Matthews Episcopal Church on Chapel Road (listed 1991);

-   The former O'Bannon Mill on Bay Spring Road (listed 1996), converted
    to senior housing;

-   The Aborn Jackson House (listed 2008), a private residence.

-   General Thomas Allin House (listed 2024), a private residence

The Town also has several historic cemeteries---including Tyler Point,
Watson, Allin, Forest Chapel, and Prince's Hill. The Barrington Cemetery
Commission has oversight and is responsible for maintenance of these
sites.  

[The Barrington Preservation Society]{.underline}

The Barrington Preservation Society, chartered by the State in 1965, is
the town's principal force for historic preservation.  The Society
occupies space on the ground level of the Town Library. The room houses
the Town Museum, archival storage of artifacts, and town records.   

Through its on-going Plaque Program, the Society has identified
approximately 174 private and public buildings that are at least 100
years in age and retain their architectural integrity. Other notable
initiatives include the installation of interpretive wayside signs along
the Barrington section of the East Bay Bike Path and in the town center,
highlighting local history. Most recently, in 2022, they supported[^2]
placement of a Rhode Island Slave History marker outside the Barrington
Public Library, to raise public awareness of the presence and
contributions of enslaved people in Barrington's early history.

The Society organizes educational events and exhibitions throughout the
year to promote local historical awareness and provides advice to the
town on preservation issues. It will continue to play an active role in
the designation and promotion of additional sites for inclusion on the
National Register.

[Scenic Views ]{.underline}

In January 1990, the RI Department of Environmental Management completed
an inventory of scenic areas throughout the State. Barrington was
included within the Narragansett Bay Area. Three areas in Barrington are
identified in the state inventory, which are also designated on Map
NR-X: 

-   Hundred Acre Cove, as seen primarily from Wampanoag Trail. 

-   An area in the southwest corner of Barrington that includes Echo
    Lake, Mussachuck Creek, and a portion of the Nayatt Point area. 
    This scenic area was described to have an attractive combination of
    open land, woodland and wetlands. 

-   The Smith Cove Marsh. This wetland with varied vegetation
    encompasses all the coastal marshes between Rumstick Point and Adams
    Point. 

Both the Echo Lake and the Smith Cove sites were defined as distinctive
landscape areas, having the highest visual appeal and containing a great
deal of variety in form.  The Hundred Acre Cove site was defined as
noteworthy, an area of lesser but important scenic value. All three
areas are threatened by coastal erosion and sea-level rise.

**[Regulation of Development Impacts ]{.underline} **

The Town has adopted measures to help protect its historic and
cultural resources, including the following: 

*Historic Sites---Review*. The Town does not have any local historic
districts or a historic district commission. The Town has put in place
additional review standards for development projects that involve,
adjoin, or occur within 200 feet of an historic property listed on the
National and State Registers of Historic Places (Sec. 185-160 of the
Zoning Ordinance). These standards include:

1.  *Retention of Original Characteristics*: Preserve the original
    characteristics, materials, and setting of historic structures
    whenever possible.

2.  *Compatibility of New Elements*: Ensure new elements are compatible
    with the original in design, material, color, texture, and other
    visual qualities.

3.  *Facade Proportions*: Maintain the original facade proportions,
    including window and door placement, and roof shape, pitch, and
    detail.

4.  *Exterior Details*: Retain, repair, or replace exterior details such
    as siding, corner boards, cornice, brackets, lintels, and window and
    door styles in kind.

5.  *Additions and New Construction*: Ensure additions or new
    construction are compatible with the design of the original
    structure and/or the style and character of the historic district.

6.  *New Building Compatibility*: New buildings within a historic
    district should match the style, scale, height, facade, and
    materials of the original buildings.

7.  *Setback and Orientation*: Match the setback pattern, building
    orientation, and spacing patterns of new buildings with those of the
    original buildings within the district.

8.  *Landscaping and Site Treatment*: Landscaping and site treatment
    should be compatible with the historic setting.

*Civic Center Historic District.* Projects impacting the Civic Center
Historic District are reviewed by the Town Council on a case-by-case
basis. Projects impacting the exterior of Town Hall or the surrounding
grounds also require the involvement and approval of the Rhode Island
Historical Preservation and Heritage Commission, which holds a historic
preservation easement on the Town Hall and surrounding property. In
2024, the Town constructed a "flagpole plaza" with the approval of the
State Historic Preservation and Heritage Commission, under the terms of
the preservation easement.

*Shore Access.* As in many coastal communities throughout Rhode Island,
there has been increasing debate in recent years about accessibility of
public rights-of-way to the shore. Barrington must balance private
property interests and the need for public safety against the right of
residents under Rhode Island Constitution to access and enjoy the shore.
As a coastal community, such access has always been a central part of
Barrington's history and heritage.

In 2023, the Barrington Harbor Commission released its comprehensive
survey and description of shore access points and public rights-of-way.
The town is currently in the midst of a multi-year project to install
granite markers at all access points to promote public awareness.  

**[ISSUES AND OPPORTUNITIES]{.underline}**

**Sowams National Heritage District** 

English colonists settled in Plymouth in 1620 and shortly thereafter
concluded a treaty with their indigenous neighbors, the Pokanoket
people. The Pokanoket then occupied a region known as "Sowams," which
included portions of modern-day Providence, East Providence, Barrington,
Warren, and Bristol in Rhode Island and Seekonk, Rehoboth, Swansea, and
Somerset in Massachusetts. The alliance between the Pokanoket and the
English settlers endured some 50 years, until the violent rupture of
King Philip's War (1675/76). 2025 marks the 350^th^ anniversary of the
conflict.

In recent years, a number of stakeholders have joined in an effort to
formally establish a regional Sowams Heritage District, with intent to
eventually seek recognition as a National Heritage Area (NHA). NHAs are
living landscapes designated by Congress to honor the important role of
a region in our country's development and to celebrate its contributions
to American history and culture. As currently conceived, a Sowams
National Heritage Area would include a variety of sites across multiple
towns, including open spaces, scenic views, historic sites, and
recreational areas.

The non-profit Sowams Heritage Area has worked since 2017 to lay the
groundwork for national recognition of Sowams as an NHA. The
organization's steering committee includes planning representatives from
four municipalities[^3], not including Barrington. Sowams Heritage Area
aims to raise awareness of the region's history (17^th^ century through
the present,) boost tourism, and coordinate and leverage local assets in
pursuit of NHA designation.

Dozens of sites within Barrington fall within the remit of the Sowams
Heritage Area as conceived. To date, there has been no official town
participation in the project.

Barrington was a key settlement area of the Pokanoket people.
Ousamequin, Massasoit (or "Great Leader") of the Pokanoket, may have had
his home at a spring at the intersection of Chachapacasset and Rumstick
Roads. The site is marked by a 1926 monument. The area around Sowams
Woods is believed to be a sacred area set aside for women and for
childbirth, a place of peace. Indigenous burial structures are also
present on the site. In 2006, the Barrington Land Conservation Trust
acquired Sowams Woods with the support of local, state and federal
funding and local philanthropists. The property encompasses 12 acres of
forested land with a network of trails bordering Echo Lake.[^4] 

A Sowams NHA would also include conserved land overlooking Hundred Acre
Cove at Nockum Hill (discussed in further detail, below,) as well as
town-owned parcels bordering Brickyard Pond, including the Barrington
Brickworks archaeological area.

The Barrington and New England Brick Companies manufactured bricks in
the Nayatt area for nearly 100 years, and this activity left lasting
impressions on the land. Resources include buried industrial sites in
the vicinity of Legion Way, standing buildings such as the former Nayatt
Hall on Middle Highway, and physical features such as Brickyard Pond (a
flooded clay pit) and Big Mussachuck Creek (a barge canal). Big
Mussachuck Creek is also one of just 18 original herring runs remaining
in Rhode Island.

Creation of a Sowams heritage district and/or NHA would provide a
framework for preserving and promoting an area of national historic
significance. Under the National Heritage Area program, the National
Park Service would provide matching grants for local investments in
projects that protect and promote the area's historic resources.
District recognition could also help leverage other grants and private
funds to further conserve open spaces within Barrington and undertake
projects to protect the unique natural and historic values of the area.

**Nockum Hill Heritage Area**

Nockum Hill is believed to be the site of the first Baptist Meeting
House in North America, built circa 1667. Early documents also identify
it as the site of the first battle of King Philip's War in June 1675.
The Town acquired the purported site of the meetinghouse in 2019 to
preserve the property from development. In 2020, initial archeological
investigations (metal-detection and ground-penetrating radar) performed
by the nonprofit Public Archeology Survey Team, Inc. (PAST), located the
possible meetinghouse site, as well as two possible 1667-75 English
homesteads. The survey also uncovered impacted musket balls in a pattern
consistent with a battle.[^5]

In 2024, Barrington was awarded a Preservation Planning Grant from the
National Park Service's American Battlefield Protection Program. With
this financial support, PAST conducted a second archeological field
survey of the historic settlement area to determine the scale of the
fighting that took place on and around Nockum Hill. The investigation
ultimately confirmed the location of the Nockum Hill meetinghouse.
Traces of defensive structures around the meetinghouse, a nearby burned
17-century homestead, impacted lead ball and shot, and a possible iron
arrow point likewise confirm that a violent confrontation between
English settlers and the local indigenous population occurred at the
site. It is likely that this battle was the flashpoint that set off King
Philip's War.

Given its significance in American history, it may be advisable to
pursue designation of the Battle of Nockum Hill site as a National
Historic Landmark (NHL). NHLs are historic properties that illustrate
the heritage of the United States and represent an outstanding aspect of
American history and culture. The National Historic Landmarks Program
operates under the auspices of the National Park Service. NPS staff
guide the nomination process for new NHLs and assist existing NHLs
throughout the country.

If designated as an NHL, Nockum Hill would be protected from any federal
action. Designation also facilitates eligibility for grants, tax
credits, and other opportunities to maintain historic character.

**Preservation of Historic Properties ** 

*Belton Court.* Of all the historic sites in Barrington, the future of
the 55,000-square-foot Belton Court remains a longstanding and locally
contentious issue. Once used for office and classroom space at the Zion
Bible Institute campus off Middle Highway, the building has remained
vacant since 2008 when the Institute relocated to a new campus in
Massachusetts. The move and later sale of Belton Court was partially
precipitated by the expense of maintaining the historic estate and
difficulty conforming to more stringent fire codes.  

The future use of the campus and Belton Court is yet to be determined.
The property is currently in private ownership and slated for
redevelopment (for further discussion, see CITE). Restoration of Belton
Court in an historically sensitive manner---in whole or in part---would
require significant investment.

*Possible State and National Historic Register Districts.* An ongoing
concern relates to development pressure on not only properties on the
Historic Register, but also Barrington's existing neighborhoods, most of
which were established 50 or more years ago. As a predominantly
residential suburban town, these neighborhoods represent the essential
qualities that define Barrington. With the exception of the Alfred Drown
Road and Jennys Lane National Register Districts, the only privately
owned residential properties with the Historic Register designation are
individual homes.  

The Town should pursue applications for additional National Register
Districts. Map X recommends several districts for further study. These
areas, most of which have multiple houses that have historic plaques
from the Preservation Society, are:

-   The Nockum Hill/George Street area. For discussion, see above.  

-   Johannis Farm.  This historic farm on Sowams Road contains
    agricultural fields, ponds, woodland, and marsh on the Palmer
    River.  

-   Lincoln Avenue from Washington Road to Townsend Street. Seven
    houses, ranging in date from 1870 to 1915, have been researched and
    plaqued by BPS, reflecting the historic popularity of this
    neighborhood as a residential district for commuters.

-   St. Andrews School. The original complex of Saint Andrews Industrial
    School, (founded in 1893) contains significant Shingle Style and
    Georgian Revival institutional buildings and has been previously
    recommended for listing in the National Register.

-   Nayatt Point. Approximately 30 houses along Nayatt Road, plus Elm
    Street and Glen Avenue, would comprise the Nayatt Point Historic
    District. This neighborhood illustrates Nayatt Point\'s history,
    from development in the 1850s as a summer resort, through the early
    twentieth century with construction of elaborate Victorian and
    Colonial Revival houses, many designed by the State\'s leading
    architects for the industrial leaders of Rhode Island. The district,
    as mapped, has nine houses with Preservation Society plaques. The
    district also contains two individual National Register properties:
    the Nayatt Point Lighthouse and the Aborn Jackson House (see Figure
    X). 

-   Honeysuckle Court. The five houses sited on Honeysuckle Court,
    ranging in date from 1950 to 1955, were built by a leading
    Barrington contractor. Each house is a variation on an historic
    Georgian design and was reputedly showcased to entice prospective
    clients to build a new home. Further study is needed to identify
    other houses in Barrington that were then built by Volpe to document
    the significance of this plat.

**Protection of Scenic Views**

Barrington enjoys a wealth of scenic resources, where the beauty of the
natural setting has been preserved or benignly modified by patterns of
development, as well as more than 20 public shore access points (see Map
HCR-X).  The Town\'s topographical character is defined by the interplay
between its land and waters, including the Bay, the various tidal
rivers, several coves and numerous freshwater ponds.  These large and
small bodies of water provide vistas ranging from the expansive to the
intimate that are enjoyed from both public and private land.

Three areas in Barrington are identified in the state inventory of
scenic areas (1990) and constitute scenic resources deemed important on
a regional and statewide level. The most significant risk to these views
may be cell tower installations, given the height of these structures.
The Town could consider impacts on scenic resources in evaluating
special use permits for cell towers. 

As yet, there has been no comprehensive survey of smaller scenic areas
that are important on a town-wide level. Such a survey would profitably
focus on the East Bay Bicycle Path corridor, especially at Brickyard
Pond and the Barrington and Warren Rivers; and along certain road
corridors, including Mathewson, Adams Point, Rumstick, Chachapacassett
and Nayatt Roads in the southern half of town. Other scenic areas are
visible from lower New Meadow Road along the Barrington River, and along
portions of the Wampanoag Trail, Washington Road and Middle Highway.  

An inventory of scenic views should also include views from the ends of
public rights of way---important view corridors that should be protected
from encroachment such as from the marina expansions or extensions of
docks off adjacent private parcels.

**Preservation of George Street's Agricultural Heritage and Rural
Character** 

George Street is an area of major historic significance for Barrington.
It is also where the preservation of the predominant agricultural use
and rural character are critical concerns. The Town's most critical
agricultural land is in this section of town, which includes portions of
the Douglas Rayner Wildlife Sanctuary at Nockum Hill and significant
parcels farmed by Four-Town Farm. However, the area also has undeveloped
parcels that, despite a lack of water and sewer and adequate roads, are
under development pressure. Currently, there are only 10 houses,
including two vacant, within the 75 acres zoned Residence
40-Conservation Development, where residential use is permitted. This
equates to just one unit per 7.5 acres of land in the R40-CD zone.  

The conversion of farmland to housing in this area consumes land
historically used for agricultural purposes, diminishes the rural
character and potentially impacts environmentally sensitive areas, in
particular the terrapin habitat within Nockum Hill. Also, more people
living in close proximity to active farming operations could make
farming more difficult due to complaints related to dust, noise, etc. 

These are not new issues. In 2012, the Town adopted a new zone for this
part of town---changing the area's Residence 40 zone to Residence
40-Conservation Development. The zone is intended to provide more
flexible subdivision design, with the goal of preserving important open
space and limiting the development footprint. The zone requires
subdivisions of five or more lots to meet the requirements of the R40-CD
zone, including setting aside at least 35 percent of the site as
contiguous open space. To date, no proposals have been submitted that
trigger the R40-CD requirements. 

An open question is whether the R40-CD zone offers sufficient protection
of the agricultural land use and rural character of the George Street
area, as the zone retains the current Residence 40 density. There are
additional measures---such as larger minimum lot size requirements or
the establishment of an agricultural zoning district---that could
further protect an area with significant agricultural resources,
sensitive habitat, and a historic, rural landscape reflective of
Barrington's early settlement. 

The Town also has had success with land acquisition and acquiring
development rights in the George Street area. The Town should emphasize
these strategies as it initiates or evaluates requests for open space
acquisition based on established acquisition criteria. 

**[GOALS, POLICIES & ACTIONS]{.underline}**

Goal HCR-1. Barrington will protect and preserve critical historic
properties and areas of historic significance throughout town.

Policy HCR-1.1: Discourage the demolition of historic buildings and
structures and encourage their adaptive use and preservation.

Policy HCR-1.2: Support the preservation of town-owned historic
buildings and the protection of town-owned properties with significant
historic or cultural values.

Actions

A.  Work with the Land Trust and other stakeholders to establish the
    proposed Sowams National Heritage District to enhance and protect
    scenic, historic, natural and recreational values within the
    district. Reinforce public awareness of the district through maps,
    wayfinding signage, educational panels, and other measures. 

B.  Appoint a Barrington representative to serve on the Steering
    Committee of the Sowams Heritage Area non-profit organization to
    represent the interests of the town in this regional initiative.

C.  Explore creation of a Nockum Hill National Historic Landmark (NHL)
    Site. Create a management plan for Nockum Hill that includes
    guidelines for public access (interpretive signage, trails, etc.)
    while conserving archeologically sensitive areas.

D.  Ensure that new construction at Belton Court occurring within the
    footprint of existing historic buildings is compatible with any
    preserved portions of the structure. Encourage utilization of
    similar materials to maintain visual consistency with preserved
    historic buildings. Recommend inclusion of informational signage,
    developed in conjunction of the Barrington Preservation Society,
    summarizing the history of the site.

E.  

F.  Develop and implement a management plan for the Civic Center
    National Register District. Plans should be developed with input
    from experts in historic preservation.

G.  Work with the Preservation Society to expand existing historic
    districts and to identify and list additional residential districts
    in the State and National Registers, per Map HCR-X.

H.  Appoint a committee to study the establishment of a Voluntary
    Historic District Commission. Such a committee could develop
    guidelines for restoration and protection of historic properties,
    based on the Secretary of the Interior's Standards for
    Rehabilitation and study possible local tax incentives.

I.  Adopt a stop-work ordinance which would allow a reasonable grace
    period for historic preservation consultation when a significant
    historic property is threatened with whole or partial destruction.

**Goal HCR-2**: Barrington will share its history and cultural heritage
with residents and visitors.

Policy HCR-2.1: Promote and encourage understanding of Barrington's
unique history through its cultural and historic sites.

Actions

A.  Support outreach and public awareness of the Barrington Preservation
    Society Museum within the Public Library.

B.  Support expansion of the BPS Museum to include archeological
    artifacts discovered at Nockum Hill, and promote awareness of the
    national significance of the site among residents and visitors.

C.  Work with community partners and stakeholders on public education
    projects that promote local history, specifically interpretive signs
    and plaques.

Goal HCR-3: Barrington will preserve its scenic resources and promote
public access and enjoyment.

Actions:

A.  Study parking access to scenic resources, particularly along the
    shore.

B.  Enforce early morning restrictions on parking for Barrington
    residents, such as Barrington Beach and Watson Park.

C.  Map smaller scenic areas that are important at local level,
    including ends of public rights of way providing views to the water.
