**[Natural RESOURCES]{.underline}**

**[Overview]{.underline}**

From the shores of Narragansett Bay to the wetlands of Hundred Acre
Cove, Barrington residents take immense pride in the Town's natural
environment and are committed to preserving and restoring its natural
resources. Barrington's fifteen square miles include scenic shorelines,
the beaches and waters of Narragansett Bay, the Barrington and Palmer
(Warren) Rivers, and a vital mosaic of woodlands, meadows, ponds, and
wetlands. It is this abundance of natural resources and scenic
landscapes that give Barrington its distinct sense of place.

Increasingly, however, the town's defining natural resource---its
lengthy shoreline---poses a significant challenge to the community.
Heightened risks of flooding and more frequent and damaging coastal
storms are projected to increase over the next twenty-five years,
underscoring the imperative to protect coastal and freshwater wetlands
and other sensitive areas from development.

Respondents to the Barrington 2050 community survey emphasized the
importance of protecting environmentally sensitive areas and mitigating
the impact of climate change on the natural and built environment. These
themes were likewise prominent in conversations with residents and
stakeholders. In coming decades, it will be crucial to balance growth
with conservation, allowing Barrington to remain a vibrant---yet
sustainable---place to call home.

Due to Barrington's geography, much of the town lies within the 100-year
flood zone (having a 1 percent or greater chance of flooding in a given
year,) and the velocity zone (coastal area within the flood zone likely
to be inundated by storm surge during hurricanes). These areas,
designated by the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) provide for
necessary floodwater retention, but also present a safety hazard to
existing and future development.

There are several Coastal Barrier Resource Systems Areas (CBRS) within
the town. CBRS encourages the conservation of flood-prone and dynamic
coastal barriers by limiting access to federal funding and financial
assistance. These areas include undeveloped and environmentally
sensitive coastal barriers that provide natural storm protection. In
general, the areas with the most significant constrains to development
occur in proximity to the shore---the Palmer, Barrington, Warren, and
Providence Rivers; Narragansett Bay; Bullock Cove; Brickyard Pond; and
Hundred Acre Cove.

[Wildlife Habitat]{.underline}

Three areas of Barrington have been identified by the Rhode Island
Natural Heritage Program as rare or endangered species habitats ([Insert
Map Ref]{.mark}). The largest and most important area is the Nockum Hill
section, which includes the town-owned Douglass Rayner Wildlife Refuge
and adjoining private conservation parcels and marshland to the east.
This land, bounding the north shore of Hundred Acre Cove, is the
critical nesting habitat for the state-endangered Northern Diamondback
Terrapin. The shallow, brackish waters of Hundred Acre Cove and the
sandy uplands of Nockum Hill provide ideal habitat, not only for the
turtle, but for other saltmarsh species and a variety of coastal and
migratory birds.

This designated Natural Heritage Area extends across Sowams Neck to the
marshland and adjacent upland bordering the Palmer River. Along the
river, it consists of a combination of tidal flats and emergent marsh as
well as shrub thickets, forested swamps and upland, including cleared
fields. The site supports at least five state-listed species of plants
and serves as a nesting site and habitat for numerous bird species.

A second Natural Heritage Area covers the coast north and east of Nayatt
Point, including the Providence River marsh at the mouth of Little
Mussachuck Creek and the saltwater wetlands south of Nayatt Road. A
third area includes the wetland along the east of Rumstick Point, Smith
Cove, and Adams Point.

No Conservation Opportunity Areas, as identified the Rhode Island State
Wildlife Action Plan (2015), lie within the boundaries of the town.

*[Management of Development Impacts]{.underline}*

The Town has adopted measures to help protect natural resources,
including the following:

-   *Wetlands Overlay District*. Barrington has stringent wetland
    protections in effect, with the adoption of the Wetlands Overlay
    District Zoning Ordinance, established as part of the Zoning
    Ordinance rewrite in 1994 (Article XXV). It requires the granting of
    a special use permit by the Zoning Board of Review, with advisory
    review by the Conservation Commission, prior to any development
    within 100 feet of either a coastal wetland or a freshwater wetland
    one-half acre or greater in size. The Wetlands Overlay District is
    more restrictive than State regulations requiring RIDEM approval for
    activity within 50 feet of a wetland. A 100-foot wetland setback,
    however, is a preferable standard in Barrington, where pressure to
    develop marginal sites will only increase as the community
    approaches build out.

Most applications for special use permits related to the wetland buffer
are granted. From January 2010 through August 2014, for example, 42
special use permit applications were before the Zoning Board for
encroachment into the wetland buffer. Of these, 38 were granted, while
two were denied (and two withdrawn).

-   *Groundwater Recharge Area*. Development proposed within the
    recharge area, as mapped on Map NCR-1, requires advisory review by
    the Conservation Commission.

*Other Standards*

-   *Stormwater design*. Applicants for qualifying development activity
    must develop and submit a stormwater management plan consistent with
    the Rhode Island Stormwater Design and Installation Standards Manual
    and the Rhode Island Soil Erosion and Sediment Control Handbook. The
    Planning Board in recent years has approved plans with low impact
    development features, such as at Walker Farm Lane, where surface
    runoff from the street drains into a retention pond, rather than
    through culverts and pipes; the development features narrow street
    widths and pervious pavement for on-street parking spaces and
    driveways. The Board has also granted waivers allowing private
    streets for subdivisions of up to two lots, allowing access through
    shared driveways that require much less pavement compared to
    development of a street to Town standards.

-   *Lighting standards*. The Town's standards require cut-off lighting
    fixtures in new parking lots; photometric plans are required to
    demonstrate neighboring properties will not be impacted by light
    pollution.

-   *Cell towers*. Cell towers, with the associated ground-mounted
    equipment and access road, can negatively impact scenic resources
    and conservation areas. The Town in 2012 adopted Zoning Ordinance
    amendments requiring an analysis of colocation opportunities and
    compliance with design standards (Sec. 185-209 of the Zoning
    Ordinance) as part of the cellular tower development plan review and
    special use permit process.

**[Issues and Opportunities]{.underline}**

*Sea Level Rise and Saltwater Ecosystems*

Coastal marshes are highly susceptible to impacts of sea-level rise
(SLR) due to increases in inundation. These marshes over time will
migrate inland---provided there are adjacent areas allowing for this
migration to occur. Many of Barrington's coastal wetlands are already
inundated at mean high tide. Map NCR-7 shows additional coastal wetland
areas that would become inundated with one, three and five feet of SLR.
The largest areas susceptible to inundation are around Hundred Acre
Cove, along the shores of the Palmer River, at Allin's Cove, at the RI
Country Club and Tillinghast Estate south of Nayatt Road, and at
Rumstick Point and Smith Cove. Virtually no areas of coastal wetlands
would not be inundated with five feet of SLR.

be lost in the event of 1', 3', and 5' of sea level rise\]

A more detailed analysis of impacts of SLR on coastal marshes has been
developed by the RI Coastal Resources Management Council (CRMC), in
partnership with other agencies and organizations. The Sea Level
Affecting Marshes Model (SLAMM) maps depict specific areas where marsh
migration might occur---based on factors such as topography as well as
impacts of roads, parking lots and other development.

Preservation of open space improves quality of life for Barrington
Residents, whether in the form of parks and trails, scenic views,
woodlands, farmlands, stream buffers, meadows, fields, or marshlands.
Open spaces serve functions vital to both the community and the natural

[Protection/Acquisition Priorities]{.underline}

Acquisitions of significant parcels throughout town for conservation and
open space protection purposes may become more difficult over time due
to the increasing land values associated with the limited supply of
available property in town, in particular with regard to developable
upland outside conservation areas. Acquisition criteria as well as input
on proposed land acquisitions from groups with potentially competing
interests --- such as the Park and Recreation Commission, Cemetery
Commission, Conservation Commission and Housing Board of Trustees ---
would help the Town weigh potential future uses of various sites.

The Town's Open Space Plan, presented to the Town Council in November
2011, offers a set of criteria for evaluating acquisition of
conservation/open space properties. Though these criteria have not been
formally adopted as official Town policy; the Planning Board has used
them as a basis to evaluate and submit recommendations to the Town
Council of requests for property donations offered by private
landowners. After fifteen years, however, it may be appropriate to
review and update the Open Space Plan to ensure it remains in alignment
with town priorities.

[Properties of Unknown Ownership]{.underline}

Acquisition of conservation/open space properties is typically a
straightforward negotiation between the Town (or other party, such as
the Land Trust,) and the property owner. This procedure is not
available, however, when ownership is unknown. Many such lots occur
within wetlands, rendering development unlikely, however acquisition
would ensure resource protection, settle ownership, and clarify
management responsibility. Targeted acquisitions in other habitat areas
would also provide an additional level of protection for these important
sites.

*Management of Open Space*

Another issue relates to the stewardship of conservation lands held in
public and private ownership. The Conservation Commission and Barrington
Conservation Land Trust have advocated adopting and enforcing official
management plans for Town-owned conservation lands across Barrington.
Concerns include encroachments from neighboring parcels, enforcement of
easements, illegal dumping and other deleterious activities, and public
access to environmentally sensitive areas.

Comprehensive management plans provide a framework for the protection of
publicly owned or otherwise protected conservation lands. At minimum,
each management plan should include a description of the area\'s
significant characteristics and features, management objectives, and a
prioritized implementation schedule. Management plans may also include:

-   Control of hydrological characteristics

-   Removal or control of nuisance/exotic plant and animal species

-   Preservation or restoration of native vegetation

-   Maintenance of habitat corridors

-   Monitoring encroachments from abutters

-   Enforcement of easements

Open space parcels should be inspected at least on an annual basis to
monitor compliance with the management plans.

The Town should also encourage development of Management Plans for
privately owned conservation areas. Plans for Land Trust and Town
conservation lands should articulate a consistent vision, given many of
these parcels are in proximity to one another. Other groups and private
landowners will be encouraged to develop management plans as well.

An issue to be evaluated in the Nockum Hill Management Plan is the
extent of the Diamondback Terrapins' habitat area. The Land Trust has
reported witnessing terrapins in areas beyond the Habitat Area mapped by
the State (see Map NCR-1). Additional study of Area 1a depicted on Map
NCR-1 is recommended to determine whether the mapped habitat area should
be enlarged.

**GOALS, POLICIES & ACTIONS**

Goal NR-1: Barrington will protect important natural resources,
including wetlands, wildlife habitats, groundwater, and coastal marshes.

Policy NR-1.1: Evaluate potential open space protections based on the
following criteria:

-   Cost: allows the Town to maximize its assets and leverage additional
    resources including grants, landowner donations, funding
    partnerships, and donations.

-   Community benefit: promotes benefits for the town at large---such
    providing land for recreational areas, expanding an existing open
    space network, or enhancing buffers.

-   Connectivity: adds to the existing or planned open space system and
    enhances ecological, hydrological, and recreational vitality.

-   Ecologically significant habitat (rare or endangered flora or
    fauna).

-   Protection of upland areas adjacent to coastal wetlands that are
    projected to migrate landward due to impacts from sea level rise.

-   Farmland preservation (preserve agricultural properties that may
    also qualify for protection under other programs).

-   Historic value (within a National Register Historic District and/or
    of significant cultural value).

-   Scenic value (aesthetic value and/or preservation of community
    character).

-   Property provides storm water storage, flood protection, or
    groundwater recharge.

-   Size of parcel (based on acreage).

-   Feasibility of purchase and likelihood of acquisition, including
    time seller can provide to allow Town to secure necessary funding.

**Actions**

A.  Review and, if necessary, update the town's existing Open Space Plan
    by 2027. \[Open Space Committee\]

B.  Protect open space parcels through acquisition, easements, or other
    mechanisms, especially within priority areas based on Policy NR-1.1.
    Conservation easements, donations and other alternatives to
    acquisition may be preferable on a case-by-case basis, particularly
    where the intent is to preserve environmentally sensitive areas in
    perpetuity.

C.  Identify and apply for grants or other funding sources to protect
    natural resources and open spaces, including sensitive habitat areas
    and farmland. \[Planning Office\]

D.  Explore opportunities for shared fund sourcing with the Barrington
    Land Trust \[Open Space Committee\]

E.  Develop a protocol for approaching private landowners about key open
    space parcels (in particular properties that are high priorities
    based on the Policy NR-1.1,) regarding owners' future intentions for
    their property and willingness to sell. The Town should work closely
    with the Barrington Land Conservation Trust on the outreach effort.

F.  Identify potential sources of funding for resource-protection
    projects, including but not limited to RI Dept. of Environmental
    Management, the Eastern Rhode Island Conservation District, or other
    private philanthropic organizations.

G.  Establish deed restrictions to ensure high priority town-owned
    conservation parcels cannot be sold or otherwise disposed of. \[?\]

H.  Rezone properties with conservation values, consistent with the
    Future Land Use Map (see X).

I.  Utilize non‐invasive native species in plantings on public property
    or public open space, provided such species are site‐appropriate and
    not cost-prohibitive. Prefer species that support native pollinators
    on public property and public open space.

Goal NR-2: Barrington will effectively steward open space to maintain
and protect critical natural resources.

Policy NR-2.1: Prioritize wildlife habitat protection, promotion of
pollinator and wildlife corridors, control of invasive species,
improvement of water quality, and flood mitigation strategies in
management of open space.

Policy NR-2.2: Encourage cooperation with private owners of open space
parcels to establish public access, enhance trail connectivity, expand
wildlife corridors, and coordinate management responsibilities.

Actions

A.  Develop and implement Open Space Management Plans for town-owned
    open space properties, including environmentally sensitive
    conservation areas, farmland, trails, and passive recreation areas
    within parks. Development of these plans will begin with priority
    sites, including:

-   Nockum Hill/Hundred Acre Cove

-   Allin's Cove

-   Hampden Meadows "Greenbelt"

-   Brickyard Wetlands

-   Walker Farm

-   Town Beach

-   St. Andrews Farm

B.  Undertake annual or semi-annual assessments of public open space to
    inspect conditions, identify maintenance needs, observe
    encroachments, and propose necessary capital investments. Annually
    review status of management plan implementation for all conservation
    areas.

C.  Work to remove invasive plants from conservation areas.

D.  Recruit volunteers to assist in assist in open space assessment and
    maintenance. Invite high school students, clubs, civic groups, and
    sports teams to participate in cleanups on an ongoing basis or
    during annual events, for example, Earth Day or the Barrington Day
    of Caring.

E.  Work with owners of private conservation parcels to develop new or
    coordinate implementation of existing management plans that are
    consistent with those adopted for public conservation areas.
    Priorities include:

-   Mussachuck Creek

-   Echo Lake/Sowams Woods

-   Johannis Farm

-   Tillinghast Estate

-   Other critical natural areas

F.  Eliminate encroachments into open space properties by abutting
    property owners. This action requires a Class 1 survey, which can be
    costly. To minimize costs, the Town should focus on identifying the
    most blatant encroachments first.

G.  Conduct a study of the habitat of the Diamondback Terrapin to
    determine whether the mapped Habitat Area on NCR-1 should be
    expanded.

Goal NR-. Barrington will support the capacity of its natural resources
to mitigate the deleterious impacts of climate change.

Policy NR-. Protect low-lying and coastal wetlands and coastal trees and
forests.

Actions

A.  Reduce stormwater runoff into wetlands and waterways via the use of
    green stormwater infrastructure (i.e. rain gardens, bioswales) on
    town properties.

B.  Explore and prioritize additional shoreline adaptation projects for
    low-lying, dead- end roads at the shore across municipality which
    may include pavement removal and green infrastructure to manage
    runoff and erosion.

C.  Identify best practices for stormwater management at Town Beach and
    install proper green stormwater infrastructure projects to minimize
    localized flooding and erosion

D.  Incorporate resilience landscapes on town-owned property and
    incentivize resilience landscapes within privately owned properties.

Goal NR-3: Barrington will expand its tree canopy to 55% coverage.

Policy NR-3.1: Preserve existing tree canopy and maintain public trees
for their value as green infrastructure and for their ability to
mitigate natural hazards such as stormwater, flooding, erosion, and
extreme heat.

Policy NR- 3.2: Promote planting of trees that are less prone to
breakage and failure and more resilient to climate change, on both
public and private property.

Actions

A.  Adopt an Urban Forest Management Plan in coordination with the Rhode
    Island Division of Forest Environment's Urban Community Forestry
    Program and the Rhode Island Emergency Management Agency.

B.  Implement a more aggressive tree maintenance and management program.
    Improve existing program to further reduce risk to people or
    property due to winds, heavy snow/ice, extreme heat, or other
    natural hazard impacts.

C.  Identify native tree species that will be more resilient to climate
    change and have higher carbon sequestration rates, for use in public
    projects and new subdivisions and land development projects. Create
    a list of undesired species which are invasive or prone to
    breakage/failure and identify these on the tree inventory for
    potential replacement.

D.  Develop outreach materials to educate the public on the benefits of
    trees and proper tree care.

E.  Conduct a land cover assessment every five years to track progress
    towards tree canopy goals.

Goal NR-4: Barrington will protect and restore surface water quality,
including in Hundred Acre Cove, Brickyard Pond, Prince's Pond, and
Annawamscutt Creek.

Policy NCR-4.1: Pursue cost-effective water-quality-improvement measures
and include projects in capital budget planning.

**Actions**

A.  Complete a watershed study within priority areas, including Town
    Beach and Brickyard Pond, to identify and prioritize future
    stormwater / water quality improvement projects, with an emphasis on
    "green infrastructure" such as rain gardens, where feasible. Prepare
    detailed plans as necessary to determine estimated costs and
    capitalize on funding opportunities available through the State or
    other sources.

B.  Prioritize and phase in implementation of watershed study
    recommendations, emphasizing the most cost-effective measures.

C.  Formalize a partnership (or consortium) with neighboring communities
    to adopt measures to improve water quality in Hundred Acre Cove.

D.  Develop an education/public outreach program on everyday uses and
    activities that contribute to the degradation of the water quality
    of local ponds and streams.
