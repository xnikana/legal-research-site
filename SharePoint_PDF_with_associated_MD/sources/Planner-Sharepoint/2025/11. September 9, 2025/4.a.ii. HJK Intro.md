**INTRODUCTION** 

Barrington 2050 is the town's 2025 update to its official Comprehensive
Community Plan, prepared in accordance with the Rhode Island
Comprehensive Planning and Land Use Act, RIGL § 45-22.2 (2024). This
plan establishes a framework for land use and growth management, while
addressing current and anticipated challenges within the town. As a
comprehensive roadmap, Barrington 2050 encompasses all aspects of the
planning process -- from vision-setting to implementation. Through
proactive planning, the Barrington 2050 aims to preserve the town's
identity as a vital and welcoming community for decades to come. 

[Plan Development]{.underline}

Meaningful comprehensive planning begins with robust community
participation. Public engagement for Barrington 2050 kicked off at a
public workshop in July 2024. More than 100 residents participated in
information-gathering exercises and round-table discussions concerning
various elements of the comprehensive plan. In addition, over 500
residents participated in the Barrington 2050 community survey. Survey
results identified priorities for Barrington's infrastructure, community
amenities, economic development, and recreational facilities.

Community stakeholders provided further insights through a series of 11
focus group interviews, which included conversations with
representatives of the Bayside YMCA, the Barrington Conservation
Commission, and local seniors. Interviews focused on current and
historic conditions, existing challenges, and emerging opportunities.

A 17-member Steering Committee participated in development of the plan.
Members were drawn from the Planning Board, Resilience and Energy
Committee, Library, Zoning Board, Parks and Recreation Commission,
School Committee, Bicycle and Pedestrian Committee, Economic Development
Committee, local nonprofits, and the general public. The Steering
Committee advised plan development and promoted public engagement.

All told, Barrington 2050 reflects the input of hundreds of Town
residents, local officials, and diverse stakeholders. With adoption by
the Town Council, Barrington 2050 is the official policy document of the
town.

[Major Themes]{.underline} 

Many issues Barrington will face in the next 25 years cut across the
specific elements described above. Through the plan-development process,
three themes emerged: 

*Access*---to diverse housing options, to recreational facilities, to
economic growth and opportunity, to a full range of town services, and
to a variety of transportation options. 

*Responsible Stewardship*---investment in upkeep of existing facilities
and infrastructure, protection of natural, cultural, and historic
resources, and preservation to those features that give Barrington it's
unique sense of character. 

*Resilience*---setting goals and pursuing policies that equip the town
to meet changing circumstances while preserving its identity. 

These guiding principles can help Barrington focus on its broader goals
in the face of competing needs and emerging challenges. Above all, this
plan envisions a town that is welcoming and accessible to all residents
and visitors, that protects its investments and resources, and that
preserves its unique sense of place in the face of inevitable change.

Prior to 1620, the lands now known as Barrington were the ancestral
homeland of the Pokanoket people. This nation, members of the Wampanoag
Confederacy, thrived amid the abundant natural resources of the area,
including rich shellfish beds along the shore, fertile agricultural
soils, mature woodlands, and navigable waters.

In 1641, a group of Pilgrim proprietors purchased an eight-mile tract of
land that included "the hundred acres of that marsh lying on the south
side of the bounds of Rehoboth now called by the name of the hundred
acres." Further purchases over the next fifteen years encompassed the
remainder of the land that now comprises the town of Barrington. The
town was initially incorporated in 1717. The area was ceded from
Massachusetts to Rhode Island in 1747, and in 1770, the Rhode Island
legislature declared Barrington an independent town.

Agriculture dominated the local economy for the next eighty years.
Development remained minimal and the area retained a predominantly rural
character. The Nayatt Brick Company was founded in 1847 and soon became
a leading local industry, attracting European laborers to the town. In
1855, the Providence, Warren & Bristol Railroad began service, spurring
further development around the rail stations.

By the mid-19^th^ century, farmland was being subdivided into new
housing plats. This development pattern persisted through the middle of
the 20^th^ century. The advent of the automobile, which eventually
supplanted the railroad as the primary means of transportation between
Barrington and Providence, strongly influenced the pattern of suburban
development throughout the town.

While Barrington's rural heritage is still evident in certain sections,
its agricultural character is greatly diminished. Major industry also
vanished by the late 20^th^ century, leaving Barrington in its current
incarnation as a suburban town defined by its mosaic of residential
neighborhoods.
