# RI Legal Research Guide — Section 8: The Open Meetings Act (§ 42-46)

Source: Rhode Island Public Records and Land Use Law: A Research Guide for Legal Practitioners
Prepared: March 2026

## 8. The Open Meetings Act (§ 42-46)

### 8.1 Overview

R.I.G.L. Chapter 42-46 governs the conduct of all meetings of Rhode Island public bodies.
Chapter index: https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE42/42-46/INDEX.htm

A "meeting" is defined broadly to include any gathering — whether in person, telephonic, or electronic — of a quorum of the members of a public body for the purpose of conducting public business. Planning boards, zoning boards, conservation commissions, and technical review committees are all "public bodies" subject to the Act.

### 8.2 Notice and Agenda Requirements

Public bodies must post notice of all meetings at least 48 hours in advance, excluding weekends and holidays. Notice must include the agenda with "a statement specifying the nature of the business to be discussed." A board cannot take action on matters not on the agenda, except in genuine emergencies.

Notice must be filed with the Secretary of State's Open Meetings portal:
https://opengov.sos.ri.gov/openmeetings

This portal maintains all meeting notices and agendas for RI public bodies and is searchable by entity, date, and keyword. A defective notice can be grounds to void action taken at the meeting.

### 8.3 Minutes and the Record

The Act requires that minutes be prepared for all public meetings, including a record of all votes taken. Minutes must be available to the public within 35 days of the meeting. For quasi-judicial land use proceedings, the minutes constitute part of the official record on appeal.

Where a public body's minutes are inadequate — failing to document the findings made, the evidence relied upon, or the votes on specific motions — this inadequacy can form an independent ground for appeal.

### 8.4 Executive Sessions

The Act permits executive (closed) sessions only for specified purposes enumerated in § 42-46-5, including attorney-client consultation, personnel matters, and certain litigation discussions.

Deliberations on a pending quasi-judicial application are not a permitted basis for executive session. Planning boards that retreat to executive session to deliberate on a comprehensive permit application are subject to challenge under the Act.
