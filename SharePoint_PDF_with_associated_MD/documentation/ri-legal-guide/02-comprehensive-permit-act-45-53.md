## 2. The Rhode Island Comprehensive Permit Act (§ 45-53)

### 2.1 Overview

R.I.G.L. Chapter 45-53, known as the "Low and Moderate Income Housing Act," is Rhode Island's primary mechanism for expedited review of affordable housing proposals. The Act supersedes (with limitations) local zoning ordinances and land development regulations.

Full chapter index: https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE45/45-53/INDEX.htm

### 2.2 Threshold and Eligibility (§ 45-53-3, § 45-53-4)

The Act applies when a municipality's affordable housing stock falls below 10% of year-round housing units. Eligible applicants must obtain a letter of eligibility from RIHousing before filing: https://www.rihousing.com/development/comprehensive-permit/

The Act's title encompasses both affordability tiers. Section 45-53-3 defines low-income housing as units at or below 80% AMI. A project providing only moderate-income units (80–120% AMI) without any low-income component may not qualify. This threshold question should be examined at the outset of any comprehensive permit proceeding.

### 2.3 Density Bonus Scale (§ 45-53-4(a)(3))

| Affordability Level | Authorized Density |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| 100% affordable | Up to 12 units/acre (maximum bonus) |
| 50% affordable | Intermediate bonus |
| 25% affordable (minimum) | 5 units/acre above base density |

This scale is frequently misapplied where developers seek maximum density while providing only the statutory minimum 25% affordability.

### 2.4 Required Findings — § 45-53-4(d)

A planning board may deny or condition a comprehensive permit application only upon finding it is not "consistent with local needs." Required findings under § 45-53-4(d)(2)(iii) include:

- Adequate fire, police, rescue, and other public safety services
- Sewer and septic capacity adequate for the proposed use
- Adequate water supply and surface water run-off controls
- No significant negative impacts on health and safety of current or future residents
- All required state permits have been or will be obtained

The board must make affirmative written findings on each element. Conclusory findings are fatal on appeal. See *Bernuth v. New Shoreham*, 770 A.2d 396 (R.I. 2001).

### 2.5 The Balancing Test — Local Concerns vs. Regional Need

The core framework requires the board to balance "the need for low and moderate income housing statewide and locally" against enumerated local concerns. A municipality's comprehensive plan constitutes a "local ordinance" within the meaning of the Act and may be relied upon as a basis for denial or conditioning. *East Bay Community Dev. Corp. v. Barrington*, 901 A.2d 1136 (R.I. 2006).

### 2.6 The Affordability Registry and Threshold Calculation

Current affordability threshold data for each municipality is maintained by RI Housing: https://www.rihousing.com/affordable-housing-resources/

A municipality above 10% may reject an application without making the balancing findings, though the developer retains appeal rights.
