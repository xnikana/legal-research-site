## 4. Rhode Island Fire Safety Code and NFPA Standards

### 4.1 The Adopted Standard: NFPA 1, 2021 Edition

Rhode Island adopted the NFPA 1 Fire Code, 2021 edition as its statewide fire safety code, effective March 1, 2026, via 450-RICR-00-00-7:
https://rules.sos.ri.gov/regulations/part/450-00-00-7

Prior to March 1, 2026, the 2018 edition was in effect. Practitioners must confirm which edition was operative at the time of the relevant application. The edition in effect at the time of building permit application typically governs the project.

Full text of NFPA 1, 2021 edition (free read-only, account required): https://link.nfpa.org/all-publications/1/2021
Free access registration: https://www.nfpa.org/for-professionals/codes-and-standards/list-of-codes-and-standards/free-access

### 4.2 Chapter 18: Fire Department Access and Water Supply

**§ 18.2.3.5.1.1 — Minimum Road Width**
Establishes a 20-foot minimum width for fire apparatus access roads. This is a floor, not a ceiling.

**§ 18.2.3.5.1.1.2 — Width Adequate for Apparatus**
"The width of fire apparatus access roads shall be increased when the minimum width specified in 18.2.3.5.1.1 is not adequate to accommodate fire apparatus."

This provision is critical in multi-family development disputes. The 20-foot minimum applies only where 20 feet is operationally sufficient for the jurisdiction's actual apparatus. For aerial ladder trucks with outrigger deployment requirements, the adequate width is apparatus-specific — determined by the truck's deployed outrigger spread, not merely its body width. Practitioners should obtain apparatus specifications from the local fire department via APRA.

**§ 18.2.3.5.2 — Surface Load Requirement**
"Fire apparatus access roads shall be designed and maintained to support the imposed loads of fire apparatus and shall be provided with an all-weather driving surface."

This requires a structural engineering certification for any fire access road incorporating permeable or pervious pavement. Modern aerial apparatus commonly exceed 75,000 lbs GVWR; when outriggers concentrate weight onto stabilizer pads, point loads can approach 261 psi — well above the 75 psi stabilizer pad limit in NFPA 1901.

**§ 18.4 — Fire Flow Requirements**
Table 18.4.5.2.1 establishes required fire flows by construction type and building size:

| Construction Type | Unreduced Flow (representative) |
|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Type I/II (non-combustible, steel/concrete) | 1,500–3,000 GPM |
| Type V (wood frame) | Up to 6,000+ GPM (large structures) |

§ 18.4.5.3.2 permits a 75% reduction with full NFPA 13 sprinkler protection. The developer must commit to the specific sprinkler standard. Where the sprinkler specification is undetermined at time of preliminary plan approval, the unreduced flow requirement applies.

**§ 18.5.1.3 — Non-Waivable**
Fire flow requirements cannot be waived based on unavailability of municipal water supply. Adequacy is a required condition, not an option.

### 4.3 The Station Nightclub Fire and Rhode Island's Post-2003 Fire Code History

The February 20, 2003 Station Nightclub fire in West Warwick killed 100 people and triggered sweeping changes to Rhode Island's fire code. The RI General Assembly enacted comprehensive amendments in 2003–2004:
- Mandatory sprinkler requirements for assembly occupancies above certain occupant loads
- Stricter egress requirements
- Enhanced inspection and certificate of occupancy requirements

Rhode Island courts and administrative bodies treat post-Station fire safety provisions with heightened seriousness.

### 4.4 Pending Single-Egress Legislation

As of early 2026, legislation was pending in the RI General Assembly that would eliminate the second-egress requirement for apartment buildings equipped with sprinkler systems. The Rhode Island Association of Fire Chiefs formally opposed this legislation.

RI General Assembly bill tracking: https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/BillText/BillText26/HouseText26/

### 4.5 NFPA 13 and Sprinkler Standards

NFPA 13 (Standard for the Installation of Sprinkler Systems), 2022 edition: https://link.nfpa.org/all-publications/13/2022

Where a developer proposes fire flow reductions based on sprinkler protection, NFPA 13 compliance must be demonstrated — not merely asserted. Key questions: (1) Is the full structure, including attached garages, covered? (2) Has the water authority confirmed supply pressure for simultaneous sprinkler demand and fire department hose stream requirements? (3) Is an NFPA 13 or NFPA 13R system proposed?

### 4.6 Apparatus Specifications via Public Records

Fire department apparatus specifications — manufacturer, model year, GVWR, outrigger spread, ladder length — are public records obtainable via APRA from the local fire department. These are essential for road width analysis under NFPA 1 § 18.2.3.5.1.1.2.

RI fire department directory: https://fire-marshal.ri.gov/ri-fire-departments
