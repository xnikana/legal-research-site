# RI Legal Research Guide — Section 6: Water Supply, Fire Flow, and Municipal Water Authority Research

Source: Rhode Island Public Records and Land Use Law: A Research Guide for Legal Practitioners
Prepared: March 2026

## 6. Water Supply, Fire Flow, and Municipal Water Authority Research

### 6.1 Research Methodology

Water supply adequacy is a required finding under § 45-53-4(d)(2)(iii). Demonstrating that fire flow requirements cannot be met from the municipal system is among the most powerful grounds for denial or conditioning — it is simultaneously a life safety issue and a technical deficiency not curable by a board condition.

**Step 1 — Identify the relevant water authority.** Rhode Island has numerous municipal and quasi-municipal water authorities. The water authority serving the development site may be a municipal department, a local water district, a regional authority (such as Bristol County Water Authority), or a private company. Identify the authority and obtain its enabling legislation, service area map, and contact information.

**Step 2 — Obtain the developer's hydraulic analysis.** Under § 45-53-4, the developer must demonstrate adequate water supply. The hydraulic analysis (typically prepared by a licensed PE) should be part of the application record. Request via APRA from the local planning department if not voluntarily submitted. Key figures: available fire flow at 20 psi residual pressure; pressure at peak domestic demand; simultaneous domestic + fire flow scenario.

**Step 3 — Obtain the water authority's response.** The water authority's "will serve" letter is a primary source document. Any letter that does not affirmatively confirm fire flow adequacy — or that conditions approval on future analysis — is itself a deficiency in the record. Under NFPA 1 § 18.5.1.3, a planning board cannot cure water supply inadequacy by condition; adequate supply must be demonstrated.

### 6.2 NFPA Fire Flow Tables

Table 18.4.5.2.1 of NFPA 1 (2021) sets unreduced fire flow requirements by building area and construction type:
- Type I/II (fire-resistive), 60,000–100,000 sq ft: unreduced requirement is 2,250–3,000 GPM
- Type V (wood frame), similar size: unreduced requirement can reach 3,500–6,000 GPM or more
- Even with the maximum sprinkler reduction (75%), required flows for large multifamily buildings substantially exceed 1,000 GPM

Where available fire flow is given as approximately 1,000 GPM, verify: (a) Has the developer committed in writing to NFPA 13 sprinkler protection throughout all structures? (b) Does 1,000 GPM represent the confirmed delivery rate at required residual pressure, or merely an estimated maximum?

### 6.3 Public Records Requests to Water Authorities

Municipal and quasi-municipal water authorities are "public bodies" under RI APRA (§ 38-2-2(4)). All correspondence between the developer and the water authority — including pre-application meetings, hydraulic analysis reviews, will-serve letters, and any follow-up information requests — are subject to APRA production.

A well-targeted APRA request to the water authority is often more productive than the same request to the planning department, as water authorities' correspondence files may contain technical detail that does not appear in the planning record.
