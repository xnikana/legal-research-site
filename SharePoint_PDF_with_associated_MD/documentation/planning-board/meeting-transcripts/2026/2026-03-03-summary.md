## 4. DEVELOPER / DPZ PRESENTATION

### DPZ Presentation — Ruth Sally Wike ("Solly"), DPZ CoDesign

Solly presented on behalf of DPZ CoDesign. She introduced the firm's credentials: 40 years in business, working in a roughly 50/60 split between public sector clients and private developers; creators of the "Smart Code"; publishers of urbanism literature; designers of walkable, economically and environmentally resilient communities.

**Key content of DPZ's presentation:**

**a. Context: Barrington Demographics and National Trends**
- 94.5% of Barrington's housing stock is single-family; only 5.4% is multi-family
- Comprehensive plan themes aligned with DPZ's approach: aging population, economic diversity, environmental resilience
- 30% of U.S. households are now single-person households
- 50% of Americans desire to live in walkable places
- 30% of U.S. households are cost-burdened
- **In four years, 1 in 5 Americans will be over 65; 75% of those will have no children; adults over 65 will outnumber those under 18** — creating a 20–30% gap in supply of walkable spaces
- **Barrington-specific:** 54% of Barrington households have NO children under 18. *"So we're seeing that trend that we saw in the national scale match up your reality today."*

**b. "Missing Middle Housing" and the Density Argument**
DPZ argued that density itself is not the enemy — form matters more than density. They showed two side-by-side diagrams: both sites are 4.4 acres with 100 units (22 units per acre), but one is a tower surrounded by parking and the other is a walkable neighborhood streetscape. The key line: *"Many of us would automatically assume that the building on the left is higher density, but the fact is that they're both equal — they're both 22 units to the acre. What's the difference? The density has been applied in a different form."*

**c. Building Types / Portfolio Examples**
DPZ walked through built examples from their portfolio:
- "Six-packs" (6-unit buildings): 36 units per acre — small multi-family
- Haversham, South Carolina: 24 units per acre townhouses
- Kentlands (Gaithersburg, MD): pedestrian-friendly townhouses at 20 units/acre
- Newtown St. Charles (Missouri): cottages at 12–15 units per acre
- Bermuda Village (Coral Gables, FL): 10 units per acre with initial community opposition that turned to acceptance after trails and open spaces won neighbors over; property values increased
- Northern Commons (Louisville, KY): mixed types

**d. The Five Master Plan Concepts for Belton Court Village**
1. Adaptive reuse of the stables and tower house (historic structures) — tower house preserved as a "terminating vista" focal point; stables adaptively reused
2. Integrated unique open spaces (cottage greens, small parks, paved plazas)
3. Higher density toward the center, tapering toward the edges
4. Mix of housing types
5. Safer streets

**e. Stormwater**
DPZ claimed the stormwater capacity provided is *"almost ten times the capacity that is required by the state"* and that it was designed as an amenity (trails, ponds) for residents.

**f. Street Design and Traffic Calming**
- Main streets proposed at 34 feet pavement width — parking both sides, two 10-foot travel lanes
- Comparison to Lakelands (MD) and Kentlands: Uber drivers naturally slow down due to parked cars on narrow streets — *"the streets are probably sized, they're properly designed and the intersections allow for their turning radius"*
- "Yield Streets" at 28 feet also proposed
- Referenced National Association of City Transportation Officials (NACTO) recommendation: *"10 feet generally provide the adequate safety in urban settings to encourage lower speeds"* — anything wider promotes unsafe speeds for pedestrians

**g. Commercial Hub**
A small-scale commercial destination proposed within Belton Court — modeled on IAM, South Carolina's small square. Intended to celebrate the history of the site and create a walkable social hub. Not yet programmed.

**h. Architecture**
Four architectural concepts: vernacular of the region; variety of housing types to serve multiple market segments; "small is beautiful"; prioritizing facade as a "gift to the street" (front doors, not garages, facing the street; garages in alleys).

**i. Historic Preservation**
Solly noted DPZ's involvement with the National Association for Historic Preservation. The plan preserves the tower and stables through adaptive reuse. The main residence/mansion building will be demolished (a waiver is being sought).

**j. DPZ's closing argument:**
*"You have the opportunity to create that value in your community and also set a bar of excellence in how you expect Barrington to move forward."*

### Traffic Impact Study Presentation — Paul Bannon, CrossFit Engineering

**Background:** Paul Bannon — 40 years practicing transportation and traffic engineering in RI; former consultant to RI State Traffic Commission for 9+ years; Barrington resident, daily driver through the project area.

**Study scope:**
- Site visits at different times/days
- Automatic traffic recorder (ATR) counts along Middle Highway: September, December, March periods (24-hour weekday and weekend)
- Intersection turning movement counts at: (1) Middle Highway / Primrose Hill Road (four-way stop); (2) Middle Highway / County Road (signalized intersection) — morning and afternoon peak hours
- Inventory of roadway conditions, sight distances
- Accident data from Barrington Police Department
- ITE (Institute of Transportation Engineers) trip generation manual
- Highway Capacity Manual (HCM) methods for level of service analysis

**Existing conditions:**
- Middle Highway: state-maintained, north-south, 2.5 miles; 25 mph posted; actual travel speeds roughly 30 mph
- North of Primrose Hill: 24–30 foot variable width, 10-foot travel lanes, 2-foot shoulders
- South of Primrose Hill: 26-foot width, two 12-foot travel lanes, 1-foot shoulders; signalized intersection at County Road
- Primrose Hill Road: approximately 0.5 miles, east-west; 26 feet wide, 11-foot lanes, 2-foot shoulders; 25 mph posted; actual speeds 30–35 mph average; **no sidewalks**; approximately 1,000 vehicles/day; 85–89 vehicles/hour AM peak; ~70 vehicles/hour PM peak
- Middle Highway along project frontage: approximately 1,600 vehicles/day; approximately 160 vehicles/hour AM and PM peak
- Sight distances at proposed access points meet state minimums
- **Accident data:** 17 total crashes across entire project area over approximately 3 years — fewer than 6 per year; mostly minor, signal-related

**Trip generation estimates (ITE method):**
- AM peak hour: 49 entering / 127 exiting = 176 total trips
- PM peak hour: 143 entering / 90 exiting = 233 total trips
- Bannon explained why numbers seem low: people do not all leave simultaneously; one household with three cars may generate zero trips in the peak hour

**Level of service findings:**
- Existing conditions: both intersections operate at LOS B or better
- Future conditions (with project traffic added): both intersections continue at LOS B; delay increase less than 5 seconds
- Three site driveways: LOS A with negligible delays

**Access improvements proposed:**
- Upgrade/formalize main intersection with the site
- New driveway to the north and south on Middle Highway
- Sidewalk installation on one side of Primrose Hill Road (concurred with Fuss & O'Neill recommendation)
- Formalized crossing at northern leg of Middle Highway/Primrose Hill intersection (curbs, sidewalks, crosswalks)
- Coordination with RIDOT for state highway work

**Responses to Fuss & O'Neill peer review comments:**
(Landry conducted a Q&A with Bannon for the record)
1. **Sidewalk on Primrose Hill Road:** Bannon concurs; consistent with town's Complete Streets plan and Safe Routes to School program; state recently completed a missing sidewalk section; Bannon agreed this is needed given increased volumes
2. **Formalizing the Primrose Hill/Middle Highway crossing:** agreed, will be upgraded with ramps and markings
3. **County Road as walking route for Primrose Hill School:** Bannon acknowledged higher pedestrian counts in the AM; noted middle school students use Middle Highway to walk/bike; completion of sidewalk links would improve safe routes
4. **Future no-build conditions clarity:** Bannon agreed they would clarify the no-build analysis presentation
5. **Trip distribution toward Route 114 (higher split to the east):** Bannon acknowledged some traffic will route via Rounds/Willet Ave to Rte 195 rather than going south to County Road and then north on 114 — he said he did not want to "inappropriately" examine that condition
6. **Route 114 U-turn safety:** reviewed, found no sightline issues at U-turn locations; agreed general driver behavior issues on 114 exist and the state should address them town-wide
7. **Driveway split allocation backup:** agreed to provide calculations
8. **Non-residential (commercial) traffic:** included conservatively using ITE methods; Fuss & O'Neill acknowledged it would be mostly internal to the community
9. **Census data undercounting (1,100–1,200 people):** no material effect on traffic calculations; only total count was questioned, not rate of change

## 7. KEY ARGUMENTS / CLAIMS MADE BY THE DEVELOPER TEAM

1. **Form matters more than density.** Two sites with 22 units/acre can look completely different depending on design. Walkable, pedestrian-first design is superior to tower-and-parking-lot design.

2. **Barrington needs this.** 54% of Barrington households have no children under 18; demographic aging is real and imminent; the "missing middle" housing market is unserved; this development provides what the community actually needs.

3. **DPZ is world-class.** They normally reject 9 of 10 projects; they accepted this one because it aligns with their vision and Barrington's comprehensive plan.

4. **Historic preservation is addressed.** The tower and stables are being adaptively reused; the main mansion is not feasible to preserve (waiver being sought).

5. **Stormwater is a feature, not a problem.** Ten times the required capacity, designed as an amenity (trails, ponds).

6. **Traffic will not be as bad as feared.** ITE and HCM standard methods (national/international standards); both key intersections remain at LOS B even with project traffic; delays increase less than 5 seconds; the development is at the minimum density bonus level; three driveways distribute traffic.

7. **Sidewalks will be improved.** Developer will work with state and town to install sidewalk on Primrose Hill Road and formalize the Primrose Hill/Middle Highway crossing.

8. **LMI affordability is real and monitored.** Rhode Island Housing's monitoring agent certifies every sale and resale; condo fees are factored into affordability calculations; 30-year minimum restrictions run with property; one in four units must be affordable throughout the build-out sequence.

9. **The project is near the minimum density bonus.** At 344 units, the developer argues this is close to the minimum density bonus under the state act, not a maximum.

10. **The development will be a 10–12 year build-out**, not immediate; phased construction.

11. **The small commercial component is internal/community-serving**, not a traffic generator.

12. **Narrow streets are intentional traffic calming.** 34-foot wide streets with parking on both sides slow cars naturally (the "Uber effect").

## 8. KEY CONCERNS RAISED BY BOARD MEMBERS

1. **Traffic analysis is too narrow.** The study only looked at two intersections; the board member wants Route 114 U-turns, turnaround points, and County Road traffic patterns to be included, particularly regarding northbound merges. *"I'm concerned we're not getting a complete picture of the traffic impacts."*

2. **Pedestrian safety on Middle Highway.** No sidewalks from Road C to Sherwood Lane on a state road the town does not control; students walking to Primrose Hill School and the middle school are at risk.

3. **Fire department access is unresolved.** Fire chief's comments not fully in the public record; ladder truck setup with parked cars on both sides of narrow streets is a concern; emergency vehicle access to interior alleys and rear of buildings is problematic; no marked-up site plan yet showing fire access modifications.

4. **Building height ambiguity.** Inconsistencies between 4-story maximum and 75-foot maximum height request; 25 feet of "architectural features" above a 4-story building is hard to justify; fire equipment capability for 75-foot buildings was not confirmed with the fire chief; no building in Barrington may be 75 feet tall.

5. **Waivers/adjustments not fully presented.** Marina Cori's absence meant the complete adjustment presentation was not made; the board has not been able to fully evaluate what is being waived.

6. **Fiscal impact analysis missing.** Existing 2023 analysis is outdated; unit types and square footages not nailed down enough for accurate analysis; school demographic impact analysis ordered by school committee but not yet available.

7. **LMI housing plan not defined.** No schedule, no site plan identifying LMI unit locations, no sample elevations for affordable units; the board is required to make affirmative findings about LMI production now, not at final approval stage.

8. **Affordability level.** Affordable units at 120% AMI (top of "moderate income") may not capture the full range of housing need in Barrington; board member encouraged exploring some units below 120% AMI.

9. **Long-term affordability with high HOA costs.** Private internal infrastructure (pervious roads, landscaping, stormwater) will generate ongoing expenses; board pressed for clarity on how monitoring accounts for high condo fees in affordability pricing.

10. **Density calculation not finalized.** Board still waiting for a precise, documented calculation of buildable acreage, density, and unit count.

11. **Setback adjustments reduce protection.** The original SRV required increasing setbacks as building heights increased; the proposed adjustment effectively allows taller buildings with the same setbacks, reducing the buffer for neighboring properties.

12. **Wetland setback compliance.** Buildings in the south are within the 100-foot jurisdictional wetland area; state approval needed.

13. **Impervious coverage language.** The application asks for maximums (35% lot coverage, 60% impervious) that are far higher than actual proposed figures (15.23% and 26.4%); the language alarmed residents and may be misleading.

14. **Alley screening standards.** Unclear which section of town ordinance governs screening between alleys and abutting residential properties.

15. **Rentals vs. ownership.** No rentals currently contemplated; board wants clarity before any building permits are pulled.

16. **The actual architect is not DPZ.** (This was noted in pre-meeting chatter; DPZ is the master planner, but "Summer Studio" — a Virginia residential design firm — appears to be the actual architect of record.)

## 11. TOPICAL INDEX — SPECIFIC MENTIONS

**DENSITY:**
- Base zoning (SRV): 6–8 units/acre; statutory density bonus: +5 units/acre = 11–13 units/acre total
- At 8+5=13 units/acre, approximately 321 units; at 6+5=11, high 200s
- Applicant's current proposed count: 344 units; applicant argues this is near the minimum density bonus
- DPZ showed example: two sites each at 22 units/acre with radically different visual forms
- Pre-meeting attendee asked why Carmelite Monastery site was at 2.5 units/acre vs. 11 here

**WAIVERS / ADJUSTMENTS:**
- Marina Cori (DPZ lead) was to present all adjustments; her absence means this presentation is deferred to a future meeting
- Key waiver identified: demolition of the historic mansion/residence building (the SRV requires historic preservation; waiver being sought)
- Height: requesting baseline up to 55 feet (vs. 35-foot SRV baseline); maximum up to 75 feet (vs. 50-foot SRV max)
- Setbacks: flat 25-foot setback for all buildings up to 55 feet regardless of abutting zoning (vs. SRV's variable setbacks)
- Coverage: applying 35%/60% standards across entire site rather than per lot
- Density bonus application methodology (exclusions of wetlands, roads, easements, floodplains from acreage)
