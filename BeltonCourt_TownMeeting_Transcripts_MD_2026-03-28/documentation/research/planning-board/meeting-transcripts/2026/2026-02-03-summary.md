### Bill Landry — Attorney Overview (approximately 30-45 minutes)

Landry introduced himself and the legal/regulatory framework, then gave a detailed overview of the project and the applicant's position:

**Regulatory framework:** He explained that the application proceeds under Rhode Island's Low and Moderate Income Housing Act — state legislation that operates above local zoning ordinances and reserves to the state legislature the authority to control affordable housing outcomes. He emphasized this is not ordinary local zoning. The state has set a benchmark of 10% of housing stock to be deed-restricted affordable in each municipality.

**Nature of the housing proposed:** Landry stressed the development is "moderate income housing" (120% of area median income), not low-income housing. He noted that for-profit developers do not receive external subsidies and must structure projects so market-rate units cross-subsidize affordable ones. He acknowledged lower income tiers may not be fully addressable through this project.

**The comprehensive plan:** Landry quoted at length from Barrington's 2010 comprehensive plan (updated 2015), which he said identifies the 33 Middle Highway site by name as perhaps the most targeted and suitable site in town for achieving affordable housing objectives. He noted the plan's first land use element statement identifies providing housing options and adding to affordable housing stock as "one of the most important land use issues." The 2015 update specifically notes the site as ideal given limited developable property with access to water/sewer. Landry argued this context makes the proposal extraordinary.

**Prior approval history:** He noted that less than 10 years ago (2016), the town had approved a project of roughly 300 units on this site — a senior residential village with independent living, cottages, and multifamily units. He acknowledged the current proposal differs (not exclusively senior) but argued the comprehensive plan itself discourages exclusively senior developments: "Rather than encouraging segregation of elderly in separate retirement institutions, urban designers should factor this social integration into more lively communities."

**Density argument:** Landry argued the density of 344 units (roughly 11 units per developable acre) is actually at or below the minimum density bonus available under state law. He explained: base zoning for the village district is 6-8 units per acre; state law provides a minimum density bonus of 5 units per acre when water and sewer are available; thus the minimum allowable density under state law is 11-13 units per acre. He stated: "We're not even getting to the minimum required density bonus amount on this project." (He later apologized for confusing his math in the initial explanation and clarified it on the record.)

**Waivers:** He acknowledged numerous waivers (now called "adjustments" under the STAAF) have been requested, but attributed this to the fact that subdivision design regulations are geared toward traditional single-family subdivisions, not campus-style multi-housing developments. He said DPZ would address these at the next meeting.

**Condominium / ownership structure:** He described the proposed condominium structure as a "land condominium" in which different housing types occupy separate land units within a single lot. He said this structure allows individual ownership of each unit type while ensuring a central homeowners association holds contracts for stormwater management, rubbish removal, utility connections, and maintenance standards. He said HOA documents would incorporate O&M conditions required by DEM and the town.

**Rhode Island Housing letter of eligibility:** He confirmed the project received a letter of eligibility from Rhode Island Housing for for-sale units, with affordable units priced mostly in the $200,000s (various price points by unit type). He said figures would be updated as the process proceeds.

**Agreement with staff/Fuss & O'Neill recommendations:** Landry walked through several areas of agreement with the January 29, 2026 staff report and Fuss & O'Neill report:
- Revisions to plans in writing as process proceeds
- Off-site improvements (sidewalks) to be addressed at final plan stage
- Dark-sky compliant lighting — agreed

- Walking trails and open space enhancements (including possible "victory gardens") — agreed to coordinate with recreation department
- Solid waste disposal/dumpster plan — agreed to provide more specifics

- Traffic study additional technical issues — traffic expert to address

- No neighborhood parking (no on-street parking outside the development) — agreed
- Rental component: if introduced, a new letter of eligibility from RI Housing would be obtained

**Belton Court / historic preservation:** Landry stated the developer proposes to demolish the main Belton Court building, a decision he said "was not made lightly" and follows five to six years of analysis. He noted there was a prior condemnation order keeping people out. He said portions — the "stables wing" and the tower — would be preserved. He emphasized feasibility testimony would follow.

### Nuriko Miyakoda — Cost Estimator Testimony (Belton Court Renovation Feasibility)

Attorney Landry qualified Miyakoda as an expert witness. She described 38 years of cost estimating experience, including as VP at Tishman Construction and Bar & Bar, and work on the MGM CityCenter ($9 billion) project in Las Vegas. She cited historic preservation project experience including Cornell University's Sage Hall, Vassar College's theater, and Morgan House (a New Jersey governor's mansion).

**Scope of her analysis:** She was engaged to estimate the cost of rehabilitating Belton Court into a 30-unit condominium complex (chosen as the use most likely to yield a return). She conducted site visits and prepared a cost estimate in 2022.

**Key finding:** Her estimate was **$50 million in construction costs** for the 30-unit rehabilitation in 2022 dollars — construction cost only (exterior and interior, structural stabilization). She testified that with project costs (adding ~25%), the total project cost would be $62.5 million in 2022 dollars. **Updated to 2025/2026 levels, she estimated $64-65 million in construction costs alone.**

She acknowledged that even 30 units was optimistic — preliminary architectural sketches suggested only 20 units might actually fit the building. At $2-3 million per unit construction cost, and with 25% required to be sold at $200,000-300,000 (affordable price points), she testified the math makes renovation economically infeasible under any scenario. She noted this analysis was submitted to the town with an invitation for peer review.

Landry asked: "Are these 50 or 65 million dollar figures anything that could be feasibly considered for any type of development that would involve the building?" She answered: "No."

### Charles Caloxi — Developer Vision Presentation

Caloxi introduced himself as principal of Shineharmony Holdings, a former managing director at Ernst & Young (real estate advisory and restructuring practices), now retired and focused on this project. He gave context for how he got involved:

**Background:** He said he was introduced to the property owners (a "genuinely wonderful family") through professional connections after they struggled with the feasibility of the 2016 senior village plan, particularly the mansion renovation. He said his initial observation — informed by prior work on a New Jersey estate rehabilitation — was that Belton Court renovation costs would be "astronomical." He engaged Miyakoda to validate that intuition.

**COVID's impact on senior housing:** He explained that COVID devastated the senior housing industry — occupancy fell, operators couldn't service debt, lenders threatened foreclosure — and the industry still has not recovered. This, combined with the renovation cost, made the 2016 senior village approach infeasible.

**The multi-generational vision:** The developer's proposal targets four groups: (1) "move-down" empty nesters / seniors who want smaller homes but want to stay in Barrington; (2) workforce / affordable housing for teachers, first responders, municipal workers, healthcare workers; (3) minimizing school-age children impact (small units, primarily 1-2 bedrooms); and (4) creating a high-quality, walkable community that elevates surrounding property values.

**Unit mix details:** Out of 344 units, the average square footage is under 1,500 sq ft; over 200 units are multifamily of various types; roughly 65% (almost three-quarters) are 1- and 2-bedroom units. He argued this mix is specifically designed to minimize school-age children. 25% of units will qualify as affordable per RI Housing requirements, interspersed throughout the community.

**Anti-senior designation stance:** Caloxi explicitly stated: "We really fight designating the community as a senior housing community." He cited longevity data showing seniors live longer in multi-generational settings and referenced his own community experience in Annapolis, Maryland and Charleston, South Carolina.

**Belton Court preservation:** He stated the "stables wing" (which lacks the problematic basement that has severe water/moisture issues) and the tower would be preserved for community use, potentially including a small retail use (e.g., a deli or market). The main mansion building would be demolished.

**Traditional Neighborhood Development (TND) design:** He described the project as a TND — associated with DPZ's founder as the "pioneer" of fighting suburban sprawl. Features include: tree-lined streets, unobstructed sidewalks, front porches, rear-loaded garages accessed by alleyways, pocket parks, village greens, reduced vehicle dominance. He showed comparative images of what they do not want (wide suburban roads, no sidewalks, no curb appeal) versus what they do want (walkable streetscapes, boutique-scale multifamily).

**Visual presentation:** He showed examples of developments in Rhode Island that exemplify the "wrong" approach (unnamed towns) and positive examples including a project in Annapolis (former hospital site, 100+ units in 5 acres, mixed housing types) and a DPZ project in South Carolina. He stated DPZ has worked in communities with similar concerns to Barrington's, and "in every single situation... the communities they designed have turned out to be the masterpieces of those towns."

**Fiscal impact / economic uplift:** He cited the Hudson Yards / Columbus Circle developer's view that the surrounding neighborhood often benefits more than the development itself from a high-quality project. He argued this development would increase property values in and around the site.

**Density plea and request for flexibility:** He stated: "We actually implore the planning board just to have an open mind and you know potential flexibility in maybe deviating from some of the conventional guidelines design guidelines that Barrington has adopted in the past."

**Affordable housing progress:** He acknowledged the project will take Barrington's affordable housing percentage from approximately 3-3.5% to approximately 5% — halfway to the 10% state goal. He noted: "I don't even know where else in Barrington something of this magnitude could be developed."

### Suez Santos (Insight Engineering) — Civil Engineering and Stormwater Presentation

Santos described herself as having 40+ years of civil engineering experience, a BS in Civil Engineering from UMass Lowell, and extensive RIDEM permitting experience.

**Site history (brief):** She walked through aerial photographs showing the site's evolution from 1939 (Peck estate) through 1953, 1962 (Barrington College dormitory/classroom construction), 1972 (large parking lot, gymnasium), and 1988 (Zion Bible purchase and subdivision creating Lion's Head subdivision).

**Existing conditions:** Site is 36.975 acres, addressed as 33 Middle Highway, zoned Senior Residential Village. Property line runs to East Providence. Existing remaining structures are the gym and Belton Court (including tower). Several buildings have already been demolished (shown as dashed red on plan). Structures to be demolished under this proposal are shown in yellow; structures to remain (tower and stables wing) shown in green.

**Access roads:** Two existing access points off Middle Highway — the main entrance (formerly served the school, intersecting Primrose Hill Road) and a divided southern entrance (formerly serving Belton Court / reflecting pool).

**Wetlands:** Wetland areas have been delineated in the field (flags placed). Wetland buffer zones range from 25 to 50 feet (depending on the type of bordering vegetative wetland) plus a 20-foot building setback beyond the buffer.

**Proposed road layout:** Three access roads from Middle Highway — Road B (north), Road A (main, 60-foot layout splitting into two one-way lanes), Road C (south). Roads B, C, D, E: 60-foot width. Road F (circular): 54-foot width. Road cross-section: 20-foot travel aisle, two 7-foot parking spaces each side, 8-foot bioretention/landscape strip, 5-foot sidewalk both sides.

**Alleyways / rear access:** No driveways or garages off main roads. Rear alleyways serve all single-family units. Parking accessed via alleyways — garages are 1-car (smaller townhouses) or 2-car (most units). On-street parking along roadways (7-foot x 20-foot spaces). No parking within 30 feet of intersections (coordinated with fire chief).

**Parking count:** Total of 517 parking spaces (on-street, garage, and off-street combined).

**Unit types on plan:** Large L-shaped multifamily condo buildings (shown in one color); six-plex multifamily (orange); townhouses 202 (dark purple); cottages (smaller single-family, various colors); larger single-family homes (blue-purple). Layout designed to intermix types rather than cluster them.

**Utilities:**
- *Water:* Single waterline connection from Middle Highway, looping through the site, one master meter/backflow preventer, billed through the HOA. Fire hydrants throughout.
- *Sewer:* By gravity through the project, tying into an existing easement created for Barrington College (the easement runs through Lion's Head Subdivision front yards and connects to County Road / East Providence sewer system). The sewer main in County Road is undersized and will need to be upgraded as part of the project.
- *Bristol County Water Authority:* Authority needs final building design details (materials, NFPA fire protection specifications) before issuing a final will-serve letter. Santos confirmed attaining compliance is well within scope once architectural design is finalized.

**Stormwater (the most detailed portion of her presentation):** She noted that at an informal planning board meeting prior to the formal application, Lion's Head subdivision neighbors raised concerns about existing stormwater impacts on their homes and asked: "Can you fix it?" The design was specifically engineered to address this.

Design approach: Low Impact Development — manage stormwater as close to where it falls as possible. Key elements:
- **Bioretention areas** (also referred to in the transcript as "fire retention areas" — likely "bio-retention") along roadways (within the 8-foot landscape strip between parking and sidewalks), planted with specific species and soils for infiltration and water quality treatment, with overflow connections to the main stormwater system for large storm events
- **Pervious pavement** (pervious asphalt) in alleyways and around the Belton Court / reflecting pool area, plus a plastic grid system around the stables for event access
- **Infiltration basins** at the upper end of the site
- **Detention pond** receiving overflow from the infiltration basin, discharging to the wetland

**Pre/post stormwater discharge comparison:**
- *Design Point A (toward Middle Highway), 10-year storm:* Existing peak discharge 8.32 cfs → Proposed 5.81 cfs (reduction of 2.51 cfs). Runoff volume: 30,529 cu ft existing → 14,723 cu ft proposed (reduction of 15,106 cu ft).
- *Design Point D (toward Lion's Head subdivision), 10-year storm:* Existing peak discharge 28.71 cfs → Proposed 25.15 cfs (reduction of 3.56 cfs). Runoff volume: 104,699 cu ft existing → 76,308 cu ft proposed (reduction of 28,390 cu ft).

Santos testified: "We can't make the problem go away completely, but we have greatly reduced the impact onto the abutting properties with our stormwater." She confirmed the design not only meets RIDEM regulations but exceeds them by also reducing runoff volumes (not just peak rates).

**Landscaping:** Stone wall along Middle Highway will be maintained. Portions of the wall will be removed for sight-distance at the new access roads (as required by road safety standards).

**Fire access:** She met with the fire chief, who provided the dimensions of the department's largest vehicle (the Barrington ladder truck). A turning radius/maneuverability analysis was prepared for every entrance and alleyway on the site. The 50-foot "docking line" around the perimeter ensures all front doors are within 50 feet of a fire truck staging position. She noted a scheduled follow-up meeting with the fire chief was cancelled due to a storm and will be rescheduled.

## 4. Developer Presentation — Key Claims Made

1. **The 344-unit density is at or below the legal minimum** under state law (base zoning 6-8 units/acre + 5-unit/acre state bonus = 11-13 units/acre minimum allowable; the project is just over 11 units/acre).

8. **The developer is not asking for low-income housing density** but moderate-income housing, targeted at workforce households (teachers, first responders, municipal workers).
9. **DPZ's Traditional Neighborhood Development design** has been implemented successfully in many communities with similar initial opposition; results have consistently been considered community assets.
10. **The developer intends to cooperate** with town on fiscal impact analysis, fire access, recreational open space, dark-sky lighting, snow removal planning, and other identified concerns.
11. **Waivers are a function of the design type**, not irregularities — campus-style development does not fit traditional subdivision design standards; DPZ will explain at March 3 meeting.

## 11. Key Topic Index

### Density
- 344 units proposed on 36.975 acres (approximately 11 units per developable acre after wetland exclusions)
- Developer argues this is at or below the state-mandated minimum density bonus (base 6-8 units/acre + 5-unit bonus = 11-13 units/acre minimum)
- Audience/residents characterize the project as 12 units/acre vs. 2.5 units/acre in Lion's Head
- Developer stated: "At the conclusion of this process, it may be more than that. It may be less than that" — then quickly walked back the suggestion of increase

### Waivers
- Multiple waivers ("adjustments") requested — attributed to mismatch between campus-style design and traditional subdivision regulations
- Waivers include (as mentioned in informal conversation): parking space size, open space per unit (300 sq ft requirement)
- DPZ to present full waiver justifications at March 3rd meeting
- Developer acknowledges waivers are extensive but argues they are standard for this type of integrated campus development

### Traffic
- Traffic engineer Al Bannon (Crossman Engineering) prepared a detailed traffic impact analysis
- Analysis reviewed by Fuss & O'Neill (town's consulting engineer); professional-to-professional comment exchange underway
- Three new access roads onto Middle Highway proposed
- No presentation made February 3; deferred to March 3
- Audience members referenced letters from the Barrington Land Conservation Trust president and others related to pedestrian/traffic concerns

### Stormwater
- Detailed presentation by Suez Santos; design exceeds RIDEM requirements at all design points
- Specifically designed to reduce (not just hold neutral) stormwater discharge to Lion's Head subdivision neighbors
- 10-year storm: flow toward Lion's Head reduced from 28.71 cfs to 25.15 cfs; volume from 104,699 to 76,308 cubic feet
- Features: bioretention strips in road cross-sections, pervious pavement alleyways, infiltration basins, detention pond
- Formal RIDEM stormwater permit to be obtained at final plan stage (not preliminary)

### Schools
- Developer argues unit mix (65% 1-2 bedroom, average <1,500 sq ft) is specifically chosen to minimize school-age children; claims "minimal impact" on school capacity
- Resident Paige Barber disputes this, alleging the $250 million school construction project (breaking ground April 2026) does not account for enrollment from this development
- Barber claims to have confirmed this with past and present school committee members and executives
- Developer's reference to fiscal impact: "the projected student loads from this project have been taken into consideration by the school committee as part of the planning for the schools"

### Parking
- 517 total parking spaces (on-street, garage, visitor)
- Garage parking at rear of units via alleyways (no driveways on main roads)
- 7-foot wide on-street parking spaces; waiver appears sought for space dimensions
- No parking within 30 feet of intersections (fire access requirement)

### Historic Preservation
- Belton Court (a/k/a Farrer Wall) listed on National Register of Historic Places in 1976
- Developer proposes demolition of main mansion; preservation of tower and "stables wing"
- Cost estimator Nuriko Miyakoda testified: $50M construction cost in 2022 dollars ($64-65M today) for rehabilitation to 30 (possibly only 20) units — infeasible
- Audience members (Paige Barber and others) strongly dispute this analysis; allege deliberate neglect by developer; plan to bring preservation experts to the record
- Condemnation order has been in place ("keep out" notices)

### Fuss & O'Neill
- Town's consulting engineer; reviewed traffic impact analysis submitted by developer's traffic engineer Al Bannon
- Prepared technical comments that are referenced in both the January 29, 2026 staff report and Bill Landry's overview of items under review
- Developer characterized Fuss & O'Neill's concerns as mostly addressable: "It's not our belief that there are any real showstoppers in terms of items that we've been asked to address"
- Engineering professionals "speaking to each other and doing their job as professionals" on traffic review
- Stormwater memorandum from Insight Engineering has been submitted and reviewed (by Fuss & O'Neill, presumably)

### Deadlines
- Original statutory decision deadline: March 29, 2026
- Extension agreed orally on the record: April 29, 2026 (one month); written extension to follow
- Continuation meeting: March 3, 2026, 7:00 p.m.
- Special meeting(s) for public comment: to be scheduled in late March or April

### Affordable Housing
- 25% of 344 units = 86 affordable units, deed-restricted and interspersed throughout the development
- Affordable units priced in the $200,000s (per Rhode Island Housing letter of eligibility, for-sale)
- Project proceeds under Rhode Island Low and Moderate Income Housing Act
- Developer is for-profit; no external subsidies available for affordable units; market-rate units cross-subsidize
- "Moderate income" = 120% of area median income (not low-income housing)
- Town currently at approximately 3-3.5% affordable housing; project would bring it to approximately 5% (state goal: 10% by 2035)
- Rhode Island Housing issued a letter of eligibility; a new one would be required if a rental component is introduced
